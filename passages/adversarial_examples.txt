! Adversarial Examples

[img[assets/adversarial_examples.svg]]

Imagine you are a master forger, but instead of painstakingly repainting a canvas, you only need to breathe on it — a change so slight that no human eye could detect it — and suddenly every art expert in the world calls the Mona Lisa a photograph of a gibbon. That is the essence of ''adversarial examples'': inputs to a machine learning model that have been deliberately perturbed by a tiny, often imperceptible amount, causing the model to produce a confident but completely wrong prediction. The image still looks like a panda to you. The [[Convolutional Neural Networks|Convolutional Neural Networks]] classifier says "gibbon" with 99.3% confidence. The gap between human perception and machine perception has never been more unsettling.

---

!! The Core Insight

Neural networks do not see images the way humans do. A human recognizes a panda by shape, texture, and context — robust features that survive small pixel changes. A neural network, by contrast, builds its decision on a combination of ''robust features'' (the ones humans also use) and ''non-robust features'' — statistical patterns in the pixel values that are genuinely predictive of the correct class on natural data but are brittle under small perturbations. Non-robust features are invisible to humans but carry real predictive signal. The model is not broken for using them; it is being rational about the training distribution. The problem is that an adversary can exploit these non-robust features by constructing a perturbation that flips their contribution while remaining invisible to the human eye.

This insight, formalized by Ilyas et al. (2019) in //Adversarial Examples Are Not Bugs, They Are Features//, reframes adversarial vulnerability not as a defect of particular architectures but as a fundamental consequence of how models learn from data. Any model that uses non-robust features — and standard training incentivizes exactly this — will be vulnerable. [[Adversarial Training]] explicitly trades off accuracy on clean data for <<link "robustness" "Robustness">><</link>> by training on adversarial examples, effectively telling the model to ignore non-robust features.

---

!! Attack Methods

Adversarial attacks fall into two broad categories: ''white-box'' attacks (the adversary has full access to the model's weights and gradients) and ''black-box'' attacks (the adversary can only query the model). White-box attacks are stronger because they can directly compute the direction in input space that maximally increases the loss.

''FGSM (Fast Gradient Sign Method)'' — proposed by Goodfellow, Shlens, and Szegedy (2014) — is the simplest white-box attack. It computes the gradient of the loss with respect to the input and takes a single step in the sign direction of that gradient. One forward pass, one backward pass, one perturbation. It is fast but not optimally strong.

''PGD (Projected Gradient Descent)'' — introduced by Madry et al. (2018) — is the iterative extension. Instead of one large step, PGD takes many small steps, projecting back onto the allowed perturbation set (typically an $\ell_\infty$ ball) after each step. PGD is widely considered the canonical first-order attack: if a defense survives PGD, it has passed a meaningful stress test.

''C&W (Carlini-Wagner)'' — Carlini and Wagner (2017) — formulates the attack as an optimization problem that directly minimizes the perturbation size subject to causing misclassification. It uses a different loss formulation to avoid gradient masking and is among the strongest white-box attacks, though it is computationally expensive.

''Black-box attacks'' exploit ''transferability'': an adversarial example crafted for one model often fools a different model trained on similar data. This means an adversary can train a surrogate model, attack it with white-box methods, and transfer the perturbation to the target. Score-based and decision-based black-box attacks query the target model directly, using estimated gradients or random search to find adversarial perturbations without access to model internals.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The goal of an adversarial attack is to find a perturbation $\delta$ that maximizes the model's loss while remaining small. Formally, given input $x$, true label $y$, model parameters $\theta$, and loss function $L$, the adversary solves:

$$\max_{\|\delta\|_p \leq \epsilon} L(f_\theta(x + \delta), y)$$

where $\|\cdot\|_p$ is typically the $\ell_\infty$ or $\ell_2$ norm and $\epsilon$ is the perturbation budget.

''FGSM'' solves this approximately in one step for $p = \infty$:

$$x_{\text{adv}} = x + \epsilon \cdot \text{sign}(\nabla_x L(f_\theta(x), y))$$

The sign function ensures every pixel is perturbed by exactly $\pm\epsilon$, maximizing the inner product $\langle \nabla_x L, \delta \rangle$ subject to $\|\delta\|_\infty \leq \epsilon$. This is the closed-form solution to the linearized inner maximization problem.

''PGD'' iterates this process with step size $\alpha < \epsilon$:

$$x^{t+1} = \Pi_{\mathcal{B}(x, \epsilon)} \left( x^t + \alpha \cdot \text{sign}(\nabla_{x^t} L(f_\theta(x^t), y)) \right)$$

where $\Pi_{\mathcal{B}(x,\epsilon)}$ projects back onto the $\epsilon$-ball around the original input. PGD with random restarts is the standard method for evaluating adversarial [[Robustness]] because it approximately solves the inner maximization that [[Adversarial Training]] uses.

The ''robust optimization'' framework views [[Adversarial Training]] as solving a min-max problem:

$$\min_\theta \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ \max_{\|\delta\|_p \leq \epsilon} L(f_\theta(x + \delta), y) \right]$$

The outer minimization trains the model; the inner maximization finds the worst-case perturbation. This is a saddle-point problem, and PGD-based adversarial training alternates between approximately solving the inner max (via PGD) and taking a <<link "gradient descent" "Gradient Descent">><</link>> step on the outer min.

---

!! Defenses and Robustness

''[[Adversarial Training]]'' remains the most reliable defense. By training on adversarial examples generated by PGD, the model learns to be invariant to perturbations within the $\epsilon$-ball. The cost is significant: adversarially trained models typically have 10-15% lower accuracy on clean data, require substantially more compute, and need larger model capacity to maintain performance. This ''accuracy-robustness tradeoff'' appears to be fundamental, not merely a limitation of current methods.

''[[Certified Defenses]]'' provide provable guarantees that no perturbation within a given budget can change the prediction. ''Randomized smoothing'' is the most scalable approach: it classifies based on the majority vote of predictions under Gaussian noise, and the resulting smoothed classifier has a provable $\ell_2$ robustness certificate. The radius of the certificate can be computed analytically. However, certified radii are typically smaller than what empirical defenses achieve, and certified accuracy drops steeply as the perturbation budget grows.

''Gradient masking'' defenses — including input transformations, distillation, and obfuscated gradients — were popular early on but have been repeatedly broken. Athalye et al. (2018) in //Obfuscated Gradients Give a False Sense of Security// showed that most defenses that appeared robust were actually just hiding useful gradients from the attacker. When the attacker circumvents the masking (via straight-through estimators, expectation over transformations, or reparameterization), the defense collapses. This paper established the modern standard for evaluating defenses: they must survive adaptive attacks that are specifically designed to defeat them.

---

!! When Defenses Break Down

''Adaptive attacks defeat non-adaptive evaluations:'' The most common failure mode in the adversarial robustness literature is evaluating a defense against attacks that are not specifically tailored to defeat it. A defense may appear robust against standard PGD but crumble against an adaptive attack that accounts for the defense's specific mechanism — gradient masking, input preprocessing, randomization. Carlini et al. (2019) showed that the majority of published defenses fall to adaptive attacks. Any defense must be evaluated against an adversary that knows the defense and actively tries to circumvent it.

''The accuracy-robustness tradeoff is steep:'' Tsipras et al. (2019) provided theoretical and empirical evidence that robust accuracy and standard accuracy are fundamentally at odds. Achieving high robustness requires the model to rely only on robust features, but non-robust features carry genuine predictive information for clean data. Throwing them away costs clean accuracy. For many practical applications, the 10-15% clean accuracy penalty of adversarial training is too steep, leaving practitioners with models that are either accurate or robust but not both. This tradeoff has not been resolved despite years of research.

''Perturbation models do not capture real threats:'' The standard $\ell_p$ threat model — small pixel changes bounded by $\epsilon$ — is mathematically convenient but does not reflect how adversaries attack systems in the real world. Real-world adversarial examples include physical patches, modified stop signs, 3D-printed objects, and domain-specific manipulations that fall outside any $\ell_p$ ball. A model that is robust to $\ell_\infty$ perturbations of size 8/255 may still be completely vulnerable to a small patch placed in the corner of the image, a change in lighting conditions, or a spatial transformation. Robustness to $\ell_p$ perturbations is a necessary but far from sufficient condition for real-world safety.

---

!! Technical Details

''Key papers:'' Szegedy et al. (2013), //Intriguing Properties of Neural Networks// — the paper that discovered adversarial examples, showing that imperceptible perturbations could cause confident misclassifications across different architectures. Goodfellow, Shlens, and Szegedy (2014), //Explaining and Harnessing Adversarial Examples// — introduced FGSM and the linear hypothesis: adversarial vulnerability arises because models are too //linear// in high-dimensional spaces, not because they are too nonlinear. The inner product $\langle w, \delta \rangle$ grows with input dimensionality even when each component of $\delta$ is tiny. Madry et al. (2018), //Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks// — formalized adversarial training as robust optimization and established PGD as the standard attack benchmark. Carlini and Wagner (2017) — demonstrated that defensive distillation and many other defenses fail against optimization-based attacks. Ilyas et al. (2019) — the features-not-bugs paper showing adversarial vulnerability stems from non-robust but predictive features.

''Transferability'' remains theoretically poorly understood. Empirically, adversarial examples transfer most reliably between models of similar architecture and training distribution. Ensemble-based attacks (crafting a perturbation that fools multiple models simultaneously) significantly improve transfer rates. This is the basis for practical black-box attacks.

''Robustness benchmarks:'' RobustBench (Croce et al., 2021) maintains a leaderboard of adversarially robust models evaluated under standardized AutoAttack (a parameter-free ensemble of diverse attacks). On CIFAR-10 with $\ell_\infty$ budget $\epsilon = 8/255$, the best models achieve roughly 65-70% robust accuracy versus 95%+ clean accuracy — quantifying the accuracy-robustness gap. On ImageNet the gap is even larger.

''Connections to generalization:'' adversarial training acts as a strong regularizer. Adversarially trained models learn smoother decision boundaries, produce more interpretable gradients (saliency maps align with human-recognizable features), and require more data to train effectively. The sample complexity of robust learning is provably higher than standard learning for certain distributions (Schmidt et al., 2018).

''Beyond classification:'' adversarial examples affect object detection (physical adversarial patches), speech recognition (imperceptible audio perturbations), natural language processing (synonym substitutions, character-level perturbations), and reinforcement learning (adversarial observations). The threat is not specific to image classification.
