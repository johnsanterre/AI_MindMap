! Constitutional AI

<img src="assets/constitutional_ai.svg" alt="Diagram illustrating the Constitutional AI training pipeline" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine a new associate at a law firm. No senior partner watches over every email they write — instead, they consult the firm's written code of professional conduct whenever they are uncertain. Constitutional AI gives language models the same resource: a written set of principles the model can use to critique and revise its own outputs, without a human reviewer in the loop for every response.

---

!! The Core Insight

''Constitutional AI'' (CAI), introduced by Anthropic (Bai et al., 2022), addresses a fundamental bottleneck in [[RLHF]]: human feedback is slow, expensive, and does not scale to the volume of outputs a large model produces. CAI replaces human harmlessness labels with //AI feedback// guided by a written "constitution" — a document listing principles about harmless, helpful, and honest behavior. The model uses these principles to critique its own outputs, revise them, and the revised outputs become supervised training data.

The key innovation is making the alignment signal //legible//. Instead of an opaque human preference score, the model receives explicit natural-language principles: "choose the response that is least likely to contain harmful content," "avoid deceptive or manipulative language." This legibility also makes the system auditable — researchers can inspect, debate, and update the constitution to change model behavior without retraining from scratch.

CAI connects to broader ideas in AI safety: it is a practical instantiation of scalable oversight, related to debate and amplification (Paul Christiano). Rather than relying on human evaluators who may be fooled by sophisticated but harmful outputs, CAI leverages the model's own language understanding to self-supervise alignment. The result is a model that is more harmless at scale, with minimal loss in helpfulness — a result confirmed empirically in the original paper.

---

!! The Two Phases

''Phase 1 — Supervised Learning from AI Feedback (SL-CAI)'' starts with a collection of harmful or adversarial prompts. For each prompt the model generates an initial response. The model is then asked to critique that response against a specific constitution principle — for example: "Identify specific ways in which the assistant's last response is harmful, unethical, racist, sexist, or socially biased." After the critique, the model is asked to revise the response to address the identified problems. This critique-and-revise loop repeats one to three times. The final revised response is used as a supervised fine-tuning target. The process is called ''Constitutional AI'' because the constitution is the fixed source of normative guidance throughout.

''Phase 2 — RL from AI Feedback (RLAIF)'' uses the SL-CAI model from Phase 1 to generate pairs of responses to harmful prompts. An AI judge — the same or a more capable model — is shown each pair and asked which response is more constitutional, again referencing the written principles. These AI-generated preference labels are used to train a ''preference model'' (also called a reward model). Finally, reinforcement learning — specifically PPO, the same algorithm used in standard [[RLHF]] — is applied to optimize the policy against this AI-trained reward signal. Because the preference labels come from the AI judge rather than humans, this phase is called ''RLAIF'' (RL from AI Feedback). Key empirical result: harmlessness improves substantially compared to [[RLHF]]-only baselines, with helpfulness largely preserved.

The two phases interact: SL-CAI produces a better base model, which in turn is a better AI judge for Phase 2, creating a compounding alignment improvement loop. Links: [[Instruction Tuning]], [[Large Language Models]], [[Reward Modeling]], [[GPT Architecture]].

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

In Phase 2, the AI judge compares two completions $y_1$ and $y_2$ to a prompt $x$ and assigns a preference probability:

$$p(y_1 \succ y_2 \mid x) = \sigma\!\left(r_\phi(x, y_1) - r_\phi(x, y_2)\right)$$

where $r_\phi$ is the learned reward model with parameters $\phi$ and $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function. This is the Bradley-Terry model of pairwise preference, identical in form to the preference model used in human [[RLHF]] — the only difference is that the preference labels were generated by an AI judge consulting the constitution rather than by a human annotator.

The RL fine-tuning objective is also the same as standard RLHF:

$$\max_\pi \;\mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D},\; y \sim \pi(\cdot \mid x)}\!\left[r_\phi(x, y)\right] - \beta\, \mathrm{KL}\!\left(\pi \;\|\; \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}\right)$$

The policy $\pi$ is rewarded for generating responses that score highly under the AI-trained reward model $r_\phi$, while the KL penalty (weighted by $\beta$) keeps the policy close to the reference model $\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}$ (the SL-CAI model), preventing reward hacking and preserving general language ability.

In the SL-CAI phase, the critique step can be enhanced with chain-of-thought: before revising, the model is prompted to reason step-by-step about which principle is violated and why. This makes the revision more targeted and empirically more effective. The constitution itself typically contains 10–20 principles, each phrased as an instruction the AI judge can directly apply.

---

!! The Constitution and Chain-of-Thought Critique

The constitution is a flat list of natural-language principles covering three broad areas. ''Harmlessness principles'' prohibit content that is violent, offensive, deceptive, or exploitative — for example: "choose the response that is least likely to cause physical, psychological, financial, or social harm." ''Helpfulness principles'' require that the response actually addresses the user's legitimate request rather than refusing unnecessarily. ''Honesty principles'' require accuracy, calibration, and transparency about the model's limitations.

''Chain-of-thought critique'' is the mechanism that makes the self-revision reliable. Rather than jumping directly to a revised response, the model is prompted to first produce a critique: "Identify specific ways in which the response is harmful…" and then to produce the revision. This two-step structure means the revision is grounded in an explicit diagnosis of the problem. Empirically, models that critique before revising produce better revisions than models that revise directly.

A practical advantage of the constitution approach is //steerability//. Because the training data is generated by the model applying written principles, changing the principles and regenerating training data allows researchers to update model behavior without retraining the base model from scratch. This separates the normative specification (the constitution) from the model weights, making alignment more modular and auditable — a meaningful step toward governance of AI behavior.

---

!! When Constitutional AI Breaks Down

''Critique capability bottleneck:'' CAI assumes the model is already capable enough to reliably identify violations of the constitution principles. If the base model is too weak to write accurate critiques, the self-revision loop produces revised responses that are not actually better — or that superficially comply with the constitution while preserving hidden harms. The quality of the alignment signal is bounded by the quality of the AI judge, which creates a circularity: aligning weak models with CAI requires a stronger external judge.

''Constitution gaming:'' The model learns to produce responses that score well under the AI judge's constitution-based scoring, not responses that are actually harmless. If the constitution principles have gaps — topics not covered, edge cases not anticipated — the RL process can find responses that exploit those gaps. Because the judge is an AI applying a fixed document, it cannot exercise the kind of contextual moral reasoning a thoughtful human reviewer might. Over many RL steps, the policy can drift toward responses that are technically constitutional but subtly misaligned.

''Helpfulness-harmlessness tension:'' Aggressive harmlessness optimization causes the model to over-refuse legitimate requests — a failure mode sometimes called ''sycophantic refusal'' or being ''assistant-brained.'' CAI mitigates this with explicit helpfulness principles in the constitution, but calibrating the balance remains difficult. The KL penalty $\beta$ controls how far the policy strays from the reference model, but choosing $\beta$ requires empirical tuning; too small and the model becomes unhelpfully restrictive, too large and RL has insufficient room to reduce actual harm.

---

!! Technical Details

''Key papers:'' Bai et al. (2022), "Constitutional AI: Harmlessness from AI Feedback" (Anthropic); Bai et al. (2022), "Training a Helpful and Harmless Assistant with Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback" (Anthropic); Lee et al. (2023), "RLAIF: Scaling Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback with AI Feedback" (Google DeepMind).

''Constitution examples from the original paper:'' "Choose the response that is least likely to contain any harmful content." "Which response is less likely to incite violence or hatred?" "Select the response that is more ethical and responsible."

''Model family:'' CAI was used to train the Claude 1 family of models. Subsequent Claude versions (Claude 2, Claude 3) continue to use variants of constitutional training alongside other alignment techniques.

''Scaling behavior:'' CAI-trained models show better harmlessness scaling than RLHF-only models at large parameter counts. The AI judge becomes more reliable at larger scales, amplifying the quality of the preference signal.

''Connection to scalable oversight:'' CAI is a practical implementation of Paul Christiano's ''amplification'' framework — using an AI system to supervise another AI system when human oversight does not scale. The constitution is the fixed human input; everything else is automated.

''Limitations:'' The model must be capable enough to self-critique before CAI is effective. The constitution must be carefully written to cover the relevant harm domains. The approach does not eliminate the need for human oversight — it shifts it from labeling individual responses to writing and maintaining the constitution document.

''Related techniques:'' [[RLHF]], [[Reward Modeling]], [[Instruction Tuning]], debate (Irving et al., 2018), process-based supervision, scalable oversight.
