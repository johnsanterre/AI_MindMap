! Network Flow

<img src="assets/network_flow.svg" alt="Network Flow: source-to-sink flow through capacitated edges, with max-flow min-cut theorem" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine a city's water system: a reservoir (the ''source'') pumps water through a network of pipes to a treatment plant (the ''sink''). Each pipe has a maximum throughput — a wide main can carry thousands of gallons per minute, while a narrow branch line carries only a trickle. ''Network flow'' is the mathematical framework for finding the most efficient way to push as much "stuff" as possible from source to sink without exceeding any pipe's capacity. The "stuff" can be water, data packets, vehicles on roads, or goods through a supply chain — the math is identical.

!! The Core Insight

The ''max-flow min-cut theorem'' (Ford and Fulkerson, 1956) states a profound duality: the maximum amount of flow you can push from source to sink equals the minimum total capacity you would need to sever to completely disconnect source from sink. In other words, the bottleneck of a network is determined by its weakest cross-section. No matter how cleverly you route flow, you can never exceed the capacity of the cheapest way to cut the network in two. This single theorem connects optimization (finding the best flow) with combinatorics (finding the smallest cut) and has implications reaching from [[Graph Theory]] to linear programming duality.

!! Augmenting Paths and Residual Graphs

The key algorithmic idea is the ''residual graph''. Given a current flow assignment, the residual graph shows where you can still push more flow forward (unused capacity) and where you can //cancel// existing flow by pushing backward. An ''augmenting path'' is any path from source to sink in this residual graph. If such a path exists, you can increase the total flow; if no augmenting path exists, the current flow is maximum.

The ''Ford-Fulkerson method'' repeatedly finds augmenting paths and pushes flow along them until none remain. The choice of //how// to find augmenting paths determines the algorithm's efficiency:

- ''Edmonds-Karp'' uses BFS (shortest augmenting path), guaranteeing $O(VE^2)$ time regardless of capacity values.
- ''Dinic's algorithm'' uses the concept of ''blocking flows'' in layered graphs, achieving $O(V^2 E)$ — faster on dense networks.
- ''Push-relabel'' (Goldberg-Tarjan) abandons global paths entirely, working locally by pushing excess flow from node to node, achieving $O(V^2 \sqrt{E})$ with the highest-label heuristic.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

A ''flow network'' is a directed graph $G = (V, E)$ with:
- A source $s \in V$ and sink $t \in V$
- A capacity function $c: E \to \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$

A ''flow'' is a function $f: E \to \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ satisfying:

''Capacity constraint:'' For every edge $(u, v) \in E$:
$$0 \leq f(u,v) \leq c(u,v)$$

''Flow conservation:'' For every node $v \in V \setminus \{s, t\}$:
$$\sum_{u:(u,v) \in E} f(u,v) = \sum_{w:(v,w) \in E} f(v,w)$$

The ''value of the flow'' is the net flow out of the source:
$$|f| = \sum_{w:(s,w) \in E} f(s,w) - \sum_{u:(u,s) \in E} f(u,s)$$

An ''$s$-$t$ cut'' $(S, T)$ partitions $V$ into $S \ni s$ and $T \ni t$ with $S \cup T = V$. Its capacity is:
$$c(S, T) = \sum_{\substack{u \in S, v \in T \\ (u,v) \in E}} c(u,v)$$

The ''max-flow min-cut theorem'' states:
$$\max_f |f| = \min_{(S,T)} c(S,T)$$

The ''residual capacity'' of edge $(u,v)$ is $c_f(u,v) = c(u,v) - f(u,v)$, and the reverse residual edge has capacity $c_f(v,u) = f(u,v)$. The residual graph $G_f$ contains all edges with positive residual capacity.

!! Applications in ML and Optimization

Network flow appears throughout machine learning and computer science in surprising ways:

''Image segmentation'' (graph cuts): Pixels are nodes, neighboring pixels are connected by edges weighted by similarity. The source represents "foreground" and the sink represents "background." Finding the minimum cut partitions the image into foreground and background segments, minimizing the total dissimilarity along the boundary. This connects directly to [[Bipartite Graphs]] through the structure of the pixel graph.

''Bipartite matching'': The maximum matching in a [[Bipartite Graphs|bipartite graph]] can be solved as a max-flow problem. Add a super-source connected to all nodes on the left, a super-sink connected to all nodes on the right, and set all capacities to 1. The max flow equals the maximum matching size. This solves assignment problems — matching workers to tasks, papers to reviewers, or training examples to clusters.

''Minimum cost flow'' generalizes max-flow by adding per-unit costs on edges. The goal becomes: push a required amount of flow at minimum total cost. This models transportation and logistics problems, and connects to optimal transport (see [[Shortest Path Algorithms]] for related shortest-path formulations).

Network flow also underpins the theory of ''linear programming duality'' — the max-flow min-cut theorem is a special case of LP strong duality.

!! When Network Flow Breaks Down

''(1) Integer capacities can hide exponential running time.'' The basic Ford-Fulkerson method with arbitrary augmenting path selection can take $O(E \cdot |f^*|)$ time, where $|f^*|$ is the maximum flow value. When capacities are large integers (or worse, irrational numbers), this can be astronomically slow or fail to terminate. The fix is to use Edmonds-Karp (BFS) or Dinic's algorithm, which guarantee polynomial time independent of capacity values. This is why algorithm choice matters — the method is correct but the implementation determines tractability.

''(2) Static graphs only — no native handling of time-varying or stochastic networks.'' Standard network flow assumes fixed capacities and a static graph. Real networks — traffic systems, communication networks, supply chains — have capacities that change over time, stochastic demands, and dynamic topology. Time-expanded networks (copying the graph for each time step) can model temporal variation, but the graph size explodes. Stochastic extensions exist but are computationally much harder.

''(3) Single-commodity assumption breaks in multi-commodity settings.'' Classic max-flow handles one type of "stuff" flowing from one source to one sink. When multiple commodities share the same network (e.g., different data streams sharing bandwidth, or multiple products sharing a supply chain), the problem becomes ''multi-commodity flow'', which is NP-hard for integer flows. Fractional multi-commodity flow can be solved via linear programming, but loses the elegant combinatorial structure and efficient algorithms of single-commodity flow.

!! Technical Details

''LP formulation:'' Max-flow is a linear program. The primal maximizes $|f|$ subject to capacity and conservation constraints. The dual minimizes the total capacity of a cut, and strong duality gives the max-flow min-cut theorem directly.

''Integral flow theorem:'' If all capacities are integers, there exists a maximum flow where every edge carries an integer flow value. This is crucial for combinatorial applications (matchings, edge-disjoint paths) where fractional solutions are meaningless.

''Menger's theorem:'' The maximum number of edge-disjoint $s$-$t$ paths equals the minimum number of edges whose removal disconnects $s$ from $t$. This is a special case of max-flow min-cut with unit capacities.

''Minimum vertex cut:'' To find the minimum number of //nodes// whose removal disconnects $s$ from $t$, split each node $v$ into $v_{\text{in}}$ and $v_{\text{out}}$ connected by a unit-capacity edge. The max-flow in this transformed graph gives the minimum vertex cut.

''Complexity landscape:'' For graphs with $V$ vertices, $E$ edges, and maximum flow value $|f^*|$: Ford-Fulkerson runs in $O(E \cdot |f^*|)$; Edmonds-Karp in $O(VE^2)$; Dinic's in $O(V^2 E)$; push-relabel in $O(V^2 E)$ or $O(V^2\sqrt{E})$ with heuristics. For unit-capacity graphs, Dinic's achieves $O(E\sqrt{V})$, which is optimal for [[Bipartite Graphs|bipartite matching]].

''Connections:'' Network flow generalizes to ''circulation problems'' (no source/sink, with demands), ''minimum cost flow'' (add per-unit costs), and ''network design'' (choose which edges to build). These connect to [[Maximum Flow]], [[Shortest Path Algorithms]], and broader [[Graph Theory]].
