! Neural ODE

[img[assets/neural_ode.svg]]

Imagine you are driving from one city to another. A traditional neural network is like hopping between a fixed set of stepping stones across a river — you land on stone one, then stone two, then stone three. A ''Neural ODE'' replaces those stepping stones with a smooth road. Instead of taking a predetermined number of discrete jumps, you glide continuously from start to finish, and an autopilot (the ODE solver) decides how many tiny corrections to make along the way. The destination is the same kind of useful representation, but the journey is fundamentally different: continuous, adaptive, and memory-efficient.

---

!! The Core Insight

A standard [[Residual Connections|residual network]] computes each layer as $h_{t+1} = h_t + f(h_t, \theta_t)$, where $f$ is a learned transformation and $t$ indexes the layer. This is an Euler discretization of a continuous dynamical system. The ''Neural ODE'' insight, introduced by Chen et al. (2018), is to take the limit: replace the sequence of discrete layers with a single [[Differential Equations|ordinary differential equation]]

$$\frac{dh(t)}{dt} = f(h(t), t, \theta)$$

and solve it from time $t_0$ to $t_1$ using a standard numerical ODE solver. The output $h(t_1)$ is the network's final representation. Instead of stacking $L$ layers with $L$ separate parameter sets, a Neural ODE uses //one// function $f$ parameterized by $\theta$ that defines the dynamics everywhere along the continuous depth axis. The solver adaptively picks how many function evaluations to perform — more in regions where the dynamics are complex, fewer where the trajectory is smooth. This means the effective "depth" of the network is not a hyperparameter you choose but a quantity the solver determines at runtime.

---

!! From Discrete Layers to Continuous Depth

The connection between residual networks and differential equations is more than an analogy. Consider a ResNet with layers indexed by $t = 0, 1, \ldots, T$. Each layer applies $h_{t+1} = h_t + f(h_t, \theta_t)$. If we set the step size to $\Delta t$ and write $h(t + \Delta t) = h(t) + \Delta t \cdot f(h(t), t, \theta)$, we recover forward Euler integration. As $\Delta t \to 0$ and $T \to \infty$ (holding $T \cdot \Delta t$ fixed), the discrete iteration converges to the ODE $dh/dt = f(h, t, \theta)$.

This perspective unlocks several advantages. ''Memory:'' a standard $L$-layer ResNet must store all $L$ intermediate activations for backpropagation, consuming $O(L)$ memory. A Neural ODE can use the ''adjoint method'' to compute gradients without storing intermediate states, reducing memory to $O(1)$ in depth — you only need the final state and can reconstruct earlier states by solving the ODE backwards. ''Adaptive computation:'' the ODE solver uses error control to place function evaluations where they matter, automatically spending more compute on inputs with complex dynamics. ''Parameter efficiency:'' since one function $f_\theta$ defines the entire depth, the parameter count does not grow with the number of effective layers.

The trade-off is compute: each forward pass requires multiple sequential evaluations of $f_\theta$ by the solver, and the number of evaluations varies per input. This makes Neural ODEs harder to parallelize and potentially slower than a fixed-depth ResNet of comparable accuracy.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The forward pass solves the initial value problem:

$$h(t_1) = h(t_0) + \int_{t_0}^{t_1} f(h(t), t, \theta) \, dt$$

The function $f_\theta$ is typically a small neural network (a few conv or linear layers) that takes $h(t)$ and the scalar time $t$ as inputs. The ODE solver (e.g., Dormand-Prince, a Runge-Kutta method) evaluates $f_\theta$ at adaptively chosen time points and assembles the integral numerically.

For backpropagation, the [[Adjoint Method]] avoids storing intermediate states. Define the ''adjoint'' $a(t) = \frac{\partial L}{\partial h(t)}$ where $L$ is the scalar loss. The adjoint satisfies its own ODE running //backwards// in time:

$$\frac{da}{dt} = -a(t)^T \frac{\partial f}{\partial h}$$

The gradient with respect to parameters is:

$$\frac{dL}{d\theta} = -\int_{t_1}^{t_0} a(t)^T \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} \, dt$$

Both the adjoint ODE and the parameter gradient integral are solved simultaneously by running the ODE solver backward from $t_1$ to $t_0$. This requires only $O(1)$ memory in depth, at the cost of recomputing the forward trajectory during the backward pass.

The continuous formulation also enables ''[[Continuous Normalizing Flows]]'': if $f_\theta$ defines a continuous transformation of a probability density, the log-density change obeys

$$\frac{\partial \log p(h(t))}{\partial t} = -\text{tr}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial h}\right)$$

This replaces the expensive determinant computations of discrete [[Normalizing Flows]] with a trace, which can be estimated cheaply using the Hutchinson trace estimator.

---

!! Applications and Extensions

''Continuous normalizing flows'' use Neural ODEs to model complex probability distributions. By defining a continuous transformation from a simple base distribution (e.g., Gaussian) to the data distribution, they avoid the architectural constraints of discrete normalizing flows — no need for coupling layers or autoregressive structure. The trace formula makes training tractable for high-dimensional data.

''Irregularly-sampled time series:'' Neural ODEs handle observations arriving at arbitrary times naturally. The hidden state evolves continuously between observations according to the ODE, and each observation updates the state. This is valuable in clinical data (lab results arriving at random intervals), financial data, and sensor networks — domains where forcing data onto a regular grid discards information or introduces artifacts.

''Latent ODE models'' combine a variational autoencoder with a Neural ODE in the latent space. The encoder maps an observed time series to an initial latent state, the ODE evolves that state forward, and a decoder generates predictions at any desired time. This provides smooth interpolation and principled uncertainty quantification.

''Augmented Neural ODEs'' address an expressiveness limitation: a standard Neural ODE cannot learn trajectories that cross themselves (since ODE solutions are unique given initial conditions). By augmenting the state with extra dimensions — lifting $h(t)$ to a higher-dimensional space — the trajectories can avoid crossing in the augmented space while projecting to complex mappings in the original space.

---

!! When Neural ODEs Break Down

''Stiff dynamics and solver cost:'' When the learned function $f_\theta$ develops sharp gradients or near-discontinuities, the ODE solver must take extremely small steps to maintain accuracy, causing the number of function evaluations (NFE) to explode. Training can become prohibitively slow because the forward pass itself becomes expensive. Unlike a fixed-depth ResNet where each forward pass costs exactly $L$ evaluations, a Neural ODE's cost is unpredictable and can spike during training as the dynamics stiffen — sometimes requiring hundreds of evaluations per input.

''Expressiveness constraints from uniqueness:'' The Picard-Lindelof theorem guarantees that ODE trajectories cannot intersect in state space. This means a Neural ODE acting directly on the data space cannot represent mappings where two different inputs need to pass through the same intermediate state. In practice, this limits the complexity of functions a Neural ODE can learn compared to a ResNet of equivalent parameter count. Augmenting the state space partially addresses this, but at the cost of additional dimensions and compute.

''Adjoint gradient inaccuracy:'' The memory-efficient adjoint method reconstructs the forward trajectory by solving the ODE backward, but numerical errors in the backward solve can accumulate, producing inaccurate gradients. When the forward dynamics are chaotic or the solver tolerance is too loose, the reconstructed trajectory diverges from the true forward trajectory. This can cause training instability. Checkpointed adjoint methods (storing a few intermediate states) trade some memory savings for gradient accuracy, but finding the right balance requires careful tuning.

---

!! Technical Details

''Key paper:'' Chen, Rubanova, Bettencourt, and Duvenaud (2018), //Neural Ordinary Differential Equations//, NeurIPS — introduced the Neural ODE framework, the adjoint method for constant-memory training, and continuous normalizing flows. Won the NeurIPS Best Paper award.

''Solver choices'' significantly affect performance. Adaptive Runge-Kutta methods (Dormand-Prince / RK45) are the default for non-stiff problems. For stiff dynamics, implicit solvers (e.g., implicit Adams or BDF methods) are necessary but more expensive per step. The tolerance parameters (atol, rtol) control the accuracy-speed tradeoff; loose tolerances speed up training but degrade gradient quality through the adjoint method.

''Regularization of dynamics:'' Adding a penalty on $\|f_\theta(h, t)\|^2$ or on the number of function evaluations encourages smoother dynamics and faster solving. Finlay et al. (2020) showed that regularizing the Frobenius norm of the Jacobian $\partial f / \partial h$ reduces NFE and improves generalization. This is analogous to encouraging the ODE to have slowly varying dynamics.

''FFJORD'' (Grathwohl et al., 2019) scales continuous normalizing flows to higher dimensions by using the Hutchinson trace estimator: $\text{tr}(\partial f / \partial h) \approx \epsilon^T (\partial f / \partial h) \epsilon$ where $\epsilon$ is a random vector. This reduces the cost of computing the log-density change from $O(d^2)$ to $O(d)$.

''Controlled differential equations'' (Kidger et al., 2020) extend the Neural ODE framework to incorporate driving signals (input paths), connecting to rough path theory. Neural CDEs are particularly effective for irregular time series and can be viewed as continuous analogues of RNNs, just as Neural ODEs are continuous analogues of ResNets.

''Comparison to discrete depth:'' empirically, Neural ODEs tend to be more parameter-efficient but slower to train and evaluate than equivalent ResNets. They are most advantageous when continuous-time modeling is intrinsically important (time series, physics simulations, normalizing flows) rather than as a drop-in replacement for standard architectures on tasks like image classification.
