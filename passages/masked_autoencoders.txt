! Masked Autoencoders

<img src="assets/masked_autoencoders.svg" alt="MAE pipeline: image patches → 75% masked → ViT encoder (visible only) → lightweight decoder → MSE reconstruction loss" style="max-width:100%; height:auto; display:block; margin:1em auto;">

Think of fill-in-the-blank exercises versus multiple-choice tests. Multiple choice lets you recognise the right answer without deeply understanding the material. Fill-in-the-blank forces retrieval from scratch. Masked Autoencoders apply this logic to images: hide 75% of the patches and make the model reconstruct the missing pixels. If you can do that reliably, you must have learned something deep about what images look like — without any human labels.

!! The Core Insight

''He et al. 2022 (MAE)'' brought BERT-style masked pretraining to vision. The recipe is deliberately simple. Divide an image into a grid of non-overlapping 16×16 pixel patches. Randomly mask 75% of them. Feed only the remaining 25% of visible patches to a standard [[Vision Transformer]] encoder — crucially, no mask tokens enter the encoder, which keeps computation low. A lightweight decoder then receives the encoded visible tokens plus learnable [MASK] tokens for every missing position, and reconstructs the pixel values of the masked patches. The training objective is mean squared error on those masked patches only. After pretraining, the decoder is discarded and the encoder is fine-tuned on downstream tasks, reaching 87.8% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet with ViT-H.

Key connections: [[Bert]], [[Vision Transformer]], [[Autoencoders]], [[Self-supervised Learning]].

!! Why 75% Masking and an Asymmetric Encoder-Decoder

The 75% figure is not arbitrary. Natural images are spatially redundant: neighbouring patches share colour, texture, and structure, so a low masking ratio (like the 15% used in [[Bert]]) is far too easy — the model can interpolate across the gap without learning anything semantic. Raising the ratio to 75% makes the task genuinely hard and forces the model to reason about the global structure of the image.

The //asymmetric// encoder-decoder design is the engineering insight that makes MAE practical. The encoder only touches visible patches — 25% of the total — so its self-attention computation is roughly 16× cheaper per image than a symmetric design where the encoder also processes masked positions. The decoder handles all N positions but is kept shallow (a few transformer blocks), so the extra cost is small. Together, the asymmetry makes MAE pretraining roughly 3× faster in wall-clock time compared to symmetric masked prediction architectures.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Let an image be divided into $N$ non-overlapping patches $\{x_1, \dots, x_N\}$, each of size $16 \times 16 = 256$ pixels. Sample a random masking subset $M \subset \{1, \dots, N\}$ with $|M| = 0.75 N$.

''Encoder'' — a ViT processes only the visible set $\{x_i : i \notin M\}$, producing encoded representations $\{z_i : i \notin M\}$.

''Decoder'' — receives a full-length sequence: encoded visible tokens at their original positions and a shared learnable [MASK] embedding at every masked position. Positional embeddings are added to both so the decoder knows where each token belongs. The decoder outputs a reconstruction $\hat{x}_i$ for each patch.

''Loss'' — computed only over the masked patches, with each patch's pixels normalised by their own mean and standard deviation:

$$\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{|M|} \sum_{i \in M} \left\| x_i - \hat{x}_i \right\|^2$$

Per-patch normalisation shifts the loss away from predicting low-frequency colour and luminance (easy) and towards predicting high-frequency texture and structure (hard and semantically meaningful).

!! MAE vs BERT vs Contrastive Methods

|                    | MAE              | BERT             | SimCLR / MoCo        |
| ''Prediction target'' | Raw pixels     | Discrete tokens  | Embedding similarity  |
| ''Masking ratio''   | 75%              | 15%              | N/A (augmentation)    |
| ''Decoder use''     | Discarded after pretraining | Same as encoder | No decoder |
| ''Scaling''         | Excellent (ViT-H wins) | Good         | Saturates earlier     |

[[Bert]] predicts discrete token IDs (from a visual tokeniser in BEiT, or a vocabulary in NLP). MAE predicts raw normalised pixel values — a simpler target that nevertheless works better at large scale. Contrastive methods like SimCLR and MoCo learn by pulling augmented views of the same image together in embedding space; they are powerful but require careful design of augmentation pairs and negative samples. MAE sidesteps this entirely. On linear probe evaluations (freezing the encoder, training only a linear head), contrastive methods can outperform MAE, but with fine-tuning, MAE's representations are stronger — particularly at ViT-L and ViT-H scale.

!! When MAE Breaks Down

''Pixel reconstruction is low-level.'' Even with patch normalisation, the model may learn to reproduce texture statistics and low-frequency structure without building truly object-centric representations. Methods like I-JEPA (LeCun's group) address this by predicting in representation space rather than pixel space, aiming for higher-level targets.

''Spatial redundancy assumption.'' High masking ratios work because natural images are spatially correlated. For data types with less spatial regularity — irregular point clouds, sparse tabular data, or structured text — the same masking ratio may not force the right inductive bias.

''Fine-tuning gap on linear probe.'' MAE's pretrained representations sometimes score noticeably lower than contrastive methods on linear probe benchmarks (no fine-tuning). This suggests the representations, while rich, may not be linearly separable without adaptation. If the downstream setting requires frozen features (e.g., fast retrieval), contrastive pretraining may be preferable.

!! Technical Details

''Original paper:'' He, K., Chen, X., Xie, S., Li, Y., Dollár, P., Girshick, R. (2022). Masked Autoencoders Are Scalable Vision Learners. CVPR 2022.

''Related work:''
- ''BEiT'' (Bao et al. 2022) — predicts discrete visual tokens from a dVAE tokeniser rather than raw pixels; closer in spirit to BERT.
- ''SimMIM'' (Xie et al. 2022) — simpler masked image modelling without the asymmetric encoder-decoder; uses a single linear head to predict pixels.
- ''VideoMAE'' — extends MAE to video with tube masking across frames; 90%+ masking ratios work because video is even more redundant than images.
- ''I-JEPA'' (Assran et al. 2023, Meta FAIR) — Joint-Embedding Predictive Architecture; predicts target representations in embedding space rather than pixel space, avoiding low-level reconstruction.

''Architectures used:'' ViT-B (86M params), ViT-L (307M), ViT-H (632M). Pretrained on ImageNet-1K. Evaluated on ImageNet classification, COCO object detection, ADE20K segmentation. MAE backbone with a Mask R-CNN head achieves strong detection performance, showing the representations transfer well beyond classification.

''Implementation note:'' Masking is implemented by random shuffling of patch indices followed by keeping the first 25%; no special masking mechanism is needed. The decoder is typically 8 transformer blocks with smaller width (512 dimensions vs. 1024 for ViT-L encoder).
