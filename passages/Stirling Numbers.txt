! Stirling Numbers

<img src="assets/stirling_numbers.svg" alt="Diagram showing Stirling numbers of first kind (permutations into cycles) and second kind (set partitions into non-empty subsets) side by side" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you have a group of people and need to seat them at round tables. How many ways can you split them into a given number of tables, where each table's seating is circular? That is exactly what ''Stirling numbers'' count. There are two flavors: the ''first kind'' counts ways to arrange $n$ elements into $k$ ''cycles'' (round tables), and the ''second kind'' counts ways to split $n$ elements into $k$ ''non-empty subsets'' (teams where order does not matter). Together they form a bridge between [[Permutations and Combinations|Permutations and Combinations]] and [[Partition Theory|Partition Theory]], connecting the algebra of polynomials to the combinatorics of grouping.

!! The Core Insight

Stirling numbers emerge from a single structural question: //what happens when you decompose a set?// Every permutation of $n$ elements can be uniquely written as a product of disjoint cycles. The ''unsigned Stirling number of the first kind'' $\left[\begin{smallmatrix}n\\k\end{smallmatrix}\right]$ (also written $|s(n,k)|$ or $c(n,k)$) counts the number of permutations of $n$ elements having exactly $k$ cycles. The ''Stirling number of the second kind'' $\left\{\begin{smallmatrix}n\\k\end{smallmatrix}\right\}$ (also written $S(n,k)$) counts the number of ways to partition an $n$-element set into exactly $k$ non-empty subsets. One is about ''arrangement'' (order within groups matters cyclically), the other is about ''grouping'' (only membership matters). Both satisfy elegant recurrences that encode a simple combinatorial choice: what does the last element do?

!! Two Kinds, Two Stories

''First kind -- cycles:'' When you add element $n$ to a permutation of $\{1, \ldots, n-1\}$, it either starts a ''new cycle by itself'' (contributing $\left[\begin{smallmatrix}n-1\\k-1\end{smallmatrix}\right]$) or ''inserts into an existing cycle'' in one of $(n-1)$ positions (contributing $(n-1)\left[\begin{smallmatrix}n-1\\k\end{smallmatrix}\right]$). This gives the recurrence:

$$\left[\begin{matrix}n\\k\end{matrix}\right] = \left[\begin{matrix}n-1\\k-1\end{matrix}\right] + (n-1)\left[\begin{matrix}n-1\\k\end{matrix}\right]$$

''Second kind -- partitions:'' When you add element $n$ to a partition of $\{1, \ldots, n-1\}$, it either forms a ''new singleton subset'' ($S(n-1, k-1)$) or ''joins one of the existing $k$ subsets'' ($k \cdot S(n-1, k)$):

$$S(n,k) = S(n-1, k-1) + k \cdot S(n-1, k)$$

The factor $(n-1)$ vs. $k$ is the key difference: in cycles, there are $(n-1)$ positions to insert into; in partitions, there are $k$ subsets to join.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''Falling and rising factorials:'' The first-kind Stirling numbers are the coefficients connecting powers of $x$ to falling factorials:

$$x^{(n)} = x(x-1)(x-2)\cdots(x-n+1) = \sum_{k=0}^{n} s(n,k)\, x^k$$

where $s(n,k) = (-1)^{n-k}\left[\begin{smallmatrix}n\\k\end{smallmatrix}\right]$ is the //signed// Stirling number of the first kind. Conversely, the second kind converts in the opposite direction:

$$x^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} S(n,k)\, x^{(k)}$$

These are ''change-of-basis'' formulas between the monomial basis $\{x^n\}$ and the falling factorial basis $\{x^{(n)}\}$ of the polynomial ring.

''Explicit formula for the second kind:''

$$S(n,k) = \frac{1}{k!}\sum_{j=0}^{k} (-1)^{k-j}\binom{k}{j} j^n$$

This is an inclusion-exclusion over surjections from an $n$-set onto a $k$-set, divided by $k!$ to forget the labeling of the subsets.

''Bell numbers:'' The total number of partitions of an $n$-set (into any number of subsets) is the ''Bell number'':

$$B(n) = \sum_{k=0}^{n} S(n,k)$$

The first few Bell numbers are $B_0 = 1, B_1 = 1, B_2 = 2, B_3 = 5, B_4 = 15, B_5 = 52$. The exponential [[Generating Functions|Generating Functions]] for Bell numbers is $\sum_{n \geq 0} B(n) \frac{x^n}{n!} = e^{e^x - 1}$.

!! Stirling Numbers in Machine Learning

''Clustering enumeration:'' When [[K-Means Clustering|K-Means Clustering]] assigns $n$ data points to $k$ clusters, the number of //combinatorially distinct// assignments (ignoring cluster labels) is exactly $S(n,k)$. This grows explosively -- $S(20,4) = 45{,}232{,}115{,}901$ -- which is why exhaustive search over cluster assignments is infeasible and heuristic methods are essential.

''Bell numbers and model complexity:'' The total number of possible clusterings of $n$ points is $B(n)$. This quantifies the //model space// that unsupervised methods must navigate. $B(20) = 51{,}724{,}158{,}235{,}372$, making brute-force evaluation impossible even for modest datasets.

''Partition-based methods:'' Spectral clustering, hierarchical clustering, and community detection all operate over the lattice of set partitions. The algebraic structure of this lattice -- whose size is counted by Bell numbers and whose $k$-level is counted by $S(n,k)$ -- determines the computational complexity of partition refinement and merging operations.

''Polynomial feature conversion:'' The change-of-basis between $x^n$ and $x^{(n)}$ via Stirling numbers appears when converting between //raw moments// and //factorial moments// in discrete probability, which matters for Poisson regression and count-based models.

!! When Stirling Numbers Break Down

''Combinatorial explosion.'' Both $\left[\begin{smallmatrix}n\\k\end{smallmatrix}\right]$ and $S(n,k)$ grow super-exponentially. For moderate $n$, the numbers exceed what standard 64-bit integers can represent, requiring arbitrary-precision arithmetic. In clustering, this means the partition space becomes computationally intractable long before $n$ reaches typical dataset sizes -- motivating greedy and stochastic optimization rather than enumeration.

''Unlabeled vs. labeled partitions.'' Stirling numbers of the second kind count //labeled// partitions (which element goes where), but in clustering the subsets are usually //unlabeled// (cluster 1 vs. cluster 2 is arbitrary). The number of //labeled// partitions is $k! \cdot S(n,k)$ (the number of surjections), and confusing the two leads to incorrect likelihood calculations in mixture models.

''Asymptotic approximation difficulty.'' Unlike binomial coefficients, which have clean asymptotic forms (Stirling's approximation for factorials, the normal approximation), Stirling numbers have complicated saddle-point asymptics. The leading-order approximation $S(n,k) \sim \frac{k^n}{k!}$ is only accurate when $k$ is fixed and $n \to \infty$. For $k$ growing with $n$, the analysis becomes much more delicate, and naive asymptotic formulas can be wildly inaccurate.

!! Technical Details

''Triangle structure:'' Both kinds form triangles analogous to Pascal's triangle. The first-kind triangle (unsigned) begins:
- $n=1$: 1
- $n=2$: 1, 1
- $n=3$: 2, 3, 1
- $n=4$: 6, 11, 6, 1

The second-kind triangle begins:
- $n=1$: 1
- $n=2$: 1, 1
- $n=3$: 1, 3, 1
- $n=4$: 1, 7, 6, 1

''Row sums:'' For the first kind, $\sum_k \left[\begin{smallmatrix}n\\k\end{smallmatrix}\right] = n!$ (every permutation has some number of cycles). For the second kind, $\sum_k S(n,k) = B(n)$.

''Orthogonality:'' The two Stirling number matrices are inverses of each other (with appropriate signs), reflecting the inverse change-of-basis between $\{x^n\}$ and $\{x^{(n)}\}$.

''Dobinski's formula:'' $B(n) = \frac{1}{e}\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{k^n}{k!}$, expressing Bell numbers as a weighted sum, useful for computation and for connecting to [[Combinatorics|Combinatorics]] and Poisson distributions.

''Historical note:'' James Stirling introduced these numbers in his 1730 //Methodus Differentialis//. The modern notation $\left[\begin{smallmatrix}n\\k\end{smallmatrix}\right]$ and $\left\{\begin{smallmatrix}n\\k\end{smallmatrix}\right\}$ was popularized by Knuth in //The Art of Computer Programming//.
