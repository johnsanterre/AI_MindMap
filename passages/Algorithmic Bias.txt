! Algorithmic Bias

<img src="assets/algorithmic_bias.svg" alt="Algorithmic Bias" width="800">

Imagine a company hires a new manager and tells her to pick future employees the same way the company has always picked them. She studies past hiring decisions, notices the patterns, and faithfully reproduces them — including the fact that the company has historically favored certain demographics over others. She is not malicious; she is simply doing exactly what she was trained to do. This is ''algorithmic bias'' in a nutshell: machine learning systems that inherit, encode, and amplify the prejudices already present in the data and decisions they learn from. The algorithm is a mirror, and what it reflects back is not always flattering.

!! The Core Insight

Algorithmic bias does not require a biased programmer or a malicious intent. It emerges from three interacting forces: //data that reflects historical inequities//, //design choices that prioritize certain objectives over others//, and //feedback loops that compound initial errors over time//. A [[Logistic Regression|Logistic Regression]] model trained on loan approval data from a bank that historically denied loans to minority neighborhoods will learn to deny loans to minority neighborhoods — not because anyone told it to discriminate, but because discrimination is the pattern in the data. When that model's predictions feed back into future training data, the cycle tightens. Each iteration makes the bias harder to detect and harder to remove, because the model's own outputs are now "confirming" the pattern it learned.

!! Where Bias Hides

Bias can infiltrate a machine learning pipeline at every stage, and the taxonomy of entry points matters for knowing where to look.

''Training data bias'' is the most obvious: if your dataset underrepresents certain groups, the model will perform worse on those groups. Facial recognition systems built predominantly on lighter-skinned faces fail dramatically on darker-skinned faces — a finding documented extensively in [[Computer Vision|Computer Vision]] research by Joy Buolamwini and Timnit Gebru.

''Label bias'' is subtler. In recidivism prediction, the label "re-arrested" is used as a proxy for "re-offended," but arrest rates are themselves shaped by policing patterns. The label does not measure what we think it measures. This connects directly to the problem of [[Spurious Correlations|Spurious Correlations]] — the model finds a statistical pattern that is real in the data but misleading about the world.

''Representation bias'' occurs when the sample does not match the deployment population. A hiring tool trained on resumes from a single tech company learns that company's idiosyncratic preferences, not general job competence. Amazon's infamous resume-screening tool penalized the word "women's" because the historical hires it learned from were predominantly male.

''Measurement bias'' arises when features are collected differently across groups. Credit scores, standardized test scores, and medical diagnostic rates all carry group-level measurement distortions that [[Neural Networks|Neural Networks]] and simpler models alike will absorb without question.

''Aggregation bias'' happens when a single model is applied to groups with different underlying distributions. A diabetes risk model trained on pooled data may work well on average but fail for specific ethnic groups whose symptoms present differently. Sometimes the responsible choice is to build separate models — though this raises its own fairness questions.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Fairness in machine learning is formalized through several competing metrics, and the central tragedy is that they cannot all be satisfied simultaneously.

Let $A$ denote a protected attribute (e.g., race or gender), $Y$ the true outcome, and $\hat{Y}$ the model's prediction.

''Demographic parity'' (also called statistical parity) requires that the prediction be independent of the protected attribute:

$$P(\hat{Y} = 1 \mid A = 0) = P(\hat{Y} = 1 \mid A = 1)$$

This says: the model should approve (or flag, or recommend) at equal rates across groups.

''Equalized odds'' requires that the true positive rate and false positive rate be equal across groups:

$$P(\hat{Y} = 1 \mid Y = 1, A = 0) = P(\hat{Y} = 1 \mid Y = 1, A = 1)$$
$$P(\hat{Y} = 1 \mid Y = 0, A = 0) = P(\hat{Y} = 1 \mid Y = 0, A = 1)$$

This says: the model should be equally accurate (and equally wrong) for each group.

''Calibration'' requires that among those receiving a given score $s$, the actual positive rate be the same across groups:

$$P(Y = 1 \mid \hat{Y} = s, A = 0) = P(Y = 1 \mid \hat{Y} = s, A = 1)$$

The ''impossibility theorem'' (Chouldechova, 2017; Kleinberg, Mullainathan, & Raghavan, 2016) proves that when base rates differ across groups ($P(Y=1 \mid A=0) \neq P(Y=1 \mid A=1)$), it is mathematically impossible to simultaneously satisfy calibration and equalized odds — except in the degenerate case of a perfect predictor. This is not a technical limitation to be engineered around; it is a fundamental constraint. Choosing which fairness criterion to satisfy is an //ethical// decision, not a mathematical one.

''Individual fairness'' takes a different approach, requiring that similar individuals receive similar predictions. Formally, for a distance metric $d$ over individuals and a distance $D$ over distributions:

$$D(\hat{Y}(x_i), \hat{Y}(x_j)) \leq d(x_i, x_j)$$

The challenge is defining what "similar" means — which itself can embed bias.

!! Fighting Bias in Practice

Debiasing interventions fall into three categories based on where in the pipeline they operate.

''Pre-processing'' methods modify the training data before it reaches the model. //Resampling// adjusts group proportions. //Reweighting// assigns higher weight to underrepresented group-outcome combinations. //Disparate impact removal// transforms feature values to reduce correlation with the protected attribute. These methods are model-agnostic, which makes them appealing, but they can distort the data distribution in ways that reduce performance or introduce new artifacts.

''In-processing'' methods modify the learning algorithm itself. //Constrained optimization// adds fairness constraints directly to the loss function, treating fairness as a side condition alongside accuracy. //Adversarial debiasing// trains an adversary network to predict the protected attribute from the model's predictions; the main model is penalized when the adversary succeeds, which connects to the broader technique of [[Adversarial Training|Adversarial Training]]. [[Regularization|Regularization]] penalties can also be shaped to discourage reliance on protected-attribute proxies.

''Post-processing'' methods adjust the model's outputs after training. //Threshold adjustment// sets different classification thresholds for different groups to equalize a chosen fairness metric. This is simple and transparent, but it means the model is explicitly treating groups differently, which can raise legal and ethical concerns.

Beyond these technical tools, ''fairness auditing'' involves systematically evaluating model performance across demographic subgroups, using tools like IBM's AI Fairness 360, Google's What-If Tool, or Microsoft's Fairlearn. Effective auditing also requires understanding causal structure — [[Causal Inference|Causal Inference]] methods help distinguish between features that are //legitimately predictive// and features that are //proxies for discrimination//. The related field of [[Differential Privacy|Differential Privacy]] addresses a complementary concern: preventing models from leaking information about individuals in the training set.

!! When Fairness Interventions Break Down

- ''Proxy persistence.'' Removing a protected attribute (e.g., race) from the feature set rarely eliminates bias because other features — zip code, name, purchasing patterns — serve as proxies. In high-dimensional spaces used by [[Neural Networks|Neural Networks]], proxies can be complex nonlinear combinations that no human auditor would think to check. The bias goes underground rather than disappearing.

- ''Fairness gerrymandering.'' A model may satisfy a fairness criterion for each protected group individually (e.g., equal accuracy for men and women, and equal accuracy for Black and white individuals) while still being unfair to //intersectional// subgroups (e.g., Black women). As the number of intersecting categories grows, the subgroups become smaller and statistical guarantees weaken, making it possible to appear fair by every standard metric while still causing harm to specific communities.

- ''Distributional shift undermines calibration.'' A model carefully calibrated for fairness at deployment time can become unfair as the world changes. If a lending model's fairness was tuned using 2019 economic data and deployed during a recession that disproportionately affects certain groups, the calibration breaks. Without continuous monitoring, the model drifts from fair to unfair silently — and the feedback loop begins generating biased training data for the next iteration, as scholars like [[Amba Kak|Amba Kak]] have emphasized in AI governance research.

!! Technical Details

The academic study of algorithmic bias draws heavily on the ''Barocas and Selbst (2016)'' taxonomy, which distinguishes five mechanisms by which bias enters automated decisions: target variable definition, training data composition, feature selection, proxy discrimination, and masking. This framework remains the standard reference.

The ''ProPublica vs. Northpointe debate'' (2016) crystallized the impossibility theorem in public discourse. ProPublica showed that the COMPAS recidivism tool had higher false positive rates for Black defendants (equalized odds violation). Northpointe responded that COMPAS was well-calibrated: among defendants assigned a given risk score, recidivism rates were similar across races. Both claims were true — and the impossibility theorem explains why fixing one would break the other when base rates differ.

''Intersectionality'' — a concept from legal scholar Kimberlé Crenshaw — has been formalized in ML fairness as the requirement to evaluate performance across combinations of protected attributes, not just individual attributes. The ''Gerrymandering'' result (Kearns et al., 2018) showed that fairness over individual subgroups does not guarantee fairness over intersections.

The ''fairness-accuracy tradeoff'' is real but often overstated. In many practical settings, the cost to accuracy from imposing fairness constraints is modest (1-3%), suggesting that unfairness is not "buying" much predictive power. However, in settings with high class imbalance or noisy labels, the tradeoff sharpens. [[Decision Trees|Decision Trees]] and interpretable models sometimes offer better auditability than deep networks, though at a potential performance cost.

''Group fairness vs. individual fairness'' remains an active tension. Group metrics (demographic parity, equalized odds) are measurable but blunt; individual fairness is appealing but requires defining a similarity metric that is itself a contested value judgment. Recent work connects individual fairness to [[Causal Inference|Causal Inference]] through counterfactual fairness: a prediction is fair if it would remain the same in a counterfactual world where the individual's protected attribute were different.

[[Transfer Learning|Transfer Learning]] introduces additional bias risks: a model pre-trained on one population's data and fine-tuned on another can carry over biases from the pre-training corpus, particularly in [[Natural Language Processing|Natural Language Processing]], where word embeddings encode gender and racial stereotypes.
