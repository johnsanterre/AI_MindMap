! Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors
[img[assets/eigenvalues_eigenvectors.svg]]

Imagine you're standing in a wind tunnel, holding dozens of different flags on poles, each pointing in a different direction. When the wind hits, most flags get whipped around — they change direction ''and'' stretch. But a few special flags barely move at all: the wind pushes them longer or shorter along the exact same line they were already pointing. Those stubborn flags are ''eigenvectors'' — the directions a transformation refuses to rotate. The amount each one stretches (or shrinks) is its ''eigenvalue''. Find these special directions and you've found the skeleton key to what a matrix //actually does//.

!! The Core Insight

Every square matrix represents a transformation — it stretches, rotates, shears, or reflects space. Most vectors get jumbled by this process, landing in some new direction. But eigenvectors are the ''invariant directions'': they go in, get scaled by a factor $\lambda$, and come out still pointing the same way. The equation is deceptively simple:

$$Av = \lambda v$$

This says: "Applying matrix $A$ to vector $v$ produces the same result as simply multiplying $v$ by the scalar $\lambda$." The eigenvector $v$ reveals ''which direction'' the matrix acts along; the eigenvalue $\lambda$ reveals ''how much'' it stretches or compresses along that direction. A negative $\lambda$ flips the vector. A $\lambda$ of zero collapses it entirely — that direction gets crushed into the null space.

Together, eigenvalues and eigenvectors decompose a complicated transformation into a set of simple, independent scalings along privileged directions. This is why they appear everywhere: from quantum mechanics to Google's PageRank to [[Principal Component Analysis|Principal Component Analysis]].

!! The Characteristic Equation

To find eigenvalues, we rearrange $Av = \lambda v$ into $(A - \lambda I)v = 0$. For a non-trivial solution ($v \neq 0$), the matrix $(A - \lambda I)$ must be ''singular'' — its determinant must vanish:

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$$

This is the ''characteristic equation''. For an $n \times n$ matrix, it yields a polynomial of degree $n$ in $\lambda$, whose roots are the eigenvalues. Once you have each $\lambda_i$, you plug it back in and solve $(A - \lambda_i I)v = 0$ to get the corresponding eigenvector(s).

For a $2 \times 2$ matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}$, the characteristic polynomial is:

$$\lambda^2 - (a+d)\lambda + (ad - bc) = 0$$

The term $(a + d)$ is the ''trace'' (sum of eigenvalues), and $(ad - bc)$ is the ''determinant'' (product of eigenvalues). These two quantities alone pin down the eigenvalues of any $2 \times 2$ matrix.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The eigendecomposition of a matrix $A$ factors it as:

$$A = Q \Lambda Q^{-1}$$

where $Q$ is the matrix whose columns are the eigenvectors and $\Lambda$ is a diagonal matrix of eigenvalues. This factorization says: //"To apply $A$, first rotate into the eigenvector coordinate system ($Q^{-1}$), then scale each axis independently ($\Lambda$), then rotate back ($Q$)."//

When $A$ is ''symmetric'' (i.e., $A = A^T$), something beautiful happens: the eigenvectors are guaranteed to be ''orthogonal'', and $Q$ becomes an orthogonal matrix ($Q^{-1} = Q^T$). This is the ''Spectral Theorem'', and it's the mathematical foundation behind [[Principal Component Analysis|Principal Component Analysis]] and [[Spectral Graph Theory|Spectral Graph Theory]].

Powers of $A$ become trivial once decomposed:

$$A^k = Q \Lambda^k Q^{-1}$$

Since $\Lambda$ is diagonal, $\Lambda^k$ just raises each eigenvalue to the $k$-th power. The largest eigenvalue dominates — this is why ''power iteration'' converges to the top eigenvector, and why the spectral radius $\rho(A) = \max|\lambda_i|$ controls stability in dynamical systems and recurrent neural networks.

The connection to [[SVD|SVD]] is direct: for a symmetric positive semi-definite matrix, the eigendecomposition ''is'' the SVD. For general matrices, SVD generalizes eigendecomposition to rectangular matrices by using two different sets of basis vectors.

!! Eigenvalues in Machine Learning

Eigenvalues and eigenvectors are the computational engine behind several core ML techniques:

''PCA (Principal Component Analysis):'' Compute the covariance matrix of your data, then find its eigenvectors. The eigenvector with the largest eigenvalue points in the direction of maximum variance — the most "informative" axis. The second eigenvector (orthogonal to the first) captures the next most variance, and so on. Truncating to the top $k$ eigenvectors gives you optimal $k$-dimensional compression. See [[Principal Component Analysis|Principal Component Analysis]].

''Spectral Clustering:'' Build a similarity graph over your data points, compute the graph Laplacian $L = D - W$, and find its smallest eigenvectors. These eigenvectors naturally partition the data into clusters, even when clusters have complex, non-convex shapes. See [[Spectral Graph Theory|Spectral Graph Theory]].

''Stability of Training:'' The eigenvalues of the Hessian matrix (second derivatives of the loss) tell you about the curvature of the loss landscape. Large eigenvalues mean sharp curvature — learning rates must be kept below $2/\lambda_{\max}$ or gradient descent diverges. The ''condition number'' $\kappa = \lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$ measures how elongated the loss surface is; a large $\kappa$ means slow, zig-zagging convergence.

''PageRank:'' Google's original algorithm finds the dominant eigenvector of a modified web adjacency matrix. The eigenvalue is 1, and the eigenvector entries give the "importance" score of each page.

!! When Eigendecomposition Breaks Down

''1. Non-diagonalizable matrices (defective matrices):'' Some matrices lack a full set of linearly independent eigenvectors. A $3 \times 3$ matrix might have only 1 or 2 independent eigenvectors instead of 3. The eigendecomposition $A = Q\Lambda Q^{-1}$ fails because $Q$ is not invertible. The fix is the ''Jordan normal form'', which introduces Jordan blocks for repeated eigenvalues with insufficient eigenvectors. In practice this is rare for real data matrices — symmetric matrices are always diagonalizable.

''2. Numerical instability with near-repeated eigenvalues:'' When two eigenvalues are extremely close (say $\lambda_1 = 2.0000$ and $\lambda_2 = 2.0001$), their corresponding eigenvectors become nearly parallel and are highly sensitive to floating-point perturbations. Small changes in the matrix can cause the eigenvectors to swing wildly. Algorithms like QR iteration handle this, but results should be interpreted cautiously. For ML tasks, [[SVD|SVD]] is often preferred because it is numerically more stable than direct eigendecomposition.

''3. Computational cost for large matrices:'' Full eigendecomposition of an $n \times n$ matrix costs $O(n^3)$ — infeasible when $n$ is in the millions (e.g., a web graph or large covariance matrix). Iterative methods like ''Lanczos'' (symmetric) or ''Arnoldi'' (general) compute only the top $k$ eigenpairs in roughly $O(nk)$ time per iteration, but they require careful handling of convergence and numerical drift. Libraries like ARPACK and modern randomized methods make large-scale problems tractable. See also [[Matrix Decomposition|Matrix Decomposition]].

!! Technical Details

''Spectrum:'' The set of all eigenvalues of $A$, sometimes written $\sigma(A)$. For symmetric matrices all eigenvalues are real. For general real matrices, complex eigenvalues come in conjugate pairs.

''Spectral radius:'' $\rho(A) = \max_i |\lambda_i|$. Governs convergence of $A^k$: if $\rho(A) < 1$, powers decay to zero; if $\rho(A) > 1$, powers explode.

''Trace–determinant relationships:'' $\text{tr}(A) = \sum \lambda_i$, $\det(A) = \prod \lambda_i$. A zero eigenvalue means $A$ is singular.

''Rayleigh quotient:'' For symmetric $A$, the scalar $R(x) = \frac{x^T A x}{x^T x}$ is minimized/maximized at the extreme eigenvectors. This is the variational characterization underlying PCA.

''Generalized eigenvalue problem:'' $Av = \lambda Bv$ arises in Fisher's discriminant analysis and canonical correlation. Solved by transforming to $B^{-1}Av = \lambda v$ when $B$ is invertible.

''Perturbation theory (Weyl's theorem):'' If $A$ is symmetric and $E$ is a perturbation, then $|\lambda_i(A+E) - \lambda_i(A)| \leq \|E\|_2$. Eigenvalues of symmetric matrices are stable under small perturbations.

''Matrix exponential:'' $e^{At} = Q e^{\Lambda t} Q^{-1}$, connecting eigenvalues to solutions of linear ODEs and continuous-time dynamical systems.
