! Spectral Methods

<img src="assets/spectral_methods.svg" alt="Spectral Methods: matrix to eigendecomposition to clustering, dimensionality reduction, and PDE solving" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are listening to a symphony orchestra, but instead of hearing individual instruments, you hear only the combined sound wave. A Fourier transform lets you decompose that wave into its constituent frequencies — isolating the oboe from the cellos from the timpani. ''Spectral methods'' do exactly this for matrices: they decompose a matrix into its eigenvalues and eigenvectors, revealing hidden structure that is invisible when you stare at the raw entries. The "spectrum" of a matrix is its set of eigenvalues, and spectral methods exploit this spectrum to cluster data, reduce dimensions, and solve differential equations.

!! The Core Insight

Every symmetric matrix $A$ can be written as $A = U \Lambda U^\top$, where $U$ contains the eigenvectors as columns and $\Lambda$ is a diagonal matrix of eigenvalues. This decomposition is not just an algebraic curiosity — it reveals the ''natural coordinate system'' of whatever the matrix represents. If $A$ is a graph adjacency matrix, the eigenvectors reveal community structure. If $A$ is a covariance matrix, the eigenvectors are the principal directions of variation. If $A$ is a differential operator discretized on a grid, the eigenvectors are the natural oscillation modes (sines and cosines on regular domains).

The key insight is that the first few eigenvectors — those corresponding to the smallest or largest eigenvalues, depending on the application — capture the most important global structure. Everything else is noise, fine detail, or high-frequency oscillation. By projecting data onto a small number of eigenvectors, you strip away complexity while preserving the essential geometry.

!! From Matrices to Meaning

Spectral methods arise in three distinct families, unified by the same mathematical operation:

''Spectral clustering'' builds a similarity graph from data, forms the <<link "Graph Laplacian" "Graph Laplacian">><</link>> $L = D - A$, and computes its smallest eigenvectors. These eigenvectors embed the data into a low-dimensional space where clusters become linearly separable. The Fiedler vector (second-smallest eigenvector of $L$) bisects the graph at its weakest connection — partitioning a social network into its most natural communities. See <<link "Spectral Clustering" "Spectral Clustering">><</link>>.

''Dimensionality reduction'' via <<link "PCA" "PCA">><</link>> computes eigenvectors of the covariance matrix $C = \frac{1}{n}X^\top X$. The top eigenvectors are the principal components — directions of maximum variance. Kernel PCA generalizes this: build a kernel matrix $K_{ij} = k(x_i, x_j)$, compute its eigenvectors, and project data into a nonlinear feature space without ever computing the features explicitly. Laplacian Eigenmaps and Diffusion Maps extend this further using the graph Laplacian, respecting local geometry rather than global variance.

''PDE solving'' expands an unknown function in an eigenbasis of the differential operator. On a rectangular domain, the Laplacian's eigenfunctions are sines and cosines — the classical Fourier series. On irregular domains or graphs, the eigenvectors of the discretized operator serve the same role. Projecting the PDE onto these modes decouples a system of coupled equations into independent scalar equations, one per mode.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Given a real symmetric matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times N}$, the eigendecomposition is:

$$A = U \Lambda U^\top = \sum_{k=1}^{N} \lambda_k u_k u_k^\top$$

where $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_N$ are eigenvalues and $u_1, \ldots, u_N$ are orthonormal eigenvectors. This is the ''spectral theorem'', guaranteed for any symmetric (or Hermitian) matrix.

''Low-rank approximation:'' keeping only the top $k$ eigenpairs gives the best rank-$k$ approximation in Frobenius norm (Eckart-Young theorem, equivalent to truncated <<link "SVD" "SVD">><</link>>):

$$A_k = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \lambda_i u_i u_i^\top, \quad \|A - A_k\|_F^2 = \sum_{i=k+1}^{N} \lambda_i^2$$

''Graph Laplacian spectral embedding:'' Given $L = D - A$ with eigenvalues $0 = \lambda_1 \leq \lambda_2 \leq \cdots \leq \lambda_N$ and eigenvectors $u_1, u_2, \ldots, u_N$, embed node $i$ as the vector $(u_2(i), u_3(i), \ldots, u_{k+1}(i)) \in \mathbb{R}^k$. The Rayleigh quotient guarantees this embedding minimizes:

$$\sum_{(i,j) \in E} w_{ij} \|y_i - y_j\|^2$$

subject to orthogonality constraints — nodes connected by strong edges land close together in the embedding.

''Kernel PCA:'' Given kernel matrix $K$ with eigendecomposition $K = U \Lambda U^\top$, the $\alpha$-th principal component of point $x_i$ in feature space is:

$$z_{i\alpha} = \sqrt{\lambda_\alpha} \, U_{i\alpha}$$

This performs nonlinear dimensionality reduction without ever computing the (possibly infinite-dimensional) feature map $\phi(x)$.

''Spectral PDE methods:'' For $\nabla^2 u = f$ on a domain with eigenfunctions $\phi_k$ satisfying $\nabla^2 \phi_k = -\mu_k \phi_k$, expand $u = \sum_k c_k \phi_k$ and $f = \sum_k f_k \phi_k$. Then $c_k = -f_k / \mu_k$ — each mode decouples into a trivial division.

!! Practical Considerations

''Choosing the number of eigenvectors $k$:'' In clustering, look for a gap in the eigenvalue spectrum — a jump from small eigenvalues to substantially larger ones indicates $k$ natural clusters. In PCA, the cumulative explained variance ratio (e.g., keep enough components for 95% variance) provides a practical threshold. In PDE solving, convergence of the solution as $k$ increases guides truncation.

''Computational cost:'' Full eigendecomposition costs $O(N^3)$. For large sparse matrices, iterative methods (Lanczos algorithm for symmetric matrices, Arnoldi for nonsymmetric) compute the top $k$ eigenpairs in roughly $O(Nk)$ time per iteration with sparse matrix-vector products. Libraries like ARPACK, SLEPc, and scipy.sparse.linalg.eigsh implement these efficiently.

''Normalization matters:'' The unnormalized Laplacian $L = D - A$, symmetric normalized $L_\text{sym} = D^{-1/2}LD^{-1/2}$, and random-walk normalized $L_\text{rw} = D^{-1}L$ produce different embeddings. Normalized variants handle degree heterogeneity better and are preferred in practice (Ng, Jordan, Weiss 2002). See <<link "Eigenvalues" "Eigenvalues">><</link>>.

!! When Spectral Methods Break Down

''(1) Computational scaling — eigendecomposition is O(N cubed).'' For datasets with millions of points, computing even the top $k$ eigenvectors of the full $N \times N$ matrix is prohibitive. The Nystrom approximation and randomized SVD partially address this by sampling a subset of columns and extrapolating, but they introduce approximation error. Spatial methods (e.g., message-passing GNNs) bypass the global eigensystem entirely for graph problems.

''(2) Sensitivity to the similarity graph construction.'' Spectral clustering results depend heavily on how you build the graph: the choice of similarity kernel, the bandwidth parameter $\sigma$ in $k(x_i, x_j) = \exp(-\|x_i - x_j\|^2 / 2\sigma^2)$, and whether you use $k$-nearest-neighbors or $\epsilon$-ball connectivity. A poor choice of $\sigma$ can merge distinct clusters or shatter a single cluster into fragments. There is no universally reliable automatic selection method.

''(3) Eigenvector instability near repeated eigenvalues.'' When two eigenvalues are nearly equal ($\lambda_i \approx \lambda_j$), the corresponding eigenvectors are determined only up to an arbitrary rotation within their shared eigenspace. Small perturbations to the data (adding a point, changing a weight) can rotate these eigenvectors dramatically, producing unstable embeddings. This is especially problematic when the spectral gap is small — exactly the regime where cluster structure is ambiguous.

!! Technical Details

''Historical origins:'' Spectral graph partitioning was introduced by Donath and Hoffman (1973) and Fiedler (1973). Shi and Malik (2000) popularized normalized cuts for image segmentation. Ng, Jordan, and Weiss (2002) established the modern spectral clustering algorithm. Spectral methods for PDEs trace to Galerkin and Fourier analysis in the 19th century, with modern formulations by Gottlieb and Orszag (1977).

''Relationship to SVD:'' For a general (possibly non-square) matrix $X$, the <<link "SVD" "SVD">><</link>> $X = U\Sigma V^\top$ is the generalization of eigendecomposition. Left singular vectors are eigenvectors of $XX^\top$; right singular vectors are eigenvectors of $X^\top X$. PCA on centered data $X$ is equivalent to SVD, connecting spectral methods for covariance analysis to matrix factorization.

''Cheeger inequality:'' For the normalized Laplacian, $\frac{\lambda_2}{2} \leq h(G) \leq \sqrt{2\lambda_2}$, where $h(G)$ is the Cheeger constant (minimum normalized cut). This provides a theoretical guarantee that the Fiedler vector yields a near-optimal graph bisection.

''Davis-Kahan theorem:'' Quantifies eigenvector perturbation: if $\hat{A} = A + E$ is a perturbed matrix, the angle between the true and perturbed $k$-dimensional eigenspaces is bounded by $\|E\| / \delta$, where $\delta$ is the spectral gap between the $k$-th and $(k+1)$-th eigenvalues. Larger spectral gaps mean more stable embeddings.

''Convergence of graph Laplacian:'' As the number of data points $n \to \infty$ sampled from a manifold, the graph Laplacian (with appropriate bandwidth scaling) converges to the Laplace-Beltrami operator on the manifold (Belkin and Niyogi 2007). This justifies using graph spectral methods for manifold learning.
