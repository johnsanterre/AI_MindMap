! KKT Conditions
<img src="assets/kkt_conditions.svg" alt="KKT Conditions" width="800">

Imagine you're trying to find the lowest valley in a mountain range, but there's a fence you can't cross. You walk downhill as far as you can, and eventually you hit the fence. At that point, you look at the slope of the ground and the direction the fence runs. If the steepest downhill path points directly into the fence — not along it, not away from it — then you know you've found your best spot. You can't do better without breaking through the fence. The ''Karush-Kuhn-Tucker (KKT) conditions'' formalize exactly this intuition: at the optimal point of a constrained optimization problem, the gradient of your objective and the gradients of your active constraints must line up in a very specific way.

!! The Core Insight

The KKT conditions are the ''first-order necessary conditions'' for a solution to be optimal in a constrained optimization problem with inequality constraints. They generalize the method of [[Lagrangian Duality|Lagrangian Duality]] (which handles equality constraints) to the richer setting where some constraints are inequalities. The key idea is that at an optimal point, you cannot improve the objective without violating at least one constraint. This geometric fact translates into an algebraic relationship: the gradient of the objective must be expressible as a non-negative combination of the gradients of the active inequality constraints plus a linear combination of the equality constraint gradients. The multipliers that appear in this combination — the ''KKT multipliers'' — carry deep economic and algorithmic meaning: each one tells you the rate at which the optimal value would improve if you relaxed the corresponding constraint.

!! Necessary Conditions for Optimality

The KKT conditions consist of four requirements that must all hold simultaneously at an optimal point $x^*$:

''1. Stationarity:'' The gradient of the objective, adjusted by the constraint gradients, must vanish. This is the generalization of "set the derivative to zero" from unconstrained optimization. At $x^*$, there is no direction that simultaneously improves the objective and remains feasible.

''2. Primal Feasibility:'' The point $x^*$ must actually satisfy all the constraints. For inequality constraints $g_i(x) \leq 0$ and equality constraints $h_j(x) = 0$, the solution must lie in the feasible region. An "optimal" point outside the feasible set is meaningless.

''3. Dual Feasibility:'' The multipliers $\lambda_i$ associated with inequality constraints must be non-negative: $\lambda_i \geq 0$. This sign condition distinguishes inequality constraints from equality constraints. It reflects the fact that an inequality constraint can only "push" the solution in one direction — you can lean against a wall but not pull on it.

''4. Complementary Slackness:'' For each inequality constraint, either the constraint is ''active'' (holds with equality, $g_i(x^*) = 0$) or its multiplier is zero ($\lambda_i = 0$), or both. Formally: $\lambda_i g_i(x^*) = 0$ for all $i$. This condition captures the intuition that if a constraint doesn't matter at the solution (you're in the interior, away from the fence), then the solution behaves as if that constraint didn't exist.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Consider the optimization problem: minimize $f(x)$ subject to $g_i(x) \leq 0$ for $i = 1, \ldots, m$ and $h_j(x) = 0$ for $j = 1, \ldots, p$. The ''Lagrangian'' is:

$$\mathcal{L}(x, \lambda, \mu) = f(x) + \sum_{i=1}^{m} \lambda_i g_i(x) + \sum_{j=1}^{p} \mu_j h_j(x)$$

The KKT conditions state that at an optimal point $x^*$ with multipliers $\lambda^*$ and $\mu^*$:

''Stationarity:''
$$\nabla f(x^*) + \sum_{i=1}^{m} \lambda_i^* \nabla g_i(x^*) + \sum_{j=1}^{p} \mu_j^* \nabla h_j(x^*) = 0$$

Equivalently: $\nabla f = \sum \lambda_i \nabla g_i + \sum \mu_j \nabla h_j$ — the objective gradient is a combination of constraint gradients.

''Complementary slackness:''
$$\lambda_i^* g_i(x^*) = 0 \quad \text{for all } i = 1, \ldots, m$$

This product being zero means that for each inequality constraint, at least one of the two factors is zero. Constraints that are not tight contribute nothing to the stationarity condition.

''Constraint qualification:'' The KKT conditions are necessary only when a ''constraint qualification'' (CQ) holds. The most common is the ''Linear Independence Constraint Qualification'' (LICQ): the gradients of all active constraints at $x^*$ must be linearly independent. Without a CQ, the KKT conditions can fail to hold even at a true optimum — the constraint geometry can be pathological enough to invalidate the first-order analysis.

!! KKT in ML

The KKT conditions are not just a theoretical curiosity — they are the engine behind some of the most important algorithms in machine learning.

''SVM Dual Derivation:'' The [[Support Vector Machines|Support Vector Machines]] optimization problem is a quadratic program with inequality constraints. Writing the KKT conditions for the primal SVM problem and applying complementary slackness yields the famous ''support vector'' property: only the training points where the constraint is active ($\lambda_i > 0$) influence the decision boundary. The KKT conditions directly give you the dual formulation, which is the basis for kernel methods.

''Constrained Fairness:'' Modern ML increasingly requires constraints — fairness constraints (equal opportunity across groups), safety constraints (bounded risk), or resource constraints (model size, latency). These naturally produce constrained optimization problems where KKT conditions characterize the optimal tradeoff between accuracy and constraint satisfaction. [[Regularization|Regularization]] itself can be viewed through a KKT lens: $L_2$ regularization is equivalent to constraining the parameter norm, and the regularization strength $\lambda$ is precisely the KKT multiplier.

''Lagrangian Relaxation:'' When direct constrained optimization is hard, ''Lagrangian relaxation'' uses the KKT multipliers as a guide. You iteratively solve the unconstrained Lagrangian and update multipliers via [[Gradient Descent|Gradient Descent]] on the dual. This is the basis of augmented Lagrangian methods and primal-dual algorithms used in large-scale ML training with constraints.

!! When KKT Conditions Don't Apply

''Constraint qualification failure:'' If the gradients of the active constraints are linearly dependent at the optimum, the KKT conditions may not hold even though the point is truly optimal. Consider minimizing $x$ subject to $x^2 \leq 0$: the only feasible point is $x = 0$, which is optimal, but $\nabla g = 2x = 0$ at the optimum, so LICQ fails and the stationarity condition becomes vacuous.

''Non-differentiable objectives or constraints:'' KKT requires that $f$ and all $g_i$, $h_j$ be differentiable at the optimum. Many ML loss functions ($L_1$ penalty, hinge loss, ReLU activations) have points of non-differentiability. At kinks, gradients don't exist in the classical sense, and the KKT machinery breaks down. Subgradient generalizations exist but require more care.

''Discrete or combinatorial constraints:'' KKT is a continuous optimization tool. If your constraints involve integer variables, binary selections, or combinatorial structures (e.g., selecting $k$ features from $n$), the KKT conditions don't apply. The feasible set isn't smooth, and the gradient-based reasoning that underlies KKT has no purchase. You need integer programming or combinatorial optimization methods instead.

!! Technical Details

''Constraint Qualifications:'' LICQ requires linear independence of active constraint gradients. ''Slater's condition'' (a weaker CQ for [[Convex Optimization|Convex Optimization]]) requires the existence of a strictly feasible point: some $x$ with $g_i(x) < 0$ for all inequality constraints. The ''Mangasarian-Fromovitz CQ'' (MFCQ) is intermediate — it requires that there exists a direction $d$ such that $\nabla g_i(x^*)^T d < 0$ for all active inequalities and $\nabla h_j(x^*)^T d = 0$ for equalities.

''Sufficiency for Convex Problems:'' For convex optimization (convex $f$, convex $g_i$, affine $h_j$), the KKT conditions are not just necessary but ''sufficient'' for global optimality. Any KKT point is a global minimizer. This is why [[Convex Optimization|Convex Optimization]] is so powerful: you only need to solve a system of equations and inequalities, not search a landscape.

''Sensitivity Analysis:'' The KKT multiplier $\lambda_i^*$ equals $-\partial f^* / \partial b_i$, where $f^*$ is the optimal value and $b_i$ is the right-hand side of the $i$-th constraint $g_i(x) \leq b_i$. This ''shadow price'' interpretation means each multiplier quantifies the value of relaxing its constraint by one unit.

''Connection to Strong Duality:'' When strong duality holds (as guaranteed by Slater's condition for convex problems), the KKT conditions are equivalent to primal-dual optimality. The KKT point $(x^*, \lambda^*, \mu^*)$ simultaneously solves the primal and dual problems, and the duality gap is zero. This connects KKT to [[Convex Duality|Convex Duality]] and the powerful theory of saddle-point problems.

''Second-Order Conditions:'' The KKT conditions are first-order (gradient-based). Second-order sufficient conditions additionally require that the Hessian of the Lagrangian is positive definite on the tangent space of the active constraints at $x^*$, ensuring the point is a strict local minimum rather than a saddle point.
