! Fixed Point Theorems

<img src="assets/fixed_point_theorems.svg" alt="Diagram showing Brouwer, Banach, and Kakutani fixed point theorems with geometric illustrations" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you have a paper map of a city and you are standing somewhere in that city. If you lay the map flat on the ground and align it perfectly with the real streets, there must be exactly one point on the map sitting directly above the real-world location it represents. That point is a ''fixed point'' — a place that the mapping function sends to itself. Fixed point theorems guarantee that under the right conditions, every function from a set to itself must have at least one such point where $f(x) = x$. These theorems are not just mathematical curiosities: they underpin the existence of <<link "Nash Equilibrium" "Nash Equilibrium">><</link>> in game theory, the convergence of the <<link "Bellman Equation" "Bellman Equation">><</link>> in reinforcement learning, and the solvability of countless optimisation problems.

!! The Core Insight

A ''fixed point'' of a function $f$ is a point $x^*$ such that $f(x^*) = x^*$. The function maps the point to itself — it does not move. The central question of fixed point theory is: //under what conditions must a function have at least one fixed point?// Three landmark theorems answer this for different settings:

''Brouwer's Fixed Point Theorem'' (1911): every continuous function from a compact convex set to itself has at least one fixed point. Stir your coffee however you like — some particle of liquid will end up in the exact position it started. No escape is possible from a compact convex world under continuous motion.

''Banach's Contraction Mapping Theorem'' (1922): if $f$ is a ''contraction'' on a complete metric space — meaning it pulls points closer together — then $f$ has exactly //one// fixed point, and repeated application of $f$ from any starting point converges to it. This is the engine behind iterative algorithms.

''Kakutani's Fixed Point Theorem'' (1941): extends Brouwer to ''set-valued maps'' (correspondences). If a correspondence from a compact convex set to itself has closed graph and convex values, it has a fixed point. This is the theorem John Nash used to prove the existence of equilibria in non-cooperative games.

!! The Three Theorems in Depth

''Brouwer'' is a pure existence result. It tells you a fixed point //exists// but gives no way to find it. The proof is topological: any continuous self-map of a disk (or ball, or cube, or any compact convex set in $\mathbb{R}^n$) must have a fixed point because the alternative — that every point moves — would imply the existence of a continuous retraction from the disk to its boundary, which contradicts the fact that the disk and its boundary have different <<link "Topology" "Topology">><</link>>.

''Banach'' is constructive. Start anywhere, apply $f$ repeatedly, and you converge to the unique fixed point. The rate of convergence is geometric: after $n$ iterations, the error shrinks by a factor of $k^n$, where $k < 1$ is the contraction constant. This is why ''value iteration'' in reinforcement learning converges: the Bellman operator is a contraction under the supremum norm with factor $\gamma$ (the discount factor).

''Kakutani'' handles the case where the function returns a //set// of values rather than a single point. In a game, each player's best-response function maps the opponents' strategies to a set of optimal strategies. Kakutani guarantees a strategy profile exists where every player's strategy is simultaneously a best response — a <<link "Nash Equilibrium" "Nash Equilibrium">><</link>>.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''Brouwer:'' let $D \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ be compact and convex, and $f: D \to D$ continuous. Then there exists $x^* \in D$ such that:
$$f(x^*) = x^*$$

The standard proof uses the ''no-retraction theorem'': there is no continuous map $r: D^n \to \partial D^n$ that fixes the boundary. Assuming $f$ has no fixed point lets us construct such a retraction (by drawing a ray from $f(x)$ through $x$ to the boundary), giving a contradiction.

''Banach:'' let $(X, d)$ be a complete metric space and $f: X \to X$ a contraction, meaning there exists $0 \le k < 1$ such that for all $x, y \in X$:
$$d(f(x), f(y)) \le k \cdot d(x, y)$$

Then $f$ has a unique fixed point $x^*$, and for any $x_0 \in X$, the sequence $x_{n+1} = f(x_n)$ satisfies:
$$d(x_n, x^*) \le \frac{k^n}{1 - k} \cdot d(x_0, f(x_0))$$

This gives an explicit error bound. In reinforcement learning, the <<link "Bellman Equation" "Bellman Equation">><</link>> defines the operator $T$ on value functions:
$$[TV](s) = \max_a \left[ R(s, a) + \gamma \sum_{s'} P(s'|s,a) V(s') \right]$$

Under the $\ell^\infty$ norm, $\|TV_1 - TV_2\|_\infty \le \gamma \|V_1 - V_2\|_\infty$, so $T$ is a $\gamma$-contraction, and value iteration converges to the unique optimal value function $V^*$.

''Kakutani:'' let $S \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ be compact and convex, and $\Phi: S \rightrightarrows S$ a set-valued map with closed graph and non-empty convex values. Then there exists $x^* \in S$ such that:
$$x^* \in \Phi(x^*)$$

Nash applied this with $S = \Delta_1 \times \cdots \times \Delta_n$ (the product of mixed-strategy simplices for $n$ players) and $\Phi$ = the best-response correspondence.

!! Applications in ML and Optimisation

''Value iteration and policy iteration'' in reinforcement learning are direct applications of Banach's theorem. The discount factor $\gamma < 1$ ensures contraction, and the theorem guarantees both convergence and a unique solution. Without the contraction property (e.g., undiscounted problems), convergence may fail.

''Gradient descent as fixed point iteration:'' the update $x_{n+1} = x_n - \eta \nabla f(x_n)$ can be written as $x_{n+1} = g(x_n)$ where $g(x) = x - \eta \nabla f(x)$. A fixed point of $g$ satisfies $\nabla f(x^*) = 0$ — a stationary point of $f$. When $g$ is a contraction (strong convexity + appropriate step size), Banach's theorem guarantees convergence to the unique minimum.

''<<link "Convex Optimization" "Convex Optimization">><</link>>:'' the KKT conditions of a convex program can be reformulated as finding a fixed point of the projection operator onto the feasible set composed with a gradient step. The ''proximal point algorithm'' and ADMM both rely on fixed point iterations.

''Existence theorems in economics:'' the existence of competitive equilibria (Arrow-Debreu) relies on Kakutani's theorem applied to the excess demand correspondence. This mathematical machinery carries over to multi-agent ML systems.

''<<link "Contraction Mapping" "Contraction Mapping">><</link>>'' is the algorithmic workhorse: whenever you can show your iterative procedure is a contraction, Banach's theorem immediately gives you uniqueness of the solution and guaranteed convergence with a computable error bound.

!! When Fixed Point Theorems Break Down

''(1) Loss of compactness or convexity (Brouwer fails):'' Brouwer requires the domain to be both compact and convex. On a non-compact set (like all of $\mathbb{R}^n$), the translation $f(x) = x + 1$ has no fixed point. On a non-convex set (like an annulus), a rotation can move every point. In practice, many ML optimisation landscapes are non-compact, which is why Brouwer alone cannot guarantee convergence — you need additional structure like contraction.

''(2) Non-contractive maps (Banach fails):'' if the contraction constant $k$ equals or exceeds 1, the theorem provides no guarantees. A rotation of the unit circle satisfies $d(f(x), f(y)) = d(x, y)$ (isometry, $k = 1$) and either has every point as a fixed point or has none. In reinforcement learning, setting $\gamma = 1$ (undiscounted) breaks the contraction property, and value iteration may diverge. Many practical iterative algorithms operate near the boundary of contraction, leading to slow convergence.

''(3) Non-convex values in Kakutani (Nash existence fails):'' Kakutani requires the correspondence to return convex sets. If a player's best-response set is non-convex (which can happen in games with non-concave payoffs), the theorem does not apply and a Nash equilibrium may not exist in mixed strategies. This arises in generative adversarial networks and other min-max formulations where the game structure is not classically well-behaved.

!! Technical Details

''Historical context:'' L.E.J. Brouwer proved his theorem in 1911 using degree theory; simpler proofs via Sperner's lemma came later. Stefan Banach published the contraction mapping theorem in his 1922 doctoral thesis. Shizuo Kakutani generalised Brouwer to set-valued maps in 1941. John Nash used Kakutani's theorem in his 1950 proof of the existence of Nash equilibria, earning the 1994 Nobel Prize in Economics.

''Schauder's theorem'' extends Brouwer to infinite-dimensional spaces: every continuous self-map of a compact convex subset of a Banach space has a fixed point. This is used in proving existence of solutions to integral equations and PDEs.

''Tarski's fixed point theorem'' applies to monotone functions on complete lattices — a completely different setting from topology. It guarantees that the set of fixed points is itself a complete lattice. This is foundational in program analysis and abstract interpretation.

''Computational complexity:'' finding a Brouwer fixed point is ''PPAD-complete'' — the canonical problem of the complexity class PPAD. This means that while a fixed point must exist, there is (under standard conjectures) no polynomial-time algorithm to find it. Computing Nash equilibria inherits this hardness.

''Connections to dynamical systems:'' fixed points of dynamical systems $\dot{x} = f(x)$ are equilibria (where $f(x) = 0$). The ''Lefschetz fixed point theorem'' uses algebraic topology to count fixed points via the Lefschetz number $\Lambda(f) = \sum (-1)^k \text{tr}(f_{*k})$. If $\Lambda(f) \ne 0$, then $f$ has a fixed point.
