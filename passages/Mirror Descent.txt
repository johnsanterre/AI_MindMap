! Mirror Descent

<img src="assets/mirror_descent.svg" alt="Mirror Descent" width="800">

Imagine you are navigating a city where some streets are wide boulevards and others are narrow alleys. If you always take the same-length step regardless of the street, you will overshoot in the alleys and crawl along the boulevards. ''Mirror descent'' is an optimization algorithm that adapts its step shape to the geometry of the problem — taking large steps where the landscape is forgiving and small, careful steps where it is not. Standard [[Gradient Descent|Gradient Descent]] implicitly assumes the world is flat and uniform (Euclidean). Mirror descent drops that assumption, replacing Euclidean distance with a ''Bregman divergence'' matched to the problem's structure.

!! The Core Insight

Every optimization algorithm carries a hidden assumption about how to measure distance. [[Gradient Descent|Gradient Descent]] uses the squared Euclidean norm $\frac{1}{2}\|x - y\|_2^2$ to penalize large steps. This works well when the feasible set is a ball and all directions are equally important, but it is a terrible fit when the feasible set is a probability simplex (where all coordinates must be non-negative and sum to one) or when some coordinates matter far more than others.

Mirror descent replaces the Euclidean penalty with a ''Bregman divergence'' $D_\phi(x, y)$ derived from a strictly convex function $\phi$, called the ''mirror map'' or ''distance-generating function.'' The algorithm works in three steps: (1) map the current point $x_t$ from the ''primal space'' to the ''dual space'' via $\nabla\phi$, (2) take a gradient step in the dual space where there are no constraints, and (3) map back to the primal space via $(\nabla\phi)^{-1}$, which automatically enforces feasibility. The genius is that by choosing $\phi$ to match the geometry of the constraint set, the algorithm stays feasible and converges at the optimal rate.

!! Beyond Euclidean Geometry

Standard gradient descent updates as $x_{t+1} = x_t - \eta \nabla f(x_t)$, which is secretly solving:

$$x_{t+1} = \arg\min_x \left\{ \langle \nabla f(x_t), x \rangle + \frac{1}{2\eta}\|x - x_t\|_2^2 \right\}$$

The squared Euclidean term $\|x - x_t\|_2^2$ is the ''proximal penalty'' — it keeps the next iterate close to the current one. But "close" is defined by the Euclidean metric, and this is a //choice//, not a law of nature.

When optimizing over the probability simplex $\Delta_n = \{x \in \mathbb{R}^n : x_i \geq 0, \sum_i x_i = 1\}$, Euclidean geometry is especially inappropriate. The simplex has sharp corners and flat faces. A Euclidean gradient step can easily land outside the simplex, requiring an expensive projection. Worse, the Euclidean metric treats all directions the same, ignoring the fact that moving from $x_i = 0.01$ to $x_i = 0$ is a far more drastic change (in distributional terms) than moving from $x_i = 0.5$ to $x_i = 0.49$.

Mirror descent fixes this by choosing a Bregman divergence that //respects// the geometry of the feasible set. On the simplex, the natural choice is the negative entropy $\phi(x) = \sum_i x_i \log x_i$, which produces the [[KL Divergence|KL Divergence]] as the Bregman divergence. This warps the geometry so that the algorithm automatically stays on the simplex and treats small probabilities with appropriate care.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ''Bregman divergence'' generated by a strictly convex, differentiable function $\phi$ is:

$$D_\phi(x, y) = \phi(x) - \phi(y) - \langle \nabla\phi(y), x - y \rangle$$

This is the gap between $\phi(x)$ and its first-order Taylor approximation at $y$. For $\phi(x) = \frac{1}{2}\|x\|_2^2$, this recovers $D_\phi(x,y) = \frac{1}{2}\|x-y\|_2^2$ (Euclidean). For $\phi(x) = \sum_i x_i \log x_i$ (negative entropy), it recovers the KL divergence $D_\phi(x,y) = \sum_i x_i \log(x_i/y_i)$.

The ''mirror descent update'' is:

$$x_{t+1} = \arg\min_{x \in \mathcal{X}} \left\{ \langle g_t, x \rangle + \frac{1}{\eta} D_\phi(x, x_t) \right\}$$

where $g_t \in \partial f(x_t)$ is a [[Subgradients|subgradient]]. In dual coordinates, this becomes:

$$\nabla\phi(x_{t+1}) = \nabla\phi(x_t) - \eta g_t$$

The ''mirror map'' $\nabla\phi$ sends the primal space to the dual space. The gradient step is //linear// in the dual space, but the inverse map $(\nabla\phi)^{-1}$ warps it back to the primal space in a geometry-aware way.

''Entropic mirror descent on the simplex:'' With $\phi(x) = \sum_i x_i \log x_i$, the mirror map is $(\nabla\phi(x))_i = \log x_i + 1$, and the update becomes:

$$x_{t+1,i} = \frac{x_{t,i} \exp(-\eta g_{t,i})}{\sum_j x_{t,j} \exp(-\eta g_{t,j})}$$

This is ''multiplicative weights'' — the most natural algorithm on the simplex.

''Connection to the [[Natural Gradient|Natural Gradient]]:'' When $\phi$ is chosen so that $\nabla^2 \phi(\theta)$ equals the Fisher information matrix $I(\theta)$, mirror descent becomes natural gradient descent. The Bregman divergence locally approximates the KL divergence between distributions, and the mirror map is the map from parameters to natural parameters. Mirror descent thus generalizes the natural gradient to non-Euclidean constraint sets beyond [[Information Geometry|Information Geometry]]'s statistical manifolds.

!! Mirror Descent in ML

''Online learning on the probability simplex.'' The classic application is the ''experts problem'': at each round, a learner must choose a distribution over $n$ experts, observe losses, and update. Entropic mirror descent yields the ''multiplicative weights algorithm'' (also called Hedge), which achieves optimal $O(\sqrt{T \log n})$ regret. The $\log n$ factor (instead of $\sqrt{n}$ for Euclidean methods) comes directly from the strong convexity of negative entropy with respect to the $\ell_1$ norm.

''Exponentiated gradient'' (Kivinen and Warmuth, 1997) applies multiplicative weights to online linear prediction with non-negative weight vectors. It is exactly entropic mirror descent and outperforms standard gradient descent when the optimal predictor is sparse — common in high-dimensional ML.

''Natural gradient as mirror descent with Fisher metric.'' When training probabilistic models, the parameter space has a natural Riemannian geometry given by the Fisher information matrix. Choosing $\phi(\theta) = \frac{1}{2}\theta^\top I(\theta) \theta$ (locally) makes mirror descent equivalent to [[Natural Gradient|natural gradient]] descent. K-FAC, TRPO, and PPO can all be viewed through this lens: they approximate mirror descent on the policy manifold with the KL divergence as the Bregman divergence.

''Optimization on structured domains.'' Mirror descent with the right $\phi$ achieves dimension-free or logarithmic-in-dimension rates on structured feasible sets. For the simplex, the $\ell_1$ ball, the spectrahedron (positive semidefinite matrices with bounded trace), and nuclear norm balls, mirror descent with an appropriate entropy-like mirror map dramatically outperforms projected gradient descent.

!! When Mirror Descent Breaks Down

''Wrong mirror map.'' The performance of mirror descent depends critically on the match between $\phi$ and the feasible set. Choosing $\phi(x) = \frac{1}{2}\|x\|_2^2$ on the simplex recovers projected gradient descent — correct but slow ($O(\sqrt{n})$ regret instead of $O(\sqrt{\log n})$). Choosing negative entropy on an $\ell_2$ ball gives no advantage. There is no universal mirror map; the practitioner must understand the geometry of their problem. In deep learning, where the loss landscape and constraint geometry are poorly understood, selecting an appropriate $\phi$ is largely an open problem.

''Stochastic gradients and variance.'' Mirror descent theory assumes exact gradients or bounded-variance stochastic gradients. In modern ML, gradient estimates are noisy (mini-batch SGD) and may have heavy tails. The multiplicative structure of entropic mirror descent amplifies noise on small probabilities: if $x_i$ is near zero, even a small gradient perturbation causes a large //relative// change. Variance reduction techniques help but add computational overhead, and the theoretical guarantees degrade with heavy-tailed noise.

''Non-convex objectives.'' Mirror descent's theoretical guarantees — optimal regret bounds, convergence rates, FTRL equivalence — are for [[Convex Optimization|convex optimization]]. Deep learning losses are non-convex, and the Bregman divergence framework does not provide global convergence guarantees in the non-convex setting. While mirror descent can still be applied heuristically (and natural gradient methods are popular in RL), the elegant theory does not directly transfer, and it is unclear when the geometry-matching benefits outweigh the added complexity.

!! Technical Details

''Regret bounds.'' For online convex optimization with bounded [[Subgradients|subgradients]] $\|g_t\|_* \leq G$ (in the dual norm), mirror descent achieves regret:

$$\text{Regret}_T \leq \frac{D_\phi(x^*, x_1)}{\eta} + \frac{\eta}{2} \sum_{t=1}^T \|g_t\|_*^2 / \sigma$$

where $\sigma$ is the ''strong convexity modulus'' of $\phi$ with respect to the primal norm. Optimizing $\eta$ gives $O(\sqrt{T})$ regret. For entropic mirror descent on the $n$-simplex, $\sigma = 1$ with respect to $\|\cdot\|_1$, and $D_\phi(x^*, x_1) \leq \log n$, yielding $O(G\sqrt{T \log n})$.

''Strong convexity modulus.'' The key quantity is the strong convexity of $\phi$ with respect to the chosen norm: $\phi(x) \geq \phi(y) + \langle \nabla\phi(y), x-y \rangle + \frac{\sigma}{2}\|x-y\|^2$. Negative entropy is $1$-strongly convex with respect to $\|\cdot\|_1$ (Pinsker's inequality). The squared $\ell_2$ norm is $1$-strongly convex with respect to $\|\cdot\|_2$. The best rates come from mirror maps with the largest $\sigma$ relative to the diameter of the feasible set.

''Relationship to proximal methods.'' Mirror descent is a ''Bregman proximal method'': it replaces the Euclidean proximal operator $\text{prox}_f(y) = \arg\min_x \{f(x) + \frac{1}{2}\|x-y\|^2\}$ with the Bregman proximal operator $\text{prox}_f^\phi(y) = \arg\min_x \{f(x) + D_\phi(x,y)\}$. The proximal point algorithm with Bregman divergence converges under the same conditions as the Euclidean version, but with rates that depend on the geometry matching.

''FTRL equivalence.'' Mirror descent is equivalent to ''Follow the Regularized Leader'' (FTRL) with regularizer $\phi$: $x_{t+1} = \arg\min_{x \in \mathcal{X}} \{\sum_{s=1}^t \langle g_s, x \rangle + \frac{1}{\eta}\phi(x)\}$. This equivalence (proven by Shalev-Shwartz) connects the primal-dual mirror descent view with the regularization view, and shows that entropic mirror descent = FTRL with entropy regularization = multiplicative weights. The choice of $\phi$ simultaneously determines the mirror map, the Bregman divergence, the regularizer, and the algorithm.

''Key references:'' Nemirovsky & Yudin (1983), //Problem Complexity and Method Efficiency in Optimization//. Beck & Teboulle (2003), mirror descent and nonlinear programming. Shalev-Shwartz (2012), online learning and online convex optimization survey.
