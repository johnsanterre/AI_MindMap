! Game Theory

<img src="assets/game_theory.svg" alt="Payoff matrix for the Prisoner's Dilemma showing Nash Equilibrium" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine two rival food trucks parked on opposite ends of a beach, each deciding where to relocate. If both move to the center, they split the crowd evenly. If one stays put while the other moves center, the mover steals most customers. Each truck reasons about what the other will do before deciding — and that mutual, recursive reasoning about strategic interdependence is the heart of game theory. It is the mathematics of situations where your best move depends on someone else's move, and they are thinking the same thing about you.

---

!! The Core Insight

''Game theory'' is the formal study of strategic interaction among rational decision-makers. Unlike optimization, where a single agent maximizes a function against a passive environment, game theory models situations where multiple agents choose actions simultaneously or sequentially, and each agent's payoff depends on the joint actions of all players. The central question is not "what is the best action?" but "what is the best action given that everyone else is also trying to find their best action?"

The foundational concept is the ''Nash Equilibrium'' — a set of strategies, one per player, where no player can improve their payoff by unilaterally changing their own strategy. At a [[Nash Equilibrium]], every player is already doing the best they can given what everyone else is doing. This does not mean the outcome is optimal for the group (the Prisoner's Dilemma proves otherwise), but it is //stable//: no individual has an incentive to deviate. John Nash proved in 1950 that every finite game with mixed strategies has at least one such equilibrium, a result that earned him the Nobel Prize in Economics.

Game theory connects deeply to [[Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning]], where multiple learning agents must adapt to each other's evolving strategies, and to [[GANs]], where a generator and discriminator play a two-player zero-sum game. It also underpins [[Mechanism Design]], which is game theory in reverse — designing the rules of a game so that self-interested players produce a desired outcome.

---

!! Types of Games and Equilibria

''Zero-sum games'' are the simplest class: one player's gain is exactly another's loss. Chess, Go, and poker are zero-sum. The optimal strategy in a two-player zero-sum game is the ''minimax'' strategy — choose the action that minimizes the maximum loss your opponent can inflict. Von Neumann's [[Minimax]] theorem (1928) proved that every two-player zero-sum game has a well-defined value and that both players have optimal mixed strategies. This result is the theoretical foundation for adversarial search in AI.

''Non-zero-sum games'' are richer and harder. The Prisoner's Dilemma is the classic example: mutual cooperation yields a better outcome for both players than mutual defection, yet the Nash Equilibrium is mutual defection because each player individually benefits from defecting regardless of what the other does. This tension between individual rationality and collective welfare is the core puzzle of cooperation in economics, biology, and multi-agent AI.

''Repeated games'' change the dynamics. When players interact multiple times and remember past actions, cooperation can emerge through strategies like tit-for-tat, which cooperates on the first round and then mirrors the opponent's previous action. Robert Axelrod's famous tournaments (1984) showed that simple reciprocal strategies dominate in repeated Prisoner's Dilemma settings — a finding with deep implications for the evolution of cooperation.

''Bayesian games'' (games of incomplete information) model situations where players are uncertain about other players' payoffs or types. Each player has a //belief// — a probability distribution over the other players' possible types — and chooses a strategy that maximizes expected payoff given those beliefs. Harsanyi (1967) showed how to convert games of incomplete information into games of imperfect but complete information using the concept of a //type space//, enabling standard equilibrium analysis.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

A game in ''normal form'' is a tuple $(N, S, u)$ where $N = \{1, \ldots, n\}$ is the set of players, $S = S_1 \times \cdots \times S_n$ is the joint strategy space, and $u = (u_1, \ldots, u_n)$ maps joint strategies to payoffs. Player $i$ chooses strategy $s_i \in S_i$ to maximize $u_i(s_i, s_{-i})$, where $s_{-i}$ denotes the strategies of all other players.

A ''Nash Equilibrium'' is a strategy profile $s^* = (s_1^*, \ldots, s_n^*)$ such that for every player $i$:

$$u_i(s_i^*, s_{-i}^*) \geq u_i(s_i, s_{-i}^*) \quad \forall \, s_i \in S_i$$

No player can improve their payoff by deviating unilaterally. In ''mixed strategies'', players randomize over actions according to probability distributions $\sigma_i \in \Delta(S_i)$, and the expected payoff becomes:

$$U_i(\sigma) = \sum_{s \in S} \left(\prod_{j=1}^n \sigma_j(s_j)\right) u_i(s)$$

Nash's existence theorem guarantees at least one mixed-strategy equilibrium exists in every finite game.

For two-player zero-sum games, the [[Minimax]] theorem states:

$$\max_{\sigma_1} \min_{\sigma_2} U_1(\sigma_1, \sigma_2) = \min_{\sigma_2} \max_{\sigma_1} U_1(\sigma_1, \sigma_2) = v$$

The value $v$ is the //game value//, and both players have optimal strategies that guarantee at least $v$ (for player 1) or at most $v$ (for player 2). This minimax solution can be computed via linear programming.

In [[GANs]], the generator $G$ and discriminator $D$ play a minimax game over the value function:

$$\min_G \max_D \, \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}}[\log D(x)] + \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}[\log(1 - D(G(z)))]$$

Training seeks a Nash Equilibrium where $G$ produces samples indistinguishable from real data and $D$ outputs $\frac{1}{2}$ everywhere — though convergence to this equilibrium is famously unstable.

---

!! Game Theory in Machine Learning

''Generative Adversarial Networks'' are the most direct application. The GAN training objective is a two-player zero-sum game, and the instabilities of GAN training — mode collapse, oscillation, failure to converge — are fundamentally game-theoretic problems. Techniques like spectral normalization, gradient penalties, and Wasserstein distance reformulations are all attempts to make the game better behaved and the equilibrium easier to reach.

''Multi-agent reinforcement learning'' ([[Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning]]) inherits the full complexity of game theory. When multiple RL agents learn simultaneously, the environment is non-stationary from each agent's perspective because the other agents' policies are changing. Standard RL convergence guarantees break down. Algorithms like independent Q-learning, Nash-Q, and LOLA (Learning with Opponent-Learning Awareness) attempt to handle this by explicitly modeling or anticipating the other agents' learning dynamics.

''Adversarial robustness'' frames the relationship between a model and an adversary as a game. The adversary chooses the worst-case perturbation to an input; the model tries to classify correctly regardless. Adversarial training — training on worst-case examples — is a minimax optimization that directly mirrors zero-sum game theory.

''[[Mechanism Design]]'' applies game theory in reverse to design auctions, incentive structures, and allocation mechanisms. In ML, this appears in federated learning (incentivizing honest participation), data markets (pricing data contributions), and RLHF (designing reward models that elicit truthful human preferences).

---

!! When Game Theory Breaks Down

''Rationality Assumptions Fail in Practice:'' Classical game theory assumes all players are perfectly rational, have unlimited computational resources, and correctly model other players as equally rational. Real agents — whether humans or bounded AI systems — satisfice rather than optimize, use heuristics, and have imperfect models of opponents. The Nash Equilibrium predicts behavior in the Prisoner's Dilemma, but in experimental settings humans cooperate far more often than the theory predicts. In multi-agent RL, agents that assume rationality in opponents that are actually learning via gradient descent can produce wildly incorrect strategy choices.

''Computational Intractability of Equilibria:'' Finding a Nash Equilibrium is PPAD-complete — believed to be computationally intractable in general. For games with many players or large strategy spaces (as arise in real-world multi-agent systems), computing or even approximating an equilibrium may be infeasible. This is not merely a theoretical concern: GAN training is an attempt to find a Nash Equilibrium via gradient descent, and the frequent failure of GANs to converge reflects the computational hardness of the underlying game-theoretic problem.

''Multiple Equilibria and Coordination Failure:'' Many games have multiple Nash Equilibria, and the theory provides no guidance on which one players will coordinate on. In the "Battle of the Sexes" game, both players prefer coordination to miscoordination, but they disagree on which equilibrium is better. In multi-agent RL, the existence of multiple equilibria means that independently learning agents may converge to different, incompatible strategies and fail to coordinate — even when a mutually beneficial equilibrium exists. Equilibrium selection remains one of the deepest unsolved problems in game theory.

!! Technical Details

''Historical foundations:'' Von Neumann and Morgenstern published //Theory of Games and Economic Behavior// (1944), establishing the field. Nash (1950) proved the existence of equilibria in non-cooperative games. Selten (1965) introduced subgame perfect equilibrium for sequential games. Harsanyi (1967) developed Bayesian games for incomplete information. Axelrod (1984) ran the iterated Prisoner's Dilemma tournaments that demonstrated the power of reciprocal strategies.

''Solution concepts beyond Nash:'' ''Correlated equilibrium'' (Aumann, 1974) relaxes independence — a mediator recommends actions to each player, and no player benefits from deviating from the recommendation. Correlated equilibria are computationally easier to find (via linear programming) and form a convex set containing all Nash Equilibria. ''Evolutionary stable strategies'' (Maynard Smith, 1973) apply game theory to biology, modeling populations of agents playing strategies that resist invasion by mutants.

''Connections to optimization:'' In zero-sum games, finding the minimax solution is a linear program (LP). In general-sum games, finding Nash Equilibria can be formulated as a linear complementarity problem (LCP). The Lemke-Howson algorithm finds one Nash Equilibrium in bimatrix games. The ''fictitious play'' algorithm — where each player repeatedly best-responds to the empirical distribution of the opponent's past actions — converges in zero-sum games but not in general.

''Key references for ML:'' Goodfellow et al. (2014) introduced GANs as a minimax game. Lanctot et al. (2017) provided a unified framework for multi-agent RL algorithms. Daskalakis et al. (2009) proved that computing Nash Equilibria is PPAD-complete. Balduzzi et al. (2019) analyzed the game-theoretic structure of multi-agent learning dynamics using smooth games and Jacobian analysis.
