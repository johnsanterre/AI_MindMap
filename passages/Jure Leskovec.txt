! Jure Leskovec
<img src="assets/jure_leskovec.svg" alt="Jure Leskovec" width="800">

Imagine you have a map of every friendship, every hyperlink, every molecular bond in a dataset — millions of nodes, billions of edges. ''Jure Leskovec'' is the computer scientist who figured out how to //teach machines to read these maps//. Rather than hand-engineering features from network topology, he showed that you can send random walkers wandering through a graph, record where they go, and use those trajectories to learn dense vector representations — [[Embeddings|Embeddings]] — that capture the structural role of every node. His work turned graph analysis from a niche corner of data mining into one of the fastest-growing areas in modern machine learning.

!! Key Contributions

''node2vec'' (2016) — A flexible algorithm for learning continuous feature representations for nodes in networks. By tuning two parameters that interpolate between breadth-first and depth-first random walks, node2vec can capture both local neighborhood structure (homophily) and global structural roles (structural equivalence). The learned [[Embeddings|Embeddings]] feed directly into downstream classifiers and link predictors.

''GraphSAGE'' (2017) — Short for //Graph SAmple and aggreGatE//, this was the first scalable framework for //inductive// node embedding. Instead of learning a fixed embedding per node (transductive), GraphSAGE learns an ''aggregation function'' that samples and combines features from a node's neighbors. A new, never-before-seen node can be embedded instantly — a breakthrough that made [[Graph Neural Networks|Graph Neural Networks]] practical for production systems with billions of nodes.

''SNAP'' (Stanford Network Analysis Platform) — An open-source library and dataset collection for large-scale network analysis. SNAP provides canonical benchmark graphs (social networks, web graphs, citation networks, biological networks) that have become the standard evaluation suite across the graph ML community. Thousands of papers cite SNAP datasets.

''Network Evolution and Community Detection'' — Leskovec's early work characterized how real networks grow over time, discovering that communities have a characteristic size beyond which they stop being meaningful. His work on [[Community Detection|Community Detection]] revealed that large-scale network structure is far richer — and harder to decompose — than simple modularity optimization would suggest.

''Information Diffusion'' — Pioneering studies on how information, influence, and memes propagate through social networks. His work with the SNAP team tracked cascades across millions of blog posts and news articles, establishing foundational models for viral spread.

''Stanford and Pinterest'' — As a professor at Stanford University, Leskovec trains the next generation of graph ML researchers. As Chief Scientist at Pinterest, he led the deployment of PinSage — a GraphSAGE variant running on a graph with 3 billion nodes and 18 billion edges — to power visual recommendations at industrial scale.

!! The Core Insight

Most machine learning assumes data points are independent — each image, each sentence, each row in a table stands alone. But in reality, //relationships between data points carry as much information as the data points themselves//. Leskovec's central insight is that real-world networks have ''learnable structure'': the pattern of connections around a node is not random noise but a rich, compressible signal. If you can learn to encode that pattern into a vector, you unlock a universal representation that transfers across tasks — node classification, link prediction, [[Community Detection|Community Detection]], and recommendation.

This is not just a technical trick. It represents a philosophical shift: from analyzing networks with hand-crafted statistics (degree distribution, clustering coefficient, betweenness centrality) to //letting the network teach you its own features//.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''node2vec'' formulates graph embedding as an optimization problem inspired by Word2Vec's skip-gram model. For a graph $G = (V, E)$, the objective is to maximize the log-probability of observing a node's network neighborhood $N_S(u)$ conditioned on its embedding $f(u)$:

$$\max_f \sum_{u \in V} \log P(N_S(u) \mid f(u))$$

The neighborhood $N_S(u)$ is defined by a second-order random walk controlled by two parameters: the //return parameter// $p$ (likelihood of immediately revisiting a node) and the //in-out parameter// $q$ (bias toward inward vs. outward exploration). When $p$ is low, the walk stays local (BFS-like); when $q$ is low, the walk explores outward (DFS-like). The conditional probability uses a softmax:

$$P(n_i \mid f(u)) = \frac{\exp(f(n_i) \cdot f(u))}{\sum_{v \in V} \exp(f(v) \cdot f(u))}$$

which is approximated with negative sampling for computational tractability.

''GraphSAGE'' replaces fixed embeddings with a learned aggregation. At each layer $k$, a node $v$ updates its representation by sampling neighbors and applying an aggregation function:

$$h_v^{(k)} = \sigma\left(W^{(k)} \cdot \text{CONCAT}\left(h_v^{(k-1)},\; \text{AGG}_k\left(\{h_u^{(k-1)} : u \in \mathcal{N}(v)\}\right)\right)\right)$$

where AGG can be a mean, LSTM, or max-pooling operation over sampled neighbor features. The key mathematical property is that this function is //inductive// — it generalizes to unseen nodes because it depends on the //aggregation function//, not on node identity.

!! Impact on the Field

''Stanford's Influence'' — Leskovec's CS224W (Machine Learning with Graphs) course has become one of the most popular ML courses worldwide, training thousands of students in graph representation learning and helping establish [[Graph Neural Networks|Graph Neural Network]] research as a mainstream subfield.

''SNAP as Standard Infrastructure'' — The SNAP dataset collection serves as the de facto benchmark suite for network analysis. Datasets like Cora, CiteSeer, and the various social network graphs are cited in virtually every graph ML paper. The SNAP C++ library and its Python wrapper (SNAP.py) provide efficient primitives for network analysis at scale.

''Pinterest and Industrial Graph ML'' — PinSage demonstrated that [[Graph Neural Networks|Graph Neural Networks]] could operate at the scale of billions of nodes. This was not merely an engineering achievement — it validated the entire paradigm of learned graph representations for production recommender systems, inspiring similar deployments at Uber, Alibaba, and other companies.

''The Graph ML Explosion'' — The combination of node2vec, GraphSAGE, and the theoretical frameworks from Leskovec's group helped catalyze the modern explosion of graph ML research, including connections to [[Graph Laplacian|Graph Laplacian]] methods, [[Spectral Graph Theory|Spectral Graph Theory]], and [[Knowledge Graphs|Knowledge Graphs]].

!! When Graph Methods Break Down

''1. Sparse or degenerate graphs.'' When a graph has very low connectivity — many isolated nodes or near-complete disconnection — random walks cannot explore meaningful structure and learned embeddings collapse toward uniform vectors. Graph methods need //enough edges// to learn from; on very sparse graphs, simple feature-based baselines often outperform graph neural networks.

''2. Dynamic, rapidly changing graphs.'' Transductive methods like node2vec must be retrained from scratch when the graph changes. Even inductive methods like GraphSAGE assume a relatively stable neighborhood structure. In graphs where edges appear and disappear at high frequency (high-frequency trading networks, real-time social feeds), the cost of re-aggregation can exceed the benefit of graph-based features.

''3. Over-smoothing at depth.'' Stacking too many aggregation layers in GraphSAGE or other [[Graph Neural Networks|Graph Neural Networks]] causes all node representations to converge toward the same vector — a phenomenon called //over-smoothing//. After $k$ layers, each node's representation is influenced by its entire $k$-hop neighborhood, which for small-world graphs quickly becomes the entire graph. This limits practical GNN depth to 2–4 layers for most real networks.
