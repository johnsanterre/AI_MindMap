! Grokking

<img src="assets/grokking.svg" alt="Diagram illustrating the Grokking phenomenon: delayed generalization after memorization" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine a student preparing for a mathematics exam by memorizing every worked example in the textbook. They ace every homework assignment and practice test — every problem matches something they have seen before. But on the real exam, with novel problems, they fail completely. Then, weeks after the course ends, while doing something unrelated, the underlying pattern suddenly //clicks//. They see why the method works, not just that it works. If they sat the makeup exam now, they would ace it — not by recall, but by genuine understanding. That delayed moment of true comprehension, arriving long after the apparent learning had stopped, is ''grokking''.

---

!! The Core Insight

In 2022, Power et al. at OpenAI trained small transformer models on a deceptively simple task: modular arithmetic of the form $(a + b) \mod 97$, where $a$ and $b$ are integers. The networks had more than enough capacity to memorize the training set, and they did exactly that — reaching 100% training accuracy within a few hundred steps. Validation accuracy, however, remained near 0%. By every conventional metric, learning had stopped. The models had overfit.

What Power et al. discovered was that if training continued — not for a little while, but for orders of magnitude more steps — validation accuracy would suddenly and sharply jump to nearly 100% as well. The transition was not gradual. It was a phase transition: the model crossed a threshold and generalized almost instantly after a prolonged plateau. The gap between the memorization phase and the generalization phase could span 10x to 100x the number of steps it took to reach full training accuracy.

This finding fundamentally challenges a core assumption of practical deep learning: that once training loss plateaus, nothing useful is happening. Grokking suggests that invisible algorithmic reorganization can continue inside a network long after the loss curve goes flat, and that validation performance is not always a reliable real-time signal of what the model is actually learning. It connects to broader phenomena like [[Overfitting]], [[Generalization Bounds]], [[Double Descent]], and the surprising capabilities that emerge in large [[Transformers]] under sustained [[Regularization]].

---

!! The Two Competing Circuits

The most illuminating mechanistic account of grokking comes from Nanda et al. (2023), who reverse-engineered exactly what happens inside the network during the grokking transition for modular addition. Using careful activation patching and Fourier analysis of the weight matrices, they identified two distinct computational circuits that co-exist in the network and compete for dominance.

The first is a ''memorization circuit'': essentially a large lookup table implemented in the attention heads and MLP layers. It works by associating each specific input pair $(a, b)$ with the correct output. Because it must store a separate representation for every training example, it requires high-norm weight matrices — it is expensive in terms of parameter magnitude. It generalizes to nothing outside the training set because it has learned no structure, only associations.

The second is a ''generalizing circuit'': a compact algorithm that implements the actual mathematical structure of modular addition using Fourier features. Specifically, the network learns to represent $a$ and $b$ as superpositions of $\cos(a\omega)$, $\sin(a\omega)$, $\cos(b\omega)$, $\sin(b\omega)$ for key frequencies $\omega$, and then exploits the trigonometric identity $$\cos((a+b)\omega) = \cos(a\omega)\cos(b\omega) - \sin(a\omega)\sin(b\omega)$$ to compute the result. This circuit is algorithmically correct and generalizes perfectly, but it requires the network to have discovered the relevant Fourier basis — a harder initialization problem. Its weight norm is much lower than the memorization circuit's.

Both circuits are present almost from the start of training. Initially the memorization circuit dominates, because it reaches low training loss faster. But weight decay (L2 [[Regularization]]) is continuously penalizing large-norm weights. Slowly, imperceptibly, it erodes the memorization circuit's advantage. At some threshold, the generalizing circuit — which has been quietly present but suppressed — achieves lower total loss (cross-entropy plus regularization penalty). At that moment, the memorization circuit collapses and the generalizing circuit takes over. That takeover is grokking.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The mathematical engine behind grokking is weight decay. The total training objective is $$\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} + \lambda \|\theta\|^2$$ where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}$ is cross-entropy loss, $\theta$ are the model parameters, and $\lambda$ is the regularization strength.

The memorization circuit minimizes $\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}$ to zero but at the cost of large $\|\theta\|^2$. The generalizing circuit also minimizes $\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}$ to near zero (once it has been discovered) but uses far smaller weights. For small $\lambda$, the penalty difference is negligible and the memorization circuit wins on initialization. But as training continues, gradient descent on $\lambda \|\theta\|^2$ continuously shrinks large weights. The memorization circuit degrades first — its accuracy begins to erode slightly on training data — while the generalizing circuit, needing only small weights, is relatively unaffected.

Experiments confirm the causal role of $\lambda$: increasing $\lambda$ dramatically accelerates grokking. The effective time to grok scales approximately as $T_{\text{grok}} \sim 1/\lambda$. Setting $\lambda = 0$ eliminates grokking entirely — the network memorizes and stays memorized. This reframes grokking from a mysterious emergent phenomenon into a predictable consequence of regularization working on two circuits with different weight norms. It is, in a sense, [[Regularization]] doing exactly what it is supposed to do — it just takes a very long time.

---

!! Grokking Beyond Modular Arithmetic

Grokking is not confined to modular arithmetic. The phenomenon has been replicated across permutation groups, sparse parity functions, graph algorithm tasks, and — most consequentially — in large language models on multi-step reasoning benchmarks. The conditions appear to be general: a limited training set relative to the full data distribution, non-zero regularization, and sufficient model capacity to support both a memorizing and a generalizing solution simultaneously.

The implications for practitioners are significant. First, models may have //already// learned a correct generalizing algorithm internally, yet fail to exhibit it on validation data because a competing memorization circuit is still dominant. Early stopping based on validation plateau could discard a model that would have grokked with more training. Second, the relationship between training time and capability is non-monotonic in ways that simple loss curves do not reveal. Third, the phenomenon resonates with ideas from statistical physics: sudden symmetry-breaking phase transitions where a system reorganizes discontinuously once a control parameter (here, effective regularization pressure) crosses a threshold.

Grokking also connects to the [[Double Descent]] phenomenon, where generalization error can worsen and then improve again as model size or training time increases, and to the lottery ticket hypothesis, where the useful sub-network is present early but takes time to dominate.

---

!! When Grokking Breaks Down

''No regularization, no grokking:'' Weight decay is not optional for grokking — it is the causal driver. When $\lambda = 0$, the memorization circuit faces no thermodynamic pressure and simply remains. Experiments removing weight decay uniformly produce networks that memorize permanently and never generalize, regardless of training duration. Practitioners relying on early stopping with no regularization will never observe grokking and may incorrectly conclude that a task is not learnable from the available data.

''Full dataset eliminates the effect:'' Grokking has only been reliably observed when the training set covers a small fraction (typically below 30%) of all possible input combinations. When the training set is large enough, memorization and generalization become nearly equivalent — there are too many examples for a lookup table to be efficient, so the generalizing circuit is preferred from early in training. The grokking gap collapses. This means the phenomenon is specifically tied to the interpolating regime where memorization is a viable strategy, not a universal property of neural network training.

''Task structure must support a compact algorithm:'' Not all tasks have two solutions with the weight-norm disparity that drives grokking. If the only way to solve a task is via a high-norm circuit — because no compact algorithmic solution exists — then weight decay erodes performance uniformly and no generalizing circuit emerges. Grokking is a property of tasks that have a short algorithmic description, making modular arithmetic, group operations, and simple graph problems ideal test beds. Tasks that are inherently high-complexity or noisy are unlikely candidates.

---

!! Technical Details

''Original paper:'' Power et al. 2022, "Grokking: Generalization Beyond Overfitting on Small Algorithmic Datasets" (OpenAI). Trained small transformers (1–2 layers, ~200k parameters) on binary operations modulo prime $p$, varying training fraction from 5% to 95%.

''Mechanistic analysis:'' Nanda et al. 2023, "Progress measures for grokking via mechanistic interpretability." Introduced the Fourier-basis decomposition of the generalizing circuit and coined the term "progress measures" — internal quantities (e.g., circuit dominance ratio) that increase monotonically even when validation accuracy is flat.

''Phase transition framing:'' Liu et al. 2022 formalized grokking as a first-order phase transition in the loss landscape, drawing on statistical mechanics. Barak et al. 2022 introduced "hidden progress measures" — loss-independent internal metrics that predict grokking timing before it occurs in validation accuracy.

''Required conditions (empirical):'' (1) Training set covers less than ~30% of all possible examples. (2) Weight decay $\lambda > 0$ (the larger, the faster the grokking). (3) Model has sufficient capacity to support both a memorizing and a generalizing solution simultaneously. (4) Task has a compact algorithmic solution with lower weight norm than the lookup-table solution.

''Relation to other phenomena:'' Grokking is a specific instance of the broader [[Double Descent]] pattern. It supports the lottery ticket hypothesis interpretation that the winning sub-network is present early. It provides a concrete mechanistic story for why training longer than apparent convergence can sometimes unlock dramatically better [[Generalization Bounds]]. The Fourier circuit discovered inside the grokking network is a rare case of a transformer learning a human-interpretable algorithm from scratch.
