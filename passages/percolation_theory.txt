! Percolation Theory

<img src="assets/percolation_theory.svg" alt="Lattice at three densities showing isolated clusters, critical threshold, and spanning giant component" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine pouring water through a bed of coffee grounds. If the grounds are packed too loosely with big air gaps, water pools in isolated pockets and never reaches the bottom — you get a sad, empty cup. Pack them just a little denser, past a critical threshold, and suddenly a connected path spans the entire filter from top to bottom. The coffee brews. That sharp transition from //disconnected// to //connected// is the ''percolation threshold'', and it is not a gradual slide — it is a sudden snap. The same mathematics governs whether a computer network survives random failures, whether an epidemic becomes a pandemic, and whether information can flow end-to-end through a <<link "neural network" "Neural Net">><</link>>.

!! The Core Insight

Percolation theory studies what happens when you randomly occupy sites or bonds in a lattice (or network) with probability $p$. The central result is a ''phase transition'' at a critical probability $p_c$:

* ''Below'' $p_c$: only small, isolated clusters exist. No path connects distant parts of the system.
* ''Above'' $p_c$: a ''giant connected component'' suddenly emerges that spans the entire system.
* ''At'' $p_c$: the system is fractal — clusters of all sizes coexist, and the geometry is self-similar.

The transition is //sharp//, not gradual. In the infinite-system limit, the probability of a spanning cluster jumps from exactly zero to strictly positive as $p$ crosses $p_c$. This is what makes percolation a true phase transition, analogous to water freezing at exactly 0°C — there is no halfway state.

!! Bond and Site Percolation

There are two fundamental flavors of percolation, depending on what you randomize:

''Bond percolation:'' Every //edge// in the lattice is independently present with probability $p$ and absent with probability $1-p$. Nodes are always present; the question is whether enough edges exist to form a connected path.

''Site percolation:'' Every //node// is independently occupied with probability $p$. Edges exist between adjacent occupied nodes. Here the randomness is in the nodes themselves.

The critical thresholds depend on the lattice geometry:

* ''Square lattice:'' $p_c \approx 0.5$ (bond), $p_c \approx 0.593$ (site)
* ''Triangular lattice:'' $p_c = 2 \sin(\pi/18) \approx 0.347$ (bond), $p_c = 0.5$ (site)
* ''Erdos-Renyi random graphs:'' $p_c = 1/N$ where $N$ is the number of nodes — even a vanishingly small connection probability suffices in large random graphs
* ''Scale-free networks:'' $p_c \to 0$ for random failure (the hubs keep things connected), but $p_c$ is //finite// for targeted attack on high-degree nodes — a critical asymmetry for <<link "network robustness" "Network Theory">><</link>>

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ''order parameter'' of percolation is $P_\infty(p)$, the probability that a randomly chosen node belongs to the infinite (spanning) cluster:

$$P_\infty(p) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } p < p_c \\ > 0 & \text{if } p > p_c \end{cases}$$

Near the critical point, $P_\infty$ follows a ''power law'':

$$P_\infty(p) \sim (p - p_c)^\beta \quad \text{for } p \to p_c^+$$

where $\beta$ is a ''critical exponent''. On a 2D lattice, $\beta = 5/36 \approx 0.139$.

At exactly $p = p_c$, the cluster size distribution follows another power law. If $n_s$ is the number of clusters of size $s$ per site:

$$n_s \sim s^{-\tau} \quad \text{at } p = p_c$$

with $\tau = 187/91 \approx 2.055$ in 2D. This tells us that at criticality, clusters of //every// size are present — there is no characteristic scale.

The ''correlation length'' $\xi$ — the typical cluster diameter — diverges as:

$$\xi \sim |p - p_c|^{-\nu}$$

with $\nu = 4/3$ in 2D. At $p_c$, $\xi \to \infty$, meaning the system is correlated at all length scales. This is ''universality'': the exponents $\beta$, $\tau$, and $\nu$ depend only on the spatial dimension, not on whether you do bond or site percolation, nor on the specific lattice type. A square lattice and a triangular lattice in 2D share the same critical exponents.

!! Percolation in ML and Networks

Percolation theory provides the mathematical backbone for several phenomena in machine learning and network science:

''Network robustness:'' <<link "Scale-free networks" "Network Theory">><</link>> (like the internet) are extraordinarily robust to //random// node failures — you can remove most nodes and the giant component persists because hubs hold everything together. But //targeted// removal of the highest-degree hubs shatters the network quickly. This is a percolation transition, and it explains why targeted attacks are so much more devastating than random ones.

''Epidemic spreading:'' The <<link "SIR model" "Markov Chains">><</link>> on a network maps exactly onto bond percolation. The transmission probability plays the role of $p$, and the epidemic threshold is $p_c$. Below threshold: the disease dies out locally. Above threshold: a pandemic spanning a finite fraction of the population.

''Dropout as percolation:'' <<link "Dropout" "Regularization">><</link>> in neural networks randomly removes nodes during training with probability $q$. This is literally site percolation run backwards — nodes are //present// with probability $1 - q$. If $q$ is too high, information cannot flow from input to output because no connected path survives. The practical dropout rates used (0.2 to 0.5) stay well above the percolation threshold for the network's architecture.

''Random graph connectivity:'' The <<link "Erdos-Renyi" "Random Walk">><</link>> result that $p_c = 1/N$ means that adding just $N/2$ edges to an $N$-node graph produces a giant component with high probability. This sharp threshold governs the connectivity of random sensor networks, ad hoc wireless networks, and randomly wired neural architectures.

''Information cascades:'' In <<link "social networks" "Network Theory">><</link>>, the spread of ideas, behaviors, or viral content follows percolation dynamics. A piece of content goes viral (spans the network) only if the per-contact sharing probability exceeds the network's percolation threshold.

!! When Percolation Theory Breaks Down

''Real networks are not random lattices.'' Classical percolation assumes uncorrelated random structure — every bond or site is independently occupied. Real networks have degree heterogeneity, clustering, and community structure that systematically shift the percolation threshold. A network with heavy-tailed degree distributions can have $p_c \to 0$, while strong community structure can create //multiple// local thresholds. Applying textbook lattice results to a real social or biological network without accounting for topology gives misleading predictions.

''Correlated failures break mean-field predictions.'' Standard percolation assumes independent failures, but real systems exhibit //cascading// failures — one node's failure overloads its neighbors, causing them to fail too. Power grid blackouts, financial contagion, and cascading router failures all involve correlated percolation, where the failure of one component makes nearby failures more likely. The resulting transitions can be //discontinuous// (first-order) rather than the smooth continuous transition of classical percolation, making them harder to predict and more catastrophic.

''Finite-size effects blur the transition.'' The sharp percolation threshold exists only in the infinite-system limit. Real networks are finite, and on a finite system the transition is a smooth crossover — there is no single $p_c$ but rather a window of probabilities where the giant component gradually emerges. For small networks (hundreds of nodes), the "threshold" can be so broad that the concept of a sharp transition is practically meaningless. Finite-size scaling theory provides corrections, but practitioners must be cautious about claiming sharp thresholds in small systems.

!! Technical Details

Percolation theory was introduced by ''Broadbent and Hammersley'' in 1957, originally motivated by the flow of fluid through a random porous medium. The standard reference is //Introduction to Percolation Theory// by Stauffer and Aharony (1994).

The connection to <<link "statistical mechanics" "Statistical Mechanics">><</link>> is deep: percolation can be formulated as the $q \to 1$ limit of the <<link "Potts model" "Ising Model">><</link>>, which generalizes the Ising model. This places percolation within the framework of ''universality classes'' — systems with the same symmetry and dimensionality share critical exponents regardless of microscopic details. The <<link "renormalization group" "Optimization">><</link>> provides the theoretical machinery for computing these exponents.

Key connections: in 2D, percolation is one of the few statistical mechanics models that can be solved exactly (Smirnov's proof of conformal invariance for critical percolation on the triangular lattice, 2001). The ''Harris criterion'' determines when disorder (randomness in $p_c$ itself) is relevant. ''Directed percolation'' — where bonds have a preferred direction — is the universality class for many non-equilibrium phase transitions.
