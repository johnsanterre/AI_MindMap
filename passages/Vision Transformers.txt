! Vision Transformers
<img src="assets/vision_transformers.svg" alt="Vision Transformers" style="width:100%;max-width:800px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine reading a book by cutting each page into a grid of small tiles, numbering them so you know their order, and then reading every tile while constantly glancing at every other tile to decide what matters. That is what a Vision Transformer does to an image: it chops the picture into a grid of patches, treats each patch as a "word," and lets the same self-attention machinery that powers large language models figure out which patches relate to which. No sliding filters, no hand-designed feature hierarchies — just raw attention across every patch simultaneously.

---
!! The Core Insight

''Vision Transformers'' (''ViT''), introduced by Dosovitskiy et al. (Google Brain, 2020), demonstrated that a pure [[Transformers|Transformers]] architecture applied directly to sequences of image patches can match or exceed the performance of state-of-the-art [[CNN|CNN]] on image classification — provided enough training data is available. The key conceptual leap is that images do not need convolutions at all: self-attention over patch embeddings is sufficient.

ViT inherits every advantage of the Transformer: global receptive field from layer one, parallelizable training, and a mature scaling playbook. The cost is data hunger — without large-scale pretraining, ViT underperforms a well-tuned ResNet. But at scale, ViT wins.

Key links: [[Attention Mechanisms|Attention Mechanisms]], [[Computer Vision|Computer Vision]], [[Transfer Learning|Transfer Learning]], [[Self-Supervised Learning|Self-Supervised Learning]].

---
!! From Patches to Predictions

''Patch tokenization:'' An input image of resolution $H \times W$ is divided into a grid of non-overlapping patches, each of size $P \times P$ pixels. For a 224x224 image with $P = 16$, this yields $N = (224/16)^2 = 196$ patches. Each patch is flattened into a vector of length $3P^2$ (for RGB) and linearly projected to dimension $D$, producing $N$ ''patch embeddings''.

''The [CLS] token:'' A learnable embedding is prepended to the sequence, bringing the total length to $N+1$. After passing through the Transformer encoder, the output at this position serves as the aggregate image representation, analogous to BERT's [CLS] token in NLP.

''Position embeddings:'' Since self-attention is permutation-invariant, the model has no built-in sense of spatial layout. Learnable 1D position embeddings are added to each patch embedding (including [CLS]) so the model can learn where each patch came from. Surprisingly, learned 1D embeddings work as well as more complex 2D-aware schemes.

''Classification head:'' The [CLS] output vector is fed through a small MLP (one hidden layer with GELU activation during pretraining, a single linear layer during fine-tuning) to produce class logits.

---
!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''Patch embedding:'' Given image $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C}$, extract $N$ patches $\mathbf{x}_p^i \in \mathbb{R}^{P^2 \cdot C}$. Project with a learnable matrix $\mathbf{E} \in \mathbb{R}^{(P^2 C) \times D}$:

$$\mathbf{z}_0 = [\mathbf{x}_{\text{class}};\; \mathbf{x}_p^1 \mathbf{E};\; \mathbf{x}_p^2 \mathbf{E};\; \ldots;\; \mathbf{x}_p^N \mathbf{E}] + \mathbf{E}_{\text{pos}}$$

where $\mathbf{x}_{\text{class}}$ is the learnable [CLS] token and $\mathbf{E}_{\text{pos}} \in \mathbb{R}^{(N+1) \times D}$ contains the position embeddings.

''Self-attention over patches:'' Each Transformer layer computes multi-head self-attention across all $N+1$ tokens. For a single head with queries $\mathbf{Q}$, keys $\mathbf{K}$, values $\mathbf{V}$:

$$\text{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)\mathbf{V}$$

Every patch attends to every other patch from the first layer — unlike CNNs, where the receptive field grows gradually across layers.

''MLP classification head:'' The final [CLS] representation $\mathbf{z}_L^0$ is projected to class logits:

$$\mathbf{y} = \text{Linear}(\text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{z}_L^0))$$

The entire pipeline — patch embedding, Transformer layers, classification head — is trained end-to-end with cross-entropy loss.

---
!! ViTs in Practice

''Pretraining data requirements:'' The original ViT paper showed that pretraining on ImageNet-1k (1.3M images) was insufficient — ViT lagged behind CNNs. Only when pretrained on ImageNet-21k (14M) or JFT-300M (300M images) did ViT-Large and ViT-Huge surpass the best CNNs. This initially suggested ViTs were impractical without massive private datasets.

''DeiT — data-efficient training:'' Touvron et al. (2021) showed that careful training recipes — strong data augmentation (RandAugment, Mixup, CutMix), regularization (stochastic depth, repeated augmentation), and a distillation token trained against a CNN teacher — allow ViT to match CNNs when trained ''only on ImageNet-1k''. DeiT eliminated the large-data bottleneck and made ViTs accessible.

''Hybrid CNN-ViT architectures:'' Models like CoAtNet, LeViT, and EfficientFormer combine convolutional stems (which are better at capturing local patterns in early layers) with Transformer blocks in later layers. This hybrid approach often achieves better speed-accuracy tradeoffs than pure ViTs or pure CNNs.

''DINO and self-supervised ViTs:'' Caron et al. (2021) showed that self-supervised training with self-distillation (DINO) produces ViT features with remarkable properties: the self-attention maps in the final layer cleanly segment objects without any segmentation labels. DINOv2 (2023) scaled this to produce general-purpose visual features rivaling supervised pretraining across [[Image Segmentation|Image Segmentation]], depth estimation, and retrieval. See [[Self-Supervised Learning|Self-Supervised Learning]].

---
!! When Vision Transformers Break Down

''Small-data regimes:'' Without sufficient pretraining data or strong regularization, ViTs underperform CNNs. The absence of inductive biases like translation equivariance and locality means ViTs must //learn// spatial structure from data. On small datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10 from scratch), a ResNet-18 will often outperform a ViT-Base. CNNs encode useful priors; ViTs start from scratch.

''Computational cost at high resolution:'' Self-attention scales quadratically with the number of patches: $O(N^2 D)$. Doubling image resolution from 224 to 448 quadruples the patch count and increases attention cost 16-fold. This makes vanilla ViTs impractical for high-resolution tasks like medical imaging or satellite analysis without architectural modifications (window attention, pooling, or hierarchical designs like Swin Transformer).

''Loss of fine-grained local features:'' The 16x16 patch size means that details smaller than a patch are blurred into a single embedding vector. For tasks requiring pixel-level precision — dense prediction, small object detection, fine-grained texture discrimination — pure ViTs can lose critical local information. Hierarchical variants (Swin, PVT) and hybrid architectures partially address this by reintroducing multi-scale feature maps. See [[CNN|CNN]] for architectures with built-in local feature extraction.

---
!! Technical Details

''ViT variants (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020):''
* ViT-Base: 12 layers, 768-d, 12 heads, 86M params
* ViT-Large: 24 layers, 1024-d, 16 heads, 307M params
* ViT-Huge: 32 layers, 1280-d, 16 heads, 632M params
* Patch sizes: /14 and /16 are most common (ViT-L/16 means Large model with 16x16 patches)

''Scaling laws:'' Zhai et al. (2022) showed ViTs follow predictable scaling laws — performance improves log-linearly with compute, data, and model size, similar to LLMs. ViT-22B (22 billion parameters) trained on JFT-4B demonstrated continued improvement without saturation.

''Comparison with CNNs:'' At equal compute, modern CNNs (ConvNeXt, Liu et al. 2022) match ViTs by adopting Transformer-inspired design choices (larger kernels, LayerNorm, GELU, fewer activations). The gap is architectural philosophy, not fundamental capability. See [[CNN|CNN]].

''MAE pretraining:'' He et al. (2022) introduced Masked Autoencoders — mask 75% of patches and train the ViT to reconstruct the missing pixels. MAE produces strong representations with less pretraining data than contrastive methods, and scales efficiently because the encoder only processes visible (25%) patches. See also [[CVPR|CVPR]] where this work was presented.

''Swin Transformer:'' Liu et al. (2021) introduced hierarchical ViT with shifted window attention, reducing complexity to $O(N)$ while building multi-scale feature maps. Swin is the dominant backbone for dense prediction tasks (detection, segmentation) and won the ICCV 2021 Best Paper Honorable Mention.

''Fine-tuning at higher resolution:'' ViTs pretrained at 224px can be fine-tuned at 384px or higher by interpolating position embeddings. This consistently improves accuracy at modest additional cost.
