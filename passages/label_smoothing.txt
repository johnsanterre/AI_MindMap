! Label Smoothing
<img src="assets/label_smoothing.svg" alt="Hard labels vs label smoothing probability distributions" style="max-width:100%;margin:1em 0;">

A rigid teacher who only accepts "the exact right answer" creates students who guess confidently but fail to recognize nuance. A teacher who says "the best answer is X, but Y and Z have some merit" creates more calibrated students. Label smoothing does the same for classifiers: instead of training toward absolute certainty, it trains toward high-but-not-perfect confidence.

!! The Core Insight

Introduced by Szegedy et al. 2016 (Inception-v3). Instead of one-hot target labels $y_{\text{hard}}$ where $y_k = 1$ for the correct class and $0$ elsewhere, label smoothing uses:

$$y_{\text{smooth},k} = (1-\varepsilon) \cdot y_{k} + \frac{\varepsilon}{K}$$

where $\varepsilon$ is the smoothing parameter (typically 0.1) and $K$ is the number of classes. The correct class gets probability $1 - \varepsilon + \varepsilon/K$; all others get $\varepsilon/K$. This prevents the model from becoming infinitely confident — the cross-entropy loss has no finite minimum with hard labels.

See also: [[Cross-entropy]], [[Regularization]], [[Model Calibration]], [[Knowledge Distillation]], [[Softmax]], [[Overfitting]].

!! Why Hard Labels Are Harmful

Cross-entropy loss with hard labels: $\mathcal{L} = -\log p(y)$, minimized when $p(y) \to 1$ and $p(\text{other}) \to 0$, which requires the logit for the correct class to approach $+\infty$. This drives the model to produce extreme logits, leading to:

- ''Overconfidence:'' predicted probabilities near 1.0 even when wrong
- ''Poor calibration:'' predicted 99% probability does not correspond to 99% accuracy
- ''Information loss:'' useful inter-class similarity information is suppressed in intermediate layers

Label smoothing bounds the optimal logits to a finite value, naturally limiting these failure modes.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

With label smoothing $\varepsilon$, the cross-entropy loss decomposes as:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{LS}} = (1-\varepsilon)\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} + \varepsilon \cdot H(\text{uniform})$$

This is a convex combination of standard cross-entropy and a maximum-entropy (uniform) regularizer. The optimal logit gap between the correct class and any other class is approximately:

$$f^* = \log\frac{(1-\varepsilon)(K-1)}{\varepsilon}$$

For $K = 1000$, $\varepsilon = 0.1$: $f^* \approx \log(9 \times 999 / 100) \approx \log(90) \approx 4.5$ — finite and bounded, compared to $\infty$ under hard labels.

Hinton et al. 2015 (knowledge distillation): soft targets from a teacher network are equivalent to label smoothing with an //informed// $\varepsilon$ that encodes real inter-class structure.

!! Calibration and Distillation Connection

''Calibration improvement:'' the model's predicted probabilities better match empirical accuracy. Expected Calibration Error (ECE) typically improves by 30–50% with label smoothing.

''Regularization effect:'' the uniform component prevents extreme parameter values, reducing overfitting in a manner analogous to weight decay.

''Knowledge distillation (Hinton 2015):'' teacher soft labels $y_{\text{teacher}}$ serve the same role as label smoothing but encode //structured// information about inter-class similarities (e.g., "cat" softly related to "dog"). This is more powerful than flat label smoothing.

''Distillation incompatibility (Müller et al. 2019):'' label smoothing in the //teacher// degrades student performance. Label smoothing erases the inter-class similarity structure that makes distillation informative — the teacher's representations become less clustered and less transferable.

!! When Label Smoothing Breaks Down

''Knowledge distillation incompatibility:'' applying label smoothing to the teacher before distillation reduces the information available to the student. Müller et al. 2019 showed this empirically across multiple tasks.

''Wrong $\varepsilon$:'' too large an $\varepsilon$ over-regularizes, hurting top-1 accuracy; too small has negligible effect. The standard 0.1 is not always optimal.

''Very small datasets:'' high $\varepsilon$ can prevent convergence — the signal from the correct class is diluted too much for the model to distinguish classes reliably.

!! Technical Details

- ''Szegedy et al. 2016'' — Rethinking the Inception Architecture; introduced label smoothing with $\varepsilon = 0.1$ on ImageNet
- ''Pereyra et al. 2017'' — Regularizing neural networks by penalizing confident output distributions (confidence penalty; related approach via entropy maximization)
- ''Müller et al. 2019'' — When does label smoothing help? Identifies distillation conflict; shows that label smoothing clusters penultimate representations differently
- ''Yuan et al. 2020'' — Revisiting knowledge distillation via label smoothing regularization
- Typical $\varepsilon$: 0.1 for ImageNet classification, 0.1 for NLP sequence tasks
- ECE improvement: 30–50% reduction in calibration error on standard benchmarks
- Label smoothing is equivalent to adding an entropy bonus: $-H(p)$ to the loss, weighted by $\varepsilon$
