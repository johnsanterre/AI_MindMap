! Score Matching
<img src="assets/score_matching.svg" alt="Score matching diagram showing score vectors, neural network training, and Langevin dynamics sampling" style="max-width:100%;margin:1em 0;">

Imagine you're lost in a hilly landscape at night and can feel the slope underfoot but can't see the peaks. The gradient of the terrain at each point tells you which way is uphill — toward the peaks. Score matching teaches a neural network to estimate this "slope" of a probability density everywhere, then uses that slope field to navigate from random noise to high-probability regions. No need to ever compute the height of the peaks — just the direction of steepest ascent.

!! The Core Insight

''Hyvärinen 2005'' defined the ''score function'' as $s(x) = \nabla_x \log p(x)$ — the gradient of the log-density with respect to the data point $x$. Training a model to match this score sidesteps computing the intractable normalizing constant $Z$ in $p(x) = \tilde{p}(x)/Z$, because $\nabla_x \log p(x) = \nabla_x \log \tilde{p}(x)$ — $Z$ cancels.

The naive score matching objective is:

$$\mathcal{J}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{p(x)}\!\left[\tfrac{1}{2}\|s_\theta(x) - \nabla_x \log p(x)\|^2\right]$$

This requires the true score, which we don't have. But via integration by parts, Hyvärinen showed it can be rewritten in terms of quantities we //can// compute from data — trace of the Jacobian of $s_\theta$ plus $\tfrac{1}{2}\|s_\theta\|^2$. This makes score matching a tractable, likelihood-free training objective and forms the theoretical foundation of modern [[Diffusion Models]].

Links: [[Diffusion Models]], [[diffusion]], [[VAE]], [[GAN]], [[Normalizing Flows]]

!! Denoising Score Matching

''Vincent 2011'' introduced a simpler, scalable variant. Perturb a data point $x$ with Gaussian noise: $\tilde{x} = x + \varepsilon$, where $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2 I)$. Train the network to predict the score of the //noisy// distribution $p_\sigma(\tilde{x})$:

$$\mathcal{J}_\text{DSM}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x,\,\tilde{x}}\!\left[\left\|s_\theta(\tilde{x}) - \frac{x - \tilde{x}}{\sigma^2}\right\|^2\right]$$

The key insight is that the ''optimal score of the noisy distribution'' equals the denoiser direction $(x - \tilde{x})/\sigma^2$ — the vector pointing from the noisy sample back toward the clean data. This reframes score estimation as a denoising regression problem, which scales well with mini-batches and large networks. It also reveals a deep connection between score matching and [[Denoising Autoencoders]].

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Once we have a trained score network $s_\theta(x)$, we can sample from $p(x)$ using ''Langevin dynamics'' — a Markov chain that follows score arrows plus injected noise:

$$x_{t+1} = x_t + \frac{\varepsilon}{2}\,s_\theta(x_t) + \sqrt{\varepsilon}\,z_t, \qquad z_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$$

As step size $\varepsilon \to 0$ and steps $T \to \infty$, this chain's stationary distribution is exactly $p(x)$. The drift term $\frac{\varepsilon}{2} s_\theta(x_t)$ pushes iterates toward high-density regions; the noise term $\sqrt{\varepsilon}\,z_t$ prevents collapse to a single mode and ensures proper mixing.

''Song & Ermon 2019'' (NCSN) showed that using a //single// noise level fails in low-density regions. Their fix: train score models at multiple noise levels $\sigma_1 > \sigma_2 > \cdots > \sigma_L$ and anneal during sampling — start with large $\sigma$ (easy to estimate scores everywhere), end with small $\sigma$ (sharp samples).

''Song et al. 2021'' unified all of this under a continuous-time SDE framework: the forward process adds noise according to a stochastic differential equation $dx = f(x,t)\,dt + g(t)\,dW$, and the reverse process uses the score to denoise: $dx = [f(x,t) - g(t)^2 \nabla_x \log p_t(x)]\,dt + g(t)\,d\bar{W}$. DDPM and NCSN are special cases with different $f$ and $g$.

!! Connection to Diffusion Models

''Ho et al. 2020'' (DDPM) showed that the "epsilon prediction" network in their diffusion model is equivalent to denoising score matching up to a scaling factor: $\varepsilon_\theta(x_t, t) \propto -s_\theta(x_t, t)$. Predicting the noise added is the same as predicting the direction back toward the data — i.e., the score.

''Karras et al. 2022'' (EDM) showed most diffusion model variants are instances of score matching with different preconditioning schemes for the network and noise schedule. The choice of preconditioning dramatically affects training stability and sample quality. ''DDIM'' (Song et al. 2020) derived a deterministic ODE sampler from the score network, allowing high-quality generation in far fewer steps by removing the stochastic noise injection.

Score matching explains //why// diffusion models work: they learn a vector field that points toward the data manifold, and sampling is simply following that field from noise to data.

!! When Score Matching Breaks Down

''Low-density regions:'' Score estimation is unreliable where training data is sparse. If $p(x) \approx 0$ somewhere, there are no examples to supervise the score network there, so Langevin dynamics can get stuck or diverge — the multi-scale noise trick in NCSN is the main mitigation.

''Mode coverage imbalance:'' Score-based models can assign unequal probability mass to modes even when the score is well-estimated near each mode. Long-range structure (which mode to land in) is harder to capture than local geometry (how to refine within a mode).

''Slow sampling:'' Langevin dynamics requires many small steps — hundreds to thousands — to produce good samples. Deterministic ODE samplers (DDIM, DPM-Solver) and consistency models address this but introduce their own tradeoffs.

!! Technical Details

''Key papers:'' Hyvärinen 2005 (score matching, integration-by-parts trick); Vincent 2011 (denoising score matching); Song & Ermon 2019 (NCSN, multi-scale noise); Ho et al. 2020 (DDPM, epsilon prediction); Song et al. 2021 (score SDEs, continuous-time unification); Karras et al. 2022 (EDM, preconditioning analysis); Song et al. 2020 (DDIM, deterministic sampling).

''Sliced score matching'' (Song et al. 2020): projects the Jacobian trace onto random directions, reducing the $O(d^2)$ Jacobian cost to $O(d)$ — essential for high-dimensional data.

''Consistency models'' (Song et al. 2023): train a network to map any point on a score-model trajectory directly to the endpoint, enabling single-step generation while retaining score-matching quality.

''Relation to energy-based models:'' the score is the negative gradient of an energy function $E(x) = -\log \tilde{p}(x)$. Score matching is one principled way to train energy-based models without contrastive divergence.
