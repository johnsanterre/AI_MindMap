! Control Theory

<img src="assets/control_theory.svg" alt="Diagram showing a feedback control loop with PID controller and mapping to reinforcement learning" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Think of a thermostat. It measures the room temperature, compares it to the setting you dialled in, and adjusts the heater — more heat if the room is too cold, less if it is too warm. That loop — //measure, compare, correct// — is the core of ''control theory''. It is the same loop that keeps a drone stable in a crosswind, holds a self-driving car in its lane, and (it turns out) guides a [[Reinforcement Learning]] agent as it optimises its [[policy gradient|policy]]. Control theory is the original science of making systems do what you want through feedback, and modern ML quietly inherits many of its ideas.

---

!! The Core Insight

''Control theory'' studies how to make dynamical systems behave the way you want by observing their output and feeding corrective inputs back in. The system you are trying to control is called the ''plant''. The plant has its own dynamics — potentially unknown, nonlinear, or unstable. The ''controller'' observes the plant's output $y(t)$, compares it to a reference signal $r(t)$ (the desired behaviour), computes the error $e(t) = r(t) - y(t)$, and applies a corrective input $u(t)$.

Three questions dominate the field. ''Stability:'' does the system's output stay bounded, or does it diverge? ''Controllability:'' can you steer the system from any initial state to any desired state by choosing the right inputs? ''Observability:'' can you infer the plant's internal state from its outputs alone? These three pillars — stability, controllability, observability — have direct analogs in reinforcement learning. A [[Markov Decision Process]] //is// a discrete-time controlled dynamical system. The [[bellman equation|Bellman equation]] //is// a discrete-time version of the Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation from optimal control. The policy //is// the controller. The connections run deep.

---

!! PID Control: The Workhorse

The ''PID controller'' (Proportional-Integral-Derivative) is the most widely deployed feedback controller in history — it runs over 90% of industrial control loops worldwide. Its control signal combines three terms:

$$u(t) = K_p \, e(t) \;+\; K_i \int_0^t e(\tau)\,d\tau \;+\; K_d \, \frac{de}{dt}$$

The ''Proportional'' term $K_p \, e(t)$ responds to the //current// error: if the temperature is 5 degrees below target, push harder. The ''Integral'' term $K_i \int e\,d\tau$ accumulates //past// error and eliminates steady-state offset — if the system has been slightly below target for a long time, the integral builds up and pushes the output to where it should be. The ''Derivative'' term $K_d \, de/dt$ anticipates //future// error by responding to the rate of change — if the error is shrinking fast, ease off to avoid overshoot.

Tuning these three gains $K_p, K_i, K_d$ is an art. The classic ''Ziegler-Nichols method'' increases proportional gain until the system oscillates, then sets all three gains from the oscillation period. Modern tuning uses optimisation. But PID has fundamental limits: it assumes roughly linear dynamics and struggles with long delays, strong nonlinearities, and high-dimensional systems. This is exactly where neural network controllers — learned end-to-end — are taking over.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The standard framework is ''state-space representation''. The plant is described by two equations:

$$\dot{x}(t) = Ax(t) + Bu(t), \qquad y(t) = Cx(t) + Du(t)$$

Here $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the state vector, $u \in \mathbb{R}^m$ is the control input, $y \in \mathbb{R}^p$ is the measured output, and $A, B, C, D$ are matrices that encode the system's dynamics. In the Laplace domain the system becomes a ''transfer function'' $G(s) = C(sI - A)^{-1}B + D$, turning differential equations into algebra.

''Stability'' comes down to the eigenvalues of $A$: if every eigenvalue has a negative real part (poles in the left half-plane), perturbations decay and the system is stable. If any eigenvalue has a positive real part, the system blows up.

The crown jewel of classical optimal control is the ''Linear Quadratic Regulator'' (LQR). It minimises a quadratic cost:

$$J = \int_0^\infty \bigl(x^T Q\, x + u^T R\, u\bigr)\,dt$$

where $Q$ penalises state deviation and $R$ penalises control effort. The optimal controller is $u = -Kx$ where $K = R^{-1}B^T P$ and $P$ solves the ''algebraic Riccati equation''. This is the continuous-time analog of the [[bellman equation|Bellman equation]] — LQR is optimal control for linear dynamics with quadratic cost, just as value iteration is optimal control for discrete MDPs with arbitrary reward.

---

!! Control Theory Meets Reinforcement Learning

The mapping between control theory and RL is more than analogy — it is mathematical equivalence in special cases.

| ''Control Theory'' | ''Reinforcement Learning'' |
| State $x(t)$ | Observation $s_t$ |
| Control input $u(t)$ | Action $a_t$ |
| Cost function $J$ | Negative cumulative reward |
| Plant dynamics $\dot{x} = f(x,u)$ | Environment transition $P(s'|s,a)$ |
| Controller $u = \pi(x)$ | Policy $\pi(a|s)$ |
| Kalman filter | Optimal state estimation (POMDP belief) |

''LQR'' is the optimal policy for linear dynamics + quadratic cost. ''Model Predictive Control'' (MPC) plans over a finite horizon using a known model — essentially the same idea as model-based RL with planning, as in [[World Models]]. The ''Kalman filter'' is optimal state estimation under linear-Gaussian assumptions — the observer that lets you infer hidden state from noisy measurements.

Modern research lives at the intersection: learning controllers from data (''system identification'' maps to model-based RL), neural [[Lyapunov Functions|Lyapunov functions]] for verifying stability of learned policies, ''differentiable simulators'' that backpropagate through physics, and sim-to-real transfer in robotics where control-theoretic robustness guarantees meet [[Deep Learning]] representations.

---

!! When Control Theory Breaks Down

''Nonlinear systems:'' Classical control theory is built on linearity — superposition, transfer functions, eigenvalue analysis. Real systems like robotic arms, biological networks, and turbulent flows are deeply nonlinear. Linearisation around an operating point works locally but fails globally. Nonlinear control (feedback linearisation, sliding mode control) exists but is far harder to apply, which is why learned controllers trained end-to-end are increasingly preferred for complex nonlinear systems.

''Model uncertainty:'' Control design typically assumes you know the plant dynamics (the $A, B, C, D$ matrices). When the model is wrong — and it always is — performance degrades or the system destabilises. ''Robust control'' ($H_\infty$ methods) handles bounded uncertainty, but large model errors remain dangerous. This is precisely why sim-to-real transfer in robotics is hard: the simulator's dynamics never perfectly match reality.

''High dimensionality:'' Classical control scales poorly to systems with thousands of state variables. A humanoid robot has dozens of joints; an image-based controller has millions of pixel inputs. Control-theoretic tools like LQR require solving matrix Riccati equations that scale cubically in state dimension. Deep RL sidesteps this by using neural networks as function approximators, but sacrifices the stability guarantees that make classical control trustworthy.

---

!! Technical Details

''Historical milestones:'' Nyquist stability criterion (1932), Bode plots (1945), Kalman filter and LQR (1960), Pontryagin's maximum principle (1956), Zames $H_\infty$ robust control (1981). Standard textbook: Astrom & Murray, //Feedback Systems// (2008, free online).

''Lyapunov stability theory:'' A system $\dot{x} = f(x)$ is stable at an equilibrium if there exists a ''Lyapunov function'' $V(x) > 0$ with $\dot{V}(x) \leq 0$. Finding Lyapunov functions is in general hard; recent work uses neural networks to learn them, providing stability certificates for learned controllers.

''Adaptive control:'' When plant parameters are unknown, the controller estimates them online and adjusts its gains — a form of online learning that predates modern ML by decades.

''Model Predictive Control (MPC):'' At each timestep, solve an optimisation problem over a finite horizon using a model of the plant, apply the first control action, then re-plan. MPC handles constraints naturally and is widely used in process control, autonomous driving, and robotics. It connects directly to planning in model-based RL.

''Connections:'' [[Reinforcement Learning]], [[Markov Decision Process]], [[bellman equation|Bellman equation]], [[policy gradient|Policy Gradient]], [[Actor-critic]], [[dynamic programming|Dynamic Programming]], [[Q Learning]].
