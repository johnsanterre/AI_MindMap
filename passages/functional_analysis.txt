! Functional Analysis

<img src="assets/functional_analysis.svg" alt="Diagram showing finite vs infinite dimensional spaces, Banach-Hilbert hierarchy, operators and kernels, and foundational theorems" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you have a spreadsheet with a column of numbers — a vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Now imagine that column has //infinitely// many rows. Better yet, imagine each "vector" is an entire //function// — a continuous curve, a signal, a probability density. You can still add them, scale them, and measure their length. ''Functional analysis'' is the mathematics that makes all of this rigorous: it studies infinite-dimensional vector spaces and the operators (transformations) that act on them. Every time a kernel method implicitly maps data into "feature space," it is working inside a structure that functional analysis built.

!! The Core Insight

In finite dimensions, all norms are equivalent and every linear map is continuous. In infinite dimensions, //none of this is true//. A sequence of functions can converge in one sense but diverge wildly in another. An operator can be linear yet unbounded. Functional analysis identifies the precise conditions — ''completeness'', ''boundedness'', ''compactness'' — under which the familiar tools of linear algebra survive the jump to infinity.

The key hierarchy of spaces:
- A ''normed space'' has a notion of length: $\|f\|$.
- A ''Banach space'' is a normed space that is ''complete'' — every Cauchy sequence converges within the space.
- A ''Hilbert space'' is a Banach space whose norm comes from an ''inner product'': $\|f\| = \sqrt{\langle f, f \rangle}$.

Hilbert spaces are the "nicest" infinite-dimensional spaces because the inner product gives you geometry — angles, orthogonality, and projections — just like in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

!! Spaces, Norms, and Inner Products

The most important concrete example is $L^2$, the space of square-integrable functions:

$$L^2([a,b]) = \left\{ f : \int_a^b |f(x)|^2 \, dx < \infty \right\}$$

with inner product $\langle f, g \rangle = \int_a^b f(x) g(x) \, dx$. This is a Hilbert space: complete, with a geometry that lets you decompose functions into orthogonal components (Fourier series, wavelets, eigenfunctions).

Other key spaces:
- $L^1$: integrable functions (Banach, not Hilbert — no inner product)
- $L^\infty$: essentially bounded functions (Banach, not Hilbert)
- $\ell^2$: square-summable sequences $\{x_n\}$ with $\sum |x_n|^2 < \infty$ (Hilbert)
- ''Sobolev spaces'' $H^k$: functions whose derivatives up to order $k$ are in $L^2$ (Hilbert, used in PDE theory and finite element methods)

The ''Riesz representation theorem'' states that in a Hilbert space, every bounded linear functional $\phi: H \to \mathbb{R}$ has the form $\phi(f) = \langle f, g \rangle$ for a unique $g \in H$. This means the dual space $H^*$ is isomorphic to $H$ itself — a property that fails in general Banach spaces and is what makes Hilbert spaces so tractable.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

An ''operator'' $T: V \to W$ between function spaces is the infinite-dimensional analog of a matrix. Key types:

''Bounded (continuous) operators'': $T$ is bounded if there exists $C$ such that $\|Tf\| \leq C\|f\|$ for all $f$. The ''operator norm'' is:

$$\|T\| = \sup_{\|f\|=1} \|Tf\|$$

This is the direct generalization of the largest singular value of a matrix.

''Compact operators'' map bounded sets to sets with compact closure — they are the "nearly finite-dimensional" operators. Every compact operator on a Hilbert space has a ''singular value decomposition'':

$$Tf = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \sigma_n \langle f, u_n \rangle v_n$$

where $\sigma_n \to 0$. This is the infinite-dimensional SVD.

The ''spectral theorem'' for self-adjoint compact operators states:

$$Tf = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \lambda_n \langle f, e_n \rangle e_n$$

where $\{e_n\}$ are orthonormal eigenfunctions and $\lambda_n \to 0$. This underpins PCA on function spaces (functional PCA) and the Mercer expansion of kernels.

''Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS)'': A Hilbert space $H$ of functions on a set $X$ is an RKHS if point evaluation $f \mapsto f(x)$ is a bounded linear functional for every $x$. By Riesz representation, there exists $k_x \in H$ such that $f(x) = \langle f, k_x \rangle$. The ''kernel function'' is:

$$k(x, y) = \langle k_x, k_y \rangle$$

''Mercer's theorem'' says that any continuous positive-definite kernel $k(x,y)$ admits the expansion:

$$k(x, y) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \lambda_n e_n(x) e_n(y)$$

This is the spectral decomposition that makes kernel methods work: the kernel implicitly computes inner products in a (potentially infinite-dimensional) feature space without ever constructing that space explicitly.

!! Functional Analysis in Machine Learning

''Kernel methods'': Support vector machines, Gaussian processes, and kernel PCA all operate in an RKHS. The <<link "Kernel Methods" "Kernel Methods">><</link>> page covers the algorithmic side; functional analysis provides the theoretical guarantee that these spaces are well-behaved Hilbert spaces where optimization is tractable.

''Representer theorem'': In an RKHS, the minimizer of any regularized empirical risk functional lies in the span of the kernel evaluations at training points: $f^* = \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i k(\cdot, x_i)$. This reduces an infinite-dimensional optimization to a finite one.

''Neural tangent kernel (NTK)'': In the infinite-width limit, a neural network's training dynamics are governed by a fixed kernel — placing deep learning inside the functional analysis framework.

''Integral operators'' appear throughout: the kernel integral operator $(T_k f)(x) = \int k(x,y) f(y) \, dy$ connects kernel functions to spectral theory, and its eigendecomposition determines the effective dimensionality of the RKHS.

!! When Functional Analysis Breaks Down

''Not all operators have eigenvalues.'' The spectral theorem for compact self-adjoint operators is clean, but general bounded operators on infinite-dimensional spaces can have ''continuous spectrum'' — values that are "almost eigenvalues" but lack actual eigenvectors. The shift operator on $\ell^2$ has no eigenvalues at all, yet its spectrum is the entire unit disk. Naively searching for eigenvectors in such cases leads nowhere.

''Weak and strong topologies diverge.'' In infinite dimensions, a sequence can converge ''weakly'' ($\langle f_n, g \rangle \to \langle f, g \rangle$ for all $g$) without converging in norm. The unit ball in an infinite-dimensional Hilbert space is weakly compact but //not// norm-compact. This causes subtle errors when convergence arguments from finite dimensions are applied carelessly — an optimization algorithm may "converge" in one topology while failing in another.

''Unbounded operators are unavoidable.'' The most important operators in physics (momentum $-i\frac{d}{dx}$, Hamiltonians) and in PDEs (the Laplacian $\nabla^2$) are unbounded — they are not defined on all of $H$, only on dense subdomains. Their spectral theory requires the machinery of ''unbounded self-adjoint operators'', including domain questions that have no finite-dimensional analog. Getting the domain wrong can produce physically meaningless or mathematically inconsistent results.

!! Technical Details

The four pillars of functional analysis are the ''Hahn-Banach theorem'' (extending linear functionals), the ''Banach-Steinhaus theorem'' (uniform boundedness principle), the ''open mapping theorem'' (surjective bounded operators map open sets to open sets), and the ''closed graph theorem'' (a closed linear operator between Banach spaces is bounded).

''Weak-* topology'': On the dual space $X^*$, the weak-* topology is the coarsest topology making evaluation maps continuous. The ''Banach-Alaoglu theorem'' states the closed unit ball of $X^*$ is weak-* compact — a crucial tool for existence proofs in optimization and PDE theory.

''Fredholm theory'': A bounded operator $T$ is ''Fredholm'' if it has finite-dimensional kernel and cokernel. The ''Fredholm index'' $\text{ind}(T) = \dim\ker T - \dim\text{coker}\, T$ is a topological invariant. Fredholm operators arise in integral equations and regularization theory.

''Sobolev embedding theorems'' give conditions under which Sobolev spaces embed into spaces of continuous functions: $H^k(\mathbb{R}^d) \hookrightarrow C^0(\mathbb{R}^d)$ when $k > d/2$. This determines when an RKHS consists of pointwise-evaluable functions.

The ''trace class'' and ''Hilbert-Schmidt operators'' are operator analogs of $\ell^1$ and $\ell^2$ sequences: $\sum \sigma_n < \infty$ (trace class) or $\sum \sigma_n^2 < \infty$ (Hilbert-Schmidt). Kernel integral operators with $\int\!\int |k(x,y)|^2 \, dx\, dy < \infty$ are Hilbert-Schmidt.
