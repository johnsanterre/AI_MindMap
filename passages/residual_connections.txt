! Residual Connections

<img src="assets/residual_connections.svg" alt="Diagram comparing networks with and without residual skip connections" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are editing a photograph. Instead of rebuilding the entire image from scratch with each adjustment, you start with the original photo and just add small corrections — a little more brightness here, a bit more contrast there. If any single correction is bad, the original image is mostly preserved. That is exactly what residual connections do inside a neural network: each layer only has to learn the //correction// to apply to its input, not a complete new representation from scratch. This single idea unlocked the training of networks hundreds of layers deep, when previously even twenty layers could be intractable.

---

!! The Core Insight

The fundamental problem with very deep networks is that gradients — the training signals flowing backward through <<link "Backpropagation and Chain Rule" "Backpropagation and Chain Rule">><</link>> — must pass through every single layer. Each layer multiplies the gradient by its weight matrix. If those multipliers are consistently less than one, the gradient shrinks exponentially and effectively vanishes by the time it reaches the early layers. This is the <<link "vanishing gradient" "vanishing gradient">><</link>> problem, and it made networks deeper than about 15–20 layers essentially untrainable.

He et al. (2015) proposed a stunningly simple fix in their <<link "ResNets" "ResNets">><</link>> paper: instead of asking a stack of layers to learn the desired mapping $H(x)$ directly, add a ''shortcut connection'' that bypasses the layers and adds the input $x$ back to the output. The layers now only need to learn the ''residual'' $F(x) = H(x) - x$, so the output is $y = F(x) + x$. If the ideal transformation is close to the identity (which is often true in deep networks), then $F(x)$ is close to zero — much easier to learn than the full mapping. More critically, during backpropagation the gradient has a direct path through the addition, bypassing the problematic weight multiplications entirely.

---

!! Why Identity Shortcuts Work So Well

The power of the identity shortcut becomes clear when you think about what happens during training. Without residual connections, the gradient at layer $l$ depends on the product of Jacobians across all subsequent layers — a chain of matrix multiplications that can either explode or vanish. With residual connections, the gradient decomposes into a sum of paths: one that goes directly through the skip connection (carrying a gradient of exactly 1) and others that pass through the learned layers.

This means that even if the learned layers produce poor gradients early in training, the skip connection guarantees that //some// gradient signal reaches every layer. The network can never do worse than the identity function, because setting $F(x) = 0$ (all weights to zero) recovers the input perfectly. Each residual block is essentially asking: "What small correction should I add?" rather than "What entirely new representation should I produce?"

This also explains why ''depth'' suddenly became beneficial rather than harmful. Before residual connections, adding more layers to a network often //hurt// performance on the training set — not just the test set — because optimisation failed. With residual connections, a 152-layer ResNet outperformed an 18-layer one, and even 1000+ layer networks could be trained successfully. The <<link "Transformers" "Transformers">><</link>> architecture relies on residual connections around every self-attention and feed-forward sub-layer, making them essential infrastructure for modern AI.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Consider a residual block with input $x$. The block computes:

$$y = F(x, \{W_i\}) + x$$

where $F$ represents the stacked nonlinear layers (convolutions, activations, <<link "Batch Normalization" "Batch Normalization">><</link>>). During backpropagation, if $\mathcal{L}$ is the loss, the gradient with respect to $x$ is:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial y} \cdot \frac{\partial y}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial y} \cdot \left(1 + \frac{\partial F}{\partial x}\right)$$

The crucial term is the ''1''. No matter what $\frac{\partial F}{\partial x}$ is — even if it is very small — the gradient is at least $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial y}$. The identity path acts as a ''gradient highway''.

For a full network with $L$ residual blocks, the output of layer $l$ is:

$$x_L = x_l + \sum_{i=l}^{L-1} F(x_i, W_i)$$

Taking the gradient:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_l} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_L} \cdot \left(1 + \frac{\partial}{\partial x_l} \sum_{i=l}^{L-1} F(x_i, W_i)\right)$$

This shows that the gradient at any layer $l$ always includes the term $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_L}$ — the gradient at the final layer — directly, without any multiplicative decay. The summation term provides additional gradient paths through the learned functions, but even if those paths vanish, the direct path survives.

When dimensions of $x$ and $F(x)$ differ (e.g., when changing the number of channels), a linear projection $W_s$ is used:

$$y = F(x) + W_s x$$

This ''projection shortcut'' adds learnable parameters but preserves the gradient highway property.

---

!! Residual Connections in Practice

''Pre-activation vs. post-activation:'' The original ResNet placed batch normalisation and ReLU //after// the addition: $y = \text{ReLU}(\text{BN}(F(x) + x))$. He et al. (2016) showed that placing BN and ReLU //before// the weight layers — the ''pre-activation'' design — improves gradient flow because the skip connection is a pure identity mapping with nothing obstructing it. Pre-activation is now the default in most implementations.

''In Transformers:'' Every sub-layer in a Transformer block uses a residual connection: $\text{output} = \text{LayerNorm}(x + \text{SubLayer}(x))$. Without these skip connections, Transformers would be unable to train at the depths routinely used (12–96 layers). The entire success of models like GPT and BERT depends on this mechanism.

''Scaling the residual:'' Some architectures scale the residual branch by a small constant $\alpha$ at initialisation: $y = x + \alpha \cdot F(x)$, with $\alpha \ll 1$. This technique (used in architectures like NFNets and deep ViTs) ensures early training stability by making the network behave approximately like an identity function initially, then gradually learning corrections.

---

!! When Residual Connections Break Down

''Dimension mismatches without proper projection:'' Residual connections require $F(x)$ and $x$ to have identical dimensions for the addition $F(x) + x$ to work. When a layer changes spatial resolution or channel count, a projection shortcut ($1 \times 1$ convolution or linear layer) must bridge the gap. If this projection is missing or poorly designed, the skip connection breaks and gradient flow degrades. Forgetting to handle dimension changes is a common source of bugs when building custom architectures.

''Excessive depth without bottleneck design:'' While residual connections enable very deep networks in principle, naively stacking hundreds of full-width layers creates enormous parameter counts and memory usage. The ''bottleneck'' design — a $1 \times 1$ convolution to reduce dimensions, a $3 \times 3$ convolution, then a $1 \times 1$ to expand back — is necessary to make extreme depth practical. Without bottlenecks, a 152-layer network would be computationally prohibitive.

''Feature reuse stagnation:'' In very deep residual networks, the skip connections can become //too dominant//, causing the learned functions $F(x)$ to contribute negligibly. The network effectively ignores many of its layers, collapsing to a much shallower effective depth. DenseNet addressed this by concatenating rather than adding skip connections, and stochastic depth training randomly drops entire residual blocks during training to force all layers to be useful.

!! Technical Details

''Original paper:'' He, Zhang, Ren, Sun (2015) "Deep Residual Learning for Image Recognition" (CVPR 2016, best paper). Demonstrated 152-layer networks on ImageNet, winning the ILSVRC 2015 classification, detection, and localisation challenges. Error rate dropped from 6.7% (GoogLeNet, 22 layers) to 3.57%.

''Identity mapping paper:'' He et al. (2016) "Identity Mappings in Deep Residual Networks" (ECCV 2016) showed that pre-activation (BN → ReLU → Conv) produces cleaner gradient paths and better results than post-activation, especially beyond 100 layers.

''Gradient flow analysis:'' For $L$ residual blocks, the gradient at layer $l$ is $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_l} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_L}(1 + \text{cross terms})$. Veit et al. (2016) showed ResNets behave like ensembles of many shallow networks of varying depths — deleting individual blocks has minimal impact.

''Variants:'' ResNeXt uses grouped convolutions within residual blocks. Wide ResNets increase channel width rather than depth. DenseNet concatenates all previous features. Highway Networks (Srivastava et al., 2015) preceded ResNets with gated shortcuts: $y = T(x) \cdot F(x) + (1 - T(x)) \cdot x$, where $T$ is a learned gating function, but the ungated identity shortcut proved simpler and more effective.

''Numerical note:'' With standard initialisation (He init), $\|F(x)\| \ll \|x\|$ at the start of training, so $y \approx x$ and the network begins as an approximate identity. Learning rate warmup and <<link "Batch Normalization" "Batch Normalization">><</link>> help maintain this property during the critical early phase.

''Connection to ODEs:'' In the continuous limit, the residual update $x_{l+1} = x_l + F(x_l)$ becomes an Euler step of the ODE $\frac{dx}{dt} = F(x, t)$. This connection led to Neural ODEs (Chen et al., 2018), which replace discrete layers with continuous dynamics.
