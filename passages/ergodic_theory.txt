! Ergodic Theory

<img src="assets/ergodic_theory.svg" alt="Diagram showing time average equals space average for an ergodic system" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

If you stir a cup of coffee once and sample it, you might get mostly cream. Stir it long enough and any sample you take will taste the same as any other — the liquid has explored every region of the cup uniformly. ''Ergodic theory'' asks: when does the time average of a dynamical system equal the space average? The answer determines whether MCMC sampling works, whether gradient descent over minibatches represents the full dataset, and whether training on sequences of data from one trajectory is statistically equivalent to sampling independently.

!! The Core Insight

The central result is ''Birkhoff's ergodic theorem'': for an ergodic measure-preserving system, the time average of any integrable function equals its space average, almost surely:

$$\frac{1}{T}\int_0^T f(T^t x)\, dt \;\xrightarrow{T\to\infty}\; \int f\, d\mu$$

The key condition is //ergodicity//: the system cannot be decomposed into two invariant subsets of positive measure. In plain terms, a single long trajectory eventually explores the entire state space, so what you see over time faithfully represents what exists across space. This is why running a Markov chain long enough gives you valid samples from the target distribution — not because each step is independent, but because the trajectory's time averages converge to the distribution's true expectations.

!! Ergodic Systems and Mixing

Ergodicity sits at the bottom of a hierarchy of increasingly strong properties:

''Ergodic → Mixing → K-system → Bernoulli''

- //Ergodic//: time averages equal space averages. The weakest useful property.
- //Mixing//: correlations between events decay over time — knowing where the system is now tells you less and less about where it will be far in the future. Formally: $\mu(A \cap T^{-n}B) \to \mu(A)\mu(B)$.
- //K-system// (Kolmogorov): positive entropy; the system generates genuine randomness at a fixed rate.
- //Bernoulli//: isomorphic to an independent coin-flip process — the strongest possible randomness property.

Most "nice" dynamical systems and well-behaved Markov chains are at least ergodic, and many are mixing. Canonical examples from mathematics include the ''baker's map'' (fold and stretch the unit square) and Arnold's ''cat map'' (a linear toral automorphism). Both are Bernoulli systems. These examples clarify the intuition: folding and stretching mixes regions of phase space the way a good shuffling of cards mixes the deck.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

A ''measure-preserving transformation'' $T$ on a probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mu)$ satisfies:

$$\mu(T^{-1}A) = \mu(A) \quad \text{for all measurable } A$$

The measure doesn't change as the system evolves — $\mu$ is a stationary distribution for $T$.

''Ergodicity'' is equivalent to: every $T$-invariant measurable set has measure 0 or 1. There are no "trapping regions" of intermediate measure.

Two convergence theorems formalize the core insight:

- ''von Neumann's mean ergodic theorem'' (1932): the time averages converge in $L^2$ norm.
- ''Birkhoff's pointwise ergodic theorem'' (1931): convergence holds //almost surely// (for $\mu$-almost every starting point $x$).

$$\frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1} f(T^k x) \;\xrightarrow{a.s.}\; \int f\, d\mu \quad \text{as } N\to\infty$$

The ''mixing condition'' is:

$$\mu(A \cap T^{-n}B) \to \mu(A)\mu(B) \quad \text{as } n\to\infty$$

This says that in the long run, the events $A$ and $T^{-n}B$ become independent — the system forgets its past. The spectral signature of mixing is that the eigenvalue 1 of the Koopman operator is //simple// (multiplicity one); all other eigenvalues have modulus strictly less than 1.

!! Ergodic Theory in ML

Ergodicity is a silent assumption underlying several core ML methods:

''MCMC correctness'': A Metropolis-Hastings or Gibbs sampler is correct because the constructed Markov chain is ergodic with the target distribution $\pi$ as its stationary measure. Birkhoff's theorem guarantees that time averages — empirical estimates of expectations — converge to true expectations under $\pi$. Without ergodicity, the chain might explore only part of the space.

''Minibatch SGD'': Using a random minibatch as an unbiased estimator of the full-dataset gradient implicitly invokes ergodicity. If data are drawn i.i.d., or if the shuffled data sequence is ergodic, then gradient estimates averaged over steps converge to the true gradient.

''Reinforcement learning'': An agent learning from a single long trajectory (online RL) relies on the MDP being ergodic — that the trajectory covers the state-action space like many independent samples. If the MDP has absorbing states or disconnected regions, this fails. The ''mixing time'' of the Markov chain underlying the MDP determines how quickly empirical statistics from the trajectory become reliable.

''Time-series learning'': Training a model on one long sequence (e.g., a language model on a corpus treated as a single token stream) is statistically valid only if the underlying process is ergodic. Ergodicity justifies treating temporal samples as representative of the distribution.

!! When Ergodic Theory Breaks Down

''(1) Non-ergodic systems.'' If the phase space has multiple attractors — different modes of a distribution — a trajectory started in one basin never visits the other. MCMC gets stuck: time averages give biased estimates of the full distribution, because the chain samples only from the local basin. This is the mode-collapse problem given a rigorous name. Multimodal posteriors in Bayesian inference, and energy landscapes in protein folding, are practical instances. The fix requires either tempering (simulated annealing, parallel tempering) or an ergodicity-restoring proposal kernel.

''(2) Slow mixing.'' The system is technically ergodic — given infinite time, every region is visited — but the mixing time is exponentially long. In practice, the chain never mixes within a feasible run. High-dimensional distributions with large energy barriers (glassy systems, multimodal posteriors with thin necks) suffer from this. The spectral gap of the chain's transition operator governs mixing time: $t_{\text{mix}} = O(1/\text{gap})$. A tiny spectral gap means astronomically slow mixing.

''(3) Non-stationary environments.'' Ergodic theory assumes a //stationary// invariant measure. In RL with non-stationary rewards, or in ML with distribution shift, the system's statistics change over time. There is no fixed $\mu$ for Birkhoff's theorem to converge to. Time averages don't represent any meaningful spatial average, because the "space" itself is moving. Continual learning and online adaptation methods must grapple with this fundamental violation.

!! Technical Details

''Historical roots'': Ludwig Boltzmann (1871) introduced the ergodic hypothesis in statistical mechanics to justify replacing time averages with phase-space averages in thermodynamics. The rigorous mathematical treatment came from Birkhoff (1931) and von Neumann (1932).

''Ergodic decomposition theorem'': Every measure-preserving system decomposes uniquely into ergodic components. Non-ergodic systems are mixtures of ergodic ones — each trajectory stays within one ergodic component.

''Entropy'': The Kolmogorov-Sinai (metric) entropy of an ergodic process equals the Shannon entropy rate. For Bernoulli systems, this equals $-\sum p_i \log p_i$.

''Mixing time bounds'': For a Markov chain with transition matrix $P$ and stationary distribution $\pi$, the mixing time to $\varepsilon$-total variation is bounded by:

$$t_{\text{mix}}(\varepsilon) \leq \frac{\log(1/(\varepsilon \pi_{\min}))}{\text{gap}(P)}$$

where $\text{gap}(P) = 1 - \lambda_2$ is the spectral gap and $\pi_{\min} = \min_x \pi(x)$.

''Connections to Kolmogorov complexity'': Ergodic sequences have maximal complexity relative to their entropy; they cannot be compressed beyond their entropy rate.
