! Graph Isomorphism Network
<img src="assets/graph_isomorphism_network.svg" style="width:100%;max-width:800px;display:block;margin:0.5em auto 1em;">

Imagine you have two social networks printed on separate sheets of paper. You want to know if they are actually the same network drawn differently — same people, same friendships, just rearranged on the page. The ''Weisfeiler-Lehman (WL) test'' is a classical algorithm that iteratively recolors each person based on their friends' colors, checking whether the two networks can be distinguished. A ''Graph Isomorphism Network (GIN)'' is a neural network that provably matches this test's discriminative power — it is the most expressive possible message-passing <<link "Graph Neural Networks" "Graph Neural Networks">><</link>>, able to distinguish any pair of graphs that the WL test can distinguish.

!! The Core Insight

Introduced by Xu, Hu, Leskovec, and Jegelka (ICLR 2019), GIN starts from a theoretical question: ''how powerful are graph neural networks?'' The answer depends on what ''aggregation function'' you use when a node collects information from its neighbors. Most GNNs use MEAN or MAX pooling — but these lose information. MEAN conflates a node with two identical neighbors and a node with ten identical neighbors. MAX discards everything except the extreme value. Only ''SUM'' preserves the full ''multiset'' of neighbor features. GIN pairs SUM aggregation with a multilayer perceptron (MLP) to create an update rule that is ''injective'' — different inputs always produce different outputs. This <<link "Injectivity" "Injectivity">><</link>> is exactly what gives the <<link "WL Test" "WL Test">><</link>> its power, and GIN is the neural network equivalent. Connections: <<link "Message Passing Neural Networks" "Message Passing Neural Networks">><</link>>, <<link "Graph Convolutional Networks" "Graph Convolutional Networks">><</link>>.

!! Why Injectivity Is Everything

Consider three nodes with the following neighbor multisets: node A has neighbors $\{1, 1, 2\}$, node B has neighbors $\{1, 2\}$, and node C has neighbors $\{1, 1, 1\}$. A ''SUM'' aggregator produces 4, 3, and 3 — it distinguishes A from B and C but conflates B and C only if their self-features differ. A ''MEAN'' aggregator produces $4/3$, $3/2$, and $1$ — different answers, but consider $\{1, 1\}$ vs $\{1\}$: MEAN gives $1$ for both. A ''MAX'' aggregator produces $2$, $2$, $1$ — it conflates A and B entirely. The critical insight is that SUM over a multiset, combined with a sufficiently expressive function (the MLP), forms an injective mapping over multisets of bounded size. No simpler aggregation can achieve this. This is not a heuristic claim — it is a theorem proved using results on the representational power of functions over multisets, connecting to the universal approximation theorem.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

At each layer $k$, GIN updates node $v$'s representation:

$$h_v^{(k)} = \text{MLP}^{(k)}\!\left(\left(1 + \epsilon^{(k)}\right) \cdot h_v^{(k-1)} + \sum_{u \in \mathcal{N}(v)} h_u^{(k-1)}\right)$$

where $\epsilon^{(k)}$ is a learnable scalar (or fixed at 0) that controls how much weight the node places on its own features versus its neighbors'. The MLP must have at least two layers with a nonlinearity to achieve universal approximation over countable multisets.

For graph-level tasks, GIN concatenates SUM-pooled node features from ''every'' layer, not just the final one:

$$h_G = \text{CONCAT}\!\left(\text{READOUT}^{(k)}\!\left(\{h_v^{(k)} \mid v \in G\}\right) \;\middle|\; k = 0, 1, \ldots, K\right)$$

where $\text{READOUT}^{(k)}$ is a SUM over all nodes. Using all layers is critical because different WL iterations capture structural information at different scales — hop-1 neighborhoods, hop-2 neighborhoods, and so on. Discarding intermediate layers would lose this multi-scale signal.

The theoretical backbone is a lemma showing that for any countable multiset $X$, there exists a function $f$ such that $h(X) = \phi\!\left(\sum_{x \in X} f(x)\right)$ is a unique mapping. The MLP provides $\phi \circ f$, and SUM provides the aggregation. Together they yield an injective function over multisets.

!! Practical Graph Classification

GIN was designed and benchmarked primarily for ''graph classification'' — predicting a label for an entire graph rather than individual nodes. This is central to molecular property prediction (is this molecule toxic?), protein function classification, and social network analysis. A typical GIN architecture uses 5 message-passing layers, each containing a 2-layer MLP with batch normalization, followed by SUM readout and a linear classifier. On standard benchmarks (MUTAG, PTC, PROTEINS, COLLAB, IMDB), GIN consistently matches or outperforms GCN and GraphSAGE, confirming the theoretical prediction that greater expressiveness translates to better empirical performance. Importantly, GIN's advantage is most pronounced on tasks that require distinguishing graphs with subtle structural differences — precisely the cases where MEAN and MAX aggregation lose information.

!! When Graph Isomorphism Networks Break Down

''Beyond WL expressiveness:'' GIN is provably bounded by the 1-WL test, which means it cannot distinguish certain non-isomorphic graphs — for instance, regular graphs where every node has the same degree and same local neighborhood structure. Graphs like the Decalin and Bicyclopentyl molecules are non-isomorphic but 1-WL equivalent; GIN will produce identical representations for both. Higher-order GNNs (k-WL) or subgraph-based methods are needed.

''Oversmoothing at depth:'' as with all message-passing architectures, stacking too many GIN layers causes node representations to converge toward a common vector. After 5–7 layers, node features become nearly indistinguishable — a problem since deeper layers correspond to larger receptive fields. Residual connections and normalization help but do not eliminate this fundamental limitation.

''Scalability on large graphs:'' SUM aggregation over very high-degree nodes (such as hub nodes in social networks or citation graphs with 10,000+ neighbors) can produce feature vectors with enormous magnitudes, leading to numerical instability and gradient issues. MEAN-based methods like GCN are more numerically stable in these settings, even though they sacrifice expressiveness.

!! Technical Details

''Key paper:'' Xu, Hu, Leskovec, Jegelka — "How Powerful are Graph Neural Networks?" (ICLR 2019). ''Aggregation:'' SUM (proven maximally expressive for multisets); alternatives MEAN and MAX are strictly less powerful. ''Update function:'' 2-layer MLP with batch normalization and ReLU. ''Epsilon:'' either learnable (GIN-$\epsilon$) or fixed at 0 (GIN-0); learnable $\epsilon$ provides marginal improvements. ''Readout:'' concatenation of SUM-pooled node features across all $K$ layers. ''Typical hyperparameters:'' $K = 5$ layers, hidden dimension 64, dropout 0.5, trained with Adam. ''Relation to WL:'' GIN with injective aggregation + injective readout = 1-WL test. Cannot distinguish $k$-WL equivalent graphs for $k \geq 2$. ''Graph-level benchmarks:'' MUTAG (89.4%), PTC (64.6%), PROTEINS (76.2%), COLLAB (80.2%), IMDB-BINARY (75.1%), IMDB-MULTI (52.3%). ''Complexity:'' $O(|E| \cdot d)$ per layer where $|E|$ is the edge count and $d$ is the feature dimension — same as GCN.
