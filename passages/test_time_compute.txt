! Test-Time Compute
<img src="assets/test_time_compute.svg" style="width:100%;max-width:800px;display:block;margin:0.5em auto 1em;" alt="Test-Time Compute diagram showing Best-of-N and sequential chain-of-thought scaling"/>

Like a student who double-checks work and tries multiple approaches before handing in an exam, ''test-time compute scaling'' lets an AI system spend more computation //at inference time// — after training — to produce better answers. The insight is simple: thinking longer, or trying more options and picking the best, improves quality without retraining.

!! The Core Insight

The classic scaling law concerns training compute: more parameters and more data produce better models. ''Test-time compute scaling'' (Snell et al. 2024, Brown et al. 2024) reveals a parallel law: you can also trade //inference// compute for accuracy. Two main mechanisms exist:

# ''Best-of-N:'' generate N candidate solutions in parallel, score each with a verifier, return the highest-scoring.
# ''Sequential search / chain of thought:'' generate a long internal reasoning trace — "thinking tokens" — that searches the solution space before committing to an answer.

OpenAI's o1 (2024) demonstrated that a smaller model with large test-time compute budget can match or exceed a much larger model running standard greedy decoding. Links: [[Chain of Thought]], [[Process Reward Models]], [[Reward Modeling]], [[Large Language Models]], [[Monte Carlo Tree Search]].

!! Best-of-N and Verifiers

In best-of-N, the generator produces N independent candidate solutions. A ''verifier'' (outcome reward model or process reward model) scores each candidate; the highest-scoring answer is returned.

Performance scales roughly as $O(\log N)$ — each doubling of $N$ yields a fixed accuracy increment. At $N = 256$, a small model frequently matches the accuracy of a model ten times larger at $N = 1$.

''Bottleneck:'' the verifier must be more reliable than the generator. A miscalibrated reward model confidently selects wrong answers. Best-of-N is embarrassingly parallel: all candidates are independent, so distribution across hardware is trivial.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Let $p$ be the probability that a single generation is correct. The probability that at least one of $N$ samples is correct is:

$$P(\text{at least one correct}) = 1 - (1-p)^N$$

This rises rapidly from $p$ toward 1 as $N$ grows, even for small $p$.

For sequential compute, a reasoning model generates $T$ thinking tokens before producing its answer. Empirically, accuracy scales as $O(T^\alpha)$ for some exponent $\alpha > 0$.

''MCTS over reasoning steps:'' treat each partial reasoning chain as a node, branch into multiple continuations, evaluate branches with a process reward model, and prune unpromising paths — analogous to AlphaZero search over game states.

''Dynamic budget allocation:'' route easy questions to small compute budgets and hard questions to large ones, maximising efficiency across a distribution of tasks.

!! The o1 / o3 Paradigm

OpenAI's o1 (September 2024) was trained with reinforcement learning to produce extended chain-of-thought "thinking" before every answer. The thinking trace is hidden from users but consumes substantial inference compute. On mathematical olympiad problems and coding benchmarks, o1 outperformed much larger models running standard decoding.

o3 (late 2024) extended this further. The ARC-AGI benchmark — designed to require general fluid intelligence and long thought to be unsolvable by LLMs — was largely solved by scaling inference compute with o3. The key reframing: ''reasoning is a form of search, and search scales with compute.''

!! When Test-Time Compute Breaks Down

''Verifier reliability:'' if the reward model is miscalibrated or reward-hacked, best-of-N selection amplifies errors rather than correcting them. A wrong verifier chosen with high confidence is worse than random selection.

''Compute cost:'' running 256 forward passes at inference is expensive. Test-time compute scaling is only economically justified for high-value tasks where quality outweighs cost — batch processing or low-latency applications may not tolerate it.

''Overthinking and error compounding:'' very long chains of thought can introduce compounding reasoning errors. Each step has a small failure probability; a 100-step chain compounds those failures. The model may also "talk itself out of" a correct early answer through excessive deliberation.

!! Technical Details

Key papers: Snell et al. 2024 ("Scaling LLM Test-Time Compute Optimally"), Brown et al. 2024 ("Large Language Monkeys: Scaling Inference Compute with Repeated Sampling"), OpenAI o1 System Card 2024, ARC-AGI evaluation of o3 (Chollet 2024).

MCTS for reasoning: Zhang et al. 2024 apply AlphaZero-style tree search over reasoning steps, using a PRM as the value function.

''Beam search'' over reasoning chains maintains a beam of partial chains, expanding and pruning at each step.

''Budget forcing:'' train the model with a special token signalling the thinking budget; the model learns to pace its reasoning to fill exactly $T$ tokens before answering.

''Process reward models (PRMs)'' provide step-level correctness signals, enabling pruning mid-chain rather than only at the final answer — critical for effective MCTS.
