! Online Learning

<img src="assets/online_learning.svg" alt="Diagram illustrating online learning with sequential prediction, regret curves, and the three main algorithm families" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are betting on horse races, one race at a time, with no chance to study the full season's results before placing your next wager. You pick a horse, the race runs, you see how you did, and you adjust your strategy for the next race. ''Online learning'' is the mathematical framework for exactly this situation: a learner makes predictions sequentially, suffers a loss after each prediction, and updates its strategy — all without ever seeing the full dataset at once. The fundamental question is not whether the learner makes mistakes, but whether its cumulative mistakes grow much slower than the number of rounds played. If so, the learner achieves ''no regret'': its average performance converges to that of the best fixed strategy in hindsight.

---

!! The Core Insight

Batch machine learning assumes you have all the data up front: you optimize a loss function over the entire training set, then deploy the model. Online learning abandons this assumption entirely. Data arrives one example at a time, possibly chosen adversarially, and the learner must commit to a prediction //before// seeing the outcome. The natural performance measure is ''regret'' — the gap between the learner's cumulative loss and the cumulative loss of the single best fixed action in hindsight:

$$R_T = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \ell_t(w_t) - \min_{w^*} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \ell_t(w^*)$$

A good online algorithm ensures $R_T$ grows sublinearly in $T$, typically as $O(\sqrt{T})$. This means the average regret $R_T / T \to 0$: the learner's per-round performance converges to that of the best expert, even though it never knew which expert was best. Crucially, this guarantee holds against //adversarial// data sequences — no probabilistic assumptions are needed. This adversarial robustness is what separates online learning from classical statistical learning, where data is assumed to be drawn i.i.d. from some distribution.

The framework connects directly to game theory: online learning can be viewed as a repeated game between the learner and an adversary, and no-regret algorithms provide approximate equilibrium strategies. This connection is why online learning has become central to [[Reinforcement Learning]], mechanism design, and algorithm design far beyond traditional prediction tasks.

---

!! Algorithms: From Experts to Bandits

The simplest online learning setting is the ''experts problem''. You have $N$ experts who each give advice. Each round, you choose a distribution over experts, the adversary reveals losses for all experts, and you suffer the expected loss under your distribution. The ''Multiplicative Weights Update'' (MWU) algorithm maintains a weight for each expert, multiplicatively downweighting experts that perform poorly:

$$w_{i,t+1} = w_{i,t} \cdot (1 - \eta \cdot \ell_{i,t})$$

where $\eta$ is the learning rate. After normalization, this yields a distribution that shifts probability mass toward historically better experts. MWU achieves regret $O(\sqrt{T \ln N})$, which is optimal for the experts setting. This algorithm is remarkably general — it appears in boosting (AdaBoost is MWU in disguise), zero-sum game solvers, and online portfolio selection.

''Follow the Regularized Leader'' (FTRL) generalizes this to continuous decision spaces. Instead of maintaining weights, the learner picks the action that minimizes cumulative past loss plus a regularization term:

$$w_{t+1} = \arg\min_{w \in \mathcal{W}} \left( \sum_{s=1}^{t} \ell_s(w) + R(w) \right)$$

The regularizer $R(w)$ prevents wild oscillations between rounds. When $R$ is the negative entropy, FTRL recovers MWU. When $R$ is the squared $\ell_2$ norm, FTRL recovers ''Online Gradient Descent'' (OGD), which connects directly to [[Gradient Descent]] in the online setting. The choice of regularizer determines the geometry of the updates and the resulting regret bound.

The ''bandit'' setting is harder: after each round, the learner observes only the loss of its //chosen// action, not the losses of alternatives. This is the [[Explore-Exploit Tradeoff]] in its purest form. The EXP3 algorithm solves the adversarial [[Multi-Armed Bandit]] problem with regret $O(\sqrt{TN \ln N})$ — worse than the full-information case by a factor of $\sqrt{N}$, which is the information-theoretic price of partial feedback.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The regret analysis of MWU reveals a clean mathematical structure. Define the ''potential function'' $\Phi_t = \ln\left(\sum_i w_{i,t}\right)$. Each round, the potential drops by approximately $\eta \cdot \hat{\ell}_t$ (the learner's loss) but increases by at most $\eta^2$ due to the multiplicative update. Telescoping over $T$ rounds:

$$\sum_{t=1}^T \hat{\ell}_t - \min_i \sum_{t=1}^T \ell_{i,t} \leq \frac{\ln N}{\eta} + \eta T$$

Setting $\eta = \sqrt{\ln N / T}$ balances the two terms, giving the optimal regret $O(\sqrt{T \ln N})$.

For FTRL with a general convex regularizer $R$, the regret bound involves the ''Bregman divergence'' $D_R$ associated with $R$:

$$R_T \leq D_R(w^*, w_1) + \sum_{t=1}^{T} D_R(w_t, w_{t+1})$$

The first term measures how far the best action is from the initial point; the sum of divergences measures the ''stability'' of the algorithm's iterates. A good regularizer keeps iterates stable (small step-to-step divergence) while covering the full action space (bounded initial divergence). This is why the regularizer must be ''strongly convex'' with respect to the norm used to measure gradient size — a requirement from [[Convex Optimization]] that directly controls the regret.

Online Gradient Descent uses the update $w_{t+1} = \Pi_{\mathcal{W}}(w_t - \eta \nabla \ell_t(w_t))$, projecting back onto the feasible set $\mathcal{W}$ after each gradient step. For convex losses, it achieves regret $O(\sqrt{T})$. For strongly convex losses, the regret improves to $O(\log T)$ — nearly as good as knowing the best action in advance.

In the bandit setting, the key trick is ''importance-weighted estimation''. Since the learner only observes one loss, it constructs an unbiased estimate of the full loss vector: $\hat{\ell}_{i,t} = \ell_{i,t} / p_{i,t}$ if action $i$ was chosen (with probability $p_{i,t}$), and 0 otherwise. This estimator is unbiased ($\mathbb{E}[\hat{\ell}_{i,t}] = \ell_{i,t}$) but has high variance — the variance scales as $1/p_{i,t}$, which is why exploration (keeping $p_{i,t}$ away from zero) is essential.

!! Applications in Practice

Online learning algorithms power many production systems. ''Online advertising'' is the canonical example: ad platforms must decide which ad to show each user, observe whether the user clicks, and update their strategy — a classic bandit problem at enormous scale. The [[Explore-Exploit Tradeoff]] is literal: showing a known-good ad exploits current knowledge, while trying a new ad explores for potentially better options.

''Online portfolio selection'' applies MWU-style algorithms to financial asset allocation. The Cover universal portfolio algorithm, which uses FTRL with log-barrier regularization, achieves wealth that grows at the same exponential rate as the best constant-rebalanced portfolio in hindsight. This is a direct application of no-regret learning to sequential investment.

''Online convex optimization'' (OCO) is the general framework that subsumes most online learning settings. At each round, the learner picks a point $w_t$ in a convex set, then a convex loss function $\ell_t$ is revealed. OCO with appropriate algorithms provides the foundation for streaming data analysis, adaptive control, and online versions of nearly every batch optimization algorithm used in machine learning. The connection to [[Gradient Descent]] is tight: stochastic gradient descent on a fixed dataset can be analyzed as an online learning algorithm where the data order is the adversary.

---

!! When Online Learning Breaks Down

''Non-stationary environments with fast drift:'' Standard regret compares against the single best //fixed// action in hindsight. If the best action changes frequently, the fixed-comparator regret bound becomes vacuous — even $O(\sqrt{T})$ regret may be terrible if the optimal action shifts every round. ''Adaptive regret'' and ''dynamic regret'' formulations address this by comparing against time-varying sequences, but the bounds degrade with the number of shifts, and the algorithms require tuning a drift rate that is rarely known in advance. When the environment is genuinely non-stationary, online learning's worst-case guarantees lose their bite.

''Delayed or batched feedback:'' Online learning theory assumes the learner observes the loss immediately after each prediction. In practice — ad click attribution, medical treatment outcomes, supply chain effects — feedback may arrive days or weeks late. Delayed feedback inflates regret by a factor proportional to the delay. If the delay exceeds the effective horizon, the learner cannot adapt quickly enough and degenerates to near-random guessing. Batched variants exist but sacrifice the fine-grained adaptivity that makes online learning attractive in the first place.

''Combinatorial or structured action spaces:'' When the action space is exponentially large (e.g., choosing a path in a network, a ranking of items, or a schedule), even constructing importance-weighted loss estimates becomes computationally intractable. The EXP3 regret bound of $O(\sqrt{TN \ln N})$ is meaningless when $N$ is exponential. Efficient algorithms exist for special structures (e.g., online shortest paths, online linear optimization), but for general combinatorial bandits, computation — not information — becomes the bottleneck, and the clean regret bounds of basic online learning no longer apply.

!! Technical Details

''Key papers:'' Littlestone and Warmuth (1994), //The Weighted Majority Algorithm//, introduced the multiplicative weights method and proved its $O(\sqrt{T \ln N})$ regret bound. Freund and Schapire (1997), //A Decision-Theoretic Generalization of On-Line Learning//, connected multiplicative weights to boosting and game-theoretic equilibria. Zinkevich (2003), //Online Convex Optimization and Generalized Infinitesimal Gradient Ascent//, established online gradient descent for online convex optimization with $O(\sqrt{T})$ regret. Auer et al. (2002), //The Nonstochastic Multiarmed Bandit Problem//, introduced the EXP3 algorithm for adversarial bandits. Shalev-Shwartz (2012), //Online Learning and Online Convex Optimization//, provides the standard survey unifying the field.

''Minimax optimality:'' The $O(\sqrt{T \ln N})$ regret for the experts setting and $O(\sqrt{T})$ for online convex optimization with bounded gradients are minimax optimal — no algorithm can achieve better worst-case regret. For bandits, the minimax rate is $\Theta(\sqrt{TN})$, and EXP3 nearly matches it (up to $\sqrt{\ln N}$). These lower bounds are proved via information-theoretic arguments and game-theoretic minimax duality.

''Connection to equilibria:'' If both players in a zero-sum game use no-regret algorithms, their average strategies converge to a Nash equilibrium. This is the basis for modern game solvers, including the Counterfactual Regret Minimization (CFR) algorithm used to solve poker. The rate of convergence to equilibrium is determined by the regret bound: $O(\sqrt{T \ln N})$ regret implies $O(1/\sqrt{T})$ convergence to the equilibrium value.

''FTRL vs. Online Mirror Descent (OMD):'' These are the two main algorithmic templates. FTRL selects $w_{t+1}$ by minimizing cumulative loss plus regularization from scratch each round. OMD takes a mirror descent step from the current iterate. For many settings they produce identical iterates, but they can differ when the feasible set has boundaries. The equivalence holds when the regularizer is a Legendre function and the loss is linear. Understanding both perspectives is necessary for advanced algorithm design in online [[Convex Optimization]].

''Bandit convex optimization:'' When the loss is convex but the learner only observes the scalar loss value (not the gradient), one-point gradient estimators using random perturbations achieve $O(T^{3/4})$ regret. Two-point estimators (requiring two function evaluations per round) recover $O(\sqrt{T})$. This setting connects online learning to zeroth-order optimization and derivative-free methods.
