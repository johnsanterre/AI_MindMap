! Topology

<img src="assets/topology.svg" alt="Diagram showing topological spaces, homeomorphisms, and persistent homology for data analysis" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

A topologist famously cannot tell a coffee cup from a donut: both have exactly one hole, and one can be continuously deformed into the other without tearing or gluing. Topology studies properties that survive continuous deformation — connectedness, holes, loops — rather than rigid geometric properties like distances or angles. In ML, the ''manifold hypothesis'' says high-dimensional data (images, text) lives on low-dimensional curved surfaces — manifolds — embedded in high-dimensional space. Topology gives us the tools to reason about these shapes, understand what neural networks learn about them, and detect structures that geometry alone misses.

!! The Core Insight

A ''topological space'' is a set equipped with a notion of "nearness" — defined via open sets — but //without// any notion of distance. Two spaces are ''homeomorphic'' if there exists a continuous bijection between them whose inverse is also continuous: they are "topologically equivalent" shapes that can be continuously deformed into each other without tearing or gluing.

''Topological invariants'' are properties preserved by homeomorphisms: the number of holes, connectedness, the fundamental group. These invariants are powerful precisely because they are //robust// — they survive distortions that would destroy geometric measurements like distances or curvature.

The ''manifold hypothesis'' is the central link to ML: real-world high-dimensional data (e.g., the space of all natural images) does not fill its ambient space uniformly. Instead, it concentrates near a low-dimensional curved surface — a manifold — of dimension far smaller than the ambient pixel count. Understanding that manifold's topology tells us something deep about the structure of the data.

!! Key Topological Concepts

''Connectedness'' asks the most basic question about a space: can you travel continuously between any two points? A space that falls into two or more disconnected pieces has $\beta_0 > 1$.

''Simple connectedness'' goes further: a space is simply connected if every loop can be continuously contracted to a point. A sphere is simply connected; a torus (donut) is not — a loop that threads through the hole cannot be shrunk away.

The ''Euler characteristic'' $\chi = V - E + F$ (vertices minus edges plus faces) is a topological invariant of polyhedral surfaces. For a sphere, $\chi = 2$; for a torus, $\chi = 0$. It generalises to higher dimensions.

''Betti numbers'' $\beta_k$ count $k$-dimensional holes:
$$\beta_0 = \text{number of connected components}$$
$$\beta_1 = \text{number of independent loops (1-dimensional holes)}$$
$$\beta_2 = \text{number of enclosed voids (2-dimensional holes)}$$

A circle has $\beta_0 = 1$, $\beta_1 = 1$. A disk has $\beta_0 = 1$, $\beta_1 = 0$. A torus has $\beta_0 = 1$, $\beta_1 = 2$, $\beta_2 = 1$.

The ''fundamental group'' $\pi_1(X)$ captures loop structure more finely: it is the group of equivalence classes of loops based at a point, where two loops are equivalent if one can be continuously deformed into the other. For a circle, $\pi_1(S^1) = \mathbb{Z}$ — you can wind around any integer number of times.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''Persistent homology'' is the core tool of topological data analysis. Given a finite point cloud (e.g., a dataset), we cannot directly compute its topology — but we can build a sequence of simplicial complexes that approximate it at different scales.

For a scale parameter $\varepsilon$, the ''Vietoris-Rips complex'' $\mathcal{R}_\varepsilon$ places an edge between any two points within distance $\varepsilon$, a triangle when three points are mutually within $\varepsilon$, and so on. As $\varepsilon$ grows from $0$ to $\infty$, we get a ''filtration'':
$$\mathcal{R}_0 \subseteq \mathcal{R}_{\varepsilon_1} \subseteq \mathcal{R}_{\varepsilon_2} \subseteq \cdots \subseteq \mathcal{R}_\infty$$

Topological features (components, loops, voids) are ''born'' at some scale $b$ and ''die'' at some scale $d > b$. The ''persistence diagram'' plots each feature as a point $(b, d)$ in the plane:

$$\text{persistence} = d - b$$

Features far from the diagonal (high persistence) correspond to //genuine// topological structure in the data. Features near the diagonal (low persistence) are likely noise.

Two persistence diagrams can be compared using the ''bottleneck distance'':
$$d_B(D_1, D_2) = \inf_{\gamma} \sup_{x \in D_1} \|x - \gamma(x)\|_\infty$$
where the infimum is over all matchings $\gamma$ between the diagrams (with unmatched points mapped to the diagonal).

The ''stability theorem'' guarantees that small perturbations in the data produce small changes in the persistence diagram — a crucial property for ML applications where data is noisy.

!! Topology in ML

''The manifold hypothesis in practice:'' natural images are $(256)^3$-dimensional vectors (for RGB) but the "true" degrees of freedom — object pose, lighting, identity — number perhaps in the hundreds. Dimensionality reduction methods like UMAP and t-SNE attempt to find low-dimensional coordinates that preserve topological structure (local neighbourhood relations) better than purely geometric methods like PCA.

''UMAP'' (Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection) formalises this using ''fuzzy simplicial sets'' — a topological framework that models the data manifold as a weighted simplicial complex and optimises a low-dimensional representation to match it. This is a rare case of serious topology entering a mainstream ML algorithm.

''Topological data analysis (TDA)'' applies persistent homology to real scientific problems: detecting loops in gene expression data, characterising brain connectivity from fMRI, classifying molecular shapes in drug discovery, and identifying phase transitions in materials science.

''Neural network loss landscapes:'' topological analysis of the sublevel sets of the loss function reveals how many connected components of low-loss regions exist, how they are connected, and whether the landscape is "benign" for gradient-based optimisation. Mode connectivity — the observation that two trained minima can often be connected by a low-loss path — is a topological statement.

''Representation learning:'' a well-trained encoder should map data to a latent space whose topology matches the data manifold. If the data has loops (e.g., cyclic time-series), a good latent space should too. Topological regularisation methods penalise mismatch between the topology of the data and the topology of learned representations.

''Shape analysis:'' in 3D computer vision, persistent homology provides shape descriptors invariant to rigid motions, useful for object recognition and medical image analysis.

!! When Topology Breaks Down

''(1) Computational intractability:'' computing persistent homology via boundary matrix reduction scales roughly as $O(n^3)$ for $n$ points, and storing the full simplicial complex requires $O(n^k)$ space for $k$-dimensional features. For the massive point clouds typical of ML (millions of high-dimensional data points), exact TDA is completely infeasible. Approximations — witness complexes, random subsampling, landmark-based methods — reduce cost but introduce their own errors and can miss genuine topological features.

''(2) Choice of filtration:'' persistent homology depends on the filtration used to build the simplicial complexes. The Vietoris-Rips complex, the Cech complex, and the Alpha complex all give different persistence diagrams for the same point cloud, and there is no canonical "correct" choice for ML data. The Vietoris-Rips complex is easy to compute but overestimates topology in high dimensions; the Cech complex is theoretically cleaner but computationally harder. This ambiguity limits the interpretability of TDA results and makes comparisons across studies difficult.

''(3) Topology vs geometry:'' topology captures only coarse qualitative structure — holes, connectivity — and is deliberately blind to distances, curvature, and other geometric details. Two manifolds can be topologically identical (homeomorphic) yet geometrically very different: a flat torus and a doughnut-shaped torus are homeomorphic but have very different curvatures. For many ML tasks, the geometric structure (how distances are organised, how the manifold curves) matters far more than the topology, and TDA tools provide no purchase on these questions.

!! Technical Details

''Historical roots:'' Leonhard Euler's solution to the Königsberg bridge problem (1736) is often called the first topological result. Henri Poincaré founded algebraic topology (1895), introducing the fundamental group and homology. Enrico Betti introduced Betti numbers (1871). The modern computational framework is due to Edelsbrunner & Harer, //Computational Topology// (2010).

''Key algorithms:'' the standard persistence algorithm reduces the boundary matrix $\partial$ of the filtration to a canonical form in $O(n^3)$ time. Ripser (Bauer 2021) is the fastest practical implementation for Vietoris-Rips persistence. The ''Mapper algorithm'' (Singh, Mémoli, Carlsson 2007) provides a topological lens on high-dimensional data using partial clustering and nerve complexes, producing network-like summaries widely used in single-cell genomics.

''UMAP's topology:'' McInnes et al. (2018) construct a weighted fuzzy simplicial set from the $k$-nearest-neighbour graph and find a low-dimensional representation minimising the cross-entropy between fuzzy set representations. The theoretical foundation is Spivak's work on fuzzy simplicial sets and the nerve theorem.

''Universal approximation and manifolds:'' the universal approximation theorem guarantees that neural networks can approximate any continuous function on compact subsets of $\mathbb{R}^n$. When data lies on a manifold, this effectively reduces the problem to approximating functions on the manifold — and the manifold's topology constrains what functions are globally definable on it.

''Topological regularisation:'' methods like TopoReg (Hofer et al. 2019) add a loss term that penalises the mismatch between the persistence diagrams of the data and the latent representation, encouraging the encoder to preserve topological structure.
