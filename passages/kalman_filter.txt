! Kalman Filter

<img src="assets/kalman_filter.svg" alt="Diagram of the Kalman filter predict-update cycle showing prior estimate, prediction, Kalman gain, and posterior update" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are navigating a ship through dense fog. You know roughly where you started and how fast you are moving, so you can //predict// where you should be now — but that estimate drifts with every wave and gust of wind. Occasionally, a lighthouse beacon cuts through the fog and gives you a noisy position fix. The ''Kalman filter'' is the mathematically optimal way to blend those two sources of information: your physics-based prediction and your imperfect sensor reading. It weights each source by how much you trust it, producing a fused estimate that is always better than either alone.

!! The Core Insight

The Kalman filter is an ''optimal recursive estimator'' for systems that can be described by linear equations with Gaussian (bell-curve) noise. "Optimal" means no other linear estimator can do better in the mean-squared-error sense. "Recursive" means it updates its estimate one step at a time, discarding raw measurements once they have been absorbed — making it extremely efficient for real-time systems.

The filter operates in a continuous ''predict-update cycle''. During the predict step, the filter rolls its current best estimate forward in time using a model of the system's dynamics, and it tracks how uncertain that estimate is. During the update step, a new measurement arrives and is fused with the prediction. The fusion is weighted by the ''Kalman gain'': a quantity that automatically shifts trust toward whichever source — model or sensor — is currently more reliable.

!! The Predict-Update Cycle

''Predict step (time update):'' Starting from the current ''posterior estimate'' $\hat{x}_{t|t}$ and its covariance $P_{t|t}$, the filter projects forward:

$$\hat{x}_{t+1|t} = F\hat{x}_{t|t} + Bu_t$$
$$P_{t+1|t} = FP_{t|t}F^T + Q$$

Here $F$ is the ''state transition matrix'' (the physics model), $B$ maps any known control input $u_t$, and $Q$ is the ''process noise covariance'' representing how much the model is trusted to capture reality. Uncertainty always grows during prediction.

''Update step (measurement update):'' When a measurement $z_{t+1}$ arrives, the filter computes the ''Kalman gain'' $K$, which determines how much to trust the new observation relative to the prior prediction. The posterior is then a weighted blend:

$$\hat{x}_{t+1|t+1} = \hat{x}_{t+1|t} + K(z_{t+1} - H\hat{x}_{t+1|t})$$

The term $(z_{t+1} - H\hat{x}_{t+1|t})$ is called the ''innovation'' or residual — how surprised the filter is by the measurement. After the update, uncertainty shrinks.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The system is described by two linear equations. The ''state equation'' models how the hidden state $x_t$ evolves:

$$x_{t+1} = Fx_t + Bu_t + w_t, \quad w_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, Q)$$

The ''measurement equation'' relates the hidden state to what the sensor actually reports:

$$z_t = Hx_t + v_t, \quad v_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, R)$$

Here $H$ is the ''observation matrix'' (which aspects of the state are measured), $Q$ is process noise, and $R$ is ''measurement noise covariance''. Both noise sources are assumed Gaussian and independent.

The ''Kalman gain'' is the heart of the filter:

$$K = PH^T(HPH^T + R)^{-1}$$

Intuitively: $PH^T$ measures how much the state uncertainty maps onto measurement space; $(HPH^T + R)$ is the total variance in measurement space (model uncertainty plus sensor noise). The gain $K$ is the fraction of the innovation to incorporate. When $R \to 0$ (perfect sensor), $K \to H^{-1}$ and the filter trusts the measurement completely. When $Q \to 0$ (perfect model), $K \to 0$ and the filter ignores measurements entirely.

The ''covariance update'' after the measurement step:

$$P_{t+1|t+1} = (I - KH)P_{t+1|t}$$

This contraction of uncertainty is what makes the Kalman filter so satisfying: every good measurement makes the estimate more precise.

!! Extensions and Variants

The standard Kalman filter assumes linearity and Gaussian noise — constraints that break down constantly in the real world. Three major extensions address this:

The ''Extended Kalman Filter (EKF)'' handles nonlinear systems by linearizing around the current estimate using first-order Taylor expansion (Jacobian matrices). It is widely used in robotics and aerospace but can diverge if the linearization is poor.

The ''Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF)'' is a more principled approach to nonlinearity. Rather than linearizing the equations, it propagates a carefully chosen set of ''sigma points'' through the true nonlinear function and reconstructs the mean and covariance from the results. It captures nonlinear effects to third order and is more robust than the EKF.

''Particle filters'' (Sequential Monte Carlo methods) abandon Gaussian assumptions entirely, representing the posterior distribution as a cloud of weighted random samples. They can handle arbitrary noise distributions and highly nonlinear dynamics at the cost of much greater computational expense.

Conceptually, the Kalman filter predict-update cycle has deep connections to sequence models in deep learning. The hidden state of an <<link "RNN" "Recurrent Neural Network">><<link>> and the cell/hidden state of an <<link "LSTM" "Long Short-Term Memory">><<link>> perform an analogous role: maintaining a compressed summary of past observations that is updated at each time step. The Kalman filter is the exact optimal solution when the world is linear-Gaussian; neural sequence models learn flexible approximations for more complex worlds.

!! When the Kalman Filter Breaks Down

''1. Non-Gaussian noise.'' The filter's optimality guarantees evaporate when noise is heavy-tailed, multimodal, or otherwise non-Gaussian. A single large outlier measurement (a sensor glitch, a GPS multipath reflection) can violently corrupt the estimate because the filter has no concept of "this reading is implausible." Robust variants use outlier-resistant update rules, but they sacrifice the clean optimality guarantees.

''2. Nonlinear dynamics breaking the linearity assumption.'' The standard filter assumes that both $F$ (state transition) and $H$ (observation) are linear. Real systems — a spacecraft maneuvering, a robot arm rotating — have nonlinear dynamics. The EKF's linearization can introduce bias and cause the covariance to become inconsistent (underestimating true uncertainty), eventually leading to ''filter divergence'' where the filter confidently reports wrong answers and ignores corrective measurements.

''3. Model mismatch when process or measurement matrices are wrong.'' The filter is only as good as the matrices $F$, $H$, $Q$, and $R$ used to define it. In practice these are often hand-tuned or estimated from data imperfectly. If $Q$ is set too small, the filter over-trusts its model and becomes sluggish to react to real changes. If $R$ is set too small, it over-trusts noisy sensors. Tuning these matrices — ''filter design'' — is as much art as science, and a mis-specified filter can be worse than a simpler heuristic.

!! Technical Details

The Kalman filter was published by Rudolf E. Kálmán in his landmark 1960 paper //A New Approach to Linear Filtering and Prediction Problems// in the ASME Journal of Basic Engineering. It was almost immediately adopted by NASA for the Apollo guidance computer — one of the most consequential applications of mathematical statistics in engineering history.

''SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping)'' in robotics is a canonical application: a robot must simultaneously estimate its own pose and build a map of its environment, both of which are uncertain. EKF-SLAM and graph-based SLAM variants underpin most practical mobile robotics systems. See <<link "SLAM" "SLAM">><</link>>.

The Kalman filter sits at the intersection of several mathematical fields:

- ''<<link "Bayesian" "Bayesian Inference">><</link>> inference:'' The predict step is a prior, the update step is Bayes' rule applied to a Gaussian likelihood. The Kalman filter //is// the Bayes filter for linear-Gaussian systems.
- ''<<link "Stochastic Processes" "Stochastic Processes">><</link>>:'' The state-space model is a linear dynamical system driven by Brownian-motion-like noise.
- ''<<link "Optimal Control" "Optimal Control">><</link>> and <<link "Control Theory" "Control Theory">><</link>>:'' The Linear-Quadratic-Gaussian (LQG) controller pairs a Kalman filter (estimator) with an LQR controller (policy), and the separation principle guarantees this combination is globally optimal.
- ''<<link "Hidden Markov Machine" "Hidden Markov Machine">><</link>>:'' The Kalman filter is a continuous-state analog of the HMM forward algorithm. Both maintain a belief state over a hidden Markov process.
- ''<<link "Reinforcement Learning" "Reinforcement Learning">><</link>>:'' State estimation via Kalman filtering is a prerequisite for many partially-observable RL problems (POMDPs).

The ''Rauch-Tung-Striebel (RTS) smoother'' is the offline companion: given a full sequence of measurements, it runs the Kalman filter forward then backward to produce optimal estimates at //every// time step, not just the current one.

Computational cost per step is $O(n^3)$ in state dimension $n$ due to the matrix inversion in the Kalman gain — a bottleneck for very high-dimensional state spaces, motivating ensemble Kalman filters and sparse approximations used in weather forecasting.
