! GPU Revolution

<img src="assets/gpu_revolution.svg" alt="Timeline of the GPU Revolution in deep learning" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you need to move a mountain of sand. You could hire one incredibly strong worker with a wheelbarrow — fast on each trip, but only one trip at a time. Or you could hire ten thousand workers, each carrying a small bucket. None of them is as strong as the wheelbarrow worker, but together they finish in a fraction of the time. The ''GPU revolution'' is the story of how a chip designed to carry millions of tiny pixel-buckets for video games turned out to be the perfect tool for carrying millions of tiny matrix-multiplication-buckets for <<link "Deep Learning" "Deep Learning">><</link>>. This accident — a graphics card repurposed for math — is arguably the single most important hardware event in modern AI history.

---

!! The Core Insight

''Training a neural network is almost entirely matrix multiplication'' — and matrix multiplication is //embarrassingly parallel//. A CPU executes a few operations very fast, one after another. A GPU executes thousands of simpler operations //simultaneously//. When <<link "AlexNet" "AlexNet">><</link>> trained on two NVIDIA GTX 580 GPUs in 2012 and crushed the <<link "ImageNet" "ImageNet">><</link>> competition by an unprecedented margin, it was not because convolutions were a new idea — <<link "CNN" "CNN">><</link>>s had existed since the 1980s. It was because GPU parallelism finally made deep convolutional networks //trainable in days instead of months//. The algorithm had been waiting for its hardware moment.

The deeper lesson: ''the history of AI breakthroughs is inseparable from the history of compute availability''. Ideas that require matrix operations at scale — <<link "Backpropagation and Chain Rule" "Backpropagation and Chain Rule">><</link>>, <<link "Transformers" "Transformers">><</link>>, <<link "Large Language Models" "Large Language Models">><</link>> — flourished once GPUs made those operations cheap. Ideas that require sparse, irregular, or symbolic computation still languish, not necessarily because they are worse, but because GPUs cannot accelerate them. This is the <<link "Hardware Lottery" "Hardware Lottery">><</link>>.

---

!! From CUDA to GPU Clusters

''Phase 1 — The Gaming GPU (pre-2006).'' NVIDIA built GPUs to shade pixels: each frame of a video game requires the same floating-point operation applied to millions of independent pixels. This is exactly the Single Instruction, Multiple Data (SIMD) pattern that also describes a matrix multiply: apply the same dot product to thousands of independent vector pairs.

''Phase 2 — CUDA (2006).'' NVIDIA released CUDA, a general-purpose programming model for GPUs. For the first time, researchers could write arbitrary parallel code — not just graphics shaders — and run it on thousands of GPU cores. Theano (2007) was among the first deep learning frameworks to target CUDA. Without CUDA, there would have been no software pipeline to connect neural network code to GPU hardware.

''Phase 3 — AlexNet and the Tipping Point (2012).'' Krizhevsky, Sutskever, and Hinton trained <<link "AlexNet" "AlexNet">><</link>> on two GTX 580 GPUs. The result — a 10.8 percentage-point improvement over the runner-up on ImageNet — shocked the computer vision community and triggered an avalanche of industry investment in GPU-accelerated deep learning.

''Phase 4 — Tensor Cores and Mixed Precision (2017).'' NVIDIA's Volta architecture introduced ''Tensor Cores'': specialised units that perform a $4 \times 4$ matrix multiply-accumulate in a single clock cycle using <<link "Mixed Precision Training" "Mixed Precision Training">><</link>> (FP16 inputs, FP32 accumulation). This multiplied effective throughput by roughly $8\times$ for matrix operations, directly enabling the training of <<link "Transformers" "Transformers">><</link>> at scale.

''Phase 5 — GPU Clusters and the LLM Era (2020–present).'' Training <<link "Large Language Models" "Large Language Models">><</link>> like GPT-3 required thousands of A100 GPUs connected via high-bandwidth NVLink and InfiniBand. <<link "Tensor Parallelism" "Tensor Parallelism">><</link>> splits individual matrix multiplications across GPUs; data parallelism replicates the model and splits batches. Modern training runs consume $10^{23}$–$10^{25}$ FLOPs and cost tens of millions of dollars in GPU time.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The GPU advantage reduces to one core operation: the ''General Matrix Multiply'' (GEMM). A single layer of a neural network computes:

$$Y = XW + b$$

where $X$ is a batch of inputs (shape $B \times d_{\text{in}}$), $W$ is the weight matrix ($d_{\text{in}} \times d_{\text{out}}$), and $b$ is a bias vector. This requires $B \times d_{\text{in}} \times d_{\text{out}}$ multiply-add operations. For a Transformer layer with $d = 4096$ and batch size $B = 2048$, a single linear projection involves:

$$\text{FLOPs} = 2 \times B \times d \times d = 2 \times 2048 \times 4096 \times 4096 \approx 6.9 \times 10^{10}$$

A GPU like the H100 delivers roughly $10^{15}$ FLOPs per second (1 PFLOPS at FP16). So this single operation takes about $69 \,\mu\text{s}$ — but on a CPU running at $10^{11}$ FLOPs/s, it would take $690 \,\text{ms}$, a $10{,}000\times$ slowdown.

The key metric is ''arithmetic intensity'':

$$I = \frac{\text{FLOPs}}{\text{Bytes accessed}}$$

Dense matrix multiplications have high arithmetic intensity ($I \gg 100$), meaning the GPU's compute cores stay busy rather than waiting on memory. Element-wise operations like activation functions have low intensity ($I \approx 1$) and are memory-bound. The GPU's architecture — thousands of cores with fast shared memory — is optimised for the high-$I$ operations that dominate neural network training.

For the full training run of a model with $N$ parameters on $D$ tokens, Kaplan et al. (2020) showed total compute scales as:

$$C \approx 6ND$$

A 70B-parameter model trained on $1.4 \times 10^{12}$ tokens requires $C \approx 5.9 \times 10^{23}$ FLOPs — months on thousands of GPUs.

!! The Ecosystem Lock-In

The GPU revolution was not just a hardware event — it created a self-reinforcing ecosystem. NVIDIA released CUDA; deep learning frameworks (Theano, TensorFlow, PyTorch) built on CUDA; researchers bought NVIDIA GPUs because the frameworks ran on them; NVIDIA invested profits into AI-specific hardware features (Tensor Cores, NVLink, HBM integration); this made NVIDIA GPUs even more attractive. The cycle repeated for nearly two decades.

This lock-in has consequences. ''Google's TPUs'' — custom ASICs designed specifically for matrix operations — offer competitive performance but lack the broad software ecosystem CUDA provides. ''AMD's ROCm'' platform has narrowed the gap but still trails in framework support and developer mindshare. ''Startups'' building photonic, neuromorphic, or analog compute chips face an almost impossible bootstrapping problem: without software, there are no users; without users, there is no software.

The result is a ''hardware monoculture'' that shapes what research gets done. Architectures that map well to dense GPU matrix multiplications — <<link "Transformers" "Transformers">><</link>>, large <<link "CNN" "CNN">><</link>>s, feed-forward networks — receive disproportionate attention. Architectures requiring sparse, conditional, or event-driven computation get starved of both hardware support and researcher time.

---

!! When the GPU Revolution Breaks Down

''1. Memory wall.'' GPU compute has grown faster than GPU memory bandwidth. Modern models increasingly hit the //memory wall//: the weight matrices are too large to fit in on-chip SRAM, so the GPU spends most of its time waiting for data to arrive from HBM rather than computing. <<link "Mixed Precision Training" "Mixed Precision Training">><</link>> and <<link "Gradient Checkpointing" "Gradient Checkpointing">><</link>> are workarounds, not solutions. As models grow to trillions of parameters, the memory wall becomes the dominant bottleneck.

''2. Communication bottleneck in multi-GPU training.'' When training is distributed across thousands of GPUs, each gradient synchronisation step requires all-reduce communication across the cluster. Network bandwidth between nodes (typically 400–800 Gb/s InfiniBand) is orders of magnitude slower than on-chip bandwidth. <<link "Tensor Parallelism" "Tensor Parallelism">><</link>> minimises this but cannot eliminate it. At extreme scale, GPUs spend a growing fraction of time waiting for each other rather than computing.

''3. Diminishing returns for sparse and irregular workloads.'' Not all AI computation is dense matrix multiplication. Graph neural networks, sparse mixture-of-experts routing, tree search, symbolic reasoning, and reinforcement learning environments all involve irregular, data-dependent computation patterns. GPUs handle these poorly — thousands of cores sit idle when the workload is not uniform. The GPU revolution accelerated one //type// of AI; it left other types behind.

!! Technical Details

''Key GPU generations and their AI impact:''
- ''GTX 580 (2010):'' 1.6 TFLOPS FP32, 1.5 GB VRAM. Enabled AlexNet training on two cards.
- ''K80 (2014):'' 8.7 TFLOPS FP32, 24 GB VRAM (dual GPU). Made large LSTM sequence models practical.
- ''V100 (2017):'' 125 TFLOPS FP16 with Tensor Cores, 16–32 GB HBM2. Enabled BERT and GPT-scale pre-training.
- ''A100 (2020):'' 312 TFLOPS FP16, 40–80 GB HBM2e. Enabled GPT-3 (175B parameters) across thousands of cards.
- ''H100 (2022):'' 989 TFLOPS FP16, 80 GB HBM3. Introduced FP8 Tensor Cores and Transformer Engine for automatic mixed precision.
- ''B200 (2024):'' ~2.25 PFLOPS FP16, 192 GB HBM3e. Designed for trillion-parameter models.

''CUDA ecosystem:'' Over 4 million developers, 900+ GPU-accelerated applications. cuDNN (deep learning primitives), cuBLAS (linear algebra), NCCL (multi-GPU communication) form the backbone of every major training framework.

''Tensor Core operation:'' A Tensor Core computes $D = A \times B + C$ where $A$, $B$ are $4 \times 4$ FP16 matrices and $C$, $D$ are $4 \times 4$ FP32 matrices — performing 64 FMA (fused multiply-add) operations per clock cycle per core. An H100 has 528 Tensor Cores.

''NVLink:'' GPU-to-GPU interconnect providing up to 900 GB/s bidirectional bandwidth (H100). Essential for <<link "Tensor Parallelism" "Tensor Parallelism">><</link>>, where a single matrix multiply is split across multiple GPUs and partial results must be exchanged every layer.

''Energy and cost:'' Training GPT-3 consumed an estimated 1,287 MWh of electricity. An H100 GPU draws ~700W at peak. A 10,000-GPU training cluster consumes ~7 MW — comparable to a small town. The economic and environmental cost of the GPU revolution is increasingly scrutinised.

''Alternative accelerators:'' Google TPU v5e (FP8 matmuls, 256 GB HBM), Cerebras CS-3 (wafer-scale, 900K cores, 44 GB on-chip SRAM), Graphcore IPU (designed for sparse/irregular workloads), Intel Gaudi (cost-competitive for inference). None has displaced the NVIDIA GPU + CUDA ecosystem for training frontier models.
