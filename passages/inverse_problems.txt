! Inverse Problems

<img src="assets/inverse_problems.svg" alt="Diagram showing forward problem (cause to observation) versus inverse problem (observation back to cause), with Hadamard ill-posedness conditions and four regularization strategies" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you hear a clap in a cathedral and want to know the exact shape of the room from the echo alone. The clap-to-echo direction is easy — physics tells you precisely how sound bounces off walls. But going backwards, from echo to room shape, is treacherous: many different rooms could produce a nearly identical echo. That backwards direction is an ''inverse problem'' — reconstructing the hidden cause from its observable effect. CT scanners do it with X-ray projections, astronomers do it with blurred telescope images, and geophysicists do it with seismograph readings. The forward direction is clean; the inverse direction is almost always ill-posed and demands extra assumptions — ''regularization'' — to get a useful answer.

---

!! The Core Insight

Every inverse problem has two directions. The ''forward problem'' maps a cause $x$ through a known physical or mathematical process $A$ to produce an observation $y = Ax + \varepsilon$ (where $\varepsilon$ is noise). This direction is typically well-behaved: given the cause, the observation is uniquely and stably determined. The ''inverse problem'' asks: given $y$, recover $x$. This direction is almost never well-behaved.

Hadamard (1902) defined a problem as ''well-posed'' if it has a solution, that solution is unique, and it depends continuously on the data. An inverse problem is ''ill-posed'' when any of these conditions fails — and most interesting inverse problems fail all three. Small measurement noise in $y$ gets amplified into enormous errors in the recovered $x$. Multiple causes produce indistinguishable observations. The raw mathematical solution, if it exists at all, is useless.

The universal remedy is <<link "Regularization" "Regularization">><</link>> — injecting prior knowledge about what the true solution should look like. Without regularization, the inverse problem is not just hard; it is meaningless. With the right regularization, it becomes one of the most powerful frameworks in science and engineering.

---

!! From CT Scans to Deblurring

The most celebrated inverse problem is ''CT reconstruction''. An X-ray source rotates around a patient, and detectors measure how much radiation passes through at each angle. The forward model is the Radon transform — it integrates the tissue density along each ray. The inverse problem is: given these line integrals, reconstruct the 2D density image of the patient's body. Radon proved in 1917 that the inverse is mathematically possible, but the practical algorithm — filtered back-projection — was not developed until the 1970s (Hounsfield, Cormack; Nobel Prize, 1979). Modern CT uses iterative methods with total-variation <<link "Regularization" "Regularization">><</link>> to reduce radiation dose while maintaining image quality.

''Image deblurring'' is another canonical example. Camera shake or lens imperfections convolve the sharp image with a blur kernel. The forward model is $y = k * x + \varepsilon$, where $*$ denotes convolution. Deconvolution — dividing in the Fourier domain — amplifies high-frequency noise catastrophically. The solution is to regularize: <<link "Tikhonov Regularization" "Tikhonov Regularization">><</link>> penalizes rough solutions, total-variation preserves edges, and modern deep learning approaches use <<link "Diffusion Models" "Diffusion Models">><</link>> as learned image priors.

''Source localization'' — finding where a signal originated from sensor array measurements — appears in radar, sonar, MEG brain imaging, and gravitational wave detection. The forward model propagates waves from source to sensors; the inverse problem finds the source location, often from noisy, incomplete measurements.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The generic linear inverse problem is:

$$y = Ax + \varepsilon$$

where $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ is the forward operator, $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the unknown cause, $y \in \mathbb{R}^m$ is the observation, and $\varepsilon$ is noise. The naive solution $\hat{x} = A^{-1} y$ fails for three reasons: $A$ may not be square, it may not be invertible, and even if it is, $A^{-1}$ amplifies noise.

The ''<<link "SVD" "SVD">><</link>>'' reveals why. Decompose $A = U \Sigma V^T$. The naive inverse is $A^{-1} = V \Sigma^{-1} U^T$, where $\Sigma^{-1}$ divides by each singular value $\sigma_i$. When $\sigma_i$ is tiny — and ill-posed problems always have singular values decaying toward zero — dividing by $\sigma_i$ amplifies the noise component by $1/\sigma_i$. A singular value of $10^{-8}$ amplifies noise by a factor of $10^8$.

''<<link "Tikhonov Regularization" "Tikhonov Regularization">><</link>>'' replaces the dangerous $1/\sigma_i$ with $\sigma_i / (\sigma_i^2 + \lambda)$:

$$\hat{x}_{\lambda} = \arg\min_x \|Ax - y\|^2 + \lambda \|x\|^2$$

The closed-form solution is $\hat{x}_{\lambda} = (A^T A + \lambda I)^{-1} A^T y$. The regularization parameter $\lambda$ controls the bias-variance trade-off: large $\lambda$ gives a stable but biased estimate; small $\lambda$ gives an accurate but noisy one. Choosing $\lambda$ well — via the L-curve, generalized cross-validation, or the discrepancy principle — is critical.

The ''<<link "Bayesian Inference" "Bayesian Inference">><</link>>'' perspective unifies regularization naturally. Treat $x$ as a random variable with prior $p(x)$ and the likelihood $p(y|x)$ determined by the noise model. The posterior is:

$$p(x|y) \propto p(y|x) \, p(x)$$

Tikhonov regularization is equivalent to a Gaussian prior and Gaussian noise: $p(x) = \mathcal{N}(0, \lambda^{-1} I)$. Sparsity-promoting L1 regularization corresponds to a Laplace prior. <<link "Diffusion Models" "Diffusion Models">><</link>> provide learned, highly expressive priors that capture the true manifold of natural images far better than any hand-designed penalty.

---

!! Regularization Strategies in Practice

The choice of regularizer encodes assumptions about what the true solution looks like:

''Tikhonov / L2 regularization'' assumes the solution is smooth and has small norm. It is the workhorse of classical inverse problems — simple, fast, and closed-form. It works well when the true solution is indeed smooth, but it over-smooths edges and sharp features.

''Total variation (TV) regularization'' assumes the solution is piecewise constant, penalizing the total amount of gradient magnitude: $\text{TV}(x) = \sum |\nabla x|$. It preserves edges brilliantly but creates staircase artifacts in smooth regions.

''Sparsity / L1 regularization'' (compressed sensing) assumes the solution is sparse in some basis — only a few coefficients are non-zero. Candes, Romberg, and Tao (2006) showed that under restricted isometry conditions, L1 minimization recovers sparse signals exactly from far fewer measurements than the Nyquist rate would require.

''Learned priors'' represent the modern frontier. A neural network — typically a <<link "Diffusion Models" "Diffusion Models">><</link>> trained on a large dataset of natural images — serves as an implicit regularizer. During reconstruction, the optimization alternates between fitting the observed data and projecting onto the learned manifold. This approach now dominates medical image reconstruction, super-resolution, and inpainting benchmarks.

---

!! When Inverse Problems Break Down

''Wrong forward model:'' Every inverse method assumes the forward operator $A$ is known accurately. In practice, $A$ itself may be uncertain — the blur kernel is unknown (blind deconvolution), the CT geometry has calibration errors, the physics model is approximate. When the assumed forward model differs from reality, the reconstruction is systematically biased in ways that no amount of regularization can fix. Model error is often the dominant failure mode in real-world applications.

''Regularization mismatch:'' The regularizer encodes assumptions about the solution. When those assumptions are wrong — using a smoothness prior on an image with sharp edges, or a sparsity prior on a dense signal — the reconstruction is pulled toward the wrong part of solution space. The result can look plausible but be quantitatively wrong, which is especially dangerous in medical imaging where false confidence in a reconstruction can affect clinical decisions.

''Noise amplification at the boundary:'' Even with good regularization, there are components of the solution that the data simply cannot constrain — the ''null space'' of $A$. Information in the null space is invisible to the observations. No algorithm can recover it; it must come entirely from the prior. When the null space is large (highly under-determined problems), the reconstruction is mostly prior and very little data, creating outputs that reflect the regularizer's biases rather than physical reality.

---

!! Technical Details

''Hadamard (1902)'' formalized well-posedness for PDEs: existence, uniqueness, continuous dependence on data. Most PDE boundary-value problems going "backwards in time" are ill-posed.

''Picard condition:'' A necessary condition for a solution to exist — the Fourier coefficients of the data must decay faster than the singular values of the operator. When noise violates this condition, no exact solution exists.

''Truncated SVD:'' Regularize by keeping only the $k$ largest singular values and setting the rest to zero. Equivalent to projecting onto the $k$-dimensional subspace best supported by the data. The <<link "SVD" "SVD">><</link>> provides the optimal such subspace.

''Iterative methods:'' Conjugate gradient, Landweber iteration, and ADMM are used for large-scale inverse problems where forming $A^T A$ explicitly is infeasible. Early stopping of iterative solvers acts as implicit regularization — the iteration first reconstructs large-scale features (high singular values) and only later begins fitting noise.

''Nonlinear inverse problems:'' When the forward model is nonlinear — $y = f(x) + \varepsilon$ — linearization (Gauss-Newton) or fully nonlinear optimization (Levenberg-Marquardt) is required. Neural network inversion (e.g., inverting a generative model) is a nonlinear inverse problem.

''Discrete ill-posedness:'' The condition number $\kappa(A) = \sigma_{\max} / \sigma_{\min}$ quantifies sensitivity. For ill-posed problems discretized on a grid, $\kappa(A)$ grows exponentially with grid refinement — finer discretization makes the problem harder, not easier.

''Connection to compressed sensing:'' If $x$ is $s$-sparse and $A$ satisfies the restricted isometry property (RIP) with $\delta_{2s} < \sqrt{2} - 1$, then $x$ is exactly recoverable from $m = O(s \log(n/s))$ measurements via L1 minimization — far fewer than the $n$ measurements that classical theory requires.
