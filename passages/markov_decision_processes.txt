! Markov Decision Processes

[img[assets/markov_decision_processes.svg]]

Imagine you are navigating a city where every intersection is a choice: turn left toward the highway (fast but risky traffic), or stay on side streets (slow but predictable). At each intersection you cannot see the entire map — you only know where you are //right now//, and you must pick a direction based solely on that. A ''Markov Decision Process (MDP)'' is the mathematical framework that formalises exactly this kind of sequential decision-making under uncertainty. It gives an agent a precise language for describing states, actions, probabilistic outcomes, and rewards — and then asks: what is the best strategy for navigating from here to a goal?

MDPs are the theoretical backbone of <<link "Reinforcement Learning" "Reinforcement Learning">><</link>>. Every RL algorithm — from tabular <<link "Q-Learning" "Q-Learning">><</link>> to deep <<link "Policy Gradient" "Policy Gradient">><</link>> methods — is implicitly or explicitly solving an MDP. Understanding MDPs means understanding //what// RL is trying to optimise, not just //how//.

---

!! The Core Insight

An MDP captures a simple but powerful idea: the future depends on the present state and the action you take, but //not// on how you got to the present state. This is the ''Markov property'' — the past is irrelevant once you know where you are now. This memorylessness is not a limitation of the framework; it is a modelling choice that makes the problem tractable. If history matters, you expand the state to include the relevant history, and the Markov property holds again.

An MDP is defined by a tuple $(S, A, P, R, \gamma)$: a set of ''states'' $S$, a set of ''actions'' $A$, a ''transition function'' $P(s'|s,a)$ giving the probability of landing in state $s'$ after taking action $a$ in state $s$, a ''reward function'' $R(s,a,s')$ assigning a numerical signal to each transition, and a ''discount factor'' $\gamma \in [0,1)$ that controls how much the agent cares about future versus immediate rewards.

The goal is to find a ''policy'' $\pi(a|s)$ — a mapping from states to actions (or distributions over actions) — that maximises the expected cumulative discounted reward over time. This is the problem that all of reinforcement learning is built to solve.

---

!! States, Actions, Rewards, and Transitions

''States'' represent everything the agent needs to know about the world at a given moment. In a board game, the state is the board configuration. In robotics, it might be joint angles and velocities. The Markov property demands that the state be //sufficient// — it must encode all information relevant to future outcomes.

''Actions'' are the choices available to the agent in each state. The action set can be finite (move left, right, up, down) or continuous (apply a torque of any magnitude). Different states may have different available actions, though many formulations assume a fixed action set for simplicity.

''Transitions'' $P(s'|s,a)$ encode the environment's response to an action. They capture stochasticity: taking the same action in the same state might produce different outcomes. A robot gripper may slip. A card game may deal a random card. The agent cannot control transitions — it can only choose actions and let the environment respond.

''Rewards'' $R(s,a,s')$ are scalar signals that tell the agent what is desirable. Positive rewards encourage behaviour; negative rewards (penalties) discourage it. Designing good reward functions is one of the hardest practical challenges in RL — it connects to the broader problem of alignment between what we //specify// and what we //want//.

The ''discount factor'' $\gamma$ balances short-term and long-term thinking. When $\gamma = 0$ the agent is purely myopic, grabbing immediate reward. As $\gamma \to 1$, the agent weighs future rewards almost equally with present ones. In practice, $\gamma$ between 0.95 and 0.99 is typical, giving the agent a planning horizon of roughly $1/(1-\gamma)$ steps.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The central quantity in an MDP is the ''state-value function'' under a policy $\pi$:

$$V^\pi(s) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t R(s_t, a_t, s_{t+1}) \;\middle|\; s_0 = s\right]$$

This is the expected total discounted reward starting from state $s$ and following policy $\pi$ forever. There is also the ''action-value function'' (Q-function):

$$Q^\pi(s,a) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t R(s_t, a_t, s_{t+1}) \;\middle|\; s_0 = s,\; a_0 = a\right]$$

Both satisfy recursive relationships known as the ''Bellman equations''. For the value function:

$$V^\pi(s) = \sum_{a} \pi(a|s) \sum_{s'} P(s'|s,a)\!\left[R(s,a,s') + \gamma\, V^\pi(s')\right]$$

The optimal policy $\pi^*$ satisfies the ''Bellman optimality equation'':

$$V^*(s) = \max_{a} \sum_{s'} P(s'|s,a)\!\left[R(s,a,s') + \gamma\, V^*(s')\right]$$

This equation says: the value of the best strategy equals the best action's expected immediate reward plus discounted future value. The <<link "Bellman Equation" "Bellman Equation">><</link>> is the recursive heartbeat of every MDP solver — value iteration, policy iteration, and their deep-learning descendants all revolve around making this equation hold.

---

!! Solving MDPs

When the transition and reward functions are //known//, an MDP can be solved exactly using ''<<link "Dynamic Programming" "Dynamic Programming">><</link>>'' methods.

''Value iteration'' starts with an arbitrary value function and repeatedly applies the Bellman optimality operator: $V(s) \leftarrow \max_a \sum_{s'} P(s'|s,a)[R + \gamma V(s')]$. This converges to $V^*$ because the Bellman operator is a contraction mapping — each application brings the estimate closer to the truth. The optimal policy is then extracted greedily: $\pi^*(s) = \arg\max_a Q^*(s,a)$.

''Policy iteration'' alternates between two steps: (1) ''policy evaluation'' — compute $V^\pi$ for the current policy by solving the linear Bellman equation, and (2) ''policy improvement'' — set $\pi(s) = \arg\max_a Q^\pi(s,a)$. This converges in remarkably few iterations (often fewer than 10, regardless of state-space size).

When the model is //unknown// — the agent cannot access $P$ or $R$ directly — we enter the territory of reinforcement learning. The agent must learn from interaction: <<link "Q-Learning" "Q-Learning">><</link>> estimates $Q^*$ from sampled transitions without ever building a model, while <<link "Policy Gradient" "Policy Gradient">><</link>> methods directly optimise the policy by estimating gradients of expected return from rollouts.

---

!! When MDPs Break Down

''Partial observability:'' The Markov property requires that the state encode everything the agent needs. In many real problems — a robot with a limited camera, a card game with hidden cards — the agent sees only a noisy, incomplete observation. The problem becomes a ''Partially Observable MDP (POMDP)'', which is vastly harder: the agent must maintain a belief distribution over possible states, and the effective state space (the space of beliefs) is continuous even when the underlying state space is finite.

''Enormous state spaces:'' MDPs with millions or billions of states cannot be solved by tabular methods. The value function must be approximated — typically with a neural network — which sacrifices the convergence guarantees of exact dynamic programming. Function approximation introduces instability, catastrophic forgetting, and the deadly triad (off-policy + function approximation + bootstrapping) that makes deep RL notoriously brittle.

''Reward misspecification:'' An MDP will faithfully optimise whatever reward function you provide, even if it does not capture your true intent. A cleaning robot rewarded for "no visible mess" may learn to hide mess in closets. This is not a failure of the MDP framework — it is a failure of the reward design. Reward shaping, inverse RL, and human-feedback methods exist to mitigate this, but it remains an open problem at the heart of AI safety.

---

!! Technical Details

''Formal definition:'' An MDP is the tuple $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, R, \gamma)$ where $\mathcal{S}$ is a (possibly infinite) state space, $\mathcal{A}$ is the action space, $P: \mathcal{S} \times \mathcal{A} \times \mathcal{S} \to [0,1]$ is the transition kernel, $R: \mathcal{S} \times \mathcal{A} \times \mathcal{S} \to \mathbb{R}$ is the reward function, and $\gamma \in [0,1)$ is the discount factor.

''Convergence:'' Value iteration converges at rate $O(\gamma^k)$ where $k$ is the iteration count. Policy iteration converges in at most $|\mathcal{A}|^{|\mathcal{S}|}$ steps (the total number of deterministic policies), but in practice converges in $O(|\mathcal{S}|)$ iterations or fewer.

''Continuous MDPs:'' When $\mathcal{S}$ or $\mathcal{A}$ is continuous, summations become integrals and exact solutions require solving integral equations. Practical approaches include discretisation, linear function approximation, and kernel-based methods.

''Relationship to control theory:'' MDPs in continuous state and action spaces with deterministic transitions and quadratic costs reduce to the ''Linear-Quadratic Regulator (LQR)'' problem from optimal control, which has a closed-form solution via the Riccati equation.

''Key extensions:'' Semi-MDPs (variable time between decisions), factored MDPs (state decomposed into independent factors), constrained MDPs (optimise reward subject to safety constraints), and multi-agent MDPs (multiple interacting decision-makers).

''Historical note:'' Richard Bellman formalised MDPs in the 1950s as part of his work on dynamic programming. The name "dynamic programming" was chosen deliberately to sound impressive to government funders — Bellman later admitted this in his autobiography.
