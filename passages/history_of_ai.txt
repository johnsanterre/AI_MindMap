! History of AI

<img src="assets/history_of_ai.svg" alt="History of AI timeline diagram" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Think of AI research as a series of gold rushes: each one starts with genuine excitement and real finds, then hits a wall when the current tools run out, and the camps empty out until someone brings a better shovel. The field has done this at least twice — two periods of hype and two hard crashes — before finally striking a vein deep enough to sustain a decade-long boom. Understanding //why// each crash happened is just as important as understanding the breakthroughs.

!! The Core Insight

''Artificial intelligence is not a single technology'' — it is a shifting collection of bets about what kind of math best approximates thought. The history of AI is the history of those bets being placed, failing, and being replaced. The field's recurring trap is mistaking a powerful technique for a general solution: when the technique hits its limits, funding collapses and researchers scatter. What survives each winter is not the technique itself, but the hard-won intuition about //what the real bottlenecks are//.

!! Key Eras

''1950s–1960s: The Optimistic Dawn.'' The field was born with almost boundless optimism. Researchers believed that human reasoning could be captured in formal logic and symbol manipulation. [[Expert Systems]] — programs built from hand-crafted "if-then" rules — could diagnose diseases, play chess, and prove theorems. The [[Perceptron]], invented by Frank Rosenblatt in 1958, showed that a single artificial neuron could learn to classify inputs. Early demos were impressive enough that government agencies funded the work lavishly.

''1970s–1980s: The First [[AI Winter]].'' Progress stalled. Expert systems required thousands of hand-coded rules and broke immediately outside their narrow domain. The Perceptron was shown to be incapable of learning non-linearly separable functions (the XOR problem). Funding dried up. The winter lasted roughly from the mid-1970s through the mid-1980s. It ended not with a single invention but with two quiet contributions: [[Backpropagation and Chain Rule|Backpropagation and Chain Rule]] (popularised by Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams in 1986) gave [[Neural Networks]] a practical training algorithm, and hardware began to improve.

''1990s: The Statistical Thaw.'' A new philosophy took hold: instead of hand-crafting rules, let data drive the model. [[Support Vector Machine|Support Vector Machine]]s (Vapnik, 1995) provided principled, theoretically grounded classifiers. Hidden Markov Models dominated speech recognition. Yann LeCun's LeNet-5 (1998) showed that [[Neural Networks]] could read handwritten digits reliably using convolutional filters. The mood was cautiously optimistic but not euphoric — neural networks still lost to SVMs on most benchmarks.

''2000s: The Second AI Winter (and its thaw).'' Despite steady progress, a second, softer winter set in around 2000–2005. Funding for neural-network research contracted again; the term "neural network" itself became unfashionable. The escape hatch opened in 2006 when Geoffrey Hinton demonstrated that deep networks could be pre-trained layer-by-layer using [[Restricted Boltzmann Machines]] stacked into [[Deep Belief Networks]]. This "greedy pre-training" trick let gradients propagate through many layers without vanishing — and it rekindled serious interest in deep architectures.

''2012: The Deep Learning Revolution.'' [[AlexNet]] — trained by Alex Krizhevsky, Ilya Sutskever, and Geoffrey Hinton — won the [[ImageNet]] Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge by a margin that shocked the computer vision community. The key ingredients: deep convolutional layers, ReLU activations, dropout regularization, and — crucially — training on two NVIDIA GPUs. This was the moment [[Statistical Machine Learning]] gave way to [[Deep Learning]] as the dominant paradigm. The [[Hardware Lottery]] had been won: GPU parallelism fit the matrix algebra of deep networks perfectly.

''2017–present: The Transformer Era.'' The 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need" introduced the [[Transformers|Transformers]] architecture, discarding recurrence entirely in favour of self-attention. Pre-training on vast text corpora followed by fine-tuning unlocked transfer learning at scale. [[Large Language Models]] like GPT, BERT, and their successors demonstrated that a single architecture, scaled up with enough data and compute, could achieve state-of-the-art results across language, vision, code, and scientific tasks.

!! What the Math Was Actually Doing

Each era corresponded to a different mathematical framework for representing knowledge and uncertainty.

''Symbolic AI'' encoded knowledge as first-order logic predicates. Inference was theorem proving — sound but brittle and exponential in the worst case.

''Statistical ML'' replaced hard rules with probability distributions. The [[Support Vector Machine]] found the maximum-margin hyperplane separating classes, solving the primal problem:

$$\min_{w,b} \frac{1}{2}\|w\|^2 \quad \text{subject to } y_i(w \cdot x_i + b) \geq 1$$

''[[Backpropagation and Chain Rule|Backpropagation]]'' made neural networks trainable by computing gradients via the chain rule. For a loss $\mathcal{L}$ and layer weights $W_\ell$:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial W_\ell} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial a_\ell} \cdot \frac{\partial a_\ell}{\partial W_\ell}$$

where each upstream gradient is passed backward through the network layer by layer.

''Self-attention'' in [[Transformers]] computes weighted sums over all positions simultaneously:

$$\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V$$

This single operation, parallelisable across the full sequence, replaced the sequential bottleneck of recurrent networks and unlocked training on internet-scale corpora.

!! Paradigm Shifts

Every major transition in AI history shared the same structure: a ''new representation'' met a ''new optimization method'' at the moment ''hardware made it tractable''.

The symbolic-to-statistical shift was driven by the failure of hand-crafted rules to generalise beyond curated domains. The statistical-to-deep shift was driven by the failure of shallow models to exploit raw perceptual data (pixels, waveforms) without hand-engineered features. The deep-to-transformer shift was driven by the failure of convolutional and recurrent architectures to efficiently model long-range dependencies.

Each shift also required a sociological change: researchers had to abandon the vocabulary, benchmarks, and publication venues of the previous era. The [[AI Winter]] was not just a funding crisis — it was the collapse of a shared research programme.

The [[Hardware Lottery]] thesis (Hooker, 2020) adds a sobering footnote: the ideas that //won// were not necessarily the best ideas available — they were the ideas that happened to fit the hardware that happened to be cheap. Biological neural networks were not the only inspiration on the table in the 1980s; they succeeded partly because matrix multiplication on GPUs turned out to be an extraordinarily efficient primitive.

!! When AI Progress Stalls

''1. The brittleness wall.'' Rule-based and early neural systems fail catastrophically outside their training distribution. Expert systems encode only what their human designers knew; they have no way to generalise. This is why both AI winters were triggered by real-world deployment failures, not theoretical refutations.

''2. The vanishing gradient problem.'' Deep networks trained with sigmoid activations suffered from gradients shrinking exponentially as they propagated backward, making early layers effectively untrainable. This was the core technical barrier that kept deep learning dormant through the 1990s, resolved by ReLU activations, careful initialisation, batch normalisation, and residual connections.

''3. The data and compute gap.'' Many powerful ideas existed years before they became practical. Convolutional networks were demonstrated in 1998; transformers were theoretically feasible long before 2017. What was missing was sufficient labelled data (solved by [[ImageNet]] and web-scale corpora) and sufficient compute (solved by GPU clusters and later TPUs). When the theory outruns the infrastructure, progress stalls until hardware catches up.

!! Technical Details

- ''Dartmouth Conference (1956)'' — the founding workshop that coined the term "artificial intelligence." Participants included McCarthy, Minsky, Shannon, and Simon.
- ''Perceptron convergence theorem (1958)'' — Rosenblatt proved that the Perceptron would converge to a correct solution //if// one existed. Minsky and Papert's 1969 book showed the "if" was severe.
- ''Backpropagation (1986)'' — Rumelhart, Hinton, Williams. Gradient descent through differentiable layers via the chain rule. Independently derived multiple times; the 1986 //Nature// paper made it standard.
- ''VC dimension (1971/1995)'' — Vapnik and Chervonenkis established the theoretical framework for generalisation bounds; the SVM operationalised this into a practical algorithm.
- ''LSTM (1997)'' — Hochreiter and Schmidhuber's Long Short-Term Memory solved the vanishing gradient problem for sequences using gated memory cells.
- ''Deep Belief Networks (2006)'' — Hinton and Salakhutdinov: layer-wise pretraining using [[Restricted Boltzmann Machines]] enabled initialisation of deep networks in a good basin.
- ''AlexNet (2012)'' — 60M parameters, trained on two GTX 580 GPUs, top-5 error of 15.3% vs. runner-up's 26.2% on [[ImageNet]]. Triggered massive industry investment.
- ''Word2Vec (2013)'' — Mikolov et al. showed that word embeddings encode semantic relationships: $\vec{\text{king}} - \vec{\text{man}} + \vec{\text{woman}} \approx \vec{\text{queen}}$.
- ''"Attention Is All You Need" (2017)'' — Vaswani et al. introduced the Transformer: multi-head self-attention + positional encodings + feed-forward sublayers. Formed the backbone of BERT, GPT, T5, and all subsequent [[Large Language Models]].
- ''Scaling laws (2020)'' — Kaplan et al. (OpenAI) showed that loss scales as a power law in model parameters $N$, dataset tokens $D$, and compute $C$: $L \propto N^{-\alpha}$, enabling principled decisions about how to allocate resources.
