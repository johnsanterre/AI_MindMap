! Imitation Learning

<img src="assets/imitation_learning.svg" alt="Diagram showing three imitation learning approaches: Behavioral Cloning, DAgger, and Inverse RL" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are learning to drive not from a textbook of traffic laws, but by sitting next to an experienced driver and copying exactly what they do — when they brake, how they steer, when they change lanes. You never ask //why// they made each choice; you just mimic the pattern. That is ''imitation learning'' in a nutshell: instead of designing a reward function and letting an agent discover behavior through trial and error, you hand it a collection of expert demonstrations and say "do what they did." This sidesteps one of the hardest problems in <<link "reinforcement learning" "Reinforcement Learning">><</link>> — specifying what "good" behavior looks like mathematically — by replacing it with examples of good behavior directly.

---

!! The Core Insight

<<link "Reinforcement learning" "Reinforcement Learning">><</link>> requires a reward signal. Defining that signal is notoriously difficult: reward functions that are too sparse leave the agent wandering aimlessly, while reward functions that are too dense create unintended shortcuts (reward hacking). Imitation learning recognizes that //humans are often better at demonstrating a task than at writing down a mathematical objective for it//.

The simplest approach — ''behavioral cloning'' — frames the problem as <<link "supervised learning" "Supervised Learning">><</link>>: given a dataset of expert state-action pairs $(s, a^*)$, train a policy $\pi_\theta(a|s)$ to predict the expert's action in each state. No reward function needed. No environment interaction during training. Just standard classification or regression.

But this simplicity hides a critical flaw: ''compounding errors''. The learner trains on states the expert visited, but at test time the learner's own small mistakes push it into states the expert never encountered. In those unfamiliar states, the policy makes worse decisions, which push it further off-distribution, which causes even worse decisions — a cascading failure known as <<link "distribution shift" "Distribution Shift">><</link>>. This is the central challenge that separates naive imitation from robust imitation learning.

---

!! From Behavioral Cloning to DAgger and Beyond

''Behavioral cloning (BC)'' is a one-shot process: collect demonstrations, train a policy, deploy. Its appeal is simplicity. Its weakness is the compounding error problem described above. If the expert trajectory has length $T$, the total error grows as $O(T^2)$ rather than $O(T)$, because each small deviation changes the future state distribution.

''DAgger'' (Dataset Aggregation) fixes this by iterating. In each round: (1) the learner executes its current policy in the environment, visiting states the //learner// actually encounters; (2) the expert labels those states with the correct actions; (3) the new labeled data is aggregated with the existing dataset; (4) the policy is retrained on the combined data. Over rounds, the training distribution converges to the learner's own state distribution, eliminating the distribution mismatch. DAgger provides a theoretical guarantee: after $N$ rounds, the policy's performance approaches the expert's with error $O(1/N)$.

''Inverse reinforcement learning (IRL)'' takes a different philosophy. Rather than copying actions, it asks: "What reward function would make the expert's behavior optimal?" Once the reward is recovered, standard RL can optimize a policy. This is more powerful — the learned reward can generalize to new situations the expert never demonstrated — but also more expensive, since it requires solving an RL problem inside the learning loop.

''GAIL'' (Generative Adversarial Imitation Learning) combines ideas from IRL and GANs. A discriminator tries to distinguish expert trajectories from learner trajectories, while the policy (generator) tries to fool the discriminator. The adversarial signal implicitly defines a reward without ever explicitly recovering a reward function. This connects imitation learning to <<link "RLHF" "RLHF">><</link>>, where human preferences replace expert demonstrations.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

In behavioral cloning, the objective is pure supervised learning:

$$\min_\theta \;\mathbb{E}_{(s,a^*) \sim \mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{expert}}}\!\left[\mathcal{L}\!\left(\pi_\theta(s),\; a^*\right)\right]$$

For discrete actions, $\mathcal{L}$ is cross-entropy; for continuous actions, it is typically mean squared error or negative log-likelihood under a Gaussian policy.

The DAgger objective trains on an aggregated dataset $\mathcal{D} = \bigcup_{i=1}^{N} \mathcal{D}_i$ where each $\mathcal{D}_i$ contains states visited by $\pi_{\theta_{i-1}}$ (the learner's policy at round $i-1$) labeled by the expert $\pi^*$:

$$\min_\theta \;\mathbb{E}_{s \sim d_{\pi_{\theta_{i-1}}},\; a^* = \pi^*(s)}\!\left[\mathcal{L}\!\left(\pi_\theta(s),\; a^*\right)\right]$$

The key difference from BC is that $s \sim d_{\pi_{\theta_{i-1}}}$: states come from the learner's distribution, not the expert's.

In inverse RL, the objective is to find a reward function $r$ such that the expert policy is optimal:

$$\max_r \;\left(\min_\pi \mathbb{E}_{\pi}\!\left[\sum_t r(s_t, a_t)\right]\right) - \mathbb{E}_{\pi^*}\!\left[\sum_t r(s_t, a_t)\right]$$

This maximizes the margin between the expert's expected return and the best alternative policy's return. The inner minimization is a full RL problem, making IRL computationally expensive.

In GAIL, the discriminator $D_\phi$ and <<link "policy" "Policy Gradient">><</link>> $\pi_\theta$ play a minimax game:

$$\min_\theta \max_\phi \;\mathbb{E}_{\pi^*}\!\left[\log D_\phi(s, a)\right] + \mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}\!\left[\log(1 - D_\phi(s, a))\right]$$

The discriminator provides an implicit reward $r(s,a) = -\log(1 - D_\phi(s,a))$ that the policy optimizes via <<link "policy gradient" "Policy Gradient">><</link>> methods.

---

!! Practical Considerations

''Data efficiency:'' Behavioral cloning is the most data-efficient when demonstrations are cheap (e.g., teleoperation in robotics, human gameplay recordings). DAgger requires interactive expert access, which is expensive — the expert must label states on-demand during each iteration. IRL and GAIL require environment interaction, but fewer demonstrations since they extract a transferable reward signal.

''Continuous control:'' In robotics, imitation learning dominates over RL because reward design for manipulation tasks is extremely difficult. Systems like RT-2 and Diffusion Policy use behavioral cloning with large models trained on thousands of teleoperated demonstrations. For self-driving, companies like Wayve use conditional imitation learning where the policy takes both sensor input and a high-level command (e.g., "turn left at the next intersection").

''Language models:'' The supervised fine-tuning (SFT) stage of language model training is behavioral cloning — the model imitates high-quality text completions. <<link "RLHF" "RLHF">><</link>> then refines the policy using human preferences, combining imitation learning with reward-based optimization.

''Multimodal policies:'' Expert demonstrations often contain multimodal behavior — the expert might turn left //or// right at an intersection, depending on intent not visible in the state. Standard BC with unimodal Gaussian outputs averages these modes, producing a policy that goes straight (and crashes). Solutions include mixture density networks, diffusion policies, and action-chunking transformers.

---

!! When Imitation Learning Breaks Down

''Compounding errors in behavioral cloning:'' Small prediction errors at each timestep accumulate quadratically over the trajectory horizon. A self-driving policy that drifts 1cm per step will be 1 meter off-course after 100 steps — in states no expert ever visited. The policy has no training signal for recovery from mistakes, because the expert demonstrations contain no mistakes. This is the fundamental limitation of offline imitation: //the training distribution does not match the deployment distribution//. DAgger addresses this specifically, but requires continued expert access.

''Causal confusion:'' The learner may latch onto spurious correlations in the demonstrations rather than the true causal structure. A classic example: a driving policy learns that brake lights ahead predict deceleration, and begins "following" brake lights even when no obstacle is present — because in the training data, brake lights and the expert's braking were correlated. The policy copies the //correlation// rather than the //cause// (obstacle detection). Causal confusion is especially dangerous because the policy can achieve low training loss while learning entirely wrong decision rules.

''Reward ambiguity in inverse RL:'' Many reward functions can explain the same set of demonstrations. A driver who always stays in the right lane might be rewarded for staying right, for avoiding the left lane, for minimizing lane changes, or for any linear combination of these. Without additional constraints or regularization (such as maximum entropy IRL), the recovered reward function may be degenerate — assigning zero reward everywhere, or capturing only a subset of the expert's true objectives. This ''underdetermination'' means the downstream RL policy may behave correctly in demonstrated states but arbitrarily in novel ones.

---

!! Technical Details

''Ross et al. (2011):'' Proved that behavioral cloning has $O(T^2)$ regret while DAgger achieves $O(T)$ regret, where $T$ is the horizon length. This result formalized the compounding error problem and established DAgger as the theoretically principled baseline.

''Computational complexity:'' BC is the cheapest — a single round of supervised learning. DAgger requires $N$ rounds of policy execution + expert labeling + retraining. IRL requires solving an RL problem in the inner loop of each reward update step, making it orders of magnitude more expensive. GAIL reduces cost by avoiding explicit reward recovery but still requires on-policy rollouts.

''Maximum Entropy IRL (Ziebart et al., 2008):'' Resolves reward ambiguity by choosing the reward function that makes the expert's behavior maximally entropic (least committal) among all policies that are optimal under that reward. This gives a unique solution and connects IRL to the Boltzmann distribution over trajectories.

''Behavior Transformers and Diffusion Policy:'' Modern approaches use expressive generative models — transformers with action tokenization or denoising diffusion models — to represent multimodal action distributions. These avoid the mode-averaging problem of Gaussian BC and achieve state-of-the-art results in robotic manipulation.

''Connection to <<link "RLHF" "RLHF">><</link>>:'' RLHF can be viewed as imitation learning where the "expert" provides preferences rather than demonstrations. The reward model in RLHF is analogous to the recovered reward in IRL. <<link "DPO" "DPO">><</link>> further simplifies this by eliminating the reward model entirely.
