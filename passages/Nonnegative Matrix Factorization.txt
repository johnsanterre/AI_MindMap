! Nonnegative Matrix Factorization
<img src="assets/nonnegative_matrix_factorization.svg" alt="Nonnegative Matrix Factorization" width="800">

Imagine you are given a smoothie and asked to figure out which fruits went into it. You know the result tastes like some combination of mango, strawberry, and banana — and crucially, you know no fruit was added in a //negative// amount. You cannot "subtract" banana to make it more like strawberry. ''Nonnegative Matrix Factorization'' (NMF) works exactly like this: it decomposes a data matrix into two smaller matrices whose entries are all non-negative, so every observation is explained as an //additive combination// of non-negative parts. This constraint — no cancellation allowed — is what gives NMF its remarkable interpretability.

!! The Core Insight

When you decompose data with methods like [[Principal Component Analysis|Principal Component Analysis]], the resulting components can have both positive and negative entries. A face might be represented as "generic face //minus// a nose shape //plus// an eye pattern." This makes mathematical sense but is hard to interpret — what does a negative nose mean? ''NMF'' forbids negative entries entirely. Because parts can only be //added//, never subtracted, the algorithm is forced to learn components that look like genuine parts of the data: actual noses, actual eyes, actual mouths. Each data point becomes a recipe — "two parts eyes, one part nose, half a part mouth" — and the recipe coefficients are themselves non-negative. This ''parts-based representation'' emerges purely from the non-negativity constraint, with no explicit instruction to find parts.

!! Parts-Based Representation

The contrast with [[Principal Component Analysis|Principal Component Analysis]] is illuminating. PCA finds directions of maximum variance. The first principal component of a face dataset is typically a holistic "average face," and subsequent components are ghostly positive-and-negative masks that add or subtract features. These components are ''holistic'' — each one spans the entire face. NMF components, by contrast, tend to be ''localized'': one component lights up the left eye region, another captures the mouth, a third covers the forehead. This happens because the non-negativity constraint prevents the kind of global cancellations that PCA relies on.

This parts-based behavior is not guaranteed in every dataset — it emerges when the data genuinely has a parts-based structure (images, text, audio spectra). When data lacks this structure, NMF still produces non-negative factors, but they may not correspond to intuitive "parts."

The interpretability advantage is enormous in practice. In ''topic modeling'', each NMF component is a topic defined by non-negative word weights — the top-weighted words directly describe the topic. In ''spectroscopy'', each component is a pure spectrum. In ''audio source separation'', each component is a non-negative spectrogram of a single source. In all these cases, non-negativity aligns the mathematical decomposition with the physical reality that concentrations, intensities, and amplitudes are inherently non-negative.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Given a non-negative data matrix $V \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^{n \times m}$ (e.g., $n$ documents, $m$ words), NMF seeks two non-negative matrices:

$$V \approx WH, \quad W \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^{n \times r}, \quad H \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^{r \times m}$$

where $r$ is the chosen rank (number of parts/topics/components), typically $r \ll \min(n, m)$.

Each column of $W$ is a ''basis element'' (a part), and each column of $H$ gives the ''activation coefficients'' (how much of each part) for the corresponding data point.

Two standard objective functions:

''Frobenius norm (squared error):''
$$\min_{W, H \geq 0} \|V - WH\|_F^2 = \sum_{ij} (V_{ij} - (WH)_{ij})^2$$

''Generalized KL divergence:''
$$\min_{W, H \geq 0} D_{\text{KL}}(V \| WH) = \sum_{ij} \left( V_{ij} \log \frac{V_{ij}}{(WH)_{ij}} - V_{ij} + (WH)_{ij} \right)$$

The KL divergence is more appropriate when data represents counts or probability-like quantities.

''Lee-Seung multiplicative update rules'' (the most famous NMF algorithm):

For Frobenius objective:
$$H_{ij} \leftarrow H_{ij} \cdot \frac{(W^T V)_{ij}}{(W^T W H)_{ij}}, \quad W_{ij} \leftarrow W_{ij} \cdot \frac{(V H^T)_{ij}}{(W H H^T)_{ij}}$$

These updates are elegant: they preserve non-negativity automatically (a non-negative number multiplied by a ratio of non-negative numbers stays non-negative) and are guaranteed to monotonically decrease the objective.

!! NMF in Practice

''Topic modeling:'' With a term-document matrix $V$ (rows = documents, columns = words), NMF discovers $r$ topics. Each row of $H$ is a topic (non-negative word weights), and $W$ gives each document's topic mixture. This is closely related to [[Latent Dirichlet Allocation|Latent Dirichlet Allocation]], which adds a probabilistic generative model on top of similar intuitions.

''Audio source separation:'' Given a magnitude spectrogram (time-frequency matrix, inherently non-negative), NMF decomposes it into basis spectra (columns of $W$, representing individual sound sources) and their activation over time (rows of $H$). This is the foundation of many music transcription and speech separation systems.

''Image decomposition:'' Applied to face images, NMF learns parts (eyes, nose, mouth regions). Applied to hyperspectral images, it recovers pure material spectra (endmembers) and abundance maps.

''Recommender systems:'' User-item rating matrices can be approximately factored with NMF, yielding interpretable user preference factors and item characteristic factors — similar to matrix factorization approaches used in collaborative filtering.

''Bioinformatics:'' Gene expression matrices decomposed by NMF reveal metagenes — non-negative combinations of genes that represent biological processes or cell types.

!! When NMF Breaks Down

''Non-unique solutions:'' Unlike [[Singular Value Decomposition|Singular Value Decomposition]], NMF does not have a unique solution. The optimization landscape is non-convex, so different random initializations can yield different factorizations. Two equally valid decompositions may tell different "stories" about the same data, making it dangerous to over-interpret any single NMF result without stability analysis.

''Sensitivity to rank selection:'' The choice of $r$ (number of components) dramatically affects results, and there is no universal criterion for choosing it. Too few components blur distinct parts together; too many split coherent parts into fragments. Cross-validation, cophenetic correlation, and stability analysis are used in practice, but none is fully satisfactory.

''Slow convergence:'' The multiplicative update rules, while simple and elegant, converge slowly — often requiring hundreds or thousands of iterations. For large-scale problems ($n, m > 10^5$), this can be prohibitive. Alternatives like hierarchical alternating least squares (HALS) or projected gradient methods converge faster but are more complex to implement.

!! Technical Details

''Lee-Seung (1999, 2001):'' The foundational papers introducing NMF with multiplicative updates for both Frobenius and KL objectives. The 1999 //Nature// paper demonstrated parts-based face decomposition and ignited interest in the method.

''Non-uniqueness and identifiability:'' NMF is unique only under restrictive conditions (e.g., ''separability'' — requiring that each basis element has at least one data point where only that basis element is active). The ''separable NMF'' assumption underpins efficient algorithms (Arora et al. 2012) that run in polynomial time with provable guarantees.

''Sparse NMF:'' Adding an $\ell_1$ penalty on $H$ (or $W$) encourages sparser activations: $\min \|V - WH\|_F^2 + \lambda \|H\|_1$. Hoyer (2004) proposed explicit sparseness constraints using the ratio of $\ell_1$ and $\ell_2$ norms. Sparse NMF often produces more interpretable parts.

''Semi-NMF:'' Relaxes the constraint on $W$ to allow negative entries while keeping $H \geq 0$. Useful when data contains mixed-sign values (e.g., centered data). Introduced by Ding et al. (2010).

''Relationship to PLSA:'' Probabilistic Latent Semantic Analysis (Hofmann 1999) is equivalent to NMF with KL divergence when the factors are appropriately normalized. [[Latent Dirichlet Allocation|Latent Dirichlet Allocation]] can be seen as a Bayesian extension of PLSA/NMF with Dirichlet priors on the mixing weights.

''Algorithms beyond multiplicative updates:'' Alternating Nonnegative Least Squares (ANLS), Hierarchical Alternating Least Squares (HALS/RRI), projected gradient descent (Lin 2007), and coordinate descent methods all offer faster convergence. For massive datasets, online NMF and mini-batch variants exist.

''Relationship to [[Dimensionality Reduction|Dimensionality Reduction]]:'' NMF is a constrained form of [[Low-Rank Approximation|Low-Rank Approximation]] — like truncated [[Singular Value Decomposition|Singular Value Decomposition]] but with non-negativity constraints replacing orthogonality. This makes NMF a member of the broader family that includes PCA, factor analysis, and independent component analysis, each distinguished by the constraints imposed on the factors.

Related topics: [[Dimensionality Reduction|Dimensionality Reduction]], [[Principal Component Analysis|Principal Component Analysis]], [[Latent Dirichlet Allocation|Latent Dirichlet Allocation]], [[Unsupervised Learning|Unsupervised Learning]], [[Low-Rank Approximation|Low-Rank Approximation]], [[Singular Value Decomposition|Singular Value Decomposition]].
