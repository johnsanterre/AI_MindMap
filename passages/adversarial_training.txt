! Adversarial Training

<img src="assets/adversarial_training.svg" alt="Diagram showing the min-max optimization loop of adversarial training with inner maximization and outer minimization" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are preparing for a debate, but instead of practicing with a friendly partner, you hire the most ruthless opponent you can find — someone who will exploit every weakness in your argument. By the time you face the real debate, the friendly questions feel easy. ''Adversarial training'' applies this same logic to machine learning: during training, each example is deliberately corrupted in the worst possible way before the model learns from it. The model never sees clean data — it only ever practices against the hardest version of every input. The result is a model that is not just accurate on typical inputs, but robust to deliberate, worst-case perturbations.

---

!! The Core Insight

Standard training minimizes the average loss over clean training data. This produces models that perform well on typical inputs but shatter when those inputs are nudged by tiny, carefully chosen perturbations — the domain of <<link "Adversarial Attacks" "Adversarial Attacks">><</link>>. The vulnerability arises because standard training never asks the model to handle worst-case inputs; it only optimizes for the average case.

Adversarial training reframes the learning objective as a ''min-max game''. The inner player (the adversary) searches for the perturbation $\delta$ that maximizes the loss on each training example. The outer player (the learner) updates the model parameters $\theta$ to minimize the loss on these worst-case examples. By solving both problems simultaneously, adversarial training produces models whose decision boundaries are pushed away from training points, creating a buffer zone of <<link "Robustness" "Robustness">><</link>> around every correctly classified example.

The key conceptual shift is from asking "what is the expected loss?" to asking "what is the worst loss an adversary could inflict within a budget?" This fundamentally changes the geometry of the learned decision boundary, forcing it to maintain margin even in directions that standard training ignores entirely.

---

!! The Adversary Within: PGD and the Inner Problem

The inner maximization — finding the worst perturbation $\delta^*$ for each input — is itself an optimization problem. The most widely used method is ''Projected Gradient Descent'' (PGD), introduced by Madry et al. (2018). PGD runs multiple steps of <<link "Gradient Descent" "Gradient Descent">><</link>> on the input space (ascending the loss), projecting back onto the allowed perturbation set after each step:

$$\delta^{(t+1)} = \Pi_{\|\delta\| \leq \epsilon}\left(\delta^{(t)} + \alpha \cdot \text{sign}\left(\nabla_{\delta} L(f(x + \delta^{(t)}; \theta), y)\right)\right)$$

Here $\alpha$ is the step size for the inner loop, $\epsilon$ is the perturbation budget (typically measured in $\ell_\infty$ or $\ell_2$ norm), and $\Pi$ denotes projection back onto the $\epsilon$-ball. PGD is essentially FGSM (Fast Gradient Sign Method) applied iteratively with random restarts.

The quality of adversarial training depends critically on how well the inner problem is solved. A weak inner adversary — one that uses too few PGD steps or a single FGSM step — produces a model that is robust against weak attacks but still vulnerable to stronger ones. This failure mode, sometimes called ''obfuscated gradients'', was a major source of false confidence in early adversarial defense papers. Madry et al. showed that 7-10 PGD steps with random restarts are typically sufficient for reliable inner optimization on standard benchmarks.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The adversarial training objective replaces the standard empirical risk minimization (ERM) with ''robust empirical risk minimization'':

$$\min_{\theta} \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \max_{\|\delta_i\| \leq \epsilon} L(f(x_i + \delta_i; \theta), y_i)$$

The outer minimization over $\theta$ adjusts model weights via gradient descent. The inner maximization over $\delta_i$ finds the worst perturbation for each sample. This is a saddle-point problem: we seek a point that is a minimum in $\theta$-space and a maximum in $\delta$-space.

The gradient of the outer objective with respect to $\theta$ is:

$$\nabla_\theta \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} L(f(x_i + \delta_i^*; \theta), y_i)$$

where $\delta_i^*$ is the (approximate) solution to the inner maximization. In practice, this means: (1) run PGD to find $\delta_i^*$ for each training example, (2) compute the loss at the perturbed point $x_i + \delta_i^*$, (3) backpropagate through the model to update $\theta$. The perturbation $\delta_i^*$ is treated as fixed during the outer gradient step — we do not differentiate through the PGD procedure itself.

The connection to <<link "Regularization" "Regularization">><</link>> is deep. Adversarial training can be viewed as a form of robust regularization: it penalizes models whose loss landscape is steep near training points. A model with small $\nabla_x L$ near each training point cannot be easily fooled by small perturbations — and adversarial training directly pressures the model to flatten this landscape. This is related to but distinct from <<link "Data Augmentation" "Data Augmentation">><</link>>, which adds random perturbations rather than worst-case ones.

---

!! The Cost of Robustness

Adversarial training is expensive. Each training step requires solving the inner maximization, which involves $K$ forward-backward passes through the model (typically $K = 7$-$10$). This multiplies training time by roughly $K$. For large models, this overhead is prohibitive without careful engineering.

Several acceleration techniques exist. ''Free adversarial training'' (Shafahi et al., 2019) reuses the gradient computation from the inner loop for the outer parameter update, achieving PGD-level robustness at roughly the cost of standard training. ''FGSM adversarial training'' with random initialization can approach PGD robustness at single-step cost, though it is less reliable.

A second and more fundamental cost is the ''accuracy-robustness tradeoff''. Adversarially trained models consistently achieve lower clean accuracy than their standard counterparts. On CIFAR-10, a standard ResNet achieves ~95% clean accuracy; the same architecture adversarially trained against $\ell_\infty$ perturbations of $\epsilon = 8/255$ drops to ~85% clean accuracy while gaining ~50% robust accuracy. This tradeoff appears to be partly intrinsic: Tsipras et al. (2019) showed theoretical settings where robustness and accuracy are fundamentally at odds. The model must allocate capacity to maintaining margin, which costs standard accuracy.

---

!! When Adversarial Training Breaks Down

''Catastrophic overfitting:'' When using single-step FGSM adversarial training (rather than multi-step PGD), robust accuracy can suddenly collapse to near zero during training. The model learns to exploit the weakness of the single-step adversary — creating sharp, curved loss surfaces that fool FGSM but not stronger attacks. The model appears robust during training but is entirely brittle. This was identified by Wong et al. (2020) and demonstrates that the inner adversary must be strong enough to prevent the model from "cheating" against it.

''Threat model mismatch:'' Adversarial training is tightly coupled to a specific threat model — the norm, the perturbation budget $\epsilon$, and the attack algorithm. A model trained against $\ell_\infty$ perturbations may remain vulnerable to $\ell_2$ attacks, spatial transformations, or other perturbation types outside the training threat model. Robustness does not transfer freely across threat models, and training against the union of multiple threat models is significantly harder than training against any single one.

''Distribution shift amplification:'' While adversarial training improves worst-case robustness within the $\epsilon$-ball, it can amplify sensitivity to natural distribution shifts — changes in lighting, viewpoint, or domain — that were not part of the adversarial threat model. The model specializes in resisting the specific adversary it was trained against, and this specialization can reduce its flexibility on naturally shifted data. This means adversarially trained models are not universally more robust; they are specifically more robust in the trained threat model.

---

!! Technical Details

''Foundational paper:'' Madry et al. (2018), //Towards Deep Learning Models Resistant to Adversarial Attacks//, ICLR. This paper established PGD adversarial training as the gold standard, showing that multi-step PGD with random restarts reliably solves the inner maximization and produces models robust to first-order attacks. The paper also connected adversarial robustness to the theory of robust optimization.

''Perturbation budgets:'' Standard benchmarks use $\ell_\infty$ with $\epsilon = 8/255$ for CIFAR-10 and $\epsilon = 4/255$ for ImageNet. $\ell_2$ budgets of $\epsilon = 0.5$ (CIFAR-10) and $\epsilon = 3.0$ (ImageNet) are also common. These values are chosen to be approximately imperceptible to humans while being large enough to fool standard models.

''TRADES'' (Zhang et al., 2019) decomposes the adversarial training objective into a clean accuracy term and a robustness regularization term: $L_{\text{TRADES}} = L(f(x;\theta), y) + \beta \cdot \max_{\|\delta\| \leq \epsilon} \text{KL}(f(x;\theta) \| f(x+\delta;\theta))$. The hyperparameter $\beta$ explicitly controls the accuracy-robustness tradeoff, offering finer control than standard PGD adversarial training.

''Certified defenses'' go beyond empirical adversarial training by providing mathematical guarantees of robustness. Randomized smoothing (Cohen et al., 2019) certifies $\ell_2$ robustness by averaging predictions over Gaussian noise. Interval bound propagation and abstract interpretation methods certify $\ell_\infty$ robustness by propagating perturbation sets through network layers. These certified methods are complementary to adversarial training — adversarial training provides strong empirical robustness, while certified methods provide provable guarantees at the cost of lower overall accuracy.

''Adversarial training and features:'' Ilyas et al. (2019), //Adversarial Examples Are Not Bugs, They Are Features//, argued that adversarial vulnerability arises because standard models exploit non-robust features — patterns that are genuinely predictive but imperceptible to humans. Adversarial training forces the model to rely on robust features that are semantically meaningful, which explains both the improved robustness and the drop in clean accuracy (since non-robust features are predictive and discarding them hurts performance on clean data).
