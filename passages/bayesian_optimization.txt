! Bayesian Optimization

<img src="assets/bayesian_optimization.svg" alt="Diagram showing the Bayesian Optimization loop: GP surrogate, acquisition function, and sequential evaluation" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are searching for the best restaurant in a new city, but every meal costs $200 and takes three hours. You cannot afford to try every restaurant. Instead, after each meal you update your mental map — which neighborhoods seem promising, which price ranges correlate with quality — and you pick your next dinner to maximize what you learn relative to what you spend. ''Bayesian optimization'' does exactly this for expensive black-box functions: it builds a probabilistic model of the objective, then uses that model to decide where to evaluate next, squeezing maximum information from the fewest possible function evaluations.

---

!! The Core Insight

Many real-world optimization problems involve functions that are expensive to evaluate — training a neural network with a particular set of hyperparameters, running a physics simulation, or conducting a wet-lab experiment. You cannot compute gradients, and each evaluation might take hours or days. Grid search and random search waste budget by ignoring what previous evaluations have revealed. Bayesian optimization is the principled alternative: it treats the unknown objective function as a random function, fits a probabilistic ''surrogate model'' (typically a <<link "Gaussian Processes" "Gaussian Processes">><</link>>) to the observed evaluations, and then uses an ''acquisition function'' to decide which point to evaluate next.

The surrogate model provides two things at every candidate point: a ''predicted value'' (what the function probably returns there) and an ''uncertainty estimate'' (how confident the model is). The acquisition function converts these two quantities into a single score that balances exploitation (querying where the predicted value is best) and exploration (querying where uncertainty is highest). This is a direct instantiation of the <<link "Explore-Exploit Tradeoff" "Explore-Exploit Tradeoff">><</link>> — the same tension that governs multi-armed bandits and reinforcement learning.

The power of the approach comes from the fact that fitting a Gaussian process to a handful of observations is cheap, and optimizing the acquisition function over the surrogate is also cheap. The expensive part — evaluating the true objective — happens only once per iteration, and the surrogate ensures that each evaluation is as informative as possible.

---

!! Surrogate Models and Acquisition Functions

The default surrogate in Bayesian optimization is the <<link "Gaussian process" "Gaussian Processes">><</link>> (GP). A GP defines a distribution over functions: given $n$ observations $(x_1, y_1), \ldots, (x_n, y_n)$, the GP posterior at any new point $x$ is a Gaussian with a closed-form mean $\mu(x)$ and variance $\sigma^2(x)$. The mean interpolates through the data (or nearly so, with observation noise), while the variance is large where data is sparse and small where data is dense. This is exactly the information the acquisition function needs.

The three most common acquisition functions are:

''Expected Improvement (EI):'' Given the current best observed value $f^* = \max_i y_i$, EI asks: in expectation, how much better than $f^*$ will a new evaluation at $x$ be? Points with high predicted mean //and// high uncertainty score well. EI naturally balances exploitation and exploration without a tuning parameter.

''Upper Confidence Bound (UCB):'' UCB scores each point as $\mu(x) + \kappa \sigma(x)$, where $\kappa$ controls the exploration-exploitation balance. A large $\kappa$ favors exploration; a small $\kappa$ favors exploitation. UCB has strong theoretical regret bounds derived from the bandit literature.

''Probability of Improvement (PI):'' PI asks: what is the probability that a new evaluation at $x$ exceeds $f^*$? This is the simplest acquisition function but tends to be too greedy — it favors points just barely above $f^*$ and underexplores.

The choice of acquisition function matters less than the choice of surrogate model. A well-calibrated GP with an appropriate kernel will produce good results with any reasonable acquisition function. A poorly specified kernel — one that assumes the wrong smoothness or length scale — will mislead all acquisition functions equally.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The GP posterior at a new point $x_*$, conditioned on observations $\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{y}$, is:

$$\mu(x_*) = \mathbf{k}_*^T (\mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I})^{-1} \mathbf{y}$$

$$\sigma^2(x_*) = k(x_*, x_*) - \mathbf{k}_*^T (\mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I})^{-1} \mathbf{k}_*$$

where $\mathbf{K}$ is the kernel matrix between observed points, $\mathbf{k}_*$ is the kernel vector between $x_*$ and all observed points, and $\sigma_n^2$ is observation noise. The kernel function $k(x, x')$ encodes assumptions about the function's smoothness and correlation structure. The squared exponential kernel $k(x, x') = \sigma_f^2 \exp\left(-\frac{\|x - x'\|^2}{2\ell^2}\right)$ is the most common choice, with length scale $\ell$ and signal variance $\sigma_f^2$ as hyperparameters fit by maximizing the marginal likelihood.

Expected Improvement has a closed-form expression under the GP posterior. Define the improvement over the current best $f^*$ as $I(x) = \max(0, f(x) - f^*)$. Since the GP posterior at $x$ is Gaussian, the expected improvement is:

$$\text{EI}(x) = (\mu(x) - f^*) \Phi(z) + \sigma(x) \phi(z), \quad z = \frac{\mu(x) - f^*}{\sigma(x)}$$

where $\Phi$ and $\phi$ are the standard normal CDF and PDF. The first term rewards points with high predicted mean (exploitation); the second term rewards points with high uncertainty (exploration). When $\sigma(x) = 0$ (the function is known exactly at $x$), EI is zero — there is nothing to learn. This elegant formula connects Bayesian optimization directly to <<link "Bayes Theorem" "Bayes Theorem">><</link>> through the GP posterior.

UCB is even simpler: $a_{\text{UCB}}(x) = \mu(x) + \kappa \sigma(x)$. The parameter $\kappa$ is often set to $\sqrt{2 \log(t)}$ at iteration $t$, following the GP-UCB algorithm of Srinivas et al. (2010), which achieves sublinear cumulative regret bounds under mild assumptions on the kernel.

---

!! Bayesian Optimization in Practice

The dominant application of Bayesian optimization is <<link "hyperparameter tuning" "Hyperparameter Search">><</link>>. Libraries like Spearmint, BoTorch, and Optuna use Bayesian optimization to search over learning rates, regularization strengths, architecture choices, and other hyperparameters. Compared to grid search (exponential in the number of hyperparameters) and random search (wasteful of budget), Bayesian optimization typically finds good configurations in 10-50 evaluations — a crucial advantage when each evaluation requires training a full model.

Beyond hyperparameters, Bayesian optimization is used in drug discovery (optimizing molecular properties with expensive assays), materials science (tuning alloy compositions), robotics (optimizing controller parameters on physical hardware), and A/B testing (sequentially allocating traffic to the most promising variants).

Practical considerations include: (1) the GP scales as $O(n^3)$ in the number of observations due to matrix inversion, which limits the method to roughly hundreds of evaluations — not a problem for expensive black-box functions but prohibitive for cheap ones; (2) high-dimensional input spaces ($d > 20$) are challenging because the GP's uncertainty does not shrink fast enough; (3) the kernel hyperparameters must be refit at each iteration, and poor kernel choices can doom the search.

<<link "Surrogate models" "Surrogate Models">><</link>> beyond GPs include random forests (used in SMAC) and tree-structured Parzen estimators (TPE, used in Hyperopt). These scale better to high dimensions and categorical variables but lack the GP's principled uncertainty quantification.

---

!! When Bayesian Optimization Breaks Down

''High-dimensional search spaces:'' Bayesian optimization with GPs struggles when the input dimension exceeds roughly 15-20. The GP's uncertainty estimate becomes unreliable because the volume of the space grows exponentially — the surrogate cannot distinguish informative regions from unexplored ones. Random search can actually outperform Bayesian optimization in very high dimensions because it does not waste computation on a misleading surrogate. Techniques like random embeddings (REMBO) and additive kernel decompositions partially mitigate this, but the curse of dimensionality is fundamental.

''Cheap or parallelizable objectives:'' If the objective function is cheap to evaluate (milliseconds per call), the overhead of fitting the GP and optimizing the acquisition function exceeds the cost of simply evaluating the objective. Random search or gradient-based methods are better choices. Similarly, if the objective can be evaluated in massive parallel batches, the sequential nature of Bayesian optimization becomes a bottleneck. Batch acquisition functions exist but sacrifice much of the method's sample efficiency.

''Highly noisy or discontinuous objectives:'' The GP assumes a smooth, continuous function (controlled by the kernel). If the true objective has sharp discontinuities, plateaus, or extreme noise, the GP posterior will be badly miscalibrated — the uncertainty bands will be in the wrong places, and the acquisition function will make poor decisions. In such cases, tree-based surrogates or evolutionary strategies that make weaker smoothness assumptions can be more robust.

!! Technical Details

''Key papers:'' Jones, Schonlau, and Welch (1998), //Efficient Global Optimization of Expensive Black-Box Functions//, introduced the EGO algorithm using Expected Improvement with Gaussian process surrogates, establishing the modern Bayesian optimization framework. Srinivas, Krause, Kakade, and Seeger (2010), //Gaussian Process Optimization in the Bandit Setting//, provided the GP-UCB algorithm with cumulative regret bounds of $O(\sqrt{T \gamma_T})$ where $\gamma_T$ is the maximum information gain, connecting Bayesian optimization to information-theoretic analysis. Shahriari, Swersky, Wang, Adams, and de Freitas (2016), //Taking the Human Out of the Loop//, provided a comprehensive survey of Bayesian optimization methods and applications.

''Kernel choice'' determines the inductive bias. The squared exponential (RBF) kernel assumes infinite differentiability; the Mat&eacute;rn-5/2 kernel $k(x,x') = \sigma_f^2 \left(1 + \frac{\sqrt{5}r}{\ell} + \frac{5r^2}{3\ell^2}\right) \exp\left(-\frac{\sqrt{5}r}{\ell}\right)$ (where $r = \|x - x'\|$) assumes twice-differentiable functions, which is more realistic for most machine learning objectives. Mat&eacute;rn-5/2 is the default in most modern implementations.

''Marginal likelihood optimization'' for kernel hyperparameters involves maximizing $\log p(\mathbf{y} | \mathbf{X}) = -\frac{1}{2} \mathbf{y}^T \mathbf{K}_y^{-1} \mathbf{y} - \frac{1}{2} \log |\mathbf{K}_y| - \frac{n}{2} \log 2\pi$ where $\mathbf{K}_y = \mathbf{K} + \sigma_n^2 \mathbf{I}$. This is typically done via L-BFGS with multiple restarts. The first term penalizes data misfit; the second penalizes model complexity (an automatic Occam's razor).

''Multi-fidelity Bayesian optimization'' uses cheap approximations (e.g., training for fewer epochs, using a subset of data) to inform the surrogate before committing to expensive full evaluations. Knowledge gradient and entropy search are alternative acquisition functions that directly optimize the expected reduction in uncertainty about the global optimum rather than the expected improvement at a single point. These methods are more computationally expensive per iteration but can be more sample-efficient.

The ''no free lunch'' connection: Bayesian optimization's advantage comes entirely from the prior (the kernel). If the kernel matches the true function's smoothness, BO is provably near-optimal. If the kernel is wrong, BO can be worse than random search. This makes kernel selection the single most important design decision.
