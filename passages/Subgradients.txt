! Subgradients
<img src="assets/subgradients.svg" alt="Subdifferential of f(x)=|x| showing multiple supporting hyperplanes at the kink" style="max-width:100%;margin:1em 0;">

Imagine you're hiking downhill and the trail is smooth — at every point you can feel exactly which direction is steepest. Now imagine the trail has a sharp V-shaped valley. Standing right at the bottom of the V, there's no single "steepest" direction — any direction between the two valley walls counts as "downhill enough." A ''subgradient'' is any of those valid downhill directions. Where a smooth function has one gradient, a non-smooth function has a whole //set// of subgradients, and optimization still works as long as you pick any one of them.

!! The Core Insight

A ''subgradient'' $g$ of a [[Convex|convex]] function $f$ at a point $x$ is any vector that defines a supporting hyperplane that touches the function at $x$ and never rises above it anywhere. Smooth convex functions have exactly one subgradient at each point (the ordinary gradient). Non-smooth convex functions — which appear constantly in machine learning — have //multiple// subgradients at their kinks. The set of all subgradients at a point is called the ''subdifferential'' $\partial f(x)$, and it is always a convex, compact set. This machinery lets us optimize functions that classical calculus cannot differentiate.

See also: [[Convex Optimization]], [[Gradient Descent]], [[Loss Functions]], [[Regularization]]

!! Beyond Smooth Functions

Most textbook optimization assumes smooth, differentiable objectives. But machine learning is full of functions with kinks, corners, and flat regions that violate this assumption:

- ''L1 / [[Lasso]] regularization:'' $\|w\|_1 = \sum |w_i|$ is non-differentiable whenever any $w_i = 0$. This is exactly the sparsity-inducing point — the most important one.
- ''[[Activation Functions|ReLU]] activations:'' $\max(0, x)$ has a kink at $x = 0$, and every neuron in a ReLU network hits this kink during training.
- ''[[Hinge Loss]]:'' $\max(0, 1 - y \hat{y})$ used in SVMs is piecewise linear with a corner at $y\hat{y} = 1$.
- ''Max functions:'' $\max(f_1(x), f_2(x), \ldots)$ appears in robust optimization, minimax games, and piecewise models.

Without subgradients, we would have no principled way to train sparse models, ReLU networks, or SVMs. Subgradient theory provides the mathematical foundation that makes non-smooth optimization rigorous.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''Definition:'' A vector $g$ is a subgradient of a convex function $f$ at $x$ if for all $y$:

$$f(y) \geq f(x) + g^T(y - x)$$

This says the linear approximation $f(x) + g^T(y - x)$ is a global //underestimator// of $f$ — a tangent hyperplane that never crosses above the function.

''Subdifferential:'' The set of all subgradients at $x$ is:

$$\partial f(x) = \{g \mid f(y) \geq f(x) + g^T(y - x) \;\; \forall \, y\}$$

If $f$ is differentiable at $x$, then $\partial f(x) = \{\nabla f(x)\}$ — a singleton. Otherwise it is a convex set.

''Example — absolute value:'' For $f(x) = |x|$:
- At $x > 0$: $\partial f(x) = \{1\}$
- At $x < 0$: $\partial f(x) = \{-1\}$
- At $x = 0$: $\partial f(x) = [-1, 1]$ — every value in this interval is a valid subgradient

''Example — ReLU:'' For $f(x) = \max(0, x)$:
- At $x > 0$: $\partial f(x) = \{1\}$
- At $x < 0$: $\partial f(x) = \{0\}$
- At $x = 0$: $\partial f(x) = [0, 1]$

''Example — L1 norm:'' For $f(w) = \|w\|_1$, the subdifferential is componentwise:

$$[\partial f(w)]_i = \begin{cases} \{1\} & w_i > 0 \\ \{-1\} & w_i < 0 \\ [-1, 1] & w_i = 0 \end{cases}$$

This is the ''sign function'' extended to a set-valued map at zero, and it is the reason [[Lasso]] produces exactly sparse solutions — the subgradient condition at $w_i = 0$ can be satisfied without moving $w_i$ away from zero.

!! Subgradient Methods in Practice

''Subgradient descent:'' The simplest approach replaces the gradient with any subgradient $g_t \in \partial f(x_t)$:

$$x_{t+1} = x_t - \alpha_t \, g_t$$

Unlike gradient descent, subgradient descent does //not// guarantee $f(x_{t+1}) < f(x_t)$, so we track $x_{\text{best}} = \arg\min_{s \leq t} f(x_s)$. Convergence rate for a fixed step size $\alpha$: $O(1/\sqrt{T})$ — significantly slower than the $O(1/T)$ rate of smooth [[Gradient Descent]]. The step size must decrease ($\alpha_t \to 0$, $\sum \alpha_t = \infty$) for convergence to the optimum.

''Proximal operators:'' A far more powerful approach. The ''proximal operator'' of $f$ is:

$$\text{prox}_{\alpha f}(v) = \arg\min_x \left\{ f(x) + \frac{1}{2\alpha}\|x - v\|^2 \right\}$$

For $f(x) = \|x\|_1$, this is the ''soft-thresholding operator'' $S_\alpha(v)_i = \text{sign}(v_i)\max(|v_i| - \alpha, 0)$. Proximal gradient descent splits the objective into smooth $g$ + non-smooth $h$ and alternates: gradient step on $g$, then proximal step on $h$. This achieves the $O(1/T)$ rate of smooth methods.

''ADMM (Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers):'' Decomposes composite problems into subproblems that can each be solved with proximal operators. Widely used for distributed optimization, compressed sensing, and large-scale [[Regularization|regularized]] models.

''Convergence comparison:''
- Subgradient methods: $O(1/\sqrt{T})$
- Proximal gradient: $O(1/T)$
- Accelerated proximal (FISTA): $O(1/T^2)$

!! When Subgradient Methods Break Down

- ''Slow convergence with raw subgradient descent:'' The $O(1/\sqrt{T})$ rate means subgradient descent needs 100x more iterations to gain one extra digit of precision compared to smooth gradient descent. For large-scale problems, this is prohibitive — always prefer proximal methods or ADMM when the proximal operator is available in closed form.

- ''Step size sensitivity:'' Unlike gradient descent, subgradient descent is not a descent method — the function value oscillates. Choosing the step size schedule $\alpha_t$ is critical and problem-dependent. Too large causes divergence; too small wastes iterations. There is no analog of line search for non-smooth functions.

- ''Nonconvex non-smooth functions:'' The classical subgradient theory assumes convexity. For nonconvex problems (e.g., deep networks with ReLU), the subdifferential may be empty or ill-behaved. Practitioners rely on the ''Clarke subdifferential'' or simply set the gradient to a conventional value (e.g., 0 at the ReLU kink), which works in practice but lacks the clean theoretical guarantees of the convex case.

!! Technical Details

''Moreau envelope:'' The Moreau envelope $M_\alpha f(x) = \min_z \{f(z) + \frac{1}{2\alpha}\|z-x\|^2\}$ is a smooth approximation to any convex $f$. Its gradient is $\nabla M_\alpha f(x) = \frac{1}{\alpha}(x - \text{prox}_{\alpha f}(x))$. This connects proximal methods to smooth gradient descent on a modified landscape.

''Proximal gradient descent:'' For $\min_x \, g(x) + h(x)$ where $g$ is smooth and $h$ is non-smooth:

$$x_{t+1} = \text{prox}_{\alpha h}\!\left(x_t - \alpha \nabla g(x_t)\right)$$

This is the workhorse of modern sparse optimization: ISTA for [[Lasso]], nuclear norm minimization for matrix completion, total variation for image denoising.

''FISTA (Fast ISTA):'' Beck & Teboulle 2009 added Nesterov-style momentum to proximal gradient descent, achieving the optimal $O(1/T^2)$ rate for non-smooth composite objectives. Sequence: $y_{t+1} = x_t + \frac{t-1}{t+2}(x_t - x_{t-1})$; $x_{t+1} = \text{prox}_{\alpha h}(y_{t+1} - \alpha \nabla g(y_{t+1}))$.

''Clarke subdifferential:'' For locally Lipschitz nonconvex functions, Clarke 1983 defined the subdifferential as the convex hull of all limit points of gradients at nearby differentiable points: $\partial_C f(x) = \text{conv}\{\lim \nabla f(x_k) : x_k \to x, \, f \text{ differentiable at } x_k\}$. This extends subgradient theory to the nonconvex functions used in deep learning.

''Danskin's theorem:'' For $f(x) = \max_{z \in Z} \phi(x,z)$, the subdifferential is $\partial f(x) = \text{conv}\{\nabla_x \phi(x, z^*) : z^* \in \arg\max_z \phi(x,z)\}$. This is the foundation for minimax optimization and adversarial training.
