! Process Reward Models
<img src="assets/process_reward_models.svg" alt="Diagram contrasting Outcome Reward Models with Process Reward Models, showing per-step rewards vs a single final reward" style="max-width:100%;margin:1em 0;">

Think of two math teachers. The first only marks the final answer: correct or incorrect. If you wrote three steps of working and made an error in step two, she gives you a zero with no further comment. The second teacher marks each line of working as you go, circling exactly where the reasoning went wrong. A ''process reward model'' (PRM) is the second teacher — it assigns a reward to every step of a reasoning chain, not just the final answer.

!! The Core Insight

''Process Reward Models'' assign a scalar reward to each intermediate step of a multi-step reasoning chain. For tasks like mathematics, formal proofs, and code generation, a model can produce a wrong final answer through a chain of steps where only one early step is incorrect — yet an outcome reward model (ORM) gives the same zero reward regardless of whether the error was in step 2 or step 50. PRMs enable: (1) a denser training signal for reinforcement learning, (2) best-of-N search that selects solutions by step-level quality rather than just final answer, and (3) automatic verification that identifies the //first// wrong step in a chain. Links: [[Reward Modeling]], [[RLHF]], [[Chain of Thought]], [[Large Language Models]], [[Reinforcement Learning]].

!! ORM vs PRM

''Outcome Reward Model (ORM):'' assigns a single reward $r(x, y)$ to the complete solution $y$ given problem $x$. For math benchmarks this is usually binary — final answer is correct or incorrect. Pros: labels are cheap (just check the answer). Cons: cannot distinguish a correct answer reached via flawed reasoning from one reached correctly; cannot locate the error step; provides sparse signal for RL.

''Process Reward Model (PRM):'' assigns a reward $r_t(x, y_1, \ldots, y_t)$ to each step $y_t$ given the problem and all prior steps. The key question at each step: //is this step valid given what came before?// Pros: dense supervision, identifies the first wrong step, richer training signal. Cons: requires step-level labels — either expensive human annotation or automated methods.

Step-level labels can be:
- ''Human annotations'' — raters mark each step as positive, negative, or neutral (Lightman et al. 2023, PRM800K dataset).
- ''Automated Monte Carlo labels'' — for each step, sample many completions to the end and estimate the probability of reaching a correct final answer (Wang et al. 2024, Math-Shepherd).

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The PRM defines a scorer for each step:

$$r_t = f_\theta(x, y_1, \ldots, y_t) \in [0, 1]$$

To score an entire solution for best-of-N selection, two aggregation strategies are common:

$$R(y) = \min_t\, r_t \qquad \text{(minimum step score — any bad step fails the solution)}$$

$$R(y) = \prod_t r_t \qquad \text{(product — penalizes each imperfect step)}$$

''Math-Shepherd automated labeling'' (Wang et al. 2024): for each partial solution $(x, y_1, \ldots, y_t)$, sample $K$ independent completions and check what fraction reach a correct final answer. This gives a Monte Carlo estimate:

$$r_t = \hat{\mathbb{P}}[\text{correct final answer} \mid x, y_1, \ldots, y_t] = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K \mathbf{1}[\text{completion}_k \text{ correct}]$$

This bootstrap approach allows PRM training at scale without human step-level annotation.

!! Applications: Verification and RLHF

''Best-of-N verification:'' sample $N$ solutions from the language model, score each with the PRM using $R(y) = \min_t r_t$ or the product, select the highest-scoring solution. Lightman et al. 2023 demonstrated PRM best-of-N reaching ~96% accuracy on the MATH dataset using the PRM800K annotations.

''RLHF with dense rewards:'' replace the sparse outcome signal with per-step PRM rewards during policy gradient training. The denser signal reduces variance and speeds convergence compared to ORM-based RL, particularly on long reasoning chains where credit assignment over many steps is difficult.

''Test-time compute scaling:'' PRMs are a key component of o1-style inference — generate many chains, use the PRM to allocate more compute to promising partial solutions (beam search, MCTS), and verify outputs. Snell et al. 2024 show that scaling test-time compute with a PRM verifier can outperform simply scaling model size.

''First-error detection:'' step through a solution and report the earliest step where $r_t$ drops below a threshold — a practical debugging tool for math tutoring systems.

!! When Process Reward Models Break Down

''Step granularity ambiguity:'' there is no canonical way to segment a reasoning chain into steps. Different annotators split steps differently, and a PRM trained on one granularity may not transfer to another. A model can also write many small trivial steps to inflate its PRM score.

''Reward hacking:'' because the PRM is itself a learned model, the policy can learn to produce reasoning that //looks// correct to the PRM — well-formatted, plausible-sounding steps — without the logic actually being valid. The PRM becomes a proxy that diverges from true correctness.

''Non-sequential and tree-structured reasoning:'' PRMs assume reasoning is a linear sequence of steps. Tasks with parallel subproblems, recursive structure, or branching logic (e.g., proof by cases, backtracking search) do not fit the left-to-right step model, and step-level rewards become ill-defined.

!! Technical Details

Key papers: Lightman et al. 2023 //Let's Verify Step by Step// (OpenAI PRM800K — 800K human step annotations, PRM vs ORM at scale on MATH); Wang et al. 2024 //Math-Shepherd// (automated Monte Carlo step labels eliminating need for human annotation); Luo et al. 2024 (DeepSeek process supervision); Snell et al. 2024 (scaling test-time compute with process reward models as verifiers).

Implementation details: step-level reward is typically obtained by appending a special token after each step and reading off the value head at that position, rather than training a separate model per step. Token-level vs step-level reward granularity is an active research question. ORM and PRM perform comparably on simple single-step tasks; PRM advantage grows with chain length and task complexity. Process supervision is widely believed to underlie o1-style chain-of-thought reasoning models.
