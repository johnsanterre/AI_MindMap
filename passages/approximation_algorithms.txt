! Approximation Algorithms

<img src="assets/approximation_algorithms.svg" alt="Diagram showing approximation ratio guarantee versus optimal solution for NP-hard problems" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

When a problem is NP-hard, you cannot find the exact optimal solution in polynomial time (assuming P≠NP). But often you don't need perfect — you need good enough, fast. An ''approximation algorithm'' is a polynomial-time algorithm that guarantees its output is within a known factor of optimal. A 2-approximation for vertex cover means: whatever the true minimum is, you'll find a solution at most twice as large. This is the engineer's answer to intractability: trade perfection for speed, but keep a guarantee on how wrong you can be.

!! The Core Insight

The central concept is the ''approximation ratio'' $\rho$: for a minimization problem, an algorithm's output is guaranteed to satisfy $\text{ALG} \leq \rho \cdot \text{OPT}$, where $\text{OPT}$ is the cost of the true optimal solution. A $\rho$-approximation runs in polynomial time and provides a hard upper bound on how far from optimal you can land.

A ''PTAS'' (polynomial-time approximation scheme) goes further: for any $\varepsilon > 0$, it delivers a $(1 + \varepsilon)$-approximation in time polynomial in $n$ (though possibly exponential in $1/\varepsilon$). An ''FPTAS'' (fully polynomial-time approximation scheme) is also polynomial in $1/\varepsilon$, making it tractable even for very small $\varepsilon$. The hierarchy runs: exact → FPTAS → PTAS → constant-factor → logarithmic → inapproximable.

!! Classic Algorithms and Techniques

''Greedy algorithms'' are the simplest source of approximations. The ''set cover'' problem — cover all elements using the fewest sets — admits a greedy algorithm that always picks the set covering the most uncovered elements. This gives an $O(\log n)$-approximation, and the PCP theorem shows no polynomial-time algorithm can do asymptotically better unless P=NP.

''Vertex cover'' (find the smallest set of vertices touching every edge) yields to a simple greedy matching: repeatedly pick an edge, add both endpoints to the cover, delete all incident edges. This 2-approximation is elegant and tight — no polynomial-time $(2 - \varepsilon)$-approximation is known.

''Travelling Salesman'' with the triangle inequality admits the ''Christofides algorithm'' (1976): build a minimum spanning tree, find a minimum-weight perfect matching on odd-degree vertices, Eulerianize, shortcut. Ratio: $3/2$. This remained the best known for over 40 years until Karlin et al. 2021 achieved $3/2 - \varepsilon$ for a tiny $\varepsilon$.

''Semidefinite programming (SDP) relaxations'' power the ''Goemans-Williamson'' algorithm for MAX-CUT (1995): round a vector SDP solution by a random hyperplane. Approximation ratio: $\approx 0.878 \cdot \text{OPT}$. Under the Unique Games Conjecture, this is optimal.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''LP relaxation'' is the workhorse technique. Take an integer program (combinatorial problem), relax the integrality constraints (allow $x_i \in [0,1]$ instead of $\{0,1\}$), solve the LP in polynomial time, then //round// fractional solutions back to integers. The rounding scheme determines the approximation ratio.

The ''integrality gap'' of an LP relaxation is:

$$\text{integrality gap} = \max_{\text{instances}} \frac{\text{OPT}_{\text{LP}}}{\text{OPT}_{\text{IP}}}$$

This is a fundamental barrier: no LP-rounding algorithm can achieve a ratio better than the integrality gap, no matter how clever the rounding.

''Dual fitting'' provides another proof technique: show that the dual LP solution produced implicitly by the algorithm has value close to the primal LP, bounding the algorithm's cost against $\text{OPT}$.

The ''PCP theorem'' (probabilistically checkable proofs, Arora et al. 1998) revolutionized inapproximability: it implies that approximating many problems beyond a constant factor is NP-hard. For example, MAX-3SAT cannot be approximated beyond $7/8$ unless P=NP. The ''Unique Games Conjecture'' (Khot 2002) is a stronger hypothesis implying optimality of many known algorithms — the SDP-based $0.878$ for MAX-CUT, the $2$-approximation for vertex cover, and others.

!! Approximation in ML

Approximation ideas pervade modern machine learning, often implicitly.

''k-means clustering'' is NP-hard to solve optimally. The ''k-means++'' initialization gives an $O(\log k)$-approximation in expectation. Standard Lloyd's algorithm has no worst-case guarantee but performs well in practice — approximation theory motivates the initialization.

''Approximate nearest neighbors'' (HNSW, LSH) trade exactness for speed. These are explicit approximation algorithms: they return a point within $(1 + \varepsilon)$ of the true nearest neighbor's distance, in sublinear time — essential for embedding search at scale.

''Approximate attention'' in transformers (Linformer, Performer, FlashAttention variants) give $\varepsilon$-approximations to full $O(n^2)$ attention, enabling linear-time sequence modeling. The approximation is to the attention matrix, not directly to model output.

''Variational inference'': the exact posterior $p(\mathbf{z} \mid \mathbf{x})$ is intractable; variational methods find the best approximation $q \approx p$ within a tractable family by minimizing KL divergence. This is approximation in the information-geometric sense rather than combinatorial, but the philosophy is identical.

''Pruning and quantization'' approximate a large neural network with a smaller one, trading accuracy for compute. The approximation ratio here is informal — empirical rather than guaranteed.

''Greedy decoding'' in language models selects the highest-probability token at each step. This is a 1-approximation per step but globally suboptimal; beam search improves the approximation at higher cost.

!! When Approximation Algorithms Break Down

''Inapproximability.'' Some problems cannot be approximated within //any// constant factor (let alone a small one) unless P=NP. Graph coloring (chromatic number), shortest common superstring, and densest subgraph all have strong inapproximability lower bounds. The PCP theorem gives tight results: for MAX-3SAT, no algorithm exceeds $7/8$; for set cover, no algorithm beats $\ln n$. These are //provable// walls — not engineering failures, but mathematical limits.

''Worst-case vs. average-case.'' Approximation ratios are worst-case guarantees. On the typical instances arising in ML — clustered data, low-rank structure, natural language — heuristics routinely outperform their worst-case guarantees by orders of magnitude. A poor approximation ratio in theory (say, $O(\log n)$ for set cover) may be completely irrelevant in practice, where the algorithm finds near-optimal solutions on real data. Theoretical guarantees and empirical performance are not the same thing.

''The ratio doesn't capture what matters.'' Minimizing a combinatorial objective within a factor of 2 may not correspond to good ML performance. The approximation target — a combinatorial loss like vertex cover size — is often a //proxy// for the real objective (generalization error, calibration, downstream task performance). Even a 1-approximation (exact solution) to the wrong objective produces a bad model. Approximation algorithms answer the question you ask, not necessarily the question you care about.

!! Technical Details

''Standard reference:'' Hochbaum (1996), //Approximation Algorithms for NP-Hard Problems//. Vazirani (2001), //Approximation Algorithms// (Springer).

''Key results:'' PCP theorem — Arora, Lund, Motwani, Sudan, Szegedy (1998). Unique Games Conjecture — Khot (2002). Christofides algorithm — Christofides (1976); improved — Karlin, Klein, Oveis Gharan (2021). Goemans-Williamson MAX-CUT — Goemans & Williamson (1995).

''Complexity:'' PTAS and FPTAS are complexity classes. APX is the class of problems with constant-factor polynomial-time approximations. APX-hard problems (under PTAS reductions) have no PTAS unless P=NP.

''LP integrality gaps:'' Vertex cover LP has integrality gap 2 (matching the best known algorithm). Metric TSP LP (Held-Karp) has gap between 4/3 (conjectured) and 3/2.
