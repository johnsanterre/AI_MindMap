! ELBO
<img src="assets/elbo.svg" alt="Diagram showing log evidence decomposed into ELBO plus KL divergence gap, with reconstruction and regularization components" style="max-width:100%;margin:1em 0;">

Imagine you want to judge how well a factory produces a particular product, but you can only see the finished items — never the internal assembly steps. You know the factory uses hidden intermediate parts (latent variables), but inspecting every possible assembly pathway is impossibly expensive. The ''Evidence Lower Bound'' (ELBO) is like hiring an inspector who follows a //simplified// map of the assembly process and gives you a guaranteed-pessimistic score: the real quality is always at least as high as what the inspector reports. By improving the inspector's score, you push toward a better factory — without ever needing to audit every hidden pathway.

!! The Core Insight

We want to maximize the ''log evidence'' $\log p(x)$ — the probability our model assigns to observed data $x$. But computing $p(x) = \int p(x,z)\,dz$ requires integrating over all possible values of the ''latent variables'' $z$, which is almost always intractable.

The ELBO gives us a computable lower bound on $\log p(x)$ that we //can// optimize. The trick is to introduce an approximate posterior $q(z|x)$ and use Jensen's inequality (or equivalently, the non-negativity of KL divergence) to show:

$$\log p(x) \;\geq\; \mathbb{E}_{q(z|x)}\!\big[\log p(x,z) - \log q(z|x)\big] \;=\; \text{ELBO}$$

The gap between $\log p(x)$ and the ELBO is exactly $D_{\text{KL}}\!\big(q(z|x)\,\|\,p(z|x)\big)$ — the [[KL Divergence]] between our approximate posterior and the true (unknown) posterior. When $q$ perfectly matches $p(z|x)$, the bound is tight.

!! Deriving the ELBO

Start from log evidence and introduce $q(z|x)$:

$$\log p(x) = \log \int p(x,z)\,dz = \log \int q(z|x)\,\frac{p(x,z)}{q(z|x)}\,dz$$

By ''Jensen's inequality'' (since $\log$ is concave):

$$\log p(x) \;\geq\; \int q(z|x)\,\log\frac{p(x,z)}{q(z|x)}\,dz \;=\; \text{ELBO}$$

Alternatively, we can derive this from the KL decomposition. Since $p(z|x) = p(x,z)/p(x)$:

$$D_{\text{KL}}\!\big(q(z|x)\,\|\,p(z|x)\big) = \mathbb{E}_{q}\!\Big[\log q(z|x) - \log p(z|x)\Big] = \mathbb{E}_{q}\!\Big[\log q(z|x) - \log p(x,z)\Big] + \log p(x)$$

Rearranging: $\log p(x) = \text{ELBO} + D_{\text{KL}}\!\big(q(z|x)\,\|\,p(z|x)\big)$. Since $D_{\text{KL}} \geq 0$, the ELBO is a lower bound.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ELBO decomposes into two interpretable terms:

$$\text{ELBO} = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q(z|x)}\!\big[\log p(x|z)\big]}_{\text{reconstruction term}} \;-\; \underbrace{D_{\text{KL}}\!\big(q(z|x)\,\|\,p(z)\big)}_{\text{regularization term}}$$

The ''reconstruction term'' $\mathbb{E}_{q(z|x)}[\log p(x|z)]$ measures how well latent codes sampled from $q$ can reconstruct the observed data $x$ through the decoder $p(x|z)$. Maximizing this encourages the model to encode enough information in $z$ to faithfully reproduce the data.

The ''regularization term'' $-D_{\text{KL}}(q(z|x)\,\|\,p(z))$ penalizes the encoder $q(z|x)$ for deviating from the prior $p(z)$. When $p(z) = \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ and $q(z|x) = \mathcal{N}(\mu(x), \sigma^2(x)I)$, this KL has a closed-form solution:

$$D_{\text{KL}} = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{j=1}^{d}\!\Big(\mu_j^2 + \sigma_j^2 - \log \sigma_j^2 - 1\Big)$$

These two terms are in tension: the reconstruction term wants $q(z|x)$ to be informative (encode everything about $x$), while the regularization term wants $q(z|x)$ to stay close to a simple prior (forget $x$). This tension is the core tradeoff in [[Variational Autoencoder]] training.

!! ELBO in Practice

In a ''VAE'' (Kingma & Welling, 2014), the ELBO is optimized jointly over encoder parameters $\phi$ (for $q_\phi(z|x)$) and decoder parameters $\theta$ (for $p_\theta(x|z)$). The ''reparameterization trick'' makes this possible: sample $z = \mu_\phi(x) + \sigma_\phi(x) \odot \varepsilon$ with $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I)$, so gradients flow through $\mu$ and $\sigma$ via backpropagation.

''Importance-weighted ELBO'' (IWAE, Burda et al. 2016) tightens the bound using $K$ samples:

$$\mathcal{L}_K = \mathbb{E}_{z_1,\ldots,z_K \sim q}\!\left[\log \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K} \frac{p(x,z_k)}{q(z_k|x)}\right]$$

As $K \to \infty$, $\mathcal{L}_K \to \log p(x)$. In practice, $K = 5{-}50$ gives meaningful improvement.

''Beta-VAE'' (Higgins et al. 2017) introduces a weight $\beta$ on the KL term: $\mathcal{L}_\beta = \mathbb{E}_q[\log p(x|z)] - \beta \cdot D_{\text{KL}}(q(z|x)\,\|\,p(z))$. Setting $\beta > 1$ encourages disentangled representations at the cost of reconstruction quality.

!! When ELBO Optimization Breaks Down

''Posterior collapse:'' The decoder $p_\theta(x|z)$ becomes so powerful that it ignores $z$ entirely. The encoder collapses to the prior ($q(z|x) \approx p(z)$), the KL term goes to zero, and the latent space carries no information. This is common with autoregressive decoders (e.g., PixelCNN, Transformer). Mitigations include KL annealing, free bits, and delta-VAE.

''Approximate posterior gap:'' If the family of distributions for $q(z|x)$ is too restrictive (e.g., diagonal Gaussian when the true posterior is multimodal), the KL gap $D_{\text{KL}}(q\,\|\,p(z|x))$ stays large, and the ELBO becomes a loose bound. Normalizing flows, hierarchical posteriors, and auxiliary VAEs help tighten it.

''Reconstruction-regularization conflict:'' With high-dimensional data, the reconstruction term can overwhelm the KL term by orders of magnitude, or vice versa. This leads to either blurry outputs (over-regularized) or latent spaces with no structure (under-regularized). Careful tuning of $\beta$, the likelihood model (Gaussian vs. Bernoulli), and the latent dimensionality is required.

!! Technical Details

''Origin:'' The ELBO was introduced in the context of variational EM by Jordan et al. (1999) and popularized in deep learning by the VAE paper (Kingma & Welling 2014; Rezende et al. 2014).

''Tighter bounds:'' IWAE (Burda et al. 2016) uses importance weighting; IWAE with resampling (Cremer et al. 2017) further reduces variance. Thermodynamic variational objectives (Masrani et al. 2019) use annealed importance sampling for arbitrarily tight bounds.

''Connection to EM:'' The EM algorithm can be seen as coordinate ascent on the ELBO — the E-step optimizes $q$ (tightens the bound), the M-step optimizes $\theta$ (raises the bound). When $q = p(z|x)$ exactly, the E-step makes the bound tight.

''Amortized inference:'' Classical [[Variational Inference]] optimizes a separate $q$ for each data point. VAEs ''amortize'' this by training a single neural network $q_\phi(z|x)$ that outputs variational parameters for any input $x$. This is faster but introduces an amortization gap.

''Score function estimator vs. reparameterization:'' For continuous $z$, the reparameterization trick gives low-variance gradients. For discrete $z$, one must use REINFORCE-style estimators (high variance) or continuous relaxations like Gumbel-Softmax.
