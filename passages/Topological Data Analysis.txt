! Topological Data Analysis
<img src="assets/topological_data_analysis.svg" alt="Topological Data Analysis" width="800">

Imagine you are handed a ball of tangled Christmas lights. You do not care about the exact position of each bulb — you care about which wires loop around each other, which clusters of bulbs are connected, and whether there is a knot you need to undo. ''Topological Data Analysis'' (TDA) does the same thing for data: it ignores precise coordinates and instead asks //what shape does this data have?// A ring of data points stays a ring whether you stretch, rotate, or gently squash it. TDA captures exactly those structural features — connected pieces, loops, cavities — that survive continuous deformation, giving you a signature of your data that is robust to noise and invariant to the coordinate system you happened to choose.

!! The Core Insight

Traditional methods like [[Clustering]] and [[Dimensionality Reduction]] summarize data by distances and variances. TDA adds a fundamentally different lens: ''topology'', the study of properties that do not change under continuous transformation. A coffee mug and a donut are topologically identical (both have exactly one hole), while a sphere and a donut are not. By building a family of simplicial complexes at increasing scales and tracking which topological features ''persist'' across scales, TDA separates genuine structure from noise. Features that persist over a wide range of scales are real; features that flicker in and out are artifacts. This multi-scale perspective is the key idea, and it is formalized through ''persistent homology''.

!! Shape of Data

The pipeline begins with a ''filtration'' — a nested sequence of spaces built from your point cloud at increasing distance thresholds. At each threshold $r$, you connect points that are within distance $r$ of each other, forming a [[Topology|simplicial complex]]: vertices become edges, edges fill into triangles, triangles into tetrahedra.

As $r$ grows, topological features are ''born'' and ''die'':
* At small $r$, every point is its own connected component — many features of dimension 0.
* As $r$ increases, components merge (some $H_0$ features die), and loops may appear ($H_1$ features are born).
* At still larger $r$, loops get filled in (those $H_1$ features die), and cavities ($H_2$) may form and collapse.

The ''Betti numbers'' count these features at each snapshot: $\beta_0$ counts connected components, $\beta_1$ counts independent loops, and $\beta_2$ counts enclosed voids. But the real power is in tracking how Betti numbers //change// across the filtration — that is what ''persistent homology'' records.

The output is a ''persistence diagram'': a scatter plot where each feature is a point $(b, d)$ with birth time $b$ and death time $d$. Points far from the diagonal $b = d$ represent long-lived, significant features. Points near the diagonal are topological noise. Equivalently, a ''persistence barcode'' draws each feature as a horizontal bar from birth to death — long bars are signal, short bars are noise.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Given a finite point cloud $X \subset \mathbb{R}^n$, we construct the ''Vietoris-Rips complex'' $\text{VR}(X, r)$ at scale $r$: a $k$-simplex $[x_0, \ldots, x_k]$ is included whenever all pairwise distances satisfy $d(x_i, x_j) \leq r$. As $r$ increases, we get an inclusion of complexes:

$$\text{VR}(X, r_1) \hookrightarrow \text{VR}(X, r_2) \hookrightarrow \cdots \hookrightarrow \text{VR}(X, r_m)$$

Applying the $k$-th homology functor $H_k(\ \cdot\ ; \mathbb{F})$ over a field $\mathbb{F}$ yields a sequence of vector spaces connected by linear maps — a ''persistence module''. The structure theorem decomposes this module into a collection of intervals $[b_i, d_i)$, each representing the birth and death of a topological feature.

The Betti numbers at scale $r$ are:

$$\beta_k(r) = \dim H_k(\text{VR}(X, r); \mathbb{F})$$

The critical guarantee is the ''Stability Theorem'': if $f$ and $g$ are two filtration functions (e.g., built from two nearby point clouds), then the bottleneck distance between their persistence diagrams is bounded:

$$d_B(D_f, D_g) \leq \|f - g\|_\infty$$

This means small perturbations in the data produce small changes in the persistence diagram — TDA is ''provably robust'' to noise.

!! TDA in Practice

''Persistence diagrams as feature vectors:'' Since raw diagrams live in an awkward metric space, practitioners convert them into vectors via ''persistence landscapes'', ''persistence images'', or ''Betti curves'', which can then be fed into standard ML pipelines — SVMs, random forests, or [[Unsupervised Learning|unsupervised methods]].

''Topological layers in neural networks:'' Recent work integrates persistence-based loss functions directly into [[Geometric Deep Learning|deep learning architectures]], enforcing topological constraints on learned representations (e.g., ensuring a segmentation map has the correct number of connected components).

''Drug discovery and molecular shape:'' TDA characterizes the shape of binding pockets in proteins. Persistent homology of atom point clouds captures cavities ($H_2$) that traditional descriptors miss, improving virtual screening accuracy.

''Materials science:'' The pore structure of nanomaterials, grain boundaries in metals, and phase transitions in amorphous solids all have natural topological signatures that correlate with physical properties.

''Neuroscience:'' The [[Manifold Hypothesis|manifold structure]] of neural population activity has been analyzed with persistent homology, revealing toroidal topology in head-direction cells and ring-like structure in grid cells.

!! When TDA Breaks Down

''Computational cost explodes in high dimensions.'' The Vietoris-Rips complex on $n$ points can have up to $2^n$ simplices. Even with optimized algorithms, computing persistent homology beyond $H_1$ for large point clouds (tens of thousands of points in high ambient dimension) becomes impractical. Approximations like the ''sparse Rips filtration'' or ''witness complexes'' help but introduce their own trade-offs.

''Topological features do not capture all geometric information.'' Two data sets can have identical persistence diagrams but very different geometry. A flat disk and a bumpy disk are topologically equivalent — if the distinction that matters is curvature, density variation, or local geometry rather than connectivity and holes, TDA will be blind to it. Combining TDA with geometric or statistical descriptors is usually necessary.

''Choosing the right filtration is a modeling decision.'' The Vietoris-Rips complex uses Euclidean distance, but if your data lives in a non-Euclidean space (a graph, a manifold with a different metric, or a space where cosine similarity is more natural), the default filtration may produce misleading features. Kernel-based or density-weighted filtrations exist but require domain expertise to configure.

!! Technical Details

''Vietoris-Rips complex:'' Include simplex $[x_0, \ldots, x_k]$ iff $\max_{i,j} d(x_i, x_j) \leq r$. Easier to compute than the Cech complex but yields a larger complex. The Rips complex is a flag complex (determined entirely by its 1-skeleton), enabling efficient algorithms.

''Cech complex:'' Include simplex $[x_0, \ldots, x_k]$ iff the $k+1$ balls of radius $r/2$ centered at the points have a common intersection. By the Nerve Theorem, the Cech complex is homotopy equivalent to the union of balls — a stronger theoretical guarantee than Rips.

''Mapper algorithm:'' A practical tool for visualization. Given a filter function $f: X \to \mathbb{R}$, Mapper (1) covers the range of $f$ with overlapping intervals, (2) clusters the preimage of each interval, and (3) connects clusters that share points in overlapping intervals. The output is a simplicial complex (often visualized as a graph) summarizing the data's shape. Used in cancer subtyping (Lum et al., 2013) and sports analytics.

''Computational complexity:'' Standard persistent homology via matrix reduction runs in $O(n^3)$ in the number of simplices. ''Ripser'' achieves dramatic speedups through implicit representation of the coboundary matrix, lazy evaluation, and clearing optimizations — it is the current gold standard for Rips persistent homology up to $H_1$ and $H_2$.

''Software:'' ''Ripser'' (C++, fastest for Rips complexes), ''GUDHI'' (C++/Python, broad functionality including alpha complexes and Mapper), ''Dionysus'' (C++/Python), ''giotto-tda'' (Python, scikit-learn compatible API), ''Persim'' (persistence diagram distances and visualization).
