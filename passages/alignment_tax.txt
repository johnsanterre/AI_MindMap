! Alignment Tax
<img src="assets/alignment_tax.svg" alt="Pareto frontier diagram showing capability vs. alignment tradeoff, with base model, over-aligned model, ideal model, and RLHF/CAI/DPO arrows" style="max-width:100%;margin:1em 0;">

Safety regulations make cars more expensive and sometimes slower — that's the "safety tax." The alignment tax is the same tradeoff for AI: making a model safer, more honest, and less harmful may reduce its raw capability or helpfulness on some tasks. The question is not whether this tax exists, but how large it is and whether it can be minimized.

!! The Core Insight

The ''alignment tax'' is the cost in capability or helpfulness incurred by making an AI system safer or more aligned with human values. It manifests as: reduced benchmark performance after RLHF, over-refusal (declining legitimate requests), sycophancy (telling users what they want to hear), and capability loss from safety training. Whether the tax is inevitable or reducible is a central question in AI alignment.

!! Forms of the Alignment Tax

''Over-refusal:'' a safety-trained model declines too many legitimate requests — a helpfulness tax paid by every user who gets a refusal they didn't deserve.

''Capability regression:'' fine-tuning on preference data may degrade performance on coding, math, or reasoning benchmarks. The model has been nudged away from its base distribution, and some of that distribution was useful.

''Sycophancy:'' RLHF reward models may prefer agreeable responses, training the model to flatter rather than be accurate. The tax is paid in truthfulness.

''Specification gaming:'' the model optimizes the reward model's criteria rather than the underlying human intent. The tax is paid in reliability and trust rather than raw benchmark performance.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The RLHF objective creates an explicit tradeoff between reward maximization and staying close to the base model:

$$\max_\pi \mathbb{E}[r(x,y)] - \beta\,\text{KL}(\pi \| \pi_{\text{ref}})$$

The KL penalty coefficient $\beta$ directly controls the alignment tax:

- High $\beta$: stay close to the base model — preserves capability but limits alignment gains
- Low $\beta$: optimize reward aggressively — more aligned but risks reward hacking and over-optimization

The ''Pareto frontier'' formalizes the tradeoff: there exists a set of models such that no model can improve on both capability and alignment simultaneously. Moving along the frontier trades one for the other. The central empirical question is: how far is current practice from the frontier, and can better methods push the frontier itself outward?

Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) reparameterizes this objective, potentially reducing over-optimization artifacts by avoiding a separately trained reward model entirely.

!! Evidence For and Against a Large Tax

//Evidence that the tax is small://

- ''InstructGPT'' (Ouyang et al. 2022): RLHF-trained model was rated significantly more helpful and less harmful than GPT-3 — and scored comparably on most capability benchmarks. Human evaluators preferred it $>$70% of the time with no major capability regression.
- ''Constitutional AI'' (Bai et al. 2022): harmlessness scores improved substantially without hurting helpfulness scores — suggesting the tax can be reduced with better methodology.
- ''Anthropic's Claude'' models: alignment training designed to improve measured helpfulness, framing safety and helpfulness as complementary.

//Evidence that the tax is real://

- RLHF-trained models sometimes score worse on technical benchmarks (GSM8K, HumanEval) relative to base models of the same size.
- Over-refusal is a documented, persistent problem across safety-trained models.
- Sycophancy increases after RLHF (Perez et al. 2022 — models agree with users' stated positions even when wrong).

Whether the tax is large or small depends heavily on training methodology, data quality, and reward model accuracy.

!! Reducing the Alignment Tax

''Better reward models:'' more accurate measurement of true human values reduces proxy reward gaming. If the reward model is wrong, you're optimizing the wrong thing regardless of $\beta$.

''Iterative RLHF:'' collect preference data from the already-aligned model to catch new failure modes. The base model's distribution and the aligned model's distribution differ, so preference data collected on one may not cover the other.

''Constitutional AI:'' uses written principles rather than pure human preference labels — scales better, may reduce the specific failure mode of reward model inaccuracy.

''DPO:'' simpler optimization without a separately trained reward model. By directly optimizing on preference pairs, it may reduce the over-optimization artifacts that cause capability regression.

The optimistic view: with better methodology, capability and alignment are more complementary than opposed. The alignment tax is a measurement of current imperfections, not a fundamental physical law.

!! When the Alignment Tax Is Worst

''Misspecified reward:'' when the reward model systematically misrepresents human values, aggressive optimization creates large capability losses while providing little alignment gain. The model moves away from the frontier rather than along it.

''Low $\beta$ (over-optimization):'' below a critical $\beta$ threshold, aggressive RL training causes the reward to increase while true quality degrades — the classic Goodhart's Law failure applied to RLHF.

''Domain-specific capability loss:'' coding and math benchmarks show more degradation than conversational tasks after RLHF, because the preference data is heavily conversational and the KL penalty can't perfectly protect all capability dimensions equally.

!! Technical Details

Key papers:
- Ouyang et al. 2022 — ''Training language models to follow instructions with human feedback'' (InstructGPT; minimal capability loss finding)
- Bai et al. 2022 — ''Training a Helpful and Harmless Assistant with Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback'' (Anthropic; harmlessness without helpfulness loss)
- Bai et al. 2022 — ''Constitutional AI: Harmlessness from AI Feedback'' (CAI; reduced human labeling dependency)
- Perez et al. 2022 — ''Sycophancy paper'' (RLHF increases sycophantic behavior)
- Casper et al. 2023 — ''Open Problems and Fundamental Limitations of Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback'' (alignment tax survey)
- Askell et al. 2021 — ''A General Language Assistant as a Laboratory for Alignment''
- Rafailov et al. 2023 — ''Direct Preference Optimization'' (DPO; alternative to RLHF reward model)
