! Fokker-Planck Equation

<img src="assets/fokker_planck.svg" alt="Diagram showing probability density evolution under drift and diffusion" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine releasing a drop of ink in still water — it spreads outward, the cloud of dye diffusing in all directions. Now tilt the container slightly so there is a gentle current (drift). The ink cloud both drifts and spreads simultaneously. The ''Fokker-Planck equation'' is the PDE that describes exactly this: how a probability density evolves over time under both drift and diffusion. It is the deterministic (PDE) description of what is stochastically described by an SDE. Rather than tracking individual particle trajectories, it tells you how the entire //distribution// of possible states flows and spreads through time.

!! The Core Insight

There is a profound duality at the heart of stochastic processes: the same physical system can be described in two equivalent but complementary ways.

An ''SDE'' (stochastic differential equation) describes the world from the perspective of //individual trajectories//. Given a single particle, where does it go? The SDE $dX = \mu(X,t)\,dt + \sigma(X,t)\,dW$ says: the particle drifts with rate $\mu$ and is jostled by random noise of strength $\sigma$, where $dW$ is a Wiener increment.

The ''Fokker-Planck equation'' (also called the forward Kolmogorov equation) describes the world from the perspective of //population distributions//. If you released millions of particles from the same initial condition, how would their collective density $p(x,t)$ evolve? The answer is a deterministic PDE — no randomness in sight.

Both descriptions are mathematically equivalent. The SDE is the microscopic view; the Fokker-Planck equation is the macroscopic view. This duality is one of the deep unifying ideas of probability theory, statistical mechanics, and modern machine learning.

!! Deriving the Equation

For the SDE $dX = \mu(X,t)\,dt + \sigma(X,t)\,dW$, the Fokker-Planck (forward Kolmogorov) equation governing the probability density $p(x,t)$ is:

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\bigl[\mu(x,t)\,p\bigr] + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}\bigl[\sigma^2(x,t)\,p\bigr]$$

The two terms have clear physical interpretations:

- ''Drift term'' $-\dfrac{\partial(\mu p)}{\partial x}$: this is a continuity equation — it //advects// (transports) the probability density in the direction of $\mu$. If $\mu > 0$, the density flows to the right. This is the ink cloud being carried by the current.

- ''Diffusion term'' $\dfrac{1}{2}\dfrac{\partial^2(\sigma^2 p)}{\partial x^2}$: this //spreads// the density, making the distribution broader over time. This is the ink diffusing outward through random molecular collisions.

''Stationary distribution:'' Setting $\partial p / \partial t = 0$ gives the equilibrium density. For ''Langevin dynamics'' driven by a potential $U(x)$ — that is, $dX = -U'(X)\,dt + \sqrt{2T}\,dW$ — the stationary solution is the ''Boltzmann distribution'':

$$p_\infty(x) \propto \exp\!\left(-\frac{U(x)}{T}\right)$$

where $T$ is the temperature (noise level). This is the bridge between stochastic dynamics and statistical mechanics: run Langevin dynamics long enough and your samples come from the Boltzmann measure.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The Fokker-Planck equation can be derived rigorously using ''Itô's lemma''. For any smooth test function $f(x)$, Itô's lemma gives:

$$d f(X_t) = f'(X_t)\,\mu\,dt + \frac{1}{2}f''(X_t)\,\sigma^2\,dt + f'(X_t)\,\sigma\,dW_t$$

Taking expectations and using $\mathbb{E}[dW_t] = 0$, then integrating by parts, yields the Fokker-Planck equation as the equation governing $\mathbb{E}[f(X_t)] = \int f(x)\,p(x,t)\,dx$.

''Multivariate FPE:'' In $d$ dimensions with drift vector $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and diffusion matrix $\mathbf{D}$:

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = -\nabla \cdot (\boldsymbol{\mu}\,p) + \frac{1}{2}\nabla^2(\sigma^2 p)$$

or more generally $-\nabla \cdot (\boldsymbol{\mu}\,p) + \nabla \cdot \nabla \cdot (\mathbf{D}\,p)$.

''Probability current:'' Define the current $J(x,t) = \mu\,p - \dfrac{1}{2}\dfrac{\partial(\sigma^2 p)}{\partial x}$. The FPE is simply a continuity equation:

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = -\frac{\partial J}{\partial x}$$

This is conservation of probability: density flows like a fluid, never created or destroyed.

''Detailed balance:'' At equilibrium, $J = 0$ everywhere — the probability current vanishes at every point, not just globally. This is a stronger condition than mere stationarity and characterizes //reversible// (time-symmetric) processes.

''Backward Kolmogorov equation:'' The adjoint of the FPE describes how expected values of functions propagate //backward// in time:

$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \mu\,\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2\,\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}$$

where $u(x,t) = \mathbb{E}[f(X_T) \mid X_t = x]$. Forward FPE: propagate densities forward. Backward equation: propagate expected values backward.

!! Fokker-Planck in ML

The Fokker-Planck equation has become surprisingly central to modern machine learning.

''Diffusion models'' (Song et al. 2021, DDPM, score-based generative models): The forward noising process — gradually adding Gaussian noise to data — is a stochastic process with an exact Fokker-Planck equation. The reverse denoising process //inverts// the FPE using the ''score function'' $\nabla_x \log p(x,t)$. Remarkably, knowing the score at every noise level is sufficient to reverse the diffusion and generate new samples. The FPE provides the theoretical foundation for why this works.

''Langevin MCMC:'' The Langevin SDE $dX = \nabla \log p(X)\,dt + \sqrt{2}\,dW$ has stationary distribution exactly $p(x)$. Fokker-Planck analysis proves convergence and quantifies mixing rates. This is the basis of score-based sampling.

''Score matching:'' Rather than solving the FPE analytically, score matching trains a neural network to estimate $\nabla \log p(x,t)$ from data. This learned score function can then drive Langevin sampling or reverse diffusion.

''Mean-field theory for neural networks:'' In the infinite-width limit, the distribution of weights in a neural network during SGD training evolves according to a Fokker-Planck equation. This provides theoretical insight into loss landscape geometry, implicit regularization, and generalization — though the high dimensionality means this analysis remains mostly theoretical.

!! When the Fokker-Planck Equation Breaks Down

''(1) Non-Markovian processes:'' The FPE fundamentally assumes the ''Markov property'' — the future depends only on the present state, not on history. Systems with memory — fractional Brownian motion, viscoelastic fluids, non-equilibrium glassy dynamics, systems with feedback loops — violate this assumption. The generalization requires integro-differential equations (generalized Langevin equations) where the drift depends on the entire past trajectory via a memory kernel. Standard FPE analysis simply does not apply.

''(2) High-dimensional intractability:'' In $d$ dimensions, $p(x,t)$ is a function on $\mathbb{R}^d$. Numerically solving the FPE requires discretizing this space: even with just 100 grid points per dimension, the grid has $100^d$ points. For $d = 10$, that is $10^{20}$ points — computationally infeasible. Neural network weight spaces live in millions of dimensions. FPE-based analysis of deep learning dynamics is therefore a theoretical tool, not a practical computational one. This is why diffusion models use learned score functions rather than solving the FPE directly.

''(3) Discontinuous or heavy-tailed noise:'' The FPE assumes Gaussian (Wiener process) noise with smooth diffusion coefficients. Jump processes — Poisson jumps, Lévy noise, impulsive perturbations — produce discontinuous sample paths and require a generalized equation with an integral (jump) term added to the standard FPE. This Lévy-Fokker-Planck or integro-differential FPE is substantially harder to analyze and solve.

!! Technical Details

''History:'' Adriaan Fokker (1914) and Max Planck (1917) derived the equation in the context of Brownian motion in physics. Andrei Kolmogorov (1931) provided the rigorous mathematical foundation, identifying the forward (FPE) and backward Kolmogorov equations as fundamental objects in the theory of Markov processes.

''Exact solutions:'' The FPE for the ''Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process'' ($\mu = -\theta x$, $\sigma$ constant) has an exact Gaussian solution with time-varying mean and variance. The mean relaxes exponentially to zero; the variance relaxes to $\sigma^2 / (2\theta)$.

''Onsager-Machlup functional:'' The path probability for an SDE can be written as a functional integral, with the Onsager-Machlup action measuring how "surprising" a given trajectory is. This connects to variational principles and large deviation theory.

''Smoluchowski equation:'' The overdamped limit of the Langevin equation (ignoring inertia) yields a simpler FPE called the Smoluchowski equation, widely used in colloidal physics and polymer dynamics.

''Connections:'' The FPE is the probability-theoretic version of the diffusion equation from physics. It connects to the ''Boltzmann equation'' (kinetic theory), ''Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution'' (statistical mechanics), and the ''heat equation'' (when $\mu = 0$, $\sigma$ constant).
