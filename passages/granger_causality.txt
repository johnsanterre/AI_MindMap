! Granger Causality

<img src="assets/granger_causality.svg" alt="Diagram showing two time series X and Y with lagged arrows from past X to future Y, and F-test comparison of restricted versus unrestricted regression models" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are trying to figure out whether your neighbor's rooster actually causes the sunrise. The rooster crows every morning a few minutes before dawn, and if all you knew was that the crowing consistently preceded the light, you might conclude the bird is in charge. ''Granger causality'' formalizes exactly this kind of reasoning — and its limitations. It asks a precise, testable question: does knowing the past values of one time series //improve your ability to predict// another, beyond what that series' own history already tells you? If past rooster crows help predict sunrise timing better than sunrise history alone, then the rooster "Granger-causes" the sunrise. The test will happily declare this — which is why Clive Granger himself insisted that the method captures ''predictive precedence'', not true causation.

!! The Core Insight

Granger causality is fundamentally about ''incremental predictive power''. You build two models for a target series $Y$: one using only $Y$'s own past values (the ''restricted model''), and one that also includes past values of a candidate predictor $X$ (the ''unrestricted model''). If the unrestricted model produces significantly smaller prediction errors, then $X$ is said to ''Granger-cause'' $Y$.

This is a statistical notion, not a philosophical one. It makes no claim about mechanisms or interventions. Two series can be Granger-causally linked because they share a common unobserved driver, because one genuinely influences the other, or because of coincidental correlation structure. The test detects //useful predictive information//, which is valuable in its own right even when it does not settle questions of true causation. For deeper treatment of the distinction, see <<link "Causal Inference" "Causal Inference">><</link>>.

!! From Correlation to Temporal Precedence

Standard correlation is symmetric — if $X$ correlates with $Y$, then $Y$ correlates equally with $X$. Time introduces asymmetry. If past values of $X$ help predict future $Y$ but past $Y$ does not help predict future $X$, we have a directional relationship that correlation alone cannot reveal.

This temporal asymmetry is why Granger causality is so widely used in domains where controlled experiments are impossible but rich temporal data is available. In macroeconomics, researchers test whether money supply growth Granger-causes inflation, or vice versa. In neuroscience, the test is applied to neural recordings to map directional information flow between brain regions. In climate science, it helps disentangle whether sea surface temperatures predict rainfall patterns.

The key philosophical move is replacing the intractable question "does $X$ cause $Y$?" with the empirically answerable question "does $X$'s past contain information about $Y$'s future that $Y$'s own past does not?"

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The standard bivariate Granger causality test uses two <<link "ARIMA" "ARIMA">><</link>>-style linear regressions. The ''restricted model'' regresses $Y_t$ on its own $p$ lags:

$$Y_t = \alpha_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{p} \alpha_i Y_{t-i} + \epsilon_t$$

The ''unrestricted model'' adds $p$ lags of $X$:

$$Y_t = \alpha_0 + \sum_{i=1}^{p} \alpha_i Y_{t-i} + \sum_{j=1}^{p} \beta_j X_{t-j} + \eta_t$$

Granger causality amounts to testing the joint null hypothesis:

$$H_0: \beta_1 = \beta_2 = \cdots = \beta_p = 0$$

If all the $\beta_j$ coefficients are zero, then $X$'s past adds nothing. The test statistic is the standard $F$-test comparing residual sums of squares, as used in <<link "Hypothesis Testing" "Hypothesis Testing">><</link>>:

$$F = \frac{(RSS_R - RSS_U) / p}{RSS_U / (T - 2p - 1)}$$

where $RSS_R$ and $RSS_U$ are the residual sums of squares from the restricted and unrestricted models, $p$ is the number of lags, and $T$ is the sample size. Under the null, $F$ follows an $F(p, T - 2p - 1)$ distribution.

In practice, the test is often embedded in a ''Vector Autoregression'' (<<link "VAR Models" "VAR Models">><</link>>), which simultaneously models both $X_t$ and $Y_t$ as functions of both series' lags:

$$\begin{pmatrix} Y_t \\ X_t \end{pmatrix} = \mathbf{c} + \sum_{i=1}^{p} \mathbf{A}_i \begin{pmatrix} Y_{t-i} \\ X_{t-i} \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} \epsilon_{Y,t} \\ \epsilon_{X,t} \end{pmatrix}$$

Granger causality from $X$ to $Y$ then corresponds to the off-diagonal blocks of the $\mathbf{A}_i$ matrices being jointly zero.

!! Choosing Lags and Practical Considerations

The lag order $p$ is a critical choice. Too few lags miss slower causal dynamics; too many waste degrees of freedom and introduce noise. Common strategies include information criteria (AIC, BIC) applied to the VAR system, or sequential likelihood ratio tests.

Both series must be ''stationary'' — their statistical properties should not change over time. Non-stationary series (trending GDP, random-walk stock prices) produce spurious Granger causality at alarming rates. Standard practice is to difference the series or use cointegration-aware variants. This connects directly to the stationarity requirements discussed in <<link "Time Series" "Time Series">><</link>>.

For multivariate settings with more than two series, ''conditional Granger causality'' tests whether $X$ helps predict $Y$ //after// accounting for all other observed variables. This reduces (but does not eliminate) the confounding problem. Spectral variants decompose Granger causality by frequency, revealing that $X$ may Granger-cause $Y$ at business-cycle frequencies but not at seasonal frequencies.

!! When Granger Causality Breaks Down

''1. Confounding by a common unobserved cause.'' If a hidden variable $Z$ drives both $X$ and $Y$ with different time delays, $X$ may appear to Granger-cause $Y$ (or vice versa) even though neither influences the other. The rooster-sunrise problem is the classic example: the Earth's rotation (unobserved in the model) drives both. The Granger test has no way to account for variables not included in the model, making omitted-variable bias a persistent threat.

''2. Non-stationarity producing spurious results.'' When series contain unit roots or deterministic trends, the standard $F$-test's distributional assumptions collapse. Two independent random walks will appear to Granger-cause each other with probability far exceeding the nominal significance level. Practitioners must test for stationarity (ADF, KPSS tests) and either difference appropriately or use the Toda-Yamamoto procedure, which adds extra lags to restore valid inference without requiring pre-testing for unit roots.

''3. Nonlinear relationships that linear tests miss entirely.'' The standard Granger test uses linear regressions, so it is blind to nonlinear predictive relationships. If $X$'s past predicts $Y$'s future only during recessions, or only when $X$ exceeds a threshold, the linear test may fail to reject the null. Extensions include transfer entropy (an information-theoretic, model-free measure), kernel-based tests, and neural-network Granger causality, but these require more data and careful regularization.

!! Technical Details

Clive Granger introduced the concept in his 1969 paper //Investigating Causal Relations by Econometric Models and Cross-spectral Methods// in Econometrica, for which he shared the 2003 Nobel Prize in Economics with Robert Engle (who developed the complementary ARCH model for volatility).

''Transfer entropy'', proposed by Thomas Schreiber in 2000, generalizes Granger causality to nonlinear settings using conditional mutual information. It measures the reduction in uncertainty about $Y$'s future when conditioning on $X$'s past, beyond what $Y$'s own past provides. For Gaussian linear systems, transfer entropy is exactly equivalent to the log of the Granger causality $F$-statistic.

''Convergent Cross Mapping (CCM)'', developed by Sugihara et al. (2012), takes a fundamentally different approach rooted in dynamical systems theory. Rather than testing for predictive improvement, CCM asks whether the state-space reconstructions of $X$ and $Y$ are diffeomorphic — whether the "shadow" of $X$ can recover the dynamics of $Y$. It can detect causation in deterministic nonlinear systems where Granger causality fails.

In neuroscience, Granger causality applied to fMRI and EEG data requires careful attention to temporal resolution. fMRI's hemodynamic response function (HRF) smears neural activity over several seconds, potentially reversing apparent causal direction. Variations based on dynamic causal modeling (DCM) attempt to account for the HRF explicitly.

The ''Toda-Yamamoto procedure'' is a practical workaround for the non-stationarity problem: fit a VAR with $p + d_{max}$ lags (where $d_{max}$ is the maximum integration order), then test only the first $p$ lags. The extra lags absorb the non-standard asymptotics, allowing a standard chi-squared test regardless of cointegration status.

Granger causality connects to several other frameworks: it is a special case of ''Wiener-Granger causality'' from signal processing; it relates to ''Sims causality'' (1972), which uses future values of $X$ as regressors; and in the frequency domain, ''spectral Granger causality'' decomposes the test by frequency band using the spectral density matrix of the VAR.
