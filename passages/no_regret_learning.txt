! No-Regret Learning

<img src="assets/no_regret_learning.svg" alt="Diagram showing no-regret learning: algorithm cumulative loss converging toward best fixed strategy, with regret gap shrinking over time" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are picking a restaurant for lunch every day in a new city. You have no map, no reviews — just the meals you have eaten so far. At the end of the year, you look back and compare your total satisfaction to what you would have gotten if you had just gone to the single best restaurant every day from the start. ''No-regret learning'' is the guarantee that this gap — the ''regret'' — grows so slowly that on a per-day basis it vanishes to zero. You never needed to know the best restaurant in advance; the algorithm discovers it by accumulating experience, and its average performance converges to the best fixed choice in hindsight.

---

!! The Core Insight

The central question of no-regret learning is: can a sequential decision-maker, facing an unknown or even adversarial environment, perform nearly as well as the single best action chosen with the benefit of hindsight? The answer is yes, and the algorithms that achieve this are remarkably simple.

The key quantity is ''external regret''. After $T$ rounds, an algorithm has selected actions $a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_T$ and suffered losses $\ell_1(a_1), \ell_2(a_2), \ldots, \ell_T(a_T)$. The regret is the difference between the algorithm's cumulative loss and the cumulative loss of the best fixed action in hindsight:

$$R_T = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \ell_t(a_t) - \min_{a^*} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \ell_t(a^*)$$

An algorithm is ''no-regret'' if $R_T / T \to 0$ as $T \to \infty$. This means the average per-round penalty for not knowing the future vanishes. The standard achievable rate is $R_T = O(\sqrt{T})$, which is also the best possible against a fully adversarial environment.

What makes this remarkable is the adversarial setting: the losses can be chosen by an opponent who sees the algorithm's strategy. Even against a malicious adversary, these algorithms guarantee sublinear regret. This is a much stronger guarantee than assuming the environment is stochastic or stationary.

---

!! From Online Decisions to Game-Theoretic Equilibria

No-regret learning bridges [[Online Learning]] and [[Game Theory]] through one of the most elegant results in algorithmic game theory. When every player in a repeated game independently runs a no-regret algorithm, the empirical distribution of joint play converges to the set of ''coarse correlated equilibria'' of the stage game. If each player achieves the stronger property of no ''internal regret'' (also called ''swap regret''), convergence is to the set of ''correlated equilibria''.

This connection is profound. It means that rational agents who simply minimize their own regret — without any coordination, communication, or knowledge of other players' strategies — will collectively reach an equilibrium. No central planner is needed. No Nash equilibrium computation is required. The equilibrium emerges as a byproduct of each player independently learning not to regret.

This is the theoretical foundation behind ''regret matching'' in poker AI: the Counterfactual Regret Minimization (CFR) algorithm that powered Libratus and Pluribus computes Nash equilibria in large extensive-form games by having each information set run a no-regret learner. The equilibrium is not solved for analytically — it is //learned// through self-play where each decision point minimizes its own regret.

The connection also runs toward [[Multi-Armed Bandit]] problems. The bandit setting is a special case of online learning where the learner only observes the loss of the chosen action (''bandit feedback'') rather than all actions (''full information''). No-regret algorithms exist for both settings, but the bandit case is harder: the best achievable regret scales as $O(\sqrt{KT})$ where $K$ is the number of arms, compared to $O(\sqrt{T \ln K})$ in the full-information case.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The most canonical no-regret algorithm is the ''Multiplicative Weights Update'' (MWU), also known as Hedge or the Exponential Weights Algorithm. The learner maintains a weight $w_t(i)$ for each action $i$, initialized uniformly. At each round:

$$p_t(i) = \frac{w_t(i)}{\sum_j w_t(j)}, \qquad w_{t+1}(i) = w_t(i) \cdot e^{-\eta \ell_t(i)}$$

where $\eta > 0$ is the learning rate. The learner plays action $i$ with probability $p_t(i)$. Actions that have performed well accumulate larger weights; actions that have performed poorly are exponentially downweighted. With learning rate $\eta = \sqrt{\ln K / T}$, the regret satisfies:

$$R_T \leq 2\sqrt{T \ln K}$$

This is optimal up to constants. The proof uses a ''potential function'' argument: define $\Phi_t = \ln \sum_i w_t(i)$. The potential drops by at most $\eta \ell_t \cdot p_t + \eta^2$ per round (using the inequality $e^{-x} \leq 1 - x + x^2$ for $x \in [0,1]$), while the loss of any fixed action $i^*$ is bounded below by the total potential drop minus $\ln K$. Balancing these terms yields the regret bound.

A more general framework is ''Online Mirror Descent'' (OMD), which connects no-regret learning to [[Convex Optimization]]. Given a convex decision set $\mathcal{X}$ and a sequence of convex loss functions $f_t$, OMD updates:

$$x_{t+1} = \arg\min_{x \in \mathcal{X}} \left\{ \eta \langle \nabla f_t(x_t), x \rangle + D_\psi(x, x_t) \right\}$$

where $D_\psi$ is the Bregman divergence associated with a strongly convex mirror map $\psi$. When $\psi$ is the negative entropy and $\mathcal{X}$ is the simplex, OMD recovers MWU exactly. When $\psi$ is the squared $\ell_2$ norm, OMD recovers Online Gradient Descent. The regret bound becomes $O(\sqrt{T})$ for Lipschitz convex losses, a result known as ''online convex optimization''.

''Follow the Regularized Leader'' (FTRL) is the dual view: instead of incremental mirror descent updates, choose $x_t$ by minimizing the sum of all past losses plus a regularizer:

$$x_t = \arg\min_{x \in \mathcal{X}} \left\{ \sum_{s=1}^{t-1} f_s(x) + \frac{1}{\eta} \psi(x) \right\}$$

FTRL and OMD are equivalent for appropriate choices of $\psi$ and $\eta$, but the FTRL perspective is often more natural for analysis and for constructing adaptive algorithms.

!! Practical Applications Beyond Theory

No-regret learning is not merely a theoretical curiosity — it underpins several practical systems:

''Boosting:'' AdaBoost can be understood as a game between a weak learner and a distribution over training examples. The distribution update follows multiplicative weights, and boosting's convergence guarantees are essentially no-regret guarantees. This connection, identified by Freund and Schapire, was one of the early triumphs linking online learning theory to practical machine learning.

''Auction and mechanism design:'' In repeated auctions, bidders using no-regret strategies converge to equilibrium prices. This insight from algorithmic game theory has influenced how online advertising platforms design bidding systems.

''[[Reinforcement Learning]]:'' Policy optimization methods in RL can be viewed through the no-regret lens. Natural policy gradient and trust region methods are instances of mirror descent on the policy simplex. The regret framework provides convergence guarantees for policy iteration that complement the dynamic programming perspective.

''Online portfolio selection:'' The universal portfolio algorithm of Cover (1991) is a no-regret strategy for investment: its wealth growth rate converges to that of the best constant-rebalanced portfolio. This is the financial application that helped launch the field.

---

!! When No-Regret Learning Breaks Down

''Comparison to a fixed strategy is too weak:'' External regret compares performance to the best //fixed// action. If the best action changes over time — a non-stationary environment — the no-regret guarantee says nothing useful. A policy that plays action A for the first half and action B for the second half might vastly outperform any fixed action, but the learner's regret relative to the best fixed action can still be zero while its regret relative to this switching policy is large. Extensions like ''adaptive regret'' and ''tracking regret'' address this, but they require stronger assumptions or sacrifice the clean $O(\sqrt{T})$ rate.

''Bandit feedback with large action spaces:'' When the action space is enormous or continuous and only bandit feedback is available, no-regret algorithms suffer from the curse of dimensionality. The regret scales with the size of the action space, and exploration becomes prohibitively expensive. Structural assumptions — linearity, convexity, smoothness — are needed to make the problem tractable, but these assumptions may not hold in practice. Without structure, no-regret learning in high-dimensional bandit settings is statistically or computationally infeasible.

''Convergence to weak equilibria:'' While no-regret dynamics converge to coarse correlated equilibria, these equilibria can be far from Nash equilibria in important games. In many applications — security games, competitive markets, adversarial ML — the relevant solution concept is Nash or even minimax. No-regret learning alone does not guarantee convergence to Nash equilibrium in general games; it can cycle, diverge, or converge to limit cycles in the joint strategy space. Last-iterate convergence to Nash equilibrium requires additional structure (e.g., zero-sum games, monotone games) or modified algorithms (optimistic mirror descent, extra-gradient methods).

!! Technical Details

''Foundational papers:'' Littlestone and Warmuth (1994), //The Weighted Majority Algorithm//, introduced the multiplicative weights framework. Freund and Schapire (1997), //A Decision-Theoretic Generalization of On-Line Learning and an Application to Boosting//, unified the multiplicative weights method and connected it to AdaBoost. Cesa-Bianchi and Lugosi (2006), //Prediction, Learning, and Games//, is the definitive textbook covering the field. Shalev-Shwartz (2012), //Online Learning and Online Convex Optimization//, provides a modern survey emphasizing the convex optimization perspective.

''Regret bounds summary:'' Full-information setting with $K$ actions — MWU achieves $O(\sqrt{T \ln K})$ regret, which is minimax optimal. Bandit setting with $K$ arms — EXP3 achieves $O(\sqrt{KT \ln K})$, improved to $O(\sqrt{KT})$ by the Implicitly Normalized Forecaster. Online convex optimization with Lipschitz losses on a bounded convex set — Online Gradient Descent achieves $O(\sqrt{T})$. Online convex optimization with strongly convex losses — achieves $O(\ln T)$, a logarithmic regret that matches the lower bound.

''Swap regret and correlated equilibria:'' An algorithm has no swap regret if for every function $\pi$ mapping actions to actions, $\sum_t \ell_t(a_t) - \sum_t \ell_t(\pi(a_t)) = o(T)$. This is strictly stronger than external regret. Blum and Mansour (2007) showed how to convert any external-regret algorithm into a swap-regret algorithm with $K$ copies, achieving $O(K\sqrt{T \ln K})$ swap regret. When all players achieve no swap regret, the empirical play converges to the polytope of correlated equilibria.

''Counterfactual Regret Minimization (CFR):'' Zinkevich et al. (2007) introduced CFR for extensive-form games. Each information set maintains a regret vector and plays according to regret matching. The average strategy profile converges to a Nash equilibrium in two-player zero-sum games. CFR+ (Tammelin, 2014) and Monte Carlo CFR variants accelerated convergence, enabling superhuman poker play in Libratus (2017) and Pluribus (2019).

''Connection to optimization:'' Online-to-batch conversion shows that the average iterate of an online learning algorithm on i.i.d. data achieves expected excess risk $O(1/\sqrt{T})$. This connects no-regret learning to stochastic [[Convex Optimization]] and shows that online learning is at least as powerful as batch learning, up to constants.
