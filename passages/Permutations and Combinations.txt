! Permutations and Combinations

<img src="assets/permutations_and_combinations.svg" alt="Diagram comparing permutations (ordered arrangements) and combinations (unordered selections) with visual examples, Pascal's triangle, and ML applications" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you have a shelf of books. A ''permutation'' asks: in how many ways can you arrange some of those books in a row? A ''combination'' asks: in how many ways can you //choose// some books to take on a trip, where you don't care about the order in your suitcase? The entire difference between these two ideas comes down to a single question: //does the order matter?// If you're seating people in chairs, order matters — that's a permutation. If you're picking team members, order doesn't — that's a combination. This distinction, simple as it sounds, is the engine behind everything from counting search spaces in [[Combinatorics|Combinatorics]] to understanding why the [[Binomial Distribution|Binomial Distribution]] looks the way it does.

!! The Core Insight

The number of ways to choose $k$ items from $n$ and arrange them is $P(n,k) = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!}$. But every //group// of $k$ items gets counted $k!$ times (once per ordering), so the number of //unordered// selections is $C(n,k) = \binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$. The relationship $P(n,k) = k! \cdot C(n,k)$ captures the heart of the matter: ''permutations overcount by exactly $k!$'' relative to combinations. This is not merely a formula — it's a //principle//. Every time you encounter a counting problem, the first question is whether you're counting ordered or unordered structures, and the answer determines whether to divide by $k!$.

!! The Building Blocks

''The factorial'' $n! = n \times (n-1) \times \cdots \times 1$ counts the number of ways to arrange $n$ distinct objects in a line. By convention, $0! = 1$ (there is exactly one way to arrange nothing). The factorial grows ferociously: $10! = 3{,}628{,}800$, $20! \approx 2.4 \times 10^{18}$, and $100!$ has 158 digits. This explosive growth is why brute-force enumeration becomes impossible so quickly.

''Permutations'' $P(n,k) = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!}$: you fill $k$ positions from $n$ candidates. The first position has $n$ choices, the second $n-1$, and so on — the product telescope gives the formula.

''Combinations'' $\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}$: the binomial coefficient. It satisfies the symmetry $\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n}{n-k}$ (choosing what to //include// is the same as choosing what to //exclude//) and the recursion $\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k-1} + \binom{n-1}{k}$, which generates ''Pascal's triangle''.

''Permutations with repetition:'' If you have $n$ objects where there are $n_1$ of type 1, $n_2$ of type 2, ..., $n_r$ of type $r$, the number of distinct arrangements is the ''multinomial coefficient'' $\binom{n}{n_1, n_2, \ldots, n_r} = \frac{n!}{n_1! n_2! \cdots n_r!}$.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''The binomial theorem'' connects combinations to algebra:

$$(a + b)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k$$

Setting $a = b = 1$ yields $\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} = 2^n$: a set of $n$ elements has exactly $2^n$ subsets. Setting $a = 1, b = -1$ yields $\sum_{k} (-1)^k \binom{n}{k} = 0$: the even-sized and odd-sized subsets are equinumerous.

''Stars and bars:'' The number of ways to distribute $n$ identical objects into $k$ distinct bins is:

$$\binom{n+k-1}{k-1}$$

This is equivalent to counting ''multiset coefficients'' — the number of multisets of size $n$ drawn from $k$ types. The formula arises by representing a distribution as a string of $n$ stars and $k-1$ bars, then counting arrangements of that string.

''Vandermonde's identity'' encodes convolution of binomial coefficients:

$$\binom{m+n}{r} = \sum_{k=0}^{r} \binom{m}{k}\binom{n}{r-k}$$

This identity appears naturally in the product of [[Generating Functions|Generating Functions]] and in the convolution structure of [[Probability Distributions|Probability Distributions]].

''Stirling's approximation'' tames the factorial: $n! \approx \sqrt{2\pi n} \left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^n$. From this, $\log \binom{n}{k} \approx n \cdot H(k/n)$ where $H(p) = -p\log p - (1-p)\log(1-p)$ is the binary entropy. This connects counting to information theory.

!! Permutations and Combinations in Machine Learning

''Feature subset selection:'' With $d$ features, there are $\binom{d}{k}$ possible subsets of size $k$ and $2^d$ total subsets. For $d = 100$, $\binom{100}{10} \approx 1.7 \times 10^{13}$ — exhaustive search is hopeless, which is why greedy algorithms, LASSO, and random feature subsampling are necessary.

''Hyperparameter search space:'' If you have $m$ hyperparameters with $|H_1|, |H_2|, \ldots, |H_m|$ possible values, grid search evaluates $\prod_i |H_i|$ configurations (product rule). Switching from 10 values per parameter to 20 values per parameter with 5 hyperparameters inflates the grid from $10^5$ to $20^5 = 3.2 \times 10^6$ — a 32× blowup. This is why Bayesian optimization and random search outperform grid search in high dimensions.

''Data augmentation counting:'' For $n$ training examples with $t$ possible transformations (flips, crops, color jitter), the augmented dataset can have up to $n \cdot t$ entries per epoch. When augmentations compose (rotate THEN crop), the count becomes $n \cdot t_1 \cdot t_2 \cdots t_m$ — a permutation-style product.

''Multinomial in NLP:'' The number of distinct orderings of a bag of $n$ words with $n_w$ copies of word $w$ is the multinomial coefficient $\frac{n!}{\prod_w n_w!}$. This is exactly the normalizing factor in the multinomial naive Bayes model.

''The [[Pigeonhole Principle|Pigeonhole Principle]] connection:'' If $\binom{n}{k} < $ the number of configurations you need to distinguish, then $k$ items cannot cover all cases — a direct link between counting and impossibility arguments.

!! When Permutations and Combinations Break Down

''Combinatorial explosion.'' The formulas themselves never "break," but the //numbers they produce// quickly exceed any computational budget. $\binom{1000}{500} \approx 2.7 \times 10^{299}$ is larger than the number of atoms in the observable universe. Any algorithm that enumerates all subsets or permutations is doomed for even moderate $n$. In ML, this is why exhaustive feature selection, architecture search, and permutation tests must be replaced by sampling or heuristic approaches. The formulas tell you //how doomed// you are — which is itself useful information.

''Indistinguishable vs. distinct objects.'' The most common error in applied counting is misidentifying whether items are distinguishable. Distributing 5 identical balls into 3 boxes uses stars-and-bars ($\binom{7}{2} = 21$), while distributing 5 //labeled// balls uses the product rule ($3^5 = 243$). Confusing the two produces off-by-$k!$ errors that silently corrupt probability calculations. In ML, this matters when computing the number of distinct partitions in $k$-fold cross-validation or the number of distinct clusterings in $k$-means.

''Continuous analogues.'' Combinatorics counts discrete objects. When features are continuous, the number of "possible subsets" is uncountably infinite and the combinatorial framework must be replaced by measure-theoretic tools. The beta function $B(a,b) = \frac{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)}{\Gamma(a+b)}$ is the continuous generalization of the binomial coefficient (replacing factorials with the Gamma function), and it appears in the [[Binomial Distribution|Binomial Distribution]]'s conjugate prior.

!! Technical Details

''Pascal's triangle properties:'' Row $n$ sums to $2^n$. The entries along diagonals give Fibonacci numbers. The central binomial coefficient $\binom{2n}{n} \sim \frac{4^n}{\sqrt{\pi n}}$ appears in random walk return probabilities.

''Double counting (bijective proofs):'' Many combinatorial identities are best proved by counting the same set in two ways. The identity $k\binom{n}{k} = n\binom{n-1}{k-1}$ is proved by noting both sides count ways to choose a committee of $k$ from $n$ people and then pick a chair.

''Multiset coefficient:'' The number of ways to choose $k$ items from $n$ types //with repetition// is $\left(\!\binom{n}{k}\!\right) = \binom{n+k-1}{k}$. This is the stars-and-bars formula in different notation.

''Derangements:'' The number of permutations of $n$ elements with //no// fixed points is $D_n = n! \sum_{i=0}^{n} \frac{(-1)^i}{i!} \approx n!/e$. This uses the inclusion-exclusion principle.
