! Model Selection

[img[assets/model_selection.svg]]

Imagine you are hiring for a job. You have a stack of resumes and a shortlist of candidates. You could give the job to the person with the most impressive credentials, but impressive credentials do not guarantee they will perform well in //your// specific role. The right hire is the one whose skills best match the actual demands of the position — not too junior, not too overqualified. ''Model selection'' is the same problem translated to machine learning: given a set of candidate models, which one will perform best on data it has never seen? The answer is almost never the most complex model, and almost never the simplest. It is the one whose capacity best matches the true complexity of the underlying pattern.

---

!! The Core Insight

Every model makes a bet about how complex the real world is. A linear regression bets the relationship is a straight line. A deep neural network bets the relationship involves intricate hierarchical features. Model selection is the process of adjudicating between these bets using evidence from data, not intuition.

The fundamental tension is between ''fit'' and ''generalization''. A model that fits training data perfectly has likely memorized noise along with signal — it will fail on new data. A model that barely fits the training data has missed real patterns. The [[Bias-Variance Tradeoff]] formalizes this: increasing model complexity reduces bias (underfitting) but increases variance (overfitting). Model selection seeks the complexity level where the //sum// of bias and variance is minimized, which is the point of lowest expected error on unseen data.

The key practical realization is that training error is a biased, overly optimistic estimate of true performance. Every model selection method — from cross-validation to information criteria to Bayesian model comparison — is fundamentally a strategy for obtaining a less biased estimate of how the model will perform on data it was not trained on.

---

!! Information Criteria and Cross-Validation

Two major families of model selection tools have emerged, each attacking the problem from a different angle.

''Information criteria'' — most notably [[AIC and BIC]] — estimate out-of-sample prediction error by penalizing model complexity directly. AIC approximates the expected Kullback-Leibler divergence between the fitted model and the true data-generating process. BIC approximates the marginal likelihood of the data under the model, effectively asking: //how probable is the observed data if this model class generated it?// Both add a penalty term that grows with the number of parameters, discouraging unnecessary complexity. AIC tends to favor slightly more complex models (it optimizes prediction), while BIC tends to favor simpler ones (it seeks the true model).

''[[Cross-Validation]]'' takes a more direct approach: it repeatedly holds out portions of the training data, fits the model on the remainder, and measures prediction error on the held-out portion. $k$-fold cross-validation splits data into $k$ groups, trains $k$ times, and averages the $k$ held-out error estimates. Leave-one-out cross-validation is the extreme case where $k = n$. Cross-validation makes no assumptions about the model's parametric form — it works for any learning algorithm — but it is computationally expensive and has higher variance than information criteria for small datasets.

The two families are connected theoretically: AIC is asymptotically equivalent to leave-one-out cross-validation for linear models. This equivalence reveals that both methods are estimating the same quantity — expected out-of-sample prediction error — through different mathematical routes.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The goal of model selection is to choose the model $M_k$ from a set $\{M_1, \ldots, M_K\}$ that minimizes the ''expected generalization error'':

$$\text{Err}(M_k) = E\left[ L(Y, \hat{f}_{M_k}(X)) \right]$$

where $L$ is a loss function (e.g., squared error), the expectation is over new data $(X, Y)$, and $\hat{f}_{M_k}$ is the model fitted on training data. We cannot compute this directly because we do not know the true data distribution.

''AIC'' estimates $\text{Err}(M_k)$ for models fit by maximum likelihood under squared error or log-likelihood loss:

$$\text{AIC} = -2 \ln \hat{L} + 2k$$

where $\hat{L}$ is the maximized likelihood and $k$ is the number of estimated parameters. The penalty $2k$ corrects for the optimistic bias of training-set log-likelihood. For linear models with Gaussian errors, this simplifies to:

$$\text{AIC} = n \ln(\hat{\sigma}^2) + 2k$$

''BIC'' replaces the $2k$ penalty with $k \ln n$:

$$\text{BIC} = -2 \ln \hat{L} + k \ln n$$

The $\ln n$ penalty grows with sample size, making BIC increasingly harsh on complex models as data accumulates. This property gives BIC ''consistency'': as $n \to \infty$, BIC selects the true model with probability 1 (assuming the true model is among the candidates). AIC is not consistent but is ''efficient'': it minimizes the asymptotic prediction error among the candidates, even when none is the true model.

''$k$-fold cross-validation'' estimates generalization error directly:

$$\text{CV}(M_k) = \frac{1}{k} \sum_{j=1}^{k} L\left(y_{-j}, \hat{f}_{M_k}^{(-j)}(X_{-j})\right)$$

where $\hat{f}_{M_k}^{(-j)}$ is the model trained without fold $j$ and $L(y_{-j}, \cdot)$ is the loss evaluated on fold $j$.

---

!! Practical Model Selection

In practice, model selection involves more than choosing between a linear model and a polynomial. Modern pipelines compare entirely different model families — random forests versus gradient-boosted trees versus neural networks — each with their own [[Hyperparameter Tuning]] configurations. The standard workflow is:

''Nested cross-validation'' separates model selection from model evaluation. The outer loop estimates generalization error; the inner loop selects hyperparameters and model type. This prevents the selection process itself from overfitting to the test set. Without nesting, reported performance is optimistically biased because the test data influenced the choice of model.

''[[Evaluation Metrics]]'' matter: the choice of loss function (accuracy, AUC, F1, log-loss) changes which model wins. A model that is best under squared error may not be best under log-loss. The metric should align with the actual cost structure of the deployment environment — misranking a medical diagnosis has different consequences from misranking a product recommendation.

''Computational budget'' is a real constraint. Cross-validation multiplies training cost by $k$. Information criteria require only a single fit but assume specific model forms. Bayesian model selection requires computing or approximating marginal likelihoods, which is tractable for some models and intractable for others. The practical choice of method often comes down to what you can afford to compute.

''Regularization path'' offers a smooth alternative: rather than comparing discrete models, fit a single model family (e.g., [[Bias-Variance Tradeoff|ridge or lasso]]) across a continuum of regularization strengths and select the strength that minimizes cross-validated error. This treats model selection as a continuous optimization rather than a discrete comparison.

---

!! When Model Selection Breaks Down

''Small sample sizes with many candidate models:'' When the training set is small and the number of competing models is large, cross-validation estimates become noisy and unreliable. The variance of the CV estimate can exceed the differences between models, making selection essentially random. Information criteria fare better in this regime but still rely on asymptotic approximations that may not hold. In extreme cases, the selection process itself overfits — it picks whichever model got lucky on the particular folds, not the genuinely best model.

''Distribution shift between training and deployment:'' All model selection methods assume that the data used for selection is representative of the data the model will encounter in production. When this assumption fails — due to temporal drift, population shift, or covariate shift — the selected model may be optimal for yesterday's data but suboptimal or even dangerous for tomorrow's. No amount of careful cross-validation can protect against a future that looks fundamentally different from the past.

''Leakage and improper validation:'' Model selection gives misleading results whenever information from the validation or test set leaks into training. Common sources include feature engineering computed on the full dataset before splitting, target encoding that uses validation labels, or hyperparameter searches that reuse the same test set. The selected model appears better than it actually is, and the performance gap between reported and deployed accuracy can be substantial. Proper data hygiene — computing all transformations within each cross-validation fold — is essential but frequently violated.

---

!! Technical Details

''Akaike Information Criterion (AIC)'': derived by Hirotugu Akaike in 1973 as an estimate of the expected Kullback-Leibler divergence between the fitted model and the true model. For maximum likelihood estimators, the training log-likelihood overestimates the expected log-likelihood by approximately $k$ (the number of parameters), so AIC adds $2k$ as a bias correction. The corrected variant AICc adds a finite-sample correction: $\text{AICc} = \text{AIC} + \frac{2k(k+1)}{n - k - 1}$, which is recommended when $n/k < 40$.

''Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC)'': derived by Gideon Schwarz in 1978 from the Laplace approximation to the marginal likelihood $p(y|M_k) = \int p(y|\theta, M_k) p(\theta|M_k) d\theta$. The $k \ln n$ penalty emerges naturally from the Laplace integral. BIC is consistent (selects the true model as $n \to \infty$) but can underfit in finite samples because its penalty grows without bound.

''Cross-validation variance'': the folds in $k$-fold CV are not independent (they share training data), so the usual formula for variance of a mean does not apply. Bengio and Grandvalet (2004) showed there is no universally unbiased estimator of the variance of CV. Practical approaches include repeated $k$-fold (average over multiple random splits) and the ''one-standard-error rule'' (select the simplest model within one standard error of the minimum CV error).

''Bayesian model selection'' computes the posterior probability of each model: $p(M_k|y) \propto p(y|M_k) p(M_k)$. The marginal likelihood $p(y|M_k)$ automatically penalizes complexity through the prior volume effect (Occam's razor). For conjugate exponential families, marginal likelihoods are available in closed form. For complex models, approximations include the Laplace approximation (which yields BIC), variational inference, and thermodynamic integration.

''Model averaging'' sidesteps selection entirely: instead of choosing one model, weight predictions by posterior model probabilities. Bayesian model averaging (BMA) is theoretically optimal under the assumption that the true model is in the candidate set. ''Stacking'' (Wolpert, 1992; Breiman, 1996) learns optimal combination weights via cross-validation without Bayesian assumptions, and is the dominant approach in competitive machine learning.
