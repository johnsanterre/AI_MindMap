! Geometric Deep Learning

<img src="assets/geometric_deep_learning.svg" alt="Diagram showing Geometric Deep Learning unifying CNNs, GNNs, Transformers, and Gauge CNNs through symmetry groups and equivariance" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are designing a lock-picking tool. You do not build a separate tool for every lock in the world — you study the //structure// of locks: the symmetries of the pin tumbler mechanism, the rotational freedom of the cylinder, the invariances that all locks share. Once you understand the symmetry, you can design one tool that generalises to locks you have never seen. Geometric Deep Learning does the same thing for neural network architectures: instead of inventing a separate architecture for images, molecules, point clouds, and social networks, it asks what ''symmetries'' the data obeys, and derives the architecture from the answer.

---

!! The Core Insight

Every successful deep learning architecture is, at its core, an answer to one question: //what stays the same when you transform the input?// A <<link "CNN" "CNN">><</link>> works because shifting an image left does not change what is in it — that is translation symmetry. A <<link "Graph Neural Networks" "Graph Neural Networks">><</link>> works because relabelling the nodes of a molecule does not change its chemistry — that is permutation symmetry. A <<link "Transformers" "Transformers">><</link>> works because reordering a set of tokens before self-attention produces a correspondingly reordered output — again, permutation symmetry.

Geometric Deep Learning (GDL), as formalised by Bronstein, Bruna, Cohen, and Velickovic in their 2021 monograph, is the programme of making this observation precise. The unifying principle is ''equivariance with respect to a symmetry group'': if the input undergoes a group transformation $g$, the output should transform in a predictable, structured way. In the simplest case, the output does not change at all — that is ''invariance'', a special case of equivariance. In the general case, the output transforms under some (possibly different) representation of the same group.

This lens turns architecture design from an art into a systematic procedure. You specify three things: (1) the ''domain'' your data lives on (a grid, a graph, a manifold, a set), (2) the ''symmetry group'' of that domain (translations, rotations, permutations, gauge transformations), and (3) the ''signal'' on that domain (pixel values, node features, vector fields). The architecture follows: it is the most general linear map that commutes with the group action, composed with pointwise nonlinearities.

---

!! The Five Domains: Grids, Groups, Graphs, Geodesics, Gauges

GDL organises data domains into a hierarchy, each progressively more general:

''Grids'' are the simplest case. Images live on a regular 2D grid, and the relevant symmetry group is the translation group. A <<link "CNN" "CNN">><</link>> is exactly a translation-equivariant neural network: sliding a filter across an image produces the same response regardless of where the pattern appears. Weight sharing — the same filter applied at every position — is not a clever trick; it is the //unique// consequence of demanding translation equivariance.

''Groups and homogeneous spaces'' generalise grids. Instead of just translations, we may want equivariance under rotations (the group $\text{SO}(2)$ in 2D, $\text{SO}(3)$ in 3D) or the full Euclidean group. Steerable CNNs and spherical CNNs implement these symmetries for applications like cryo-EM reconstruction and omnidirectional vision.

''Graphs'' replace regular structure with arbitrary connectivity. The symmetry group is the permutation group acting on nodes, but only //local// permutations that respect the edge structure. <<link "Graph Neural Networks" "Graph Neural Networks">><</link>> achieve this through message passing: each node aggregates information from its neighbours using a permutation-invariant function (sum, mean, max). The Weisfeiler-Leman hierarchy characterises the expressive power of such architectures.

''Geodesics (manifolds)'' generalise graphs to continuous curved surfaces — meshes, point clouds, protein surfaces. There is no global coordinate system, so convolution must be defined intrinsically. Geodesic CNNs, MoNet, and DiffusionNet all define local patches using geodesic distance or the Laplace-Beltrami operator.

''Gauges'' are the most general case. On a manifold, there is a choice of local reference frame (a //gauge//) at each point, and the physics must not depend on that choice. ''Gauge-equivariant CNNs'' ensure that rotating the local frame at any point produces a correspondingly rotated output, making the network truly coordinate-free. This is the deep-learning analogue of gauge theory in physics.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The mathematical backbone of GDL is <<link "Group Theory" "Group Theory">><</link>> and representation theory. A ''group'' $G$ is a set of transformations closed under composition with an identity and inverses. A ''representation'' $\rho: G \to \text{GL}(V)$ describes how $G$ acts on a vector space $V$.

A function $f: X \to Y$ is ''equivariant'' with respect to $G$ if:

$$f(T_g(x)) = T'_g(f(x)) \quad \forall g \in G$$

where $T_g$ is the action on inputs and $T'_g$ is the (possibly different) action on outputs. When $T'_g$ is the identity for all $g$, we call $f$ ''invariant''.

The key theorem is that the most general ''linear'' equivariant map between two representations is a generalised convolution. For a function $f$ on a group $G$ with kernel $\psi$:

$$[f \star \psi](g) = \int_G f(h) \, \psi(g^{-1} h) \, dh$$

This recovers standard convolution on grids (where $G = \mathbb{Z}^2$ or $\mathbb{R}^2$), group convolution on rotation groups, and message passing on graphs (where the integral becomes a sum over neighbours):

$$h_v^{(\ell+1)} = \phi\!\left( h_v^{(\ell)},\; \bigoplus_{u \in \mathcal{N}(v)} \psi(h_v^{(\ell)}, h_u^{(\ell)}, e_{vu}) \right)$$

Here $\bigoplus$ is a permutation-invariant aggregation (sum, mean), $\psi$ is the message function, and $\phi$ is the update function. <<link "Equivariance" "Equivariance">><</link>> under node permutations follows because neither $\psi$ nor $\bigoplus$ depend on node ordering.

For gauge equivariance on manifolds, the kernel $\psi$ must transform under a gauge transformation $\rho(g)$ applied at the tangent space:

$$\psi'(\theta) = \rho(g) \, \psi(\theta - \alpha)$$

where $\alpha$ is the rotation angle relating two local frames. This constraint restricts the kernel to circular harmonics — an elegant example of symmetry dictating parameterisation.

!! From Theory to Practice

The GDL framework has driven concrete advances across domains:

''Molecular property prediction and drug discovery:'' Molecules are naturally graphs with 3D geometry. Models like SchNet, DimeNet, and MACE use SE(3)-equivariant message passing to predict quantum-mechanical properties (energies, forces) at a fraction of the cost of density functional theory. Equivariance ensures that rotating a molecule in space does not change the predicted energy — a physical constraint baked into the architecture rather than learned from data.

''Protein structure prediction:'' AlphaFold2 uses invariant point attention — a form of SE(3)-invariant self-attention — in its structure module. The equivariant treatment of 3D coordinates was essential to achieving atomic-level accuracy. GDL thinking influenced the design of the pair representation and the iterative refinement of atom positions.

''Particle physics and cosmology:'' Lorentz-equivariant networks process particle collision data respecting the symmetries of special relativity. Gauge-equivariant lattice CNNs simulate quantum chromodynamics without breaking gauge symmetry, enabling faster Monte Carlo sampling.

''Robotics and computer vision:'' SE(3)-equivariant models for point cloud processing (Vector Neurons, Tensor Field Networks) enable manipulation policies that work regardless of the orientation of objects, drastically reducing the amount of training data needed.

---

!! When Geometric Deep Learning Breaks Down

''Approximate or broken symmetries:'' GDL works best when symmetries are exact. Many real-world problems have //approximate// symmetries: a photograph has approximate but not exact translation symmetry (objects near the edge behave differently from those at the centre); protein folding has approximate SE(3) symmetry broken by gravity and membrane interactions. Enforcing exact equivariance in these cases can //hurt// performance by preventing the network from learning symmetry-breaking patterns. Relaxation strategies — soft equivariance, data augmentation, or equivariance only in early layers — are active research areas.

''Computational cost of higher-order representations:'' Equivariant networks on groups like SO(3) require tensor products of irreducible representations, and the cost of these tensor products scales as $O(L^6)$ for maximum angular momentum order $L$. For applications requiring high-frequency spatial detail (large $L$), equivariant models can be orders of magnitude slower than non-equivariant baselines. Approximations like eSCN and ESCN-$e3nn$ reduce this cost but sacrifice exactness.

''Expressiveness limits of message passing:'' The Weisfeiler-Leman framework shows that standard message-passing GNNs cannot distinguish certain non-isomorphic graphs (e.g., pairs of regular graphs with identical degree sequences). Higher-order GNNs (k-WL) gain expressiveness but at combinatorial cost. GDL identifies the problem cleanly but does not automatically solve it — the tension between equivariance and expressiveness remains fundamental.

!! Technical Details

''Key reference:'' Bronstein, Bruna, Cohen, Velickovic (2021), "Geometric Deep Learning: Grids, Groups, Graphs, Geodesics, and Gauges." This monograph unifies decades of work into a single framework, covering Cohen & Welling's group-equivariant CNNs (2016), Gilmer et al.'s message-passing neural networks (2017), and Cohen et al.'s gauge-equivariant CNNs (2019).

''Steerable CNNs:'' A filter $\psi$ on $\mathbb{R}^2$ is called steerable if rotating the input rotates the output by a known linear transformation. The steerability constraint forces $\psi$ to decompose into circular harmonics $e^{im\theta} R_m(r)$. For discrete rotation groups $C_n$, the constraint becomes a finite set of linear equations on the filter weights. This was extended to 3D by Thomas et al. (2018) using spherical harmonics $Y_l^m$.

''Weisfeiler-Leman hierarchy:'' 1-WL (colour refinement) iteratively hashes each node's colour with the multiset of its neighbours' colours. Two graphs are 1-WL equivalent if the process produces the same colour histogram. Xu et al. (2019) proved that message-passing GNNs are at most as powerful as 1-WL. Higher-order k-WL tests operate on k-tuples of nodes and are strictly more powerful but require $O(n^k)$ memory.

''Invariant theory connection:'' The set of all polynomial invariants of a group action is finitely generated (Hilbert's basis theorem). GDL leverages this: for SO(3), the invariant features of a pair of vectors $u, v$ are $\|u\|, \|v\|, \langle u, v \rangle$ — exactly the features used in invariant message-passing architectures like SchNet.

''Gauge equivariance:'' On a Riemannian manifold, parallel transport provides a canonical way to compare vectors at different tangent spaces. Gauge-equivariant CNNs use the Levi-Civita connection to transport filters along the manifold, ensuring that the convolution output does not depend on the choice of local coordinates. The gauge group is $\text{SO}(2)$ for surfaces and $\text{SO}(d)$ for $d$-dimensional manifolds.

''Relationship to physics:'' The GDL blueprint mirrors the construction of physical theories via symmetry. Noether's theorem states that every continuous symmetry implies a conservation law; analogously, every continuous equivariance in a neural network implies a structural constraint on learnable functions that reduces sample complexity and improves generalisation.
