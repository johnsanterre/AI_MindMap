! Maximum Flow

<img src="assets/maximum_flow.svg" alt="Maximum Flow: flow network with source, sink, edge capacities, flow values, and min-cut illustration" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine a city's water system: water enters at a reservoir (the ''source''), travels through pipes of varying widths, and exits at a treatment plant (the ''sink''). Each pipe has a maximum capacity — you cannot push more water through it than its diameter allows. The ''maximum flow problem'' asks: what is the greatest total volume of water you can deliver from source to sink simultaneously, without exceeding any pipe's capacity? This question turns out to be one of the most useful in all of [[Graph Theory|Graph Theory]] and [[Combinatorics|Combinatorics]], with applications stretching from network routing to machine learning.

!! The Core Insight

Every flow network has a fundamental duality: the maximum amount of flow you can push from source to sink equals the minimum total capacity you would need to cut to completely disconnect source from sink. This is the ''max-flow min-cut theorem'', proved independently by Ford and Fulkerson and by Elias, Feinstein, and Shannon in 1956. It means that finding the bottleneck (the min cut) is //exactly the same problem// as finding the optimal flow. A min cut partitions the nodes into two sets — one containing the source, one containing the sink — and the cut's capacity is the sum of capacities on edges crossing from the source side to the sink side. No matter how cleverly you route flow, you can never exceed this bottleneck.

!! Augmenting Paths and Algorithms

The ''Ford-Fulkerson method'' builds up a maximum flow incrementally. Start with zero flow everywhere. At each step, find an ''augmenting path'' — a route from source to sink in the ''residual graph'' (the graph of remaining capacity on each edge, plus backward edges representing flow that can be "undone"). Push as much flow as possible along this path. Repeat until no augmenting path exists.

The choice of augmenting path matters enormously. If you pick paths arbitrarily on graphs with irrational capacities, Ford-Fulkerson may not even terminate. The ''Edmonds-Karp algorithm'' fixes this by always choosing the //shortest// augmenting path (via BFS), guaranteeing termination in $O(VE^2)$ time regardless of capacities. ''Dinic's algorithm'' improves this to $O(V^2 E)$ by constructing layered graphs and finding blocking flows.

The ''push-relabel'' family takes a fundamentally different approach. Instead of finding source-to-sink paths, it works locally: nodes accumulate ''excess'' flow, then ''push'' excess to neighbors or ''relabel'' themselves (increase their height) when they cannot push. The highest-label variant runs in $O(V^2 \sqrt{E})$, making it the fastest known algorithm for dense graphs.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

A ''flow network'' is a directed graph $G = (V, E)$ with:
- A ''source'' $s \in V$ and a ''sink'' $t \in V$
- A ''capacity function'' $c : E \to \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$

A ''flow'' is a function $f : E \to \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ satisfying:
- ''Capacity constraint:'' $0 \leq f(u,v) \leq c(u,v)$ for all $(u,v) \in E$
- ''Flow conservation:'' $\sum_{(u,v) \in E} f(u,v) = \sum_{(v,w) \in E} f(v,w)$ for all $v \in V \setminus \{s, t\}$

The ''value'' of a flow is $|f| = \sum_{(s,v) \in E} f(s,v) - \sum_{(v,s) \in E} f(v,s)$.

The ''residual graph'' $G_f$ has edges with residual capacity:
$$c_f(u,v) = c(u,v) - f(u,v) + f(v,u)$$

''Max-flow min-cut theorem:''
$$\max_f |f| = \min_{(S,T)} \sum_{\substack{u \in S \\ v \in T}} c(u,v)$$

where the minimum is over all ''$s$-$t$ cuts'' $(S,T)$ with $s \in S$, $t \in T$, $S \cup T = V$.

The dual formulation connects directly to [[Linear Programming|Linear Programming]]: the max-flow problem is an LP, and its dual is the min-cut problem. Strong LP duality guarantees the theorem.

!! Applications in Machine Learning

''Graph-cut image segmentation.'' Given an image, construct a graph where each pixel is a node. The source represents "foreground" and the sink represents "background." Edge capacities encode pixel similarity (neighbors with similar colors get high capacity between them) and data terms (how likely each pixel is foreground vs. background). A minimum cut partitions pixels into foreground and background, minimizing both boundary irregularity and data disagreement. This is the foundation of the GrabCut algorithm and many interactive [[Image Segmentation|Image Segmentation]] tools.

''MAP inference in Markov Random Fields.'' For binary MRFs with submodular pairwise potentials, finding the maximum a posteriori (MAP) labeling is //exactly// a minimum cut problem. Each variable's two states correspond to the source side and sink side. The QPBO (Quadratic Pseudo-Boolean Optimization) framework extends this to non-submodular potentials by solving a relaxed max-flow and identifying persistently optimal variables.

''Network analysis and matching.'' Maximum flow directly computes the maximum number of edge-disjoint paths between two nodes (Menger's theorem). In [[Bipartite Graphs|Bipartite Graphs]], maximum matching reduces to max flow: add a source connected to all left nodes and a sink connected to all right nodes, with unit capacities. The max flow equals the maximum matching size. This appears in assignment problems, resource allocation, and recommendation systems.

!! When Maximum Flow Breaks Down

''(1) Integer capacities hide exponential runtime for Ford-Fulkerson.'' The basic Ford-Fulkerson method with DFS path selection runs in $O(E \cdot |f^*|)$ where $|f^*|$ is the max flow value. When capacities are large integers, this can be astronomically slow — a graph with just 4 nodes and capacity $10^9$ may require a billion iterations. Edmonds-Karp or push-relabel eliminate this pathology, but naive implementations of Ford-Fulkerson remain a common trap.

''(2) Non-submodular energies break the graph-cut guarantee.'' The elegant reduction of binary MRF inference to min cut only works when pairwise potentials are ''submodular'' (roughly: agreement is preferred over disagreement). For non-submodular potentials — common in higher-order MRFs and some texture models — the reduction produces negative capacities, and the min-cut solution may be only partial (QPBO) or require expensive move-making approximations like alpha-expansion.

''(3) Static graph assumption fails for streaming and dynamic settings.'' Standard max-flow algorithms assume the graph is fully known and fixed. In dynamic networks — communication networks with fluctuating bandwidth, evolving social graphs, online bipartite matching — edge capacities change over time. Recomputing max flow from scratch after each update is wasteful. Dynamic max-flow algorithms exist but are significantly more complex, and approximation becomes necessary at scale.

!! Technical Details

''Complexity landscape.'' For a graph with $V$ nodes and $E$ edges:
- Ford-Fulkerson (DFS): $O(E \cdot |f^*|)$ — pseudo-polynomial
- Edmonds-Karp (BFS): $O(VE^2)$
- Dinic's algorithm: $O(V^2 E)$, but $O(E\sqrt{V})$ for unit-capacity graphs
- Push-relabel (highest label): $O(V^2 \sqrt{E})$
- Orlin's algorithm: $O(VE)$ — theoretically optimal for sparse graphs

''Multi-commodity flow.'' When multiple commodities share the same network (e.g., different data streams sharing links), the problem becomes NP-hard for integer flows. The LP relaxation is polynomial but may have fractional solutions.

''Minimum cost flow.'' Adds per-unit costs on edges: minimize total cost while achieving a target flow value. Solved via successive shortest path algorithms or network simplex. Generalizes both shortest path and maximum flow.

''Gomory-Hu tree.'' For undirected graphs, all $\binom{n}{2}$ pairwise max flows can be represented by a single weighted tree with $n-1$ edges. Computing it requires only $n-1$ max-flow computations.

''Parametric max flow.'' When source/sink edge capacities depend on a parameter $\lambda$, the optimal cut changes at $O(n)$ breakpoints. This is exploited in image segmentation for efficiently solving families of graph-cut problems with varying foreground/background tradeoffs.

See also: [[Graph Theory|Graph Theory]], [[Bipartite Graphs|Bipartite Graphs]], [[Linear Programming|Linear Programming]], [[Image Segmentation|Image Segmentation]], [[Combinatorics|Combinatorics]].
