! Probability Theory

<img src="assets/probability_theory.svg" alt="Diagram showing sample space with events, random variable mapping, and probability distribution with shaded area" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are standing in a dark room full of doors. You cannot see which doors lead somewhere useful and which open onto brick walls, but you have been through this room many times before. Over those visits you have built an internal map — not of certainty, but of //tendency//. "That door on the left works about 70% of the time." ''Probability theory'' is the mathematics of that internal map. It gives you a rigorous language for quantifying uncertainty, updating beliefs when new evidence arrives, and making optimal decisions when you cannot know outcomes in advance. Every machine learning model — from a simple logistic regression to a billion-parameter language model — is, at its core, a machine for manipulating probabilities.

!! The Core Insight

Probability assigns a number between 0 and 1 to ''events'' — subsets of a ''sample space'' $\Omega$ that contains every possible outcome. The entire framework rests on three axioms (Kolmogorov, 1933):

$$P(\Omega) = 1, \quad P(A) \geq 0, \quad P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} P(A_i) \text{ for disjoint } A_i$$

From these three rules — totality, non-negativity, and ''countable additivity'' — the entire edifice of probability follows: conditional probability, independence, expectation, variance, the law of large numbers, and the central limit theorem. The power is in the compression: three axioms generate a universe of results.

!! Axioms, Random Variables, and Distributions

A ''random variable'' $X: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ is a function that assigns a numerical value to each outcome. It transforms abstract sample spaces into numbers you can compute with. The ''distribution'' of $X$ describes the probability of $X$ falling in any given set:

$$F(x) = P(X \leq x)$$

This ''cumulative distribution function'' (CDF) completely characterizes the distribution. For ''discrete'' random variables, probability concentrates on countable points via a ''probability mass function'' (PMF): $p(x) = P(X = x)$. For ''continuous'' random variables, probability spreads over intervals via a ''probability density function'' (PDF) $f(x)$ where:

$$P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_a^b f(x) \, dx$$

Key distributions appear everywhere in ML. The ''Gaussian'' (normal) distribution $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with density $f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}$ is the default model for noise and uncertainty. The ''Bernoulli'' distribution models binary outcomes. The ''categorical'' distribution generalizes to multiple classes. The ''exponential family'' — which includes Gaussian, Bernoulli, Poisson, and many others — unifies most distributions used in practice under a single algebraic form: $p(x|\theta) = h(x) \exp(\eta(\theta)^T T(x) - A(\theta))$.

''Conditional probability'' captures how evidence updates belief:

$$P(A|B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$$

This leads directly to [[Bayes Theorem|Bayes Theorem]], which inverts conditioning: $P(\theta|D) \propto P(D|\theta) P(\theta)$. Two events are ''independent'' when $P(A \cap B) = P(A) P(B)$, meaning knowing one tells you nothing about the other. ''Conditional independence'' — $P(A \cap B | C) = P(A|C) P(B|C)$ — is the structural assumption behind graphical models, naive Bayes, and much of Bayesian network reasoning.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ''expectation'' of a random variable is its probability-weighted average:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_\Omega X(\omega) \, dP(\omega) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x \, f(x) \, dx$$

This is a Lebesgue integral against the probability measure $P$. The ''variance'' $\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mathbb{E}[X])^2] = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$ measures spread. The ''covariance'' $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mathbb{E}[X])(Y - \mathbb{E}[Y])]$ measures linear dependence.

The ''law of large numbers'' (LLN) guarantees that sample averages converge to expectations:

$$\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i \xrightarrow{a.s.} \mathbb{E}[X]$$

The [[Central Limit Theorem|Central Limit Theorem]] (CLT) describes //how// they converge — the fluctuations around $\mathbb{E}[X]$ become Gaussian:

$$\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$$

''Moment-generating functions'' $M_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tX}]$ and ''characteristic functions'' $\varphi_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{itX}]$ encode all moments and uniquely determine distributions. The ''Jensen's inequality'' — $\mathbb{E}[g(X)] \geq g(\mathbb{E}[X])$ for convex $g$ — underpins the evidence lower bound (ELBO) in variational inference.

Joint distributions for multiple [[Random Variables|Random Variables]] are described by joint densities $f(x,y)$, with ''marginals'' obtained by integrating out: $f_X(x) = \int f(x,y) \, dy$. [[Conditional Probability|Conditional Probability]] densities $f(y|x) = f(x,y)/f_X(x)$ are the mathematical objects that classifiers and regressors learn to approximate.

!! Probability in Machine Learning

Probability is not an optional perspective in ML — it is the native language of the field:

''Supervised learning'' is density estimation in disguise. A classifier learns $P(Y|X)$ — the conditional distribution of labels given features. Maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) finds parameters $\hat{\theta} = \arg\max_\theta \prod_i P(x_i|\theta)$, which is equivalent to minimizing the negative log-likelihood (cross-entropy loss).

''Bayesian inference'' treats parameters as random variables with prior distributions: $P(\theta|D) \propto P(D|\theta) P(\theta)$. The posterior captures //all// uncertainty about $\theta$ given data, not just a point estimate. This connects directly to [[Bayes Theorem|Bayes Theorem]].

''Generative models'' learn the full joint $P(X)$ or $P(X, Y)$, then sample from it. VAEs maximize a lower bound on $\log P(X)$; GANs implicitly match an empirical distribution; diffusion models learn to reverse a noise process that converges to a known distribution.

''Information theory'' translates probability into communication. The entropy $H(X) = -\mathbb{E}[\log P(X)]$ measures uncertainty; KL divergence $D_{KL}(P \| Q) = \mathbb{E}_P[\log \frac{P}{Q}]$ measures distribution mismatch; cross-entropy $H(P, Q) = H(P) + D_{KL}(P \| Q)$ is the loss function for classification.

''Concentration inequalities'' — Markov, Chebyshev, Hoeffding, Bernstein — bound how far random quantities deviate from expectations. They are the workhorses of PAC learning theory, bounding generalization error for finite samples.

!! When Probability Assumptions Break Down

''Independence assumptions are rarely true.'' Naive Bayes assumes features are conditionally independent given the class — almost never literally correct. Yet it often works surprisingly well because the //ranking// of $P(Y|X)$ can be correct even when the //calibration// is wrong. More dangerously, assuming i.i.d. (independent, identically distributed) data fails for time series, spatial data, and any setting where observations influence future data collection. Models trained under false independence assumptions systematically underestimate uncertainty.

''Distributional shift breaks probabilistic guarantees.'' Every theorem in probability — LLN, CLT, generalization bounds — assumes the data-generating distribution is fixed. In deployment, the distribution shifts: new demographics appear, user behavior evolves, adversaries attack. A model that perfectly estimates $P(Y|X)$ under the training distribution may produce wildly miscalibrated probabilities under a shifted distribution. Techniques like domain adaptation and conformal prediction attempt to provide guarantees under shift, but fundamental limits exist.

''Probability cannot capture deep uncertainty.'' Probability quantifies ''aleatory uncertainty'' (inherent randomness) well, but struggles with ''epistemic uncertainty'' (ignorance about the model itself). When you do not know which probability model to use — is this Gaussian or heavy-tailed? — assigning a probability to that meta-question involves subjective priors with no ground truth. Techniques like [[Measure Theory|Measure Theory]]-based imprecise probabilities, Dempster-Shafer theory, and ensemble disagreement attempt to separate these uncertainty types, but no framework fully resolves the problem of "unknown unknowns."

!! Technical Details

''Kolmogorov's axioms'' (1933) formalized probability as a special case of [[Measure Theory|Measure Theory]]: a probability space is a triple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ where $\mathcal{F}$ is a $\sigma$-algebra and $P$ is a measure with $P(\Omega) = 1$.

''Convergence modes'' (from weakest to strongest): in distribution ($X_n \xrightarrow{d} X$), in probability ($X_n \xrightarrow{P} X$), in $L^p$ ($\mathbb{E}[|X_n - X|^p] \to 0$), almost surely ($P(\lim X_n = X) = 1$).

''Borel-Cantelli lemmas'': if $\sum P(A_n) < \infty$, then $P(\limsup A_n) = 0$ (finitely many $A_n$ occur a.s.). If events are independent and $\sum P(A_n) = \infty$, then $P(\limsup A_n) = 1$ (infinitely many occur a.s.).

''Markov's inequality'': $P(X \geq a) \leq \mathbb{E}[X]/a$ for non-negative $X$. ''Chebyshev's inequality'': $P(|X - \mu| \geq k\sigma) \leq 1/k^2$. ''Hoeffding's inequality'': for bounded i.i.d. random variables, $P(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq t) \leq 2\exp(-2nt^2/(b-a)^2)$.

''Characteristic function uniqueness'': two distributions are identical if and only if their characteristic functions agree everywhere. ''Levy's continuity theorem'': pointwise convergence of characteristic functions implies convergence in distribution.
