! Stochastic Processes
<img src="assets/stochastic_processes.svg" alt="Diagram showing random walk, Brownian motion, Markov chain, and Poisson process as examples of stochastic processes underlying diffusion models, MCMC, and RL" style="max-width:100%;margin:1em 0;">

Imagine a drunk person stumbling home, taking a random step in some direction every second. Where will they be after an hour? You can't predict the exact path, but you //can// describe the ''probability distribution'' over all possible locations — and that distribution follows beautiful mathematical laws. A ''stochastic process'' is any system whose evolution involves randomness, studied not path-by-path but through the statistics of all possible paths. Nearly every modern ML system — from [[Diffusion Models]] generating images to [[MCMC]] sampling posteriors to RL agents navigating environments — is built on the mathematics of random processes evolving over time.

!! The Core Insight

A ''stochastic process'' is a collection of random variables $\{X(t)\}$ indexed by time (or space). The key taxonomy splits along two axes: //discrete vs. continuous time// and //discrete vs. continuous state//. Discrete-time discrete-state processes give us ''Markov chains'' (PageRank, MCMC). Discrete-time continuous-state processes give us ''random walks'' and autoregressive models ([[ARIMA]]). Continuous-time continuous-state processes give us ''Brownian motion'' and diffusion — the mathematical engine behind [[Diffusion Models]].

The single most important simplification is the ''Markov property'': the future depends only on the present, not the past. Formally, $P(X_{t+1} | X_t, X_{t-1}, \ldots, X_0) = P(X_{t+1} | X_t)$. This is why [[Markov Decision Process|Markov Decision Processes]], [[Hidden Markov Machine|Hidden Markov Models]], and diffusion SDEs are mathematically tractable — the entire past compresses into the current state.

Links: [[Markov Decision Process]], [[Hidden Markov Machine]], [[MCMC]], [[Diffusion Models]], [[ARIMA]], [[Stationary Time Series]]

!! Markov Chains and Random Walks

A ''discrete-state Markov chain'' is defined by a ''transition matrix'' $P$ where $P_{ij} = P(X_{t+1} = j \mid X_t = i)$. The rows sum to 1. The central question: does the chain converge to a ''stationary distribution'' $\pi$ satisfying $\pi P = \pi$? For irreducible, aperiodic chains, the answer is yes — the ''ergodic theorem'' guarantees that time averages converge to ensemble averages.

A ''random walk'' on the integers is the simplest stochastic process: $X_{t+1} = X_t + Z_t$ where $Z_t \in \{-1, +1\}$ with equal probability. After $n$ steps, the position is approximately $\mathcal{N}(0, n)$ — the [[Central Limit Theorem]] in action. The variance grows linearly with time, which is the signature of ''diffusive'' behavior.

''PageRank'' is a Markov chain on the web graph — the stationary distribution assigns importance to each page. ''MCMC sampling'' constructs a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is the target posterior — run the chain long enough, and samples approximate the posterior. [[Metropolis-Hastings]] and Hamiltonian Monte Carlo are specific recipes for building such chains.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''Brownian motion'' $W(t)$ is the continuous-time limit of random walks. Its defining properties: $W(0) = 0$, independent increments, and $W(t) - W(s) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, t - s)$. Despite being continuous everywhere, Brownian motion is differentiable //nowhere// — it is fundamentally rough.

''Itô calculus'' extends calculus to handle this roughness. A ''stochastic differential equation'' (SDE) takes the form:

$$dX = \mu(X, t)\,dt + \sigma(X, t)\,dW_t$$

The $\mu\,dt$ term is a deterministic drift; the $\sigma\,dW_t$ term injects randomness. The ''forward SDE'' for diffusion models progressively adds noise to data:

$$dx = f(x, t)\,dt + g(t)\,dW_t$$

The ''reverse SDE'' removes noise using the learned ''score function'' $\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$:

$$dx = \left[f(x, t) - g(t)^2 \nabla_x \log p_t(x)\right]dt + g(t)\,d\bar{W}_t$$

This reverse SDE is precisely how [[Diffusion Models]] generate images — start from pure noise, follow the reverse process back to data. The score function tells the process which direction points toward the data manifold at each noise level.

''Martingales'' are processes satisfying $\mathbb{E}[X_{t+1} | X_1, \ldots, X_t] = X_t$ — "fair game" processes where the expected future value equals the current value. The ''optional stopping theorem'' and ''martingale convergence theorem'' underlie convergence proofs across RL, optimization, and statistical estimation.

!! Stochastic Processes in ML

''Diffusion models:'' The forward process is an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process (mean-reverting SDE). [[Score Matching]] learns the score function; sampling follows the reverse SDE. Song et al. 2021 unified DDPM and NCSN under this SDE framework.

''MCMC:'' [[Metropolis-Hastings]], Gibbs sampling, and Hamiltonian Monte Carlo all construct Markov chains to sample from posterior distributions. The key challenge is ''mixing time'' — how long until the chain forgets its initialization and produces independent samples.

''Reinforcement learning:'' The agent-environment interaction is a ''controlled Markov process'' — specifically a [[Markov Decision Process]] where actions influence transition probabilities. The [[Bellman Equation]] is the fundamental recursion for value functions over this process.

''Gaussian processes:'' A stochastic process where any finite collection of random variables is jointly Gaussian. Used for [[Bayesian]] regression and uncertainty quantification — the posterior over functions is itself a Gaussian process. The kernel function controls the prior over function smoothness.

''Poisson processes:'' Model event arrivals (clicks, purchases, equipment failures) where events occur independently at a constant rate $\lambda$. The count $N(t) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda t)$. Inter-arrival times are exponentially distributed. ''Hawkes processes'' extend this with self-excitation — one event increases the rate of future events, modeling social media cascades and earthquake aftershocks.

!! When Stochastic Processes Break Down

''Non-Markovian dynamics:'' Real systems often have long memory — a stock price depends on trends, not just the current value. When the Markov assumption fails, the state space is incomplete. Solutions: augment the state with history (e.g., stack frames in RL), use [[Hidden Markov Machine|Hidden Markov Models]] to model latent Markov state, or use fractional Brownian motion for long-range dependence.

''Heavy-tailed noise:'' Brownian motion assumes Gaussian increments, but financial returns, network traffic, and natural language exhibit heavy tails — rare extreme events occur far more often than Gaussians predict. Lévy processes (with stable or Pareto-distributed jumps) and Student-$t$ processes are alternatives, but lose many of the clean analytical results that make Gaussian processes tractable.

''High-dimensional state spaces:'' Classical Markov chain analysis scales poorly. Mixing times can grow exponentially with dimension — the ''curse of dimensionality'' hits MCMC hardest. Modern solutions include Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (exploits gradient geometry), variational inference (sidesteps sampling entirely), and score-based methods that learn the gradient field with neural networks.

!! Technical Details

''Historical foundations:'' Bachelier 1900 (stock price modeling — predating Einstein), Einstein 1905 (Brownian motion in physics), Wiener 1923 (rigorous construction), Kolmogorov 1931 (axiomatic probability, Chapman-Kolmogorov equation), Itô 1944 (stochastic calculus), Doob 1953 (martingale theory).

''Chapman-Kolmogorov equation:'' $p(x_3, t_3 | x_1, t_1) = \int p(x_3, t_3 | x_2, t_2)\, p(x_2, t_2 | x_1, t_1)\, dx_2$ — the Markov property expressed as an integral equation on transition densities.

''Fokker-Planck equation:'' Describes how the probability density $p(x, t)$ evolves under an SDE. For $dX = \mu\,dt + \sigma\,dW$:

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial x}[\mu\, p] + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}[\sigma^2 p]$$

''Ergodicity and mixing:'' An ergodic chain has a unique stationary distribution and time averages converge to expectations. ''Mixing time'' $t_\text{mix}$ is the number of steps until the chain is within total variation distance $1/4$ of stationarity. For many practical chains, $t_\text{mix} = O(n \log n)$ where $n$ is the state space size.

''Modern ML connections:'' Song et al. 2021 (score-based SDEs for generative modeling), Langevin dynamics (gradient-based MCMC using score functions), connection to statistical physics (Boltzmann distribution, Langevin equation, detailed balance).
