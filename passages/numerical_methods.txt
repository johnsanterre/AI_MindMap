! Numerical Methods

<img src="assets/numerical_methods.svg" alt="Diagram comparing numerical integration schemes and showing convergence rates for ODE solvers" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

A structural engineer needs to know how a bridge deforms under load — described by a system of differential equations with no closed-form solution. They discretize the structure into finite elements, approximate derivatives with differences, and solve a large sparse linear system on a computer. This is ''numerical methods'': the art of computing accurate answers to mathematical problems that have no analytical solution, with provable error bounds and stability guarantees. Every time a neural ODE is integrated, a PDE is solved in a physics-informed network, or a linear system is solved in a GNN, numerical methods are the machinery underneath.

!! The Core Insight

Continuous mathematics — derivatives, integrals, differential equations — is approximated by //discrete operations// with controllable error. The key questions are: ''accuracy'' (how close is the approximation to the true answer?), ''stability'' (do errors grow or shrink as computation proceeds?), and ''efficiency'' (how fast does the algorithm converge to a solution?). A good numerical method balances all three: it finds an answer that is close enough, remains well-behaved, and does so without excessive computation.

!! Root Finding and Linear Systems

''Newton's method'' for finding roots of $f(x) = 0$ iterates:

$$x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}$$

Near a simple root, convergence is //quadratic// — the number of correct digits roughly doubles each step. ''Bisection'' is slower ($O(\log 1/\varepsilon)$ steps) but robustly guaranteed to converge.

For linear systems $Ax = b$, ''Gaussian elimination'' costs $O(n^3)$ operations. ''LU factorization'' and ''Cholesky decomposition'' (for symmetric positive-definite $A$) are the standard direct methods. For large sparse systems — common in graph problems and PDEs — iterative methods like ''Conjugate Gradient (CG)'' and ''GMRES'' are far more practical, exploiting sparsity to avoid the $O(n^3)$ cost.

The ''condition number'' $\kappa(A) = \|A\| \cdot \|A^{-1}\|$ is the central diagnostic: it measures how much perturbations in $b$ (or in $A$) are amplified in the solution $x$. A condition number of $10^6$ means you lose 6 digits of precision — if you started with 16-digit double precision, you are left with only 10 reliable digits. Ill-conditioned systems are numerically dangerous even when theoretically well-posed.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''Finite differences'' approximate derivatives:

$$f'(x) \approx \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \quad \text{(forward, error } O(h)\text{)}$$

$$f'(x) \approx \frac{f(x+h) - f(x-h)}{2h} \quad \text{(central, error } O(h^2)\text{)}$$

Choosing $h$ is a tradeoff: smaller $h$ reduces truncation error but increases floating-point roundoff error (since you subtract nearly equal quantities). The optimal $h$ for central differences is approximately $\varepsilon_{\text{mach}}^{1/3} \approx 10^{-5}$.

''Runge-Kutta methods'' solve ODEs $\dot{y} = f(t, y)$ by taking weighted combinations of slope estimates within each time step. The classical ''RK4'' method uses four slope evaluations per step and achieves global error $O(h^4)$. ''Adaptive'' Runge-Kutta methods (e.g., Dormand-Prince / dopri5) automatically adjust $h$ to maintain a target local error tolerance.

''Numerical integration'' (quadrature) approximates $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$:

- Trapezoidal rule: error $O(h^2)$
- Simpson's rule: error $O(h^4)$
- Gaussian quadrature ($n$-point): error $O(h^{2n})$ — exponentially better for smooth functions

''Stability'' is a separate concern from accuracy. For ODEs, ''A-stability'' means the method remains bounded for all step sizes when applied to $\dot{y} = \lambda y$ with $\text{Re}(\lambda) < 0$. ''Stiff'' ODEs — with eigenvalues of very different magnitudes — require implicit methods (like implicit Euler or trapezoidal rule) that are A-stable; explicit methods require prohibitively small steps.

Machine epsilon for IEEE 754 double precision is $\varepsilon_{\text{mach}} \approx 2.2 \times 10^{-16}$, setting the floor on achievable accuracy. Errors accumulate through floating-point operations, and catastrophic cancellation (subtracting nearly equal numbers) can destroy precision instantly.

!! Numerical Methods in ML

''Neural ODEs'' use adaptive Runge-Kutta solvers (typically dopri5) to integrate the hidden-state ODE forward. Gradients are computed via the ''adjoint method'' (Pontryagin's Maximum Principle): instead of backpropagating through all solver steps (which would require storing the entire trajectory), a second ODE is solved backward in time, giving exact gradients at $O(1)$ memory cost.

''Diffusion model'' sampling is SDE/ODE solving in disguise. DDPM uses Euler-Maruyama (first-order), DDIM reformulates as an ODE solvable with higher-order methods, and solvers like DPM-Solver apply dedicated numerical ODE methods to dramatically reduce the number of function evaluations needed.

''Conjugate gradients'' appear in optimization: K-FAC (Kronecker-Factored Approximate Curvature) uses CG to approximately solve the natural gradient update, which involves inverting a large Fisher information matrix.

''Finite differences for gradient checking'' are the standard sanity check for backpropagation implementations: compute $[f(\theta + \varepsilon e_i) - f(\theta - \varepsilon e_i)] / (2\varepsilon)$ for each parameter $\theta_i$ and compare to the analytical gradient.

''Sparse matrix algorithms'' are central to <<link "Graph Neural Networks" "Graph Neural Network">><</link>>: adjacency matrix-vector products, sparse Cholesky for graph Laplacians, and spectral graph methods all rely on sparse numerical linear algebra.

!! When Numerical Methods Break Down

''(1) Stiffness.'' When an ODE has components evolving on very different timescales — for example, slow neural population dynamics coupled with fast synaptic transients — explicit solvers like RK4 must take tiny steps to remain stable, even in regions where the solution itself is barely changing. Neural ODEs with stiff dynamics can require thousands of function evaluations per forward pass, making training prohibitively slow. Implicit solvers handle stiffness but require solving a nonlinear system at each step, which is its own computational burden.

''(2) Ill-conditioning.'' When $\kappa(A) \geq 10^6$, solving $Ax = b$ in floating-point arithmetic can yield a computed solution that is completely wrong — the residual $\|Ax - b\|$ looks small but the error $\|x - x^*\|$ is large. This afflicts attention score computation (softmax of large dot products), kernel matrix inversion in Gaussian processes, and Fisher matrix inverses in natural gradient methods. Remedies include preconditioning, regularization (adding $\lambda I$ to shift eigenvalues), or reformulating the problem.

''(3) Numerical vs. automatic differentiation.'' Finite-difference gradients suffer from a fundamental tension: truncation error is $O(h)$ (forward) or $O(h^2)$ (central), while roundoff error is $O(\varepsilon_{\text{mach}}/h)$. The optimal step size $h^* \approx \sqrt{\varepsilon_{\text{mach}}} \approx 10^{-8}$ yields only about 8 correct digits — insufficient for high-precision training. Modern ML uses ''automatic differentiation'' (AD) instead, which is exact to floating-point precision by applying the chain rule to elementary operations. However, AD //through// numerical solvers (e.g., a black-box ODE integrator) requires careful treatment: non-differentiable solver control flow, discrete step-size changes, and event detection all create discontinuities that naive AD cannot handle correctly. The adjoint method is the standard solution.

!! Technical Details

Historical milestones: Euler's method (Euler, 1768); Runge-Kutta methods (Runge 1895, Kutta 1901); Gaussian elimination formalized by Gauss (~1810); CFL stability condition (Courant, Friedrichs, Lewy, 1928); IEEE 754 floating-point standard (1985); Neural ODEs connecting continuous dynamics to ResNets (Chen et al., 2018).

Key distinctions: ''numerical differentiation'' (finite differences, imprecise) vs. ''automatic differentiation'' (exact chain rule, used in all modern ML frameworks) vs. ''symbolic differentiation'' (exact but expression swell makes it impractical for large programs).
