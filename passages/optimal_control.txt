! Optimal Control

<img src="assets/optimal_control.svg" alt="Diagram showing optimal vs suboptimal trajectory and Bellman cost recursion" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine landing a rocket upright on a drone ship in the middle of the ocean. As the booster falls, you must fire thrusters at exactly the right moments — not too early, not too late, and never wastefully — so that the vehicle arrives precisely at the landing pad with zero velocity. Too much thrust wastes fuel; too little and you crash. ''Optimal control'' asks: what is the sequence of actions that minimizes total fuel spent while achieving the goal? It is the mathematics of making the best possible decisions over time in the face of physical constraints.

!! The Core Insight

Optimal control formalizes the idea of trading off //how much effort you spend// against //how far you deviate from your goal//. Rather than specifying a fixed rule for what to do, you define a ''cost functional'' $J$ — a single number summarizing how bad a trajectory is — and then find the policy that makes $J$ as small as possible.

The key structural insight, due to Richard Bellman, is the ''principle of optimality'': an optimal policy has the property that, at any point along the trajectory, the remaining decisions are themselves optimal with respect to the remaining cost. In other words, you cannot improve a globally optimal plan by changing what you do from some intermediate state onward. This recursive structure is what makes the problem tractable: instead of optimizing over entire trajectories at once, you can work backward in time, solving smaller subproblems and assembling the global solution from their answers.

This connects directly to <<link "dynamic programming" "dynamic programming">><</link>> and, when the dynamics are unknown, to <<link "Reinforcement Learning" "Reinforcement Learning">><</link>>.

!! The Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman Equation

The fundamental equation of continuous-time optimal control is the ''Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman (HJB) equation''. It characterizes the optimal ''value function'' $V^*(x, t)$ — the minimum cost achievable from state $x$ at time $t$:

$$-\frac{\partial V^*}{\partial t} = \min_u \left[ L(x, u) + \nabla_x V^* \cdot f(x, u) \right]$$

Here $L(x,u)$ is the ''running cost'' (e.g., fuel burned per unit time), $f(x,u)$ is the system dynamics, and $\nabla_x V^* \cdot f(x,u)$ is the rate at which the value function changes as the state evolves. The HJB equation says: at every moment, choose the control $u$ that balances the immediate cost against the future cost implied by moving in direction $f$.

For the special case of ''Linear Quadratic Regulation (LQR)'' — linear dynamics and quadratic costs — the HJB equation has a beautiful closed-form solution via the ''Riccati equation'', giving an optimal linear feedback law $u^* = -Kx$.

An alternative formulation is ''Pontryagin's Maximum Principle'', which gives necessary conditions for optimality in terms of a costate (adjoint) variable $\lambda(t)$, and is especially useful for problems with hard control constraints.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The formal optimal control problem is:

$$\min_{u(\cdot)} \; J = \int_0^T L(x(t), u(t)) \, dt + \phi(x(T))$$

subject to the state dynamics $\dot{x} = f(x, u)$, with $x(0) = x_0$ given. The terminal cost $\phi(x(T))$ penalizes the final state (e.g., distance from the landing pad). $L$ is the running cost penalizing effort or deviation along the way.

The HJB equation is the continuous-time analogue of the ''Bellman equation'' in discrete time:

$$V^*(x_t) = \min_u \left[ L(x_t, u_t) + V^*(x_{t+1}) \right]$$

This is exactly the recursive structure exploited by <<link "dynamic programming" "dynamic programming">><</link>> and <<link "Markov Decision Process" "Markov Decision Process">><</link>> solvers.

For the LQR special case, the dynamics are $\dot{x} = Ax + Bu$ and the cost is:

$$J = \int_0^\infty \left( x^\top Q x + u^\top R u \right) dt$$

where $Q \succeq 0$ penalizes state deviation and $R \succ 0$ penalizes control effort. The optimal policy is the linear feedback law:

$$u^* = -Kx, \quad K = R^{-1} B^\top P$$

where $P$ is the solution to the algebraic Riccati equation $A^\top P + PA - PBR^{-1}B^\top P + Q = 0$. LQR is the workhorse of classical control engineering and the foundation for many modern methods.

!! Optimal Control and Reinforcement Learning

Optimal control and <<link "Reinforcement Learning" "Reinforcement Learning">><</link>> are deeply connected — in fact, RL can be understood as optimal control with //unknown dynamics//. When $f(x,u)$ is not given analytically, an agent must learn it from experience. The value function $V^*$ that RL tries to learn is precisely the solution to the HJB (or Bellman) equation.

''Policy gradient'' methods in RL can be seen as a stochastic version of Pontryagin's Maximum Principle: the gradient of expected return with respect to policy parameters mirrors the adjoint sensitivity computation in continuous-time control.

''Model Predictive Control (MPC)'' applies optimal control online: at each timestep, solve a short-horizon optimal control problem from the current state, execute the first action, then re-solve. This receding-horizon approach handles constraints gracefully and is widely used in robotics, chemical plants, and autonomous vehicles.

''Iterative LQR (iLQR)'' and ''Differential Dynamic Programming (DDP)'' extend LQR to nonlinear systems by repeatedly linearizing around a nominal trajectory and solving local LQR problems. These are the backbone of modern trajectory optimization in robotics.

For connections to stability analysis of controlled systems, see <<link "Lyapunov Functions" "Lyapunov Functions">><</link>>. For the <<link "bellman equation" "bellman equation">><</link>> in discrete settings, and for how policies are defined over state spaces, see <<link "Markov Decision Process" "Markov Decision Process">><</link>>.

!! When Optimal Control Breaks Down

''Curse of dimensionality.'' The HJB equation is a PDE defined over the full state space. Solving it exactly requires discretizing every dimension, so the computational cost grows exponentially with state dimension. A system with 10 continuous state variables may already be intractable for grid-based methods. This is why RL and function approximation (neural networks as value functions) are needed for high-dimensional problems.

''Model sensitivity.'' An optimal policy derived from the wrong model can be catastrophically suboptimal — or outright unstable — when deployed on the real system. LQR, for example, has guaranteed gain and phase margins under certain conditions, but these guarantees vanish once the model is wrong. Robust control and probabilistic model-based RL (e.g., PILCO) address this, but at significant added complexity.

''Myopic cost functions.'' Optimizing over a short horizon, or specifying a cost function that doesn't capture what you truly care about, produces policies that sacrifice long-term performance. A robot trained to minimize a short-horizon proxy cost may learn to game the metric — reaching the goal but in a way that is physically destructive, unsafe, or brittle. Cost function design (reward shaping, inverse optimal control) is as important as the optimization itself.

!! Technical Details

Key historical milestones: Bellman (1957) introduced ''dynamic programming'' and the principle of optimality. Pontryagin et al. (1956) derived the ''Maximum Principle''. Kalman (1960) solved the LQR problem and showed the connection to the Riccati equation.

Modern trajectory optimization methods include ''iLQR'' (Tassa et al.) and ''DDP'' (Mayne 1966, revived for robotics). ''PILCO'' (Deisenroth & Rasmussen, 2011) introduced Gaussian process dynamics for sample-efficient model-based RL. ''PETS'' (Chua et al., 2018) uses probabilistic neural network ensembles for similar purposes.
