! Graph Laplacian

<img src="assets/graph_laplacian.svg" alt="Graph Laplacian: adjacency matrix A, degree matrix D, L = D - A, and eigenvalue spectrum" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine a metal plate with heat sensors at each rivet, connected by metal rods. Heat flows from hot rivets to cold rivets through every rod — and it flows faster when the temperature difference is larger. The ''Graph Laplacian'' describes exactly this process on a network. Given a graph where nodes hold values (temperatures, signal intensities, feature vectors), the Laplacian measures how different each node's value is from its neighbors. It is the discrete analog of the continuous Laplacian operator $\nabla^2$, and it encodes the entire connectivity structure of the graph in a single matrix.

!! The Core Insight

The Laplacian $L = D - A$ captures ''local contrast'': for each node $i$, it computes its degree $D_{ii}$ minus the sum of its neighbors' contributions via $A$. When you multiply $L$ by a signal vector $x$, the $i$-th entry of $Lx$ is the value at node $i$ minus the average of its neighbors — a measure of how much node $i$ ''sticks out'' from its local context.

The eigenvalues of $L$ reveal global structure. The smallest eigenvalue is always $\lambda_1 = 0$, corresponding to the constant eigenvector — a uniform signal has zero variation. The second eigenvalue $\lambda_2$, called the ''Fiedler value'' or ''algebraic connectivity'', is greater than zero if and only if the graph is connected. A small Fiedler value means the graph is nearly disconnected, with a narrow bottleneck. The corresponding ''Fiedler vector'' (second eigenvector) reveals which nodes are on either side of that bottleneck.

The full set of eigenvectors forms the ''graph Fourier basis'' — a complete set of frequency modes for signals living on the graph, from smoothly varying (low frequency, small eigenvalue) to rapidly oscillating (high frequency, large eigenvalue).

!! Spectral Graph Theory

''Spectral graph theory'' studies graphs through the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of matrices associated with them, principally the Laplacian $L$ and its normalized variants.

The ''graph Fourier transform'' of a signal $x \in \mathbb{R}^N$ is $\hat{x} = U^\top x$, where $U$ is the matrix of eigenvectors of $L$. The inverse transform reconstructs $x = U\hat{x}$. This mirrors the classical Fourier transform, with eigenvectors playing the role of sinusoids and eigenvalues playing the role of frequencies.

A key quantity is the ''Dirichlet energy'' or ''total variation'' of a signal on the graph:

$$x^\top L x = \sum_{(i,j) \in E} A_{ij}(x_i - x_j)^2$$

This is zero only for constant signals, and grows whenever neighboring nodes differ. Minimizing $x^\top Lx$ subject to constraints produces //smooth// graph signals — signals that vary slowly across edges. This principle drives ''spectral clustering'': partition nodes by minimizing the graph cut, which reduces to finding the Fiedler vector.

The ''normalized Laplacian'' $L_\text{sym} = D^{-1/2} L D^{-1/2} = I - D^{-1/2}AD^{-1/2}$ corrects for node degree, making high-degree nodes count proportionally less. Its eigenvalues lie in $[0, 2]$, enabling more stable numerical analysis. The ''random-walk Laplacian'' $L_\text{rw} = D^{-1}L = I - D^{-1}A$ has the same eigenvalues and connects directly to Markov chain analysis.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Given a graph $G = (V, E)$ with $N$ nodes and edge weights $A_{ij} \geq 0$, define:

- ''Adjacency matrix'' $A$: $A_{ij} = w_{ij}$ if $(i,j) \in E$, else $0$
- ''Degree matrix'' $D$: diagonal, $D_{ii} = \sum_j A_{ij}$
- ''Laplacian'' $L = D - A$

The quadratic form is:

$$x^\top L x = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j} A_{ij}(x_i - x_j)^2$$

The factor of $\frac{1}{2}$ accounts for counting each edge twice. This shows $L$ is ''positive semi-definite'' — $x^\top Lx \geq 0$ always — with equality iff $x$ is constant on each connected component.

''Eigendecomposition:'' $L = U \Lambda U^\top$ where $\Lambda = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_N)$ with $0 = \lambda_1 \leq \lambda_2 \leq \cdots \leq \lambda_N$, and $U$ is orthonormal.

''Heat equation on the graph:'' the continuous heat equation $\partial h / \partial t = -Lh$ describes diffusion of a signal over time. Its solution is:

$$h(t) = e^{-Lt} h(0) = U e^{-\Lambda t} U^\top h(0)$$

High-frequency components (large eigenvalues) decay rapidly; the signal smooths toward a constant (the null eigenvector). This gives a principled foundation for graph signal processing and motivates why spectral GNNs can be understood as learned heat kernels.

''Graph Fourier transform:'' $\hat{x} = U^\top x$ decomposes $x$ into graph-frequency components. Filtering in frequency domain: $y = U \hat{h}(\Lambda) U^\top x$, where $\hat{h}$ is a pointwise function of eigenvalues.

!! Graph Laplacian in GNNs

The graph Laplacian is the mathematical foundation of ''spectral graph convolutional networks''. Kipf and Welling (2017) defined graph convolution as multiplication in the graph Fourier domain:

$$y = U \hat{h}(\Lambda) U^\top x$$

where $\hat{h}(\Lambda)$ is a learned filter applied to eigenvalues. This is theoretically clean but computationally expensive: eigendecomposition of $L$ costs $O(N^3)$ and the resulting filters are not localized in space.

''ChebNet'' (Defferrard et al., 2016) approximated $\hat{h}(\Lambda)$ using Chebyshev polynomials $T_k(\tilde{\Lambda})$, giving $K$-hop localized filters without computing eigenvectors. Kipf and Welling further simplified to a first-order Chebyshev approximation, yielding the celebrated ''GCN layer'':

$$H^{(l+1)} = \sigma\!\left(\tilde{D}^{-1/2}\tilde{A}\tilde{D}^{-1/2} H^{(l)} W^{(l)}\right)$$

where $\tilde{A} = A + I$ adds self-loops. This is a //spatial// operation: each node aggregates messages from its one-hop neighborhood, weighted by degree. The spectral motivation justifies //why// this aggregation makes sense — it approximates low-pass filtering on the graph.

''Graph Transformers'' generalize further: instead of the Laplacian's fixed adjacency structure, they learn attention weights between nodes, allowing any pair to interact. Laplacian eigenvectors are often used as positional encodings in these models, preserving spectral structure while enabling flexible attention.

!! When the Graph Laplacian Breaks Down

''(1) Eigendecomposition is O(N³) — infeasible for large graphs.'' Computing the full eigensystem of $L$ for a graph with millions of nodes is computationally prohibitive. Approximations like Chebyshev polynomial filters (ChebNet) or randomized eigensolvers partially mitigate this, but true spectral methods remain restricted to small or medium graphs. Spatial message-passing GNNs sidestep this entirely, at the cost of losing the spectral interpretation.

''(2) Assumes a static graph — dynamic graphs invalidate precomputed eigenvectors.'' The Laplacian eigenvectors must be recomputed whenever the graph changes (new nodes, new edges). In dynamic settings — social networks evolving over time, molecular simulations, streaming graphs — this recomputation is prohibitively expensive. Methods that rely on eigenvector positional encodings break when graph structure shifts at inference time.

''(3) Oversquashing in deep GNNs — information from distant nodes gets exponentially diluted.'' When GNN layers stack, each node's receptive field grows, but the information from distant nodes gets compressed into a fixed-size vector. The graph Laplacian's diffusion interpretation shows why: the heat equation smooths signals, destroying high-frequency (distant, fine-grained) information. Deep spectral GNNs converge toward over-smoothed, constant features — making all node representations indistinguishable. Graph rewiring and skip connections partially address this, but it remains an open problem.

!! Technical Details

''Historical origins:'' Kirchhoff (1847) introduced the matrix tree theorem — the number of spanning trees equals any cofactor of $L$. Fiedler (1973) characterized algebraic connectivity via $\lambda_2$, founding modern spectral graph theory.

''Matrix tree theorem:'' The number of spanning trees of $G$ equals $\det(L^+)$ where $L^+$ is any $(N-1) \times (N-1)$ principal submatrix of $L$ (equivalently, $\frac{1}{N}\prod_{i=2}^{N}\lambda_i$).

''Random walk connection:'' The transition matrix $P = D^{-1}A$ is related to $L$ by $L = D(I - P)$. The stationary distribution $\pi_i \propto D_{ii}$ (degree-proportional). This connects to <<link "Page Rank" "Page Rank">><</link>> — PageRank modifies the random walk with a teleportation probability $\alpha$.

''Resistance distance:'' The effective resistance between nodes $i$ and $j$ is $r_{ij} = (e_i - e_j)^\top L^+ (e_i - e_j)$ where $L^+$ is the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse. This gives a metric on graphs invariant to graph automorphisms.

''Cheeger inequality:'' For the normalized Laplacian, $\frac{\lambda_2}{2} \leq h(G) \leq \sqrt{2\lambda_2}$ where $h(G)$ is the Cheeger constant (graph expansion / bottleneck). This bounds how well $\lambda_2$ approximates the minimum graph cut.

''Connection to continuous Laplacian:'' On a manifold, the graph Laplacian converges to the Laplace-Beltrami operator as the point cloud density increases, justifying its use in manifold learning and graph-based SSL.
