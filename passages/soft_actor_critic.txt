! Soft Actor-Critic

<img src="assets/soft_actor_critic.svg" alt="Diagram illustrating the Soft Actor-Critic architecture" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Most RL agents behave like a chess prodigy who has memorised one perfect opening: once they find a strategy that works, they commit to it and never look back. ''Soft Actor-Critic (SAC)'' plays a fundamentally different game. It explicitly rewards the agent for staying unpredictable — for keeping as many options on the table as possible while still accumulating reward. An agent that stays curious and hedges its bets tends to discover better solutions than one that commits prematurely. That willingness to remain uncertain is the essence of //maximum entropy RL//.

---

!! The Core Insight

''Soft Actor-Critic'' (Haarnoja et al., 2018) is an off-policy, actor-critic algorithm designed for continuous action spaces. Its defining innovation is augmenting the standard RL reward with an //entropy bonus//: at every timestep the agent is rewarded both for performing well and for acting as randomly as the task allows. This joint objective is called the ''maximum entropy objective'', and it changes the character of the learned policy in fundamental ways.

Because the agent is penalised for certainty, it cannot collapse onto a single action at each state. Instead it maintains a full probability distribution over actions even after convergence. This built-in hedging encourages exploration throughout training, prevents premature convergence to local optima, and yields policies that are naturally robust to small perturbations in the environment — if one action is blocked, other near-optimal actions still have meaningful probability mass.

SAC became the de-facto standard baseline for continuous-control benchmarks such as MuJoCo locomotion tasks, outperforming both on-policy algorithms like [[Proximal Policy Optimization]] and earlier off-policy methods like DDPG in sample efficiency, stability, and final performance. It sits at the intersection of [[Actor-critic]] methods, [[Q Learning]], and information-theoretic ideas, and it connects directly to the broader themes of [[Explore and Exploit]] in [[Reinforcement Learning]].

---

!! Maximum Entropy Objective

The standard RL objective maximises expected cumulative discounted reward: $J(\pi) = \mathbb{E}\bigl[\sum_t \gamma^t r(s_t, a_t)\bigr]$. SAC replaces this with the ''soft'' (maximum entropy) objective:

$$J(\pi) = \sum_t \mathbb{E}_{(s_t, a_t) \sim \rho_\pi}\!\bigl[r(s_t, a_t) + \alpha\,\mathcal{H}(\pi(\cdot|s_t))\bigr]$$

where $\mathcal{H}(\pi(\cdot|s)) = -\mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi}[\log \pi(a|s)]$ is the ''Shannon entropy'' of the policy at state $s$, and $\alpha > 0$ is the ''temperature parameter'' that controls the entropy–reward tradeoff.

At high $\alpha$ the agent explores freely, giving roughly equal weight to all actions. As $\alpha \to 0$ the entropy term vanishes and SAC recovers standard RL. The temperature $\alpha$ is itself learnable in SAC v2 (Haarnoja et al., 2019): it is tuned automatically to hit a target entropy $\mathcal{H}_\text{target} = -\dim(\mathcal{A})$, matching the dimensionality of the action space, so the practitioner does not need to hand-tune $\alpha$.

The key practical benefit: even after the policy has largely converged, it retains meaningful probability mass on multiple actions. This makes SAC policies easier to fine-tune, safer to deploy (they do not over-commit), and more robust to distributional shift.

---

!! Architecture: Actor, Critics, and Clipped Double-Q

SAC combines four interacting components.

The ''Actor'' is a neural network that outputs the parameters of a Gaussian distribution — a mean vector $\mu_\phi(s)$ and a log-standard-deviation vector $\log\sigma_\phi(s)$ — over the action space. Actions are sampled via the ''reparameterisation trick'': $a = \tanh(\mu + \sigma \cdot \varepsilon)$, $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$. This makes the sampling operation differentiable, so policy gradients can flow back through the stochastic sample into the network weights.

The ''Two Critics'' $Q_{\theta_1}$ and $Q_{\theta_2}$ are independent Q-networks trained simultaneously on the same data. To form TD targets, SAC uses $\min(Q_{\theta_1}, Q_{\theta_2})$ — the ''clipped double-Q'' trick borrowed from [[TD3]] — which provably reduces the overestimation bias that plagues single-critic methods and prevents the actor from exploiting critic errors.

The ''Target Networks'' $\bar{Q}_1, \bar{Q}_2$ are exponential moving averages of the critic weights ($\bar\theta \leftarrow \tau\theta + (1-\tau)\bar\theta$ with $\tau \approx 0.005$). They provide stable regression targets and avoid the deadly feedback loop of bootstrapping against a rapidly changing network.

The ''Replay Buffer'' stores $(s, a, r, s')$ transitions from past interactions. SAC samples mini-batches uniformly at random, decoupling data collection from training updates and dramatically improving sample efficiency relative to on-policy methods like [[Proximal Policy Optimization]].

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The soft Bellman equation defines a ''soft Q-function'' that accounts for future entropy as well as future reward:

$$Q(s_t, a_t) = r_t + \gamma\,\mathbb{E}_{s_{t+1}}\!\bigl[V(s_{t+1})\bigr]$$

where the ''soft value function'' marginalises over the policy's action distribution including the entropy bonus:

$$V(s) = \mathbb{E}_{a \sim \pi}\!\bigl[Q(s, a) - \alpha\log\pi(a|s)\bigr]$$

The critics are trained by minimising the squared soft Bellman residual using mini-batch gradient descent, with the target computed from the target networks.

The actor is trained to minimise the expected KL divergence between $\pi(\cdot|s)$ and $\exp(Q(s,\cdot)/\alpha)$, which simplifies to minimising:

$$\mathcal{L}(\phi) = \mathbb{E}_{s \sim \mathcal{D},\,\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}}\!\bigl[\alpha\log\pi_\phi(a_\phi(s,\varepsilon)|s) - Q(s, a_\phi(s,\varepsilon))\bigr]$$

where $a_\phi(s, \varepsilon) = \tanh(\mu_\phi(s) + \sigma_\phi(s) \cdot \varepsilon)$ is the reparameterised sample. The temperature $\alpha$ is updated by minimising $\mathcal{L}(\alpha) = \mathbb{E}[-\alpha\log\pi(a|s) - \alpha\mathcal{H}_\text{target}]$, pushing actual entropy toward the target.

---

!! When Soft Actor-Critic Breaks Down

''Sparse reward environments:'' The entropy bonus helps with exploration, but it cannot substitute for informative reward signals. In tasks where rewards are rare and narrowly located in state space — such as maze navigation with a single goal cell — SAC can wander indefinitely. Techniques like [[Hindsight Experience Replay]] or reward shaping are often needed on top of SAC in such settings.

''Discrete action spaces:'' SAC is designed for continuous actions and relies on the Gaussian reparameterisation trick. Adapting it to discrete actions requires non-trivial modifications (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax relaxations or exact discrete-SAC variants), and these adaptations do not always match the performance of methods designed natively for discrete spaces like [[Q Learning]] with prioritised replay.

''High-dimensional observation spaces:'' When observations are raw pixels or high-dimensional sensor arrays, the critic and actor networks must learn useful representations from scratch alongside the RL objective. Sample efficiency degrades sharply; methods that separate representation learning (e.g., data-augmentation-based approaches like DrQ) are typically needed, and the theoretical stability guarantees that make SAC attractive in low-dimensional state spaces no longer apply cleanly.

---

!! Technical Details

''Original papers:'' Haarnoja et al. 2018 (SAC v1 with fixed temperature); Haarnoja et al. 2019 (SAC v2 with automatic temperature tuning). Both are open-access on arXiv.

''Algorithm comparison:'' SAC is off-policy (like DDPG, TD3, DQN) and therefore far more sample-efficient than on-policy methods like [[Proximal Policy Optimization]] or [[policy gradient]] (REINFORCE). Versus TD3: both use clipped double-Q and deterministic-style critics, but SAC adds the entropy term, producing a stochastic policy — this usually gives better exploration and higher asymptotic performance. Versus DDPG: SAC is strictly more stable; DDPG's single critic and deterministic policy make it brittle to hyperparameter choices.

''Sample efficiency:'' On standard MuJoCo benchmarks (HalfCheetah, Ant, Humanoid), SAC typically achieves competitive final performance 5–10× faster in environment steps than PPO, and matches or exceeds TD3 with fewer hyperparameters requiring tuning.

''Automatic temperature:'' The constraint $\mathbb{E}[\mathcal{H}(\pi)] \geq \mathcal{H}_\text{target} = -\dim(\mathcal{A})$ is enforced by treating $\alpha$ as a Lagrange multiplier and optimising it jointly with the policy. This removes one of the most sensitive hyperparameters.

''Real-world applications:'' SAC has been applied to dexterous robotic manipulation (OpenAI Dactyl follow-ups), legged locomotion on physical robots, and HVAC control optimisation. Its stochastic policy is an advantage in sim-to-real transfer: the distribution over actions provides natural robustness to the reality gap.

''Limitations:'' Continuous action spaces only without modification; requires sufficient replay buffer memory; convergence proofs assume tabular or linear function approximation; deep-network versions rely on empirical stability rather than guarantees.
