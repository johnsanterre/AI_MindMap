! Neural Collapse

<img src="assets/neural_collapse.svg" alt="Diagram illustrating Neural Collapse geometry" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine sorting colored marbles into piles on a table. During most of training, each pile stays spread out — marbles of the same color scatter loosely around their general region. Then, very late in training, something unexpected happens: each pile compresses to a single point, and those points arrange themselves into the most symmetric geometry possible, like the vertices of a perfect equilateral triangle or tetrahedron. ''Neural collapse'' is that late-training compression into perfect, inevitable symmetry — and it was not designed in. It just happens.

---

!! The Core Insight

In 2020, Papyan, Han, and Donoho discovered that when a neural network is trained past the point where training loss approaches zero — the ''terminal phase of training'' — four geometric properties emerge simultaneously in the last hidden layer. These properties were not explicitly programmed or encouraged by the loss function. They arise as a consequence of training itself.

The phenomenon was surprising because most analysis of neural networks focuses on early or middle training. The terminal phase was understudied, and the finding that training produces such clean, maximally symmetric structure gave theorists a rare foothold: a precise, measurable geometric prediction about what deep networks do internally. It connects [[Deep Learning]], [[Feature Learning]], [[Embeddings]], [[Loss Functions]], and [[Cross-entropy]] in a single geometric picture.

The practical importance is twofold. First, neural collapse gives us a diagnostic: we can measure how close a network is to the collapsed state and use that to judge convergence. Second, it raises deep questions about why cross-entropy loss plus weight decay conspires to produce this particular geometry — questions that have since been answered through the unconstrained features model.

---

!! The Four NC Properties

Neural collapse is defined by four properties that appear together in the terminal phase. Each is measurable and all four are interlinked.

''NC1 — Within-class variability collapse.'' The last-layer feature vectors for samples of the same class become identical to one another. Their within-class covariance collapses to zero. Every sample of class $i$ maps to exactly the same point in feature space: the class mean $\mu_i$.

''NC2 — Convergence to a simplex ETF.'' The class means $\{\mu_1, \ldots, \mu_C\}$ arrange themselves into the vertices of a ''simplex equiangular tight frame'' (ETF). This means every pair of class means has equal norm and equal pairwise cosine similarity: $$\frac{\mu_i^T \mu_j}{\|\mu_i\|\|\mu_j\|} = -\frac{1}{C-1} \quad \text{for } i \neq j$$ For $C = 3$ classes this is an equilateral triangle; for $C = 4$ a regular tetrahedron. The ETF is the unique geometry that maximally separates $C$ vectors in terms of minimum pairwise angle.

''NC3 — Self-duality.'' The learned classifier weight vectors $\{w_1, \ldots, w_C\}$ align exactly with the class means. Up to a global scale, $w_i \propto \mu_i$. The classifier and the feature extractor have converged to the same geometry, independently.

''NC4 — Simplification to nearest-class-center.'' Because of NC1–NC3, the network's decision rule simplifies completely: it predicts whichever class center $\mu_i$ is nearest to the input's feature vector. The softmax and full classifier become equivalent to a nearest-centroid rule.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ETF is not arbitrary. It is the solution to a concrete optimization problem: place $C$ unit vectors in $\mathbb{R}^d$ (with $d \geq C - 1$) so that the minimum pairwise cosine similarity is as small as possible. The answer is the ETF, with all pairwise similarities equal to $-1/(C-1)$. This is the most spread-out possible arrangement of $C$ points on a sphere.

To understand //why// training produces an ETF, theorists introduced the ''unconstrained features model'' (UFM). Instead of treating the last-layer features as outputs of the network, treat them as free variables $H \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times n}$ — one column per sample. Then minimize the regularized cross-entropy loss over $W$, $H$, and bias $b$ jointly:

$$\min_{W, H, b} \mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}(WH + b\mathbf{1}^T) + \frac{\lambda_W}{2}\|W\|_F^2 + \frac{\lambda_H}{2}\|H\|_F^2$$

The global minimizer of this problem — provably, exactly — is the ETF geometry for the class means, with $w_i \propto \mu_i$ and within-class variance zero. This shows that neural collapse is not an artifact of any particular architecture. It is the inevitable outcome of optimizing cross-entropy with weight decay, regardless of how the features are produced. The ETF is what the loss function //wants//.

The weight decay (L2 regularization) terms play a crucial role: they push $W$ and $H$ toward zero, while cross-entropy pushes them apart to separate classes. The equilibrium of these forces is the ETF — equal norms, maximum separation.

---

!! Implications for Practice

Neural collapse gives practitioners several concrete tools and cautions.

First, it suggests a ''convergence criterion'': measuring NC metrics (within-class variability, ETF alignment) during training tells you when the model has genuinely converged, not just when the loss has plateaued. A network whose features have reached NC is in a qualitatively different regime.

Second, NC has implications for ''transfer learning''. The ETF geometry in the source domain is tuned to source-domain classes. When fine-tuning on a new domain with different classes, the pre-trained features may not collapse correctly to the new ETF, especially with limited data. This partially explains why fine-tuning the full network often outperforms head-only fine-tuning.

Third, ''class imbalance'' disrupts NC. When class sizes differ, the minority-class means do not collapse properly — they have higher within-class variance and their positions deviate from the ETF. This connects neural collapse theory to the practical failure modes of imbalanced training.

Fourth, NC provides a theoretical basis for ''fixed ETF classifiers'' in few-shot learning. If the final classifier must converge to ETF geometry anyway, one can fix $W$ to an ETF and train only the feature extractor — this works surprisingly well and saves parameters.

---

!! When Neural Collapse Breaks Down

''Class imbalance.'' When training classes have very different numbers of samples, the gradient signal is dominated by majority classes. Minority-class features receive less pressure to collapse, so within-class variability remains high for rare classes and the class means deviate from the ideal ETF geometry. The symmetric structure requires symmetric training signal — imbalance breaks the symmetry.

''Label noise.'' When a meaningful fraction of training labels are wrong, the network cannot consistently map all samples of a class to the same feature point — different samples of the "same" class actually belong to different true classes. This prevents NC1 (within-class collapse) from completing, which in turn prevents NC2 (ETF convergence). Heavy label noise leaves the terminal-phase geometry disordered.

''Insufficient overparameterization or early stopping.'' Neural collapse is a terminal-phase phenomenon: it requires training past the point of near-zero training loss. Networks that are too small to interpolate the training data, or training runs stopped before the terminal phase, will not exhibit NC. In such cases the features remain in an intermediate regime — partially separated but not collapsed — and the ETF geometry does not appear.

---

!! Technical Details

The original discovery is in ''Papyan, Han, and Donoho (2020)'', "Prevalence of Neural Collapse during the terminal phase of deep learning training," //PNAS//. They coined the term and documented all four NC properties empirically across architectures and datasets.

''Zhu et al. (2021)'', "A Geometric Analysis of Neural Collapse with Unconstrained Features," provided the UFM framework and proved that the global minimizer of the regularized cross-entropy under UFM is exactly the ETF. This converted an empirical observation into a theorem.

''Fang et al. (2021)'', "Exploring Deep Neural Networks via Layer-Peeled Model: Minority Collapse in Imbalanced Training," extended NC to imbalanced settings, showing that minority classes collapse to points inside the ETF simplex rather than at vertices — a phenomenon called minority collapse.

''Thrampoulidis et al. (2022)'', "Imbalance Trouble: Revisiting Neural-Collapse Geometry," gave a finer analysis of how class imbalance distorts the ETF.

The ETF is formally defined as a set of $C$ vectors $\{w_i\} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ satisfying $W^T W = \frac{C}{C-1}\left(I - \frac{1}{C}\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^T\right)$ where $W = [w_1, \ldots, w_C]$. This matrix is (up to scale) the only $C \times C$ matrix that is simultaneously a projection and a rescaled identity when centered.

NC has been observed in ResNets, VGGs, and Vision Transformers. In transformers, collapse occurs in the [CLS] token representations at the final layer. NC under label noise has been studied as a proxy for network memorization: networks that memorize labels show disrupted NC metrics.

Within-class variability collapse is measured by the ratio $\mathcal{E}_{\text{NC1}} = \frac{1}{C}\sum_{i=1}^C \frac{\|\Sigma_{W,i}\|_F}{\|\Sigma_B\|_F}$ where $\Sigma_{W,i}$ is the within-class covariance for class $i$ and $\Sigma_B$ is the between-class covariance. This ratio approaches zero at full NC1 collapse.
