! Model Compression

<img src="assets/model_compression.svg" alt="Diagram showing four model compression techniques: pruning, quantization, distillation, and low-rank factorization" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you have a massive encyclopaedia — fifty volumes, thousands of pages — and you need to carry it on a plane. You could tear out the blank pages and appendices nobody reads (pruning). You could photocopy every page at lower resolution so each volume is thinner (quantization). You could have a brilliant friend read the whole thing and dictate a concise summary into a single notebook (distillation). Or you could notice that most chapters repeat the same patterns and rewrite them in compressed shorthand (low-rank factorization). Each strategy shrinks the load differently, and in practice you combine several at once. ''Model compression'' applies exactly these ideas to neural networks: making them smaller, faster, and cheaper to deploy while keeping their predictions as accurate as possible.

---

!! The Core Insight

Modern neural networks are massively overparameterized. A model with hundreds of millions of weights typically has far more capacity than the task actually requires. The excess capacity helps during training — it smooths the loss landscape and makes optimization easier — but once training is finished, most of that capacity is redundant. Model compression exploits this redundancy after the fact, stripping away what the model does not truly need.

The four main compression techniques attack different sources of redundancy. ''Pruning'' removes individual weights or entire structures (neurons, filters, attention heads) that contribute little to the output. <<link "Quantization" "Quantization">><</link>> reduces the numerical precision of each weight — from 32-bit floats down to 8-bit integers or even 4-bit representations. <<link "Knowledge Distillation" "Knowledge Distillation">><</link>> trains a smaller ''student'' network to mimic the outputs of the large ''teacher'' network, transferring the teacher's learned knowledge into a compact architecture. <<link "Low-rank factorization" "Low-Rank Approximation">><</link>> replaces large weight matrices with products of smaller matrices, exploiting the fact that weight matrices often have low effective rank.

The key insight unifying all four: the information content of a trained model is much smaller than its parameter count would suggest. Compression recovers the gap between //representational capacity// and //actual information used//.

---

!! The Four Pillars of Compression

'''Pruning''' zeroes out weights that are small in magnitude (unstructured pruning) or removes entire computational units like filters or attention heads (structured pruning). Unstructured pruning can achieve extreme sparsity — 90%+ of weights removed — but sparse matrices are hard to accelerate on standard GPUs. Structured pruning produces smaller dense models that run faster on existing hardware. The <<link "Lottery Ticket Hypothesis" "Lottery Ticket Hypothesis">><</link>> provides a theoretical lens: inside every large network is a small sparse subnetwork that could have trained to the same accuracy from the start.

'''Quantization''' replaces 32-bit floating-point weights with lower-precision representations. Post-training quantization (PTQ) converts a trained model without retraining — fast but lossy. Quantization-aware training (QAT) simulates low-precision arithmetic during training so the model learns to be robust to rounding errors. Going from FP32 to INT8 typically cuts model size by 4x with minimal accuracy loss; INT4 doubles the savings but requires careful calibration.

'''Knowledge distillation''' trains a compact student network using the soft probability outputs of a larger teacher as supervision. The teacher's softmax outputs carry richer information than hard labels — they reveal which classes the teacher considers similar, providing a form of structured regularization. Temperature scaling softens the outputs further, amplifying the signal from low-probability classes.

'''Low-rank factorization''' decomposes a weight matrix $W \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ into the product of two smaller matrices $W \approx UV$ where $U \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times r}$ and $V \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times n}$ with $r \ll \min(m, n)$. This reduces the parameter count from $mn$ to $r(m + n)$ and can also speed up inference. SVD and related matrix decompositions identify the dominant modes of the weight matrix and discard the rest.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

'''Pruning''' applies a binary mask $m \in \{0, 1\}^d$ to weights $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^d$. The compressed model computes $f(x;\, m \odot \theta)$. Magnitude pruning sets $m_i = 0$ when $|\theta_i| < \tau$ for threshold $\tau$. The optimization problem underlying optimal pruning is:

$$\min_{m} \; \mathcal{L}(f(x;\, m \odot \theta)) \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \|m\|_0 \leq k$$

This is NP-hard in general, so magnitude pruning serves as a greedy heuristic. Second-order methods approximate the loss change from removing weight $i$ using the Hessian: $\Delta \mathcal{L}_i \approx \frac{1}{2} \theta_i^2 \, H_{ii}$.

'''Quantization''' maps a continuous weight $w$ to a discrete value: $\hat{w} = s \cdot \text{round}(w / s)$ where $s$ is the step size (scale factor). For $b$-bit quantization with range $[\alpha, \beta]$:

$$s = \frac{\beta - \alpha}{2^b - 1}, \qquad \hat{w} = s \cdot \text{clamp}\!\left(\text{round}\!\left(\frac{w - \alpha}{s}\right),\, 0,\, 2^b - 1\right) + \alpha$$

The quantization error $\|W - \hat{W}\|_F^2$ grows as precision decreases. QAT uses the ''straight-through estimator'' (STE) to backpropagate through the non-differentiable rounding operation.

'''Distillation''' minimizes a combination of the standard cross-entropy loss and a KL-divergence between student and teacher softmax outputs at temperature $T$:

$$\mathcal{L} = \alpha \cdot H(y,\, \sigma(z_s)) + (1 - \alpha) \cdot T^2 \cdot D_{\text{KL}}\!\left(\sigma\!\left(\frac{z_t}{T}\right) \;\bigg\|\; \sigma\!\left(\frac{z_s}{T}\right)\right)$$

where $z_t, z_s$ are teacher and student logits, $\sigma$ is softmax, and $T > 1$ softens the distribution. The $T^2$ factor compensates for the reduced gradient magnitude at higher temperatures.

'''Low-rank factorization''' finds the best rank-$r$ approximation via SVD. For $W = U \Sigma V^T$, the optimal rank-$r$ approximation is $W_r = U_r \Sigma_r V_r^T$ (Eckart-Young theorem), minimizing $\|W - W_r\|_F$. The fraction of variance retained is:

$$\frac{\sum_{i=1}^{r} \sigma_i^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \sigma_i^2}$$

where $\sigma_i$ are singular values. If the spectrum decays quickly, small $r$ captures most of the matrix's effect.

---

!! Compression in Practice

The four techniques are not mutually exclusive — production deployments typically stack several. A common pipeline: (1) train a large teacher, (2) distill into a smaller student architecture, (3) apply quantization-aware training on the student, (4) prune remaining redundant weights. Each step compounds the savings.

''Hardware matters.'' Structured pruning and quantization map well to GPU and TPU architectures. Unstructured <<link "sparsity" "Sparsity">><</link>> requires specialized sparse matrix hardware (like NVIDIA's Ampere structured sparsity, which supports 2:4 sparsity natively). Low-rank factorization reduces FLOPs but may not speed up wall-clock time if the decomposed operations are memory-bound rather than compute-bound.

''Calibration data.'' Post-training quantization and some pruning methods require a small calibration set — a few hundred examples from the training distribution — to set scale factors, identify important weights, or measure activation ranges. The quality of this calibration set directly affects compression quality.

''Layer sensitivity.'' Not all layers compress equally. Early layers and the final classification head are often more sensitive to pruning and quantization than middle layers. Sensitivity analysis — measuring accuracy degradation as each layer is independently compressed — guides non-uniform compression strategies where fragile layers keep higher precision or density.

---

!! When Model Compression Breaks Down

''Accuracy collapse at high compression:'' Every compression technique has a knee in its accuracy-vs-size curve. Beyond a certain compression ratio, accuracy degrades sharply and non-linearly. Pruning past 95% sparsity, quantizing below 4 bits, or distilling into a student that is too small all risk catastrophic accuracy loss. The safe compression ratio depends heavily on the task, dataset, and architecture — there is no universal threshold, and the failure mode is often sudden rather than gradual.

''Distribution shift vulnerability:'' Compressed models are calibrated to the training distribution. When inputs shift — new domains, adversarial examples, or out-of-distribution data — compressed models degrade faster than their uncompressed counterparts. Quantization clips activation ranges tuned to calibration data; pruning removes weights that might have been important for rare inputs; distillation transfers only the teacher's behavior on seen data. The robustness margin that overparameterization provides is exactly what compression removes.

''Fairness and bias amplification:'' Compression can disproportionately degrade performance on underrepresented subgroups. If a model's accuracy on minority classes depends on a small number of specialized weights, pruning those weights or quantizing them aggressively can widen the accuracy gap between majority and minority groups. Studies have shown that pruned language models exhibit larger performance drops on African American English than on Standard American English — compression is not accuracy-neutral across populations.

---

!! Technical Details

''Pruning granularity:'' Weight-level (unstructured), neuron/filter-level, block-level, layer-level. NVIDIA 2:4 structured sparsity: in every group of 4 consecutive weights, exactly 2 must be zero — gives 50% sparsity with hardware acceleration. SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023) prunes large language models to 50-60% unstructured sparsity with minimal accuracy loss in one shot using approximate second-order information.

''Quantization formats:'' INT8 (most mature, universally supported), INT4 (emerging for LLMs via GPTQ, AWQ), FP8 (E4M3, E5M2 — new in H100 GPUs), binary/ternary (extreme but rarely practical). Mixed-precision: keep sensitive layers at higher precision while aggressively quantizing robust layers.

''Distillation variants:'' Feature-level distillation (match intermediate representations, not just final outputs), attention transfer (match attention maps), self-distillation (model distills into itself at lower layers), online distillation (teacher and student train simultaneously).

''Low-rank methods:'' SVD-based decomposition, Tucker decomposition for convolution tensors, LoRA (<<link "Low-Rank Adaptation" "Low-Rank Approximation">><</link>>) as a parameter-efficient fine-tuning method that constrains weight updates to a low-rank subspace.

''Benchmark compression ratios:'' ResNet-50 on ImageNet: 4x pruning with <1% accuracy loss; 8x quantization (INT4) with ~1% loss. BERT: 40% pruning + INT8 quantization yields ~3x speedup with <0.5% accuracy drop. LLaMA-65B distilled to 7B parameters retains ~85% of original benchmark performance.

''Theoretical bounds:'' The minimum description length (MDL) principle provides an information-theoretic lower bound on compression: a model cannot be compressed beyond its Kolmogorov complexity on the data. In practice, we are far from these bounds.
