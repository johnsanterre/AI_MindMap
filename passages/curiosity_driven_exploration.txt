! Curiosity-Driven Exploration

<img src="assets/curiosity_driven_exploration.svg" alt="Diagram illustrating curiosity-driven exploration via prediction error as intrinsic reward" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine dropping a toddler into a room full of toys. Nobody needs to promise candy for touching each one — the child picks things up, shakes them, drops them, and squeals at the results. The //surprise itself// is the reward. ''Curiosity-Driven Exploration'' gives reinforcement learning agents the same instinct: instead of waiting for the environment to hand out sparse, distant rewards, the agent manufactures its own reward signal from how //surprised// it is by what happens next. States that are predictable become boring; states that defy expectations become irresistible. This transforms the hardest part of RL — exploring vast environments where extrinsic rewards are rare — into something the agent //wants// to do.

---

!! The Core Insight

The central problem is simple to state and brutally hard to solve: in a sparse-reward environment, an agent using only extrinsic reward will almost never stumble upon the goal by random action. The search space is too large, the signal too thin. ''Curiosity-driven exploration'' sidesteps this by giving the agent an ''intrinsic motivation'' — a self-generated reward that is high whenever the agent encounters something it cannot yet predict or explain.

The most influential formulation is the ''Intrinsic Curiosity Module'' (ICM, Pathak et al., 2017). The agent maintains a forward dynamics model $f$ that predicts the next state given the current state and action. The ''prediction error'' $\|f(s_t, a_t) - s_{t+1}\|^2$ becomes the intrinsic reward: the worse the model's prediction, the more "curious" the agent is about that region of the state space. Over time, as the model improves at predicting familiar areas, the intrinsic reward there drops to zero and the agent is naturally pushed toward unexplored territory.

This connects directly to the <<link "Explore-Exploit Tradeoff" "Explore-Exploit Tradeoff">><</link>> — curiosity provides a principled, learned exploration bonus rather than naive epsilon-greedy randomness. It also draws on ideas from <<link "Information Theory" "Information Theory">><</link>>, where surprise is formalised as negative log-likelihood, and from <<link "World Models" "World Models">><</link>>, which learn environment dynamics for planning. The broader framework of <<link "Intrinsic Motivation" "Intrinsic Motivation">><</link>> in <<link "Reinforcement Learning" "Reinforcement Learning">><</link>> encompasses curiosity as one of several self-supervised reward signals.

---

!! Flavours of Curiosity

Not all curiosity signals are built the same. Three dominant paradigms have emerged, each with distinct strengths.

''Prediction-error methods'' (ICM, Burda et al. 2019) train a forward model and use its error as reward. ICM operates in a learned ''feature space'' rather than raw pixel space, using an inverse dynamics model to extract features that capture only the aspects of the environment the agent can influence — filtering out irrelevant visual noise like swaying leaves. This is critical: raw-pixel prediction error would be dominated by stochastic, uncontrollable elements of the observation.

''Random Network Distillation (RND)'' (Burda et al., 2019) takes a different approach entirely. A fixed, randomly initialised neural network $g$ maps states to embeddings. A trainable predictor network $\hat{g}$ tries to match $g$'s outputs. For states visited many times, $\hat{g}$ converges to $g$; for novel states, the prediction error $\|\hat{g}(s) - g(s)\|^2$ is high. RND avoids the "noisy TV problem" (where a stochastic element creates permanently high prediction error) because the target function $g$ is deterministic.

''Count-based methods'' maintain visit counts $N(s)$ and reward the agent proportionally to $1/\sqrt{N(s)}$. In tabular settings this is exact; for large state spaces, pseudo-counts derived from density models (Bellemare et al., 2016) or hash-based approximations provide tractable analogues. These methods connect to classic ''upper confidence bound'' strategies from the bandit literature.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The total reward at each timestep combines extrinsic and intrinsic components:

$$r_t^\text{total} = r_t^\text{ext} + \beta \cdot r_t^\text{int}$$

where $\beta > 0$ controls the curiosity strength and $r_t^\text{int}$ is the intrinsic reward.

For ICM, the intrinsic reward is the forward model prediction error in a learned feature space $\phi$:

$$r_t^\text{ICM} = \frac{\eta}{2}\|\hat{\phi}(s_{t+1}) - \phi(s_{t+1})\|^2$$

where $\hat{\phi}(s_{t+1}) = f(\phi(s_t), a_t)$ is the forward model's prediction and $\eta$ is a scaling constant. The feature encoder $\phi$ is trained jointly via an inverse model that predicts $a_t$ from $(\phi(s_t), \phi(s_{t+1}))$, ensuring features encode action-relevant information.

For RND, with fixed random network $g: \mathcal{S} \to \mathbb{R}^k$ and learnable predictor $\hat{g}_\theta$:

$$r_t^\text{RND} = \|\hat{g}_\theta(s_{t+1}) - g(s_{t+1})\|^2$$

The predictor $\hat{g}_\theta$ is trained to minimise this error over the replay buffer. Novel states yield high error because $\hat{g}_\theta$ has not yet been trained on similar inputs.

From an information-theoretic perspective, curiosity can be framed as maximising the ''information gain'' about the environment dynamics:

$$r_t^\text{info} = D_\text{KL}\!\bigl(p(\theta | h_{t+1}) \;\|\; p(\theta | h_t)\bigr)$$

where $\theta$ parameterises the dynamics model and $h_t$ is the history up to time $t$. This measures how much a single transition updates the agent's beliefs — the Bayesian formulation of surprise. In practice, this is approximated using ensembles of dynamics models: the variance across ensemble predictions serves as a proxy for epistemic uncertainty.

---

!! Practical Applications and Scaling

Curiosity-driven exploration has proven essential in several landmark results. In Montezuma's Revenge — the canonical hard-exploration Atari game where naive RL agents score zero — RND achieved superhuman performance without any extrinsic reward shaping. The agent learned to navigate dozens of rooms, collect keys, and unlock doors purely by chasing its own surprise signal.

In robotics, curiosity modules enable sample-efficient learning of manipulation skills in environments where reward engineering is expensive. An agent exploring a tabletop can discover object affordances (pushing, stacking, grasping) without any task-specific reward, building a repertoire of skills that transfer to downstream tasks.

In open-world games like Minecraft, Go-Explore (Ecoffet et al., 2021) combines curiosity with an archive of interesting states, first exploring to find promising states and then robustifying the path to reach them. This two-phase approach addresses a fundamental limitation of pure curiosity: forgetting how to return to previously discovered novel states.

The $\beta$ parameter governing curiosity strength requires care. Too high, and the agent becomes a perpetual tourist, endlessly exploring without exploiting known reward. Too low, and curiosity fails to overcome the gravitational pull of familiar states. Adaptive schedules that decay $\beta$ as training progresses are common in practice.

---

!! When Curiosity-Driven Exploration Breaks Down

''The noisy TV problem:'' If the environment contains a source of irreducible stochasticity — a television showing random static, a leaf fluttering unpredictably — prediction-error methods assign permanently high intrinsic reward to observing it. The agent becomes "addicted" to watching noise rather than exploring meaningfully. RND partially mitigates this because its target is deterministic, but any method that confuses aleatoric uncertainty (irreducible randomness) with epistemic uncertainty (learnable ignorance) is vulnerable. Feature-space prediction (ICM) and ensemble disagreement help but do not fully eliminate the issue.

''Curiosity fading in rich environments:'' In complex, high-dimensional environments with enormous state spaces, the forward model may never become accurate enough to reduce intrinsic reward below the threshold needed for useful exploration. Conversely, in simple environments the model learns too quickly, and the curiosity signal vanishes before the agent has explored sufficiently. The timescale mismatch between model learning and policy learning is a persistent challenge with no universal solution.

''Detachment and derailment:'' As identified by Ecoffet et al. (2021), purely curiosity-driven agents suffer from ''detachment'' — they move to novel frontiers and forget how to return to previously discovered interesting states — and ''derailment'' — they get trapped in local novelty pockets, thoroughly exploring one area while ignoring others. These failure modes arise because curiosity is a greedy, local signal with no global planning component, and they motivate hybrid approaches that combine curiosity with explicit state archiving.

!! Technical Details

''ICM (Pathak et al., 2017):'' Trains three networks jointly — feature encoder (shared), inverse model, forward model. The inverse model loss ensures features capture controllable aspects of the state. Works in feature space to handle high-dimensional observations. Original experiments on VizDoom and Super Mario Bros showed agents traversing levels with zero extrinsic reward.

''RND (Burda et al., 2019):'' Uses a fixed random CNN as target and a trainable predictor CNN. Achieves mean human-normalised score of ~75% on Montezuma's Revenge. Requires observation normalisation (running mean/variance) and reward normalisation to stabilise training. Uses two value heads — one for extrinsic returns (discounted normally) and one for intrinsic returns (discounted with shorter horizon, $\gamma_\text{int} = 0.99$ vs $\gamma_\text{ext} = 0.999$) to account for the non-stationary nature of curiosity rewards.

''Count-based exploration (Bellemare et al., 2016):'' Pseudo-counts derived from CTS density models. Bonus $r^+ = \beta / \sqrt{N^+(s)}$ where $N^+$ is the pseudo-count. Provably equivalent to optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty in the tabular limit. Later work replaced CTS with PixelCNN and hashing-based approximations for scalability.

''Comparison of approaches:'' Prediction-error methods are vulnerable to stochasticity; RND handles stochasticity but ignores action-relevance; count-based methods have clean theoretical foundations but scale poorly. Ensemble disagreement (Pathak et al., 2019) offers a middle ground, using variance across $K$ independently trained dynamics models as the curiosity signal.

''Hyperparameter sensitivity:'' $\beta$ (intrinsic reward scaling), $\gamma_\text{int}$ (intrinsic discount factor), and the architecture/learning rate of the curiosity module are the critical knobs. Most methods require separate normalisation of extrinsic and intrinsic reward streams.

''Connections:'' Curiosity is one instantiation of the broader <<link "Intrinsic Motivation" "Intrinsic Motivation">><</link>> framework, which also includes empowerment (maximising mutual information between actions and future states) and surprise minimisation (free energy principle). It connects to <<link "Information Theory" "Information Theory">><</link>> through information gain, to <<link "World Models" "World Models">><</link>> through learned dynamics, and to the <<link "Explore-Exploit Tradeoff" "Explore-Exploit Tradeoff">><</link>> through principled exploration bonuses in <<link "Reinforcement Learning" "Reinforcement Learning">><</link>>.
