! Andrew Ng

<img src="assets/andrew_ng.svg" alt="Andrew Ng" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine a world-class chef who doesn't just cook — he builds cooking schools, writes the recipes everyone learns from, runs restaurant chains on two continents, and then decides the real problem is that the ingredients aren't good enough. ''Andrew Ng'' is that chef for artificial intelligence. As a researcher, he pioneered large-scale [[Unsupervised Learning|Unsupervised Learning]] and sparse representations. As an educator, he created the most-enrolled online course in machine learning history, introducing millions of people to [[Gradient Descent|Gradient Descent]] and [[Neural Networks|Neural Networks]]. As an executive, he co-founded Google Brain and led AI strategy at Baidu. And in his most recent chapter, he has become the loudest voice arguing that the bottleneck in AI is not bigger models but better data — the ''data-centric AI'' movement. No single person has done more to move AI from an elite research pursuit into a global public skill.

---

!! Key Contributions

''Stanford AI Lab (SAIL):'' Ng joined Stanford's computer science faculty in 2003 and led SAIL from 2014. His early research focused on applying [[Deep Learning|Deep Learning]] to robotics and perception — teaching helicopters to fly autonomously using reinforcement learning, and applying sparse coding to visual features. His Stanford courses CS229 (Machine Learning) and CS230 (Deep Learning) became templates that shaped how the subject is taught worldwide.

''Google Brain (2011):'' Ng co-founded Google Brain with Jeff Dean and Greg Corrado. The team's landmark result used a cluster of 16,000 CPU cores to train a large neural network on unlabeled YouTube frames. The network spontaneously learned a neuron selective for cat faces — a dramatic demonstration that [[Unsupervised Learning|Unsupervised Learning]] at scale could discover high-level concepts without human labels. This project helped convince Google's leadership that deep learning was ready for production, catalyzing the company's massive investment in neural network infrastructure.

''Coursera and the ML MOOC (2012):'' Ng co-founded Coursera with Daphne Koller. His //Machine Learning// course, originally recorded in 2011, became the platform's flagship offering. Over five million students enrolled. The course taught [[Linear Regression|Linear Regression]], [[Logistic Regression|Logistic Regression]], [[Neural Networks|Neural Networks]], and [[Gradient Descent|Gradient Descent]] with a focus on intuition over formalism — an approach that lowered the barrier to entry for an entire generation of practitioners. In 2022, Ng released an updated version using Python and modern frameworks.

''Baidu AI Group (2014–2017):'' As chief scientist, Ng built Baidu's Silicon Valley AI lab and led research in speech recognition and [[Computer Vision|Computer Vision]]. His team developed Deep Speech 2, an end-to-end deep learning system for speech recognition that matched human-level accuracy on certain benchmarks, demonstrating that large-scale neural networks could replace hand-engineered speech pipelines.

''DeepLearning.AI (2017):'' After leaving Baidu, Ng founded DeepLearning.AI to scale AI education further. The //Deep Learning Specialization// on Coursera — five courses covering [[Neural Networks|Neural Networks]], optimization, structured ML projects, convolutional networks, and sequence models — became the standard onramp for practitioners entering the field. Later short courses covered [[Transfer Learning|Transfer Learning]], prompt engineering, and LLM application development.

''Landing AI (2017):'' Ng founded Landing AI to bring deep learning to manufacturing and industries where data is scarce and expensive. This work led him to articulate the ''data-centric AI'' philosophy: instead of iterating on model architecture, hold the model fixed and systematically improve data quality, labeling consistency, and data coverage. Landing AI's tools focus on visual inspection, where a factory may have only 50 images of a defect rather than millions.

''Sparse autoencoder work:'' Ng's early research on sparse [[Autoencoders|Autoencoders]] showed that imposing a sparsity penalty on hidden activations forces a network to learn interpretable features resembling Gabor filters and edge detectors — echoing how the visual cortex represents information. This work connected deep learning to neuroscience and influenced the broader study of learned representations.

---

!! The Core Insight

Ng's career embodies two convictions that have shaped the field.

''Democratizing AI education:'' Ng recognized early that AI's impact would be bottlenecked not by research breakthroughs but by the number of people who understood how to apply it. His response was to build educational infrastructure at massive scale — first through Stanford's open courseware, then through Coursera, then through DeepLearning.AI. The result: millions of engineers who would never have entered a Stanford classroom learned to implement [[Gradient Descent|Gradient Descent]], tune [[Neural Networks|Neural Networks]], and deploy models in production. This created the workforce that powers the AI industry today.

''Data-centric AI:'' Most ML research focuses on model-centric development — hold the data fixed, improve the algorithm. Ng argues this is backwards for real-world applications. In many industrial settings, you have small datasets where the marginal value of cleaning one label exceeds the marginal value of adding another attention head. The data-centric philosophy says: fix the model architecture (use a known good one), then invest all effort in data quality, consistent labeling, and targeted data augmentation. This shift is especially powerful in domains like medical imaging and manufacturing inspection where data is expensive and domain expertise matters more than architectural novelty.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''Sparse autoencoder objective:'' Ng's sparse autoencoder adds a sparsity penalty to the standard reconstruction loss. Given input $x$, encoder output $h = \sigma(Wx + b)$, and reconstruction $\hat{x}$, the objective is:

$$J(W) = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^{m}\|x^{(i)} - \hat{x}^{(i)}\|^2 + \lambda \sum_j \text{KL}(\rho \| \hat{\rho}_j)$$

where $\hat{\rho}_j = \frac{1}{m}\sum_i h_j(x^{(i)})$ is the average activation of hidden unit $j$, $\rho$ is a target sparsity level (typically $\rho \approx 0.05$), and $\text{KL}(\rho\|\hat{\rho}_j)$ is the KL divergence penalizing deviations from the target. This forces most neurons to be inactive for any given input, producing sparse, interpretable features.

''Large-scale gradient descent on GPUs:'' The Google Brain cat-neuron experiment scaled [[Gradient Descent|Gradient Descent]] to a billion-parameter network by distributing computation across 16,000 cores using model parallelism. Each machine computed gradients on a subset of the network, with parameter updates synchronized via a central parameter server. The update rule remained standard SGD:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \nabla_\theta L(\theta_t)$$

but the engineering challenge was computing $\nabla_\theta L$ for a network too large to fit on any single machine. This distributed training paradigm became the blueprint for all subsequent large-scale [[Deep Learning|Deep Learning]] systems, from GPT to modern diffusion models.

!! Impact on the Field

''Millions of students:'' The ML MOOC and Deep Learning Specialization collectively reached over 10 million enrollments. Many practitioners working in AI today cite Ng's courses as their entry point. By standardizing the curriculum — the same notation, the same progression from linear models to neural networks to CNNs — Ng created a shared language for the field that transcends institutional boundaries.

''Industry AI adoption:'' At Google Brain, Ng demonstrated that deep learning could work at internet scale, helping trigger the industry-wide adoption of neural networks that followed AlexNet (2012). At Baidu, he showed that Chinese tech companies could compete at the frontier of AI research, accelerating the global spread of deep learning expertise.

''The data-centric movement:'' Ng's advocacy for data-centric AI — through talks, competitions (the data-centric AI competition at NeurIPS), and Landing AI's tools — shifted the conversation in applied ML. Many practitioners in industrial AI now spend more time on data pipelines, labeling guidelines, and data quality metrics than on model architecture, a direct result of Ng's influence.

---

!! When AI Education Meets Reality

''The gap between courses and production:'' Ng's courses excel at teaching algorithms but cannot fully prepare students for the messy reality of deploying ML systems — data pipeline engineering, monitoring, retraining loops, organizational politics, and regulatory compliance. Many MOOC graduates discover that the hardest part of ML is not implementing [[Gradient Descent|Gradient Descent]] but getting clean data from a reluctant business unit. This gap between classroom ML and production ML has spawned an entire subfield (MLOps) that Ng's later courses increasingly address.

''Data-centric AI assumes a good-enough model exists:'' The data-centric philosophy works well when the model architecture is not the bottleneck — which is true for many vision and tabular tasks. But in frontier research (e.g., reasoning in large language models, protein structure prediction), architectural innovation still drives progress. Holding the model fixed and improving data is not always the right strategy, and the line between model-centric and data-centric improvement is blurrier than the slogan suggests.

''Scaling education vs. scaling depth:'' MOOCs can teach millions of people the basics, but deep research intuition — the kind that leads to new architectures or theoretical breakthroughs — is still transmitted through apprenticeship in research labs. Ng's courses create practitioners, not necessarily researchers. The field needs both, and the spectacular success of the courses can create an illusion that watching videos is a substitute for years of hands-on research experience.

!! Technical Details

''Key papers:'' Ng and Jordan (2001), //On Discriminative vs. Generative Classifiers// — showed logistic regression can outperform naive Bayes with enough data, a foundational result in statistical learning theory. Le et al. (2012), //Building High-level Features Using Large Scale Unsupervised Learning// — the Google Brain cat-neuron paper, 1 billion connections trained on 10 million YouTube thumbnails. Coates et al. (2011), //An Analysis of Single-Layer Networks in Unsupervised Feature Learning// — demonstrated that simple architectures with enough data and proper preprocessing could match deep networks on several benchmarks. Amodei, Ananthanarayanan, ..., Ng et al. (2016), //Deep Speech 2// — end-to-end speech recognition using RNNs with batch normalization and CTC loss.

''Stanford courses:'' CS229 (Machine Learning), CS230 (Deep Learning), CS294A (Sparse Autoencoder). The CS229 lecture notes on supervised learning, generative models, and the EM algorithm remain widely referenced.

''Awards and recognition:'' Named to MIT Technology Review's TR35 (2008). Time 100 Most Influential People (2012). Fellow of AAAI. Member of the National Academy of Engineering.

''The data-centric AI framework:'' Ng's practical prescription: (1) start with a known good model architecture, (2) perform error analysis on the validation set to identify data problems, (3) systematically improve labeling consistency, (4) use data augmentation targeted at underperforming slices, (5) iterate on data rather than model architecture. This workflow is implemented in Landing AI's LandingLens platform.
