! Support Vector Machines
<img src="assets/support_vector_machines.svg" alt="Support Vector Machines" width="800">

Imagine you are a referee drawing a line on a field to separate two rival teams. You could draw the line anywhere between them, but the smartest choice is the one that leaves the widest possible buffer zone on each side -- so even if a few players drift, they still stay on their team's side. That is exactly what a ''Support Vector Machine'' (SVM) does: it finds the boundary that maximises the cushion between two classes of data, making it one of the most geometrically elegant classifiers in machine learning.

Developed by [[Vladimir Vapnik|Vladimir Vapnik]] and colleagues in the 1990s, SVMs dominated practical classification tasks for over a decade before deep learning took the spotlight. They remain a gold-standard baseline and a window into the beautiful interplay between geometry, optimisation, and generalisation theory.

!! The Core Insight

Most classifiers simply find //some// boundary that separates the training data. SVMs insist on finding the //best// one -- the ''maximum-margin hyperplane''. The margin is the perpendicular distance from the decision boundary to the nearest data point on each side. By maximising this margin, SVMs achieve the tightest possible generalisation bound among linear classifiers: a wider margin means fewer ways the model can be fooled by unseen data.

The data points that sit exactly on the margin boundary are called ''support vectors''. They are the critical few that fully determine where the [[Hyperplane|Hyperplane]] sits. Remove any non-support-vector point, and the boundary does not move at all. This sparsity property makes SVMs both interpretable and memory-efficient.

!! Maximum Margin Classification

Picture a two-dimensional scatter plot with red dots on one side and blue dots on the other. Infinitely many lines could separate them. The SVM picks the line with the largest //gap// to the nearest red and nearest blue points.

In higher dimensions, the "line" becomes a [[Hyperplane|Hyperplane]] -- a flat surface of dimension $d-1$ living in $d$-dimensional space. The margin is measured perpendicular to this surface.

Three geometric facts anchor the intuition:

* The decision boundary is fully specified by the support vectors alone -- typically only a small fraction of all training points.
* The margin width is $\frac{2}{\|w\|}$, where $w$ is the weight vector normal to the hyperplane. To maximise margin, we minimise $\|w\|$.
* In the //non-separable// case, we allow some points to violate the margin using ''slack variables'' $\xi_i$, penalised by a parameter $C$. Large $C$ means "tolerate few violations"; small $C$ means "accept a wider, softer margin."

This geometric picture connects directly to [[Convex Optimization|Convex Optimization]]: the SVM objective is a convex quadratic program with linear constraints, guaranteeing a single global optimum -- no saddle points, no local minima.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ''primal formulation'' of the hard-margin SVM seeks:

$$\min_{w,b} \; \frac{1}{2}\|w\|^2 \quad \text{s.t.} \quad y_i(w^T x_i + b) \geq 1, \;\; i = 1,\dots,n$$

Each constraint demands that every training point $x_i$ with label $y_i \in \{-1,+1\}$ falls on the correct side of the margin.

For the ''soft-margin'' case, we introduce slack variables $\xi_i \geq 0$:

$$\min_{w,b,\xi} \; \frac{1}{2}\|w\|^2 + C \sum_{i=1}^{n} \xi_i \quad \text{s.t.} \quad y_i(w^T x_i + b) \geq 1 - \xi_i$$

This is equivalent to minimising the [[Hinge Loss|Hinge Loss]] with $\ell_2$ [[Regularization|Regularization]].

Using [[Lagrangian Duality|Lagrangian Duality]], we form the ''dual problem'':

$$\max_{\alpha} \; \sum_{i=1}^{n} \alpha_i - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j} \alpha_i \alpha_j y_i y_j \, x_i^T x_j \quad \text{s.t.} \quad 0 \leq \alpha_i \leq C, \;\; \sum_i \alpha_i y_i = 0$$

The dual reveals two crucial facts. First, the solution depends only on ''inner products'' $x_i^T x_j$. Second, data points with $\alpha_i > 0$ are the support vectors.

The ''kernel trick'' exploits the first fact: replace every inner product $x_i^T x_j$ with a kernel function $K(x_i, x_j) = \langle \phi(x_i), \phi(x_j) \rangle$, implicitly mapping data into a high- (even infinite-) dimensional feature space without ever computing the mapping $\phi$ explicitly. This is the gateway to [[Kernel Methods|Kernel Methods]] and the theory of [[RKHS|RKHS]].

!! SVMs in Practice

''Kernel selection'' is the most consequential design choice:

* ''Linear kernel'' $K(x,x') = x^T x'$ -- fast, scalable, works well when features already capture the structure (text classification, genomics).
* ''RBF (Gaussian) kernel'' $K(x,x') = \exp(-\gamma\|x-x'\|^2)$ -- the default "try first" nonlinear kernel. $\gamma$ controls how local the decision boundary is.
* ''Polynomial kernel'' $K(x,x') = (x^T x' + c)^d$ -- useful when interactions of a known degree matter.

The ''C parameter'' controls the bias-variance trade-off: too large and the model overfits noisy margins; too small and it underfits by ignoring the data's structure. Grid search or cross-validation over $(C, \gamma)$ is standard practice.

''Multi-class strategies'': SVMs are inherently binary classifiers. Multi-class problems use either //one-vs-rest// (train $K$ classifiers) or //one-vs-one// (train $\binom{K}{2}$ classifiers and vote).

''SVMs vs. neural networks'': SVMs offer convex optimisation (guaranteed global optimum), strong theoretical guarantees, and work brilliantly on small-to-medium structured datasets. Neural nets scale better to massive data and learn feature representations automatically. In the era before 2012, SVMs were often the winning method on benchmarks; after [[ImageNet|ImageNet]] and AlexNet, deep learning pulled ahead on perceptual tasks while SVMs remain competitive on tabular and structured data.

!! When SVMs Break Down

''1. Large-scale datasets.'' Training a kernel SVM has time complexity $O(n^2)$ to $O(n^3)$. Datasets with millions of points make exact kernel SVM training impractical. Approximations (random Fourier features, Nystroem method) or switching to linear SVMs with stochastic gradient descent are necessary workarounds.

''2. Noisy, overlapping classes.'' When the two classes are heavily interleaved and no kernel cleanly separates them, SVMs struggle -- the soft margin becomes so wide that the model degenerates toward a trivial classifier. Ensemble methods or models that naturally handle class overlap (e.g., gradient-boosted trees) often outperform here.

''3. Sensitivity to feature scaling and kernel hyperparameters.'' SVMs with RBF kernels are notoriously sensitive to the scale of input features and to the choice of $\gamma$. Failing to standardise features or to tune hyperparameters can produce wildly poor results, yet the model gives no obvious warning -- it simply draws a bad boundary with high confidence.

!! Technical Details

''Sequential Minimal Optimization (SMO)'' is the dominant algorithm for solving the SVM dual. Introduced by John Platt in 1998, SMO breaks the large QP into the smallest possible sub-problems -- optimising just two $\alpha_i$ at a time -- making the algorithm practical for tens of thousands of examples.

''Kernel types and Mercer's condition'': A function $K$ qualifies as a valid kernel if and only if the induced Gram matrix is positive semi-definite for all possible datasets (Mercer's theorem). Common kernels:
* RBF: $K(x,x') = \exp(-\gamma\|x-x'\|^2)$
* Polynomial: $K(x,x') = (\gamma \, x^T x' + c)^d$
* Sigmoid (hyperbolic tangent): $K(x,x') = \tanh(\gamma \, x^T x' + c)$ -- not always Mercer-valid; use with caution.

''VC dimension'': The class of linear classifiers in $\mathbb{R}^d$ has VC dimension $d+1$. SVM's margin maximisation effectively reduces the //effective// VC dimension, tightening generalisation bounds even in high-dimensional spaces -- a result formalised by Vapnik's structural risk minimisation principle.

''nu-SVM'' (Scholkopf et al.) reparametrises the soft-margin SVM by replacing $C$ with a parameter $\nu \in (0,1]$ that upper-bounds the fraction of margin errors and lower-bounds the fraction of support vectors, giving more intuitive control. This variant is closely associated with the work of [[Bernhard Schölkopf|Bernhard Schölkopf]].

''One-class SVM'' learns a boundary around a single class for anomaly detection, solving $\min \frac{1}{2}\|w\|^2 + \frac{1}{\nu n}\sum \xi_i - \rho$ subject to $w^T\phi(x_i) \geq \rho - \xi_i$.
