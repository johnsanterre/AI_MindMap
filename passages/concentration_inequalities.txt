! Concentration Inequalities

<img src="assets/concentration_inequalities.svg" alt="Diagram showing the hierarchy of concentration inequalities from Markov to sub-Gaussian bounds" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you flip a fair coin 1,000 times. You expect about 500 heads, but how surprised should you be if you see 600? ''Concentration inequalities'' answer exactly this question: they provide quantitative upper bounds on the probability that a random variable deviates from its expected value by more than a specified amount. They are the mathematical engine behind guarantees in machine learning — from bounding the gap between training and test error to certifying that a randomized algorithm will work. Without concentration inequalities, the <<link "Law of Large Numbers" "Law of Large Numbers">><</link>> tells you convergence //happens// but not //how fast//. Concentration inequalities supply the speed.

---

!! The Core Insight

The <<link "Law of Large Numbers" "Law of Large Numbers">><</link>> says that the sample average converges to the population mean as $n \to \infty$. But practitioners do not have infinite samples. Concentration inequalities convert this asymptotic promise into a finite-sample guarantee: for any fixed $n$ and any tolerance $t$, they bound $P(|\bar{X} - \mu| > t)$ from above. The tighter the bound, the fewer samples you need to be confident.

The key tradeoff is between assumptions and tightness. ''Markov's inequality'' assumes almost nothing — just that the random variable is nonnegative — and gives a correspondingly loose bound. ''Chebyshev's inequality'' adds a finite variance assumption and tightens the bound to $1/t^2$ decay. ''Chernoff bounds'' and ''Hoeffding's inequality'' assume bounded or sub-Gaussian random variables and achieve exponential decay $e^{-ct^2}$. ''McDiarmid's inequality'' extends exponential concentration to functions of independent random variables that satisfy a bounded-differences condition. Each step up the ladder demands more structure from the problem but rewards you with a dramatically sharper guarantee.

This hierarchy is not academic bookkeeping. In <<link "PAC Learning" "PAC Learning">><</link>>, the sample complexity — how many training examples you need to guarantee generalization — is directly determined by which concentration inequality applies to your loss function. Tighter concentration means fewer samples, which means faster, cheaper learning.

---

!! The Ladder of Bounds

The concentration inequalities form a strict hierarchy, each one a refinement of the previous.

''Markov's inequality'' is the foundation. For a nonnegative random variable $X$ and $t > 0$: $P(X \geq t) \leq E[X] / t$. It follows from a single line of measure theory and requires no moment assumptions beyond existence of the mean. Its weakness is that it only gives $1/t$ decay, which is nearly useless for large deviations.

''Chebyshev's inequality'' applies Markov to the squared deviation $(X - \mu)^2$: $P(|X - \mu| \geq t) \leq \text{Var}(X) / t^2$. This is a significant improvement — quadratic rather than linear decay — and it uses both the mean and variance. For $n$ i.i.d. copies with variance $\sigma^2$, Chebyshev gives $P(|\bar{X} - \mu| \geq t) \leq \sigma^2 / (nt^2)$. Still polynomial, still often too loose for ML applications, but a genuine step forward.

''Chernoff bounds'' introduce the moment-generating function (MGF). For any $s > 0$: $P(X \geq t) = P(e^{sX} \geq e^{st}) \leq E[e^{sX}] / e^{st}$. Optimizing over $s$ yields exponentially decaying tail bounds whenever the MGF is well-behaved. For sums of independent Bernoulli or bounded random variables, Chernoff bounds give multiplicative error guarantees that are essentially optimal.

''Hoeffding's inequality'' is a workhorse specialization. If $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are independent with $X_i \in [a_i, b_i]$, then: $$P\left(\bar{X} - \mu \geq t\right) \leq \exp\left(\frac{-2n^2 t^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n (b_i - a_i)^2}\right)$$. For identically bounded variables with range $R = b - a$, this simplifies to $\exp(-2nt^2 / R^2)$. The exponential decay in $n$ is what makes finite-sample guarantees practical.

''McDiarmid's inequality'' (the bounded-differences inequality) generalizes Hoeffding from sums to arbitrary functions. If $f(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$ satisfies $\sup_{x_i, x_i'} |f(\ldots, x_i, \ldots) - f(\ldots, x_i', \ldots)| \leq c_i$ for each coordinate, then: $$P\left(f - E[f] \geq t\right) \leq \exp\left(\frac{-2t^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n c_i^2}\right)$$. This is the inequality that directly powers <<link "Generalization Bounds" "Generalization Bounds">><</link>> in statistical learning theory: the empirical risk is a function of $n$ independent training examples, and changing one example changes the empirical risk by at most $1/n$ (for bounded loss), so McDiarmid immediately yields exponential concentration of the empirical risk around its expectation.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The Chernoff method — the engine behind Hoeffding, Bernstein, and McDiarmid — works by exponential tilting. To bound $P(S_n \geq t)$ where $S_n = \sum X_i$, you introduce an exponential: $$P(S_n \geq t) = P(e^{sS_n} \geq e^{st}) \leq \frac{E[e^{sS_n}]}{e^{st}}$$ by Markov's inequality applied to the nonnegative random variable $e^{sS_n}$. When the $X_i$ are independent, the MGF factors: $E[e^{sS_n}] = \prod_{i=1}^n E[e^{sX_i}]$. Each factor is bounded using the specific distributional assumption (boundedness, sub-Gaussianity, etc.). The parameter $s > 0$ is free, and the final step is to optimize: $\inf_{s > 0} e^{-st} \prod E[e^{sX_i}]$.

For a ''sub-Gaussian'' random variable with parameter $\sigma$, the defining property is: $$E[e^{s(X - \mu)}] \leq e^{s^2 \sigma^2 / 2} \quad \text{for all } s \in \mathbb{R}$$. This says the MGF is dominated by that of a Gaussian with variance $\sigma^2$. Optimizing the Chernoff bound with this MGF gives: $P(X - \mu \geq t) \leq e^{-t^2/(2\sigma^2)}$, which is the ''sub-Gaussian tail bound''. Any bounded random variable on $[a, b]$ is sub-Gaussian with parameter $\sigma = (b-a)/2$ (Hoeffding's lemma), which is why Hoeffding's inequality is a consequence of the sub-Gaussian framework.

''Bernstein's inequality'' provides a sharper alternative when you know the variance. For independent zero-mean random variables with $|X_i| \leq M$ and $\sum E[X_i^2] = V$: $$P\left(\sum X_i \geq t\right) \leq \exp\left(\frac{-t^2/2}{V + Mt/3}\right)$$. For small $t$ (relative to $M$), this behaves like $e^{-t^2/(2V)}$, recovering a variance-dependent Gaussian-like tail. For large $t$, it transitions to $e^{-t/(2M)}$, an exponential tail. Bernstein is tighter than Hoeffding whenever $V \ll nM^2$, i.e., when the variance is much smaller than the worst case implied by boundedness alone.

The <<link "Central Limit Theorem" "Central Limit Theorem">><</link>> provides the asymptotic benchmark: $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X} - \mu) \to N(0, \sigma^2)$ in distribution, so tail probabilities decay like $e^{-t^2/(2\sigma^2/n)}$ for large $n$. Concentration inequalities give //non-asymptotic// versions of this fact, valid for any finite $n$, at the cost of slightly worse constants.

---

!! Applications in Machine Learning

Concentration inequalities are not abstract tools — they are the foundation of nearly every generalization guarantee in ML.

''Generalization bounds:'' The <<link "Generalization Bounds" "Generalization Bounds">><</link>> that relate empirical risk to true risk are direct applications of McDiarmid or Hoeffding. If the loss function $\ell$ is bounded in $[0, 1]$, then with probability at least $1 - \delta$: $$|R_{\text{emp}}(h) - R(h)| \leq \sqrt{\frac{\ln(2/\delta)}{2n}}$$ for any fixed hypothesis $h$. Extending this to a uniform bound over a hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}$ requires a union bound (for finite $\mathcal{H}$) or covering numbers / Rademacher complexity (for infinite $\mathcal{H}$), but the core probabilistic engine is still concentration.

''PAC learning:'' The <<link "PAC Learning" "PAC Learning">><</link>> framework asks: how many samples $n$ suffice to ensure that, with probability $\geq 1 - \delta$, the learned hypothesis has error $\leq \epsilon$? The answer is obtained by inverting a concentration inequality: $n \geq \frac{\ln(|\mathcal{H}|/\delta)}{2\epsilon^2}$ for finite hypothesis classes, directly from Hoeffding.

''Randomized algorithms:'' Random projections (Johnson-Lindenstrauss), hashing, and sketching algorithms all rely on concentration to guarantee that random dimensionality reduction preserves distances or inner products. The JL lemma is fundamentally a sub-Gaussian concentration result.

''Bandit algorithms:'' Upper confidence bound (UCB) methods in multi-armed bandits use Hoeffding or Bernstein bounds to construct confidence intervals around estimated rewards. Tighter concentration translates directly to lower regret.

---

!! When Concentration Inequalities Break Down

''Heavy-tailed distributions:'' When the random variable lacks a finite MGF — for example, Pareto or Cauchy-distributed data — the Chernoff method does not apply. You are stuck with Chebyshev (if the variance exists) or Markov (if only the mean exists). In these regimes, deviations from the mean are common, not rare, and exponential concentration is simply false. Robust mean estimation under heavy tails is an active research area precisely because the standard concentration toolkit fails. Using Hoeffding on unbounded data produces vacuous or incorrect bounds.

''Dependent random variables:'' All the standard inequalities (Hoeffding, Chernoff, McDiarmid) assume independence. When training examples are correlated — as in time series, graph-structured data, or data collected via an adaptive policy in reinforcement learning — these bounds do not hold as stated. Extensions exist (martingale concentration, mixing conditions, Azuma-Hoeffding for martingale differences), but they require different assumptions and often produce weaker bounds. Blindly applying Hoeffding to dependent data gives false confidence in generalization guarantees.

''Loose bounds from worst-case assumptions:'' Hoeffding bounds depend on the range $[a, b]$ of the random variable, not on the actual variance. If the variable is bounded in $[0, 100]$ but has variance $0.01$, Hoeffding treats it as if the variance were $(100-0)^2/4 = 2500$, which is absurdly conservative. In these cases, Bernstein or empirical Bernstein bounds are dramatically tighter. Many ML papers cite Hoeffding bounds that are orders of magnitude looser than necessary because the authors did not account for the actual variance structure of their loss.

!! Technical Details

''Hoeffding's lemma'' is the key technical ingredient: if $X \in [a, b]$ with $E[X] = 0$, then $E[e^{sX}] \leq \exp(s^2(b-a)^2/8)$ for all $s$. The proof uses convexity of $e^{sx}$: since $X \in [a, b]$, the function $e^{sX}$ is bounded above by its chord, and the expectation of the chord is bounded by $\exp(s^2(b-a)^2/8)$ via a Taylor expansion of $\ln E[e^{sX}]$ around $s=0$, retaining the first non-trivial term.

''Sub-Gaussian random variables'' form a class closed under addition and linear transformation. If $X$ is $\sigma$-sub-Gaussian, then $cX$ is $|c|\sigma$-sub-Gaussian. If $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are independent and $\sigma_i$-sub-Gaussian, then $\sum X_i$ is $(\sum \sigma_i^2)^{1/2}$-sub-Gaussian. This algebraic closure is why the sub-Gaussian framework is so powerful: it reduces concentration for sums to concentration for individual terms.

''Sub-exponential'' random variables satisfy $E[e^{s(X-\mu)}] \leq e^{s^2 v^2 / 2}$ only for $|s| < 1/\alpha$ (not all $s$). Chi-squared random variables and products of sub-Gaussians are sub-exponential. Bernstein's inequality is the natural concentration result for sub-exponential sums, producing Gaussian tails for small deviations and exponential tails for large deviations.

''Matrix concentration'' extends scalar inequalities to random matrices. The matrix Chernoff bound (Tropp, 2012) states that for independent random positive semidefinite matrices $X_i$ with $\|X_i\| \leq R$: $P(\|\sum X_i - E[\sum X_i]\| \geq t) \leq d \cdot \exp(-t^2 / (2\|\sum E[X_i^2]\| + Rt/3))$, where $d$ is the matrix dimension. This is essential for random matrix theory applications in compressed sensing and dimensionality reduction.

''Talagrand's concentration inequality'' is the deepest result in the hierarchy: it shows that any convex Lipschitz function of independent random variables concentrates around its median with sub-Gaussian tails. McDiarmid can be derived as a special case. Talagrand's inequality is the go-to tool for proving concentration of suprema of empirical processes, which underlies uniform convergence in <<link "Statistical Learning Theory" "Statistical Learning Theory">><</link>>.

The ''empirical Bernstein bound'' (Maurer & Pontil, 2009) replaces the unknown population variance with the sample variance, giving a fully data-dependent confidence interval. With probability $\geq 1 - \delta$: $|\bar{X} - \mu| \leq \sqrt{\frac{2\hat{V}\ln(3/\delta)}{n}} + \frac{3\ln(3/\delta)}{n}$, where $\hat{V}$ is the sample variance. This is the practical version of Bernstein that is used in adaptive algorithms.
