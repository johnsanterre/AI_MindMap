! Concentration Inequalities

<img src="assets/concentration_inequalities.svg" alt="Diagram showing a hierarchy of concentration bounds from Markov (loosest) to sub-Gaussian (tightest), each requiring progressively stronger assumptions" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you pour a million cups of coffee, each with a slightly different amount of sugar. Common sense says the average sweetness across your million cups will be very close to the "true" expected sweetness — it would be shocking if the average were wildly off. ''Concentration inequalities'' are the mathematical machinery that makes "shocking" precise: they bound the probability that a random variable (or a sum of random variables) deviates far from its expected value. The stronger your assumptions about the variable's behavior, the tighter the bound you earn — ranging from the almost-trivially-loose Markov inequality all the way to razor-sharp sub-Gaussian tail bounds. In <<link "statistical learning theory" "Statistical Learning Theory">><</link>>, these bounds are the reason you can trust that a model trained on finite data will generalize to unseen examples.

---

!! The Core Insight

The fundamental question concentration inequalities answer is: //how likely is it that a random quantity lands far from where you expect it?// This matters everywhere in machine learning because learning itself is an act of estimation. When you train a model on a finite sample and hope it generalizes, you are implicitly betting that your sample average (empirical risk) is close to the population average (true risk). Concentration inequalities are the theorems that justify that bet.

The key trade-off is between ''assumptions'' and ''tightness''. Markov's inequality assumes almost nothing (just non-negativity) and gives a correspondingly weak bound. Chebyshev adds a finite-variance assumption and gets a quadratic improvement. Hoeffding and Chernoff demand bounded or independent variables and deliver exponential tail decay. Sub-Gaussian bounds require moment-generating-function control and produce the sharpest results. Each step up the ladder demands more structure from the random variable but rewards you with a dramatically tighter guarantee — and those tighter guarantees translate directly into stronger <<link "generalization bounds" "Generalization Gap">><</link>>.

The <<link "Central Limit Theorem" "Central Limit Theorem">><</link>> tells you that sample averages //eventually// look Gaussian, but it is an asymptotic statement — it says nothing about how many samples you need. Concentration inequalities are the non-asymptotic counterpart: they say exactly how much data suffices for a given level of confidence, right now, for your finite sample.

---

!! A Hierarchy of Bounds

The concentration inequalities form a natural hierarchy. Each one builds on the previous by adding assumptions and sharpening the conclusion.

''Markov's inequality'' is the foundation. For any non-negative random variable $X$ and $t > 0$: the probability $P(X \geq t)$ is at most $E[X]/t$. It requires nothing beyond non-negativity — no variance, no independence, no boundedness. The price is that the bound decays only as $1/t$, which is almost useless for large deviations.

''Chebyshev's inequality'' applies Markov to $(X - \mu)^2$ and yields $P(|X - \mu| \geq t) \leq \sigma^2 / t^2$. By requiring a finite variance $\sigma^2$, you gain $1/t^2$ decay — a meaningful improvement, but still polynomial.

''Chernoff bounds'' use the moment-generating function (MGF) trick: for any $\lambda > 0$, $P(X \geq t) = P(e^{\lambda X} \geq e^{\lambda t}) \leq E[e^{\lambda X}] / e^{\lambda t}$. Optimizing over $\lambda$ produces exponentially decaying bounds. This technique is the engine behind all the sharper inequalities that follow.

''Hoeffding's inequality'' specializes the Chernoff approach to sums of independent bounded random variables. If $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are independent with $X_i \in [a_i, b_i]$, then the sample mean concentrates exponentially around its expectation. This is the inequality most commonly invoked in PAC learning proofs and <<link "VC dimension" "VC Dimension">><</link>> arguments.

''McDiarmid's inequality'' (the bounded differences inequality) extends Hoeffding to any function $f(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$ of independent variables, as long as changing any single $X_i$ changes $f$ by at most $c_i$. This makes it directly applicable to complex quantities like empirical risk, which is a function of the entire training set.

''Bernstein's inequality'' improves on Hoeffding when the variance is small relative to the range. Instead of depending only on the range $[a_i, b_i]$, Bernstein's bound depends on both the variance and the range, giving tighter results when the variables are not spread out to their extremes. This variance-sensitive behavior makes it especially useful in <<link "Monte Carlo methods" "Monte Carlo Methods">><</link>> where variance reduction techniques are employed.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''Markov's inequality.'' For $X \geq 0$ and $t > 0$:

$$P(X \geq t) \leq \frac{E[X]}{t}$$

Proof in one line: $E[X] \geq E[X \cdot \mathbf{1}_{X \geq t}] \geq t \cdot P(X \geq t)$.

''Chebyshev's inequality.'' Apply Markov to $(X - \mu)^2$:

$$P(|X - \mu| \geq t) = P\left((X - \mu)^2 \geq t^2\right) \leq \frac{\text{Var}(X)}{t^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{t^2}$$

''Chernoff bound (generic form).'' For any random variable $X$ and $\lambda > 0$:

$$P(X \geq t) \leq \inf_{\lambda > 0} e^{-\lambda t} \, E\left[e^{\lambda X}\right] = \inf_{\lambda > 0} e^{-\lambda t} \, M_X(\lambda)$$

where $M_X(\lambda) = E[e^{\lambda X}]$ is the moment-generating function. The optimization over $\lambda$ produces the tightest exponential bound.

''Hoeffding's inequality.'' Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be independent with $X_i \in [a_i, b_i]$ and let $\bar{X} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$. Then:

$$P\left(\bar{X} - E[\bar{X}] \geq t\right) \leq \exp\left(-\frac{2n^2 t^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n (b_i - a_i)^2}\right)$$

The key lemma (Hoeffding's lemma) bounds the MGF of a bounded random variable: if $X \in [a, b]$ with $E[X] = 0$, then $E[e^{\lambda X}] \leq \exp\left(\frac{\lambda^2 (b-a)^2}{8}\right)$.

''McDiarmid's inequality.'' Let $f: \mathcal{X}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfy the bounded differences condition: for each $i$,

$$\sup_{x_1, \ldots, x_n, x_i'} \left|f(x_1, \ldots, x_i, \ldots, x_n) - f(x_1, \ldots, x_i', \ldots, x_n)\right| \leq c_i$$

Then for independent $X_1, \ldots, X_n$:

$$P\left(f(X_1, \ldots, X_n) - E[f] \geq t\right) \leq \exp\left(-\frac{2t^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n c_i^2}\right)$$

''Bernstein's inequality.'' Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be independent with $E[X_i] = 0$, $\text{Var}(X_i) \leq \sigma^2$, and $|X_i| \leq M$. Then:

$$P\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i \geq t\right) \leq \exp\left(-\frac{n t^2 / 2}{\sigma^2 + Mt/3}\right)$$

Notice the denominator interpolates between a ''Hoeffding-like regime'' (when $t$ is small relative to $\sigma^2/M$, the $\sigma^2$ term dominates and the bound decays as $e^{-nt^2/2\sigma^2}$) and a ''Poisson-like regime'' (when $t$ is large, the $Mt/3$ term dominates and the bound decays as $e^{-3nt/2M}$, which is linear in $t$ rather than quadratic).

!! Concentration in Machine Learning

Concentration inequalities are the mathematical backbone of <<link "generalization theory" "Generalization Gap">><</link>>. Here are the key connections:

''Generalization bounds.'' The empirical risk $\hat{R}(h) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \ell(h(x_i), y_i)$ is a sample average. By Hoeffding's inequality (if the loss is bounded), $\hat{R}(h)$ concentrates around the true risk $R(h)$ for any fixed hypothesis $h$. The hard part — handled by <<link "VC dimension" "VC Dimension">><</link>> and Rademacher complexity — is making this uniform over all hypotheses simultaneously (union bounds + concentration).

''PAC learning.'' The PAC framework asks: with probability at least $1 - \delta$, find a hypothesis with true risk within $\epsilon$ of optimal. Setting Hoeffding's bound equal to $\delta$ and solving for $n$ gives the sample complexity: $n = O\left(\frac{1}{\epsilon^2}\log\frac{|\mathcal{H}|}{\delta}\right)$ for a finite hypothesis class, where the $\log|\mathcal{H}|$ factor comes from a union bound over all hypotheses. For infinite classes, <<link "VC dimension" "VC Dimension">><</link>> replaces $\log|\mathcal{H}|$.

''Random matrix theory.'' Concentration inequalities for functions of random matrices (matrix Chernoff and Bernstein bounds) underpin results about random projections, compressed sensing, and the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma. These explain why dimensionality reduction works: random projections preserve pairwise distances with high probability.

''<<link "Bias-variance tradeoff" "Bias-Variance Tradeoff">><</link>>.'' The variance component of the bias-variance decomposition is precisely about concentration. A model with high variance means its predictions do not concentrate tightly around the expected prediction — it is sensitive to the particular training sample. Concentration inequalities quantify how much data you need to bring variance under control.

''Regularization and stability.'' Algorithmic stability — the property that changing one training example does not change the output much — is exactly the bounded-differences condition of McDiarmid's inequality. Stable algorithms (like regularized ERM) satisfy McDiarmid's condition with small constants $c_i$, yielding tight generalization bounds without needing to reason about the complexity of the hypothesis class.

''<<link "Monte Carlo methods" "Monte Carlo Methods">><</link>>.'' When estimating an expectation by sampling, concentration inequalities tell you how many samples you need for a given accuracy. Bernstein's inequality is particularly valuable here because variance reduction techniques (importance sampling, control variates) shrink the effective variance, and Bernstein's variance-sensitive bound captures this improvement while Hoeffding's range-based bound does not.

---

!! When Concentration Inequalities Break Down

''Heavy-tailed distributions:'' Hoeffding, Chernoff, and sub-Gaussian bounds all assume that tails decay at least exponentially. Many real-world quantities — financial returns, network degree distributions, gradients in deep learning — follow heavy-tailed distributions where the moment-generating function does not exist. Applying Hoeffding to heavy-tailed data produces bounds that are vacuously large or simply invalid. The remedy is to use median-of-means estimators or truncation-based techniques that achieve sub-Gaussian-like concentration even with only finite-variance assumptions, at the cost of constant factors.

''Dependence between variables:'' The classical inequalities (Hoeffding, McDiarmid, Bernstein) all require independence or at most mild mixing conditions. In time-series data, Markov chain <<link "Monte Carlo" "Monte Carlo Methods">><</link>> samples, or mini-batch SGD iterates, successive observations are strongly correlated. Applying independent-variable bounds to dependent data dramatically underestimates tail probabilities. Extensions exist — martingale concentration inequalities (Azuma-Hoeffding), mixing-time bounds — but they require careful analysis of the dependence structure and typically produce looser bounds.

''Unbounded or high-dimensional functions:'' McDiarmid's inequality requires that changing any one input changes the function by a bounded amount. Many quantities of interest in ML — the spectral norm of a weight matrix, the output of an unbounded activation function, the loss of an unregularized model — violate this condition. When the bounded-differences condition fails, McDiarmid gives no guarantee at all. In high dimensions, even when individual bounded-difference constants are small, their sum $\sum c_i^2$ can grow with dimension, making the bound vacuous. Dimension-free concentration results (e.g., Talagrand's inequality) address some of these cases but require substantially more sophisticated analysis.

!! Technical Details

''Sub-Gaussian random variables'' are defined by the property that their MGF satisfies $E[e^{\lambda(X - \mu)}] \leq e^{\lambda^2 \sigma^2 / 2}$ for all $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$, where $\sigma^2$ is the ''sub-Gaussian parameter'' (not necessarily the variance). Equivalently, the tails satisfy $P(|X - \mu| \geq t) \leq 2e^{-t^2/2\sigma^2}$. Gaussian, Bernoulli, and bounded random variables are all sub-Gaussian. Sums of independent sub-Gaussians are sub-Gaussian with parameter equal to the sum of individual parameters — this is why Hoeffding's inequality has the form it does.

''Sub-exponential random variables'' satisfy a weaker MGF condition: $E[e^{\lambda(X - \mu)}] \leq e^{\lambda^2 \nu^2 / 2}$ only for $|\lambda| < 1/\alpha$, where $(\nu, \alpha)$ are the sub-exponential parameters. Chi-squared and exponential random variables are sub-exponential but not sub-Gaussian. Bernstein's inequality is essentially the concentration result for sub-exponential variables.

''Symmetrization and Rademacher complexity.'' A powerful technique in empirical process theory replaces the deviation $\sup_{h \in \mathcal{H}} |R(h) - \hat{R}(h)|$ with the Rademacher complexity $\hat{\mathcal{R}}_n(\mathcal{H}) = E_\sigma\left[\sup_{h \in \mathcal{H}} \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i \ell(h(x_i), y_i)\right]$, where $\sigma_i$ are independent Rademacher (random sign) variables. McDiarmid's inequality then shows that the empirical Rademacher complexity concentrates around its expectation, connecting <<link "VC dimension" "VC Dimension">><</link>> to sample-based complexity measures.

''Talagrand's concentration inequality'' for product measures gives dimension-free bounds for convex Lipschitz functions of independent variables: if $f$ is $L$-Lipschitz (in $\ell_2$) and convex, then $P(f - E[f] \geq t) \leq e^{-t^2/2L^2}$. This is strictly stronger than McDiarmid for functions that are smooth rather than merely bounded-difference, and is the key tool behind many results in high-dimensional <<link "probability theory" "Probability Theory">><</link>>.

''Matrix concentration'' (Tropp, 2012): For independent random matrices $\mathbf{X}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{X}_n$ with $E[\mathbf{X}_i] = 0$ and $\|\mathbf{X}_i\| \leq R$, the matrix Bernstein inequality gives $P\left(\left\|\sum_i \mathbf{X}_i\right\| \geq t\right) \leq (d_1 + d_2) \exp\left(-\frac{t^2/2}{\sigma^2 + Rt/3}\right)$, where $\sigma^2 = \max\left\{\left\|\sum E[\mathbf{X}_i \mathbf{X}_i^T]\right\|, \left\|\sum E[\mathbf{X}_i^T \mathbf{X}_i]\right\|\right\}$. This underpins random projection guarantees and spectral analysis of random graphs.

''The empirical Bernstein bound'' (Maurer & Pontil, 2009) replaces the unknown population variance with the sample variance, giving a fully data-dependent confidence interval. With probability $\geq 1 - \delta$: $|\bar{X} - \mu| \leq \sqrt{\frac{2\hat{V}\ln(3/\delta)}{n}} + \frac{3\ln(3/\delta)}{n}$, where $\hat{V}$ is the sample variance. This is the practical version of Bernstein used in adaptive algorithms and <<link "bandit methods" "Explore-Exploit Tradeoff">><</link>>.
