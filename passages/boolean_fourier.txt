! Boolean Fourier Analysis

<img src="assets/boolean_fourier.svg" alt="Diagram showing a Boolean function on the hypercube decomposed into Fourier coefficients at different levels" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

An equalizer on a stereo breaks a song into bass, mid, and treble — low, medium, and high frequency components. Boolean Fourier analysis does the same thing to yes/no functions. Every function that takes $n$ binary inputs and returns a real number can be decomposed into "frequencies" corresponding to individual variables, pairs of variables, triples, and so on. A function whose energy is concentrated in the low frequencies depends mostly on individual variables acting alone; a function with lots of high-frequency energy depends on complex interactions among many variables simultaneously. This decomposition turns combinatorial questions about discrete functions into algebraic questions about their spectra.

!! The Core Insight

Every function $f:\{-1,+1\}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ has a unique ''Fourier expansion'' as a multilinear polynomial over the ''parity functions'' $\chi_S(x) = \prod_{i \in S} x_i$. The parity functions form an orthonormal basis under the uniform distribution on $\{-1,+1\}^n$, so any Boolean function can be written as a weighted sum of these basis elements. The ''Fourier coefficient'' $\hat{f}(S)$ measures how much $f$ correlates with the parity $\chi_S$, and the ''level'' of a coefficient — the size $|S|$ — plays the role of frequency. Level-0 is the mean of $f$, level-1 captures individual variable effects, level-2 captures pairwise interactions, and so on.

The power of this framework is that many natural properties of Boolean functions — noise sensitivity, influence of variables, learnability, hardness of approximation — translate directly into spectral conditions. A function that is easy to learn from random examples is one whose Fourier spectrum is concentrated on low levels. A function that is sensitive to random noise has significant weight on high levels. The spectrum is a fingerprint that reveals the function's computational and statistical character.

!! Fourier Expansion on the Boolean Cube

The ''Boolean hypercube'' $\{-1,+1\}^n$ has $2^n$ vertices, and the space of all functions $f:\{-1,+1\}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is a $2^n$-dimensional real vector space. The ''parity functions'' (also called ''characters'') $\{\chi_S : S \subseteq [n]\}$ form an orthonormal basis with respect to the inner product:

$$\langle f, g \rangle = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \{-1,+1\}^n}[f(x) \cdot g(x)] = \frac{1}{2^n} \sum_{x \in \{-1,+1\}^n} f(x) g(x)$$

The parity function for a subset $S \subseteq [n]$ is $\chi_S(x) = \prod_{i \in S} x_i$. For example, $\chi_{\{1,3\}}(x) = x_1 x_3$. The empty parity $\chi_\emptyset(x) = 1$ is the constant function. Orthonormality means $\langle \chi_S, \chi_T \rangle = 1$ if $S = T$ and $0$ otherwise.

Every function $f$ therefore has a unique expansion:

$$f(x) = \sum_{S \subseteq [n]} \hat{f}(S)\, \chi_S(x)$$

where $\hat{f}(S) = \langle f, \chi_S \rangle = \mathbb{E}[f(x) \chi_S(x)]$.

The ''Fourier weight at level $k$'' is $W^k[f] = \sum_{|S|=k} \hat{f}(S)^2$. For a $\{-1,+1\}$-valued function, ''Parseval's identity'' gives $\sum_S \hat{f}(S)^2 = \mathbb{E}[f(x)^2] = 1$, so the Fourier weights at different levels form a probability distribution over levels. The ''spectral sample'' draws $S$ with probability $\hat{f}(S)^2$, and its distribution encodes the complexity of $f$.

The ''influence'' of variable $i$ on $f$ is $\mathrm{Inf}_i[f] = \Pr[f(x) \neq f(x^{\oplus i})]$ where $x^{\oplus i}$ flips coordinate $i$. In Fourier terms:

$$\mathrm{Inf}_i[f] = \sum_{S \ni i} \hat{f}(S)^2$$

The ''total influence'' $I[f] = \sum_i \mathrm{Inf}_i[f] = \sum_S |S| \cdot \hat{f}(S)^2$ is the expected level under the spectral sample — functions with high total influence have spectra concentrated at higher levels.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ''noise operator'' $T_\rho$ at noise rate $\rho \in [0,1]$ acts on Fourier coefficients by damping:

$$\widehat{T_\rho f}(S) = \rho^{|S|} \hat{f}(S)$$

Applying noise to $f$ is the same as independently keeping each input bit with probability $(1+\rho)/2$ and flipping it otherwise. In the Fourier domain, this is a simple scaling: high-level coefficients are damped exponentially more than low-level ones, exactly like a low-pass filter. The ''noise sensitivity'' of $f$ at rate $\rho$ is:

$$\mathrm{NS}_\rho[f] = \Pr[f(x) \neq f(y)] = \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{2} \sum_S \rho^{|S|} \hat{f}(S)^2$$

where $y$ is obtained from $x$ by independently flipping each bit with probability $(1-\rho)/2$.

The ''Bonami-Beckner (hypercontractive) inequality'' is the deepest tool in the theory. For $q > 2$:

$$\|T_\rho f\|_q \leq \|f\|_2 \quad \text{when } \rho \leq \frac{1}{\sqrt{q-1}}$$

This inequality implies that functions with low-degree Fourier spectra cannot be too "spiky" — they must spread their mass relatively evenly. Hypercontractivity is the engine behind the ''KKL inequality'':

$$\max_i \mathrm{Inf}_i[f] \geq \mathrm{Var}[f] \cdot \Omega\!\left(\frac{\log n}{n}\right)$$

which says every balanced Boolean function must have at least one variable with influence $\Omega(\log n / n)$. This has consequences for social choice theory (Arrow's theorem), circuit complexity, and hardness of approximation.

The ''Friedgut junta theorem'' states: if $f:\{-1,+1\}^n \to \{-1,+1\}$ has total influence $I[f] = K$, then $f$ is $\epsilon$-close to a ''junta'' — a function depending on at most $2^{O(K/\epsilon)}$ variables. Functions with bounded total influence are essentially simple.

''Parseval's identity'' decomposes variance:

$$\mathrm{Var}[f] = \sum_{S \neq \emptyset} \hat{f}(S)^2 = \sum_{k=1}^{n} W^k[f]$$

This connects the "energy" of $f$ to its spectral profile, much like Parseval's theorem in classical [[Fourier Analysis|Fourier Analysis]] connects the energy of a signal to its frequency components.

!! Boolean Analysis in Machine Learning

''PAC learning low-degree functions.'' The Linial-Mansour-Nisan (LMN) theorem (1993) showed that any function computable by a polynomial-size circuit of depth $d$ has its Fourier spectrum $\epsilon$-concentrated on levels up to $O(\log(n/\epsilon))^d$. Since low-degree Fourier coefficients can be estimated from random samples, this implies AC$^0$ functions are PAC learnable in quasipolynomial time. The algorithm simply estimates $\hat{f}(S)$ for all $|S| \leq k$ and uses the resulting low-degree polynomial as a hypothesis. This is one of the deepest connections between circuit complexity and learning.

''Feature selection and variable importance.'' The influence $\mathrm{Inf}_i[f]$ directly measures how much variable $i$ matters to $f$. Variables with zero influence can be discarded. The total influence $I[f]$ bounds the number of relevant variables (via Friedgut's theorem). In practice, computing influences from data gives a principled feature selection method for Boolean-valued prediction tasks.

''Decision tree complexity.'' A decision tree of depth $d$ computes a function with all Fourier weight at level $\leq d$. More precisely, the expected depth of a random-input query equals $I[f]$, and decision tree size is related to the sparsity of the Fourier spectrum. Functions with concentrated spectra admit small decision trees; functions with diffuse, high-level spectra require deep trees.

''Property testing.'' Testing whether $f$ is a ''dictator'' (depends on one variable), a ''junta'' (depends on few variables), or ''monotone'' can be done with $O(1)$ or $O(\log n)$ queries using Fourier-analytic techniques. The test for being a junta examines whether most Fourier weight is concentrated on a small number of coordinates.

''Neural network expressivity.'' Parity functions $\chi_S$ with $|S| = n$ require $\Omega(2^n)$ neurons in shallow networks. This is a Fourier-analytic proof of depth separation: deep networks compute functions with high-level Fourier coefficients more efficiently than shallow ones. The relationship between depth and the ''Fourier degree'' a network can express connects [[Statistical Learning Theory|Statistical Learning Theory]] to circuit complexity.

!! When Boolean Fourier Analysis Breaks Down

''The curse of exponential coefficients.'' A function on $n$ bits has $2^n$ Fourier coefficients. For moderate $n$ (say $n = 100$), enumerating all subsets $S \subseteq [n]$ is impossible. Learning algorithms that estimate low-degree coefficients scale as $\binom{n}{k}$, which is polynomial for fixed $k$ but grows rapidly as $k$ increases. Functions whose significant Fourier weight lives at levels $k = \Omega(n)$ — like parity on all $n$ bits — are provably hard to learn from random examples under any distribution-free algorithm. The Fourier approach gives clean learnability results only when the spectrum is concentrated at low levels.

''Real-valued and continuous inputs.'' Boolean Fourier analysis applies to functions on $\{-1,+1\}^n$, not to continuous inputs. Extending to $\mathbb{R}^n$ requires switching to Hermite polynomials (for Gaussian inputs) or other orthogonal systems, and many of the cleanest combinatorial results — the KKL inequality, Friedgut's theorem, hypercontractivity bounds with tight constants — do not transfer directly. Most ML models operate on continuous features, so the Boolean theory is a proof technique and source of insight rather than a direct computational tool.

''Uniform distribution assumption.'' The standard theory assumes inputs are drawn uniformly from $\{-1,+1\}^n$. Real data is almost never uniform — features are correlated, some patterns are far more common than others. The Fourier basis under the uniform measure is not orthogonal under non-uniform distributions, so the entire decomposition changes. Generalizations exist (e.g., using product distributions and biased parity functions), but they require distribution-specific analysis and lose much of the elegance of the uniform theory.

!! Technical Details

''Historical development.'' Boolean Fourier analysis traces to the work of Ben-Or and Linial (1985) on collective coin-flipping, and Kahn, Kalai, and Linial (1988) on influences and threshold phenomena. The LMN theorem (1993) connected it to computational learning theory. O'Donnell's textbook //Analysis of Boolean Functions// (2014) is the standard reference.

''Key results.'' The ''KKL theorem'' (1988): every balanced Boolean function has a variable with influence $\Omega(\log n / n)$. ''Friedgut's theorem'' (1998): functions with bounded total influence are close to juntas. The ''invariance principle'' (Mossel, O'Donnell, Oleszkiewicz 2010): low-influence, low-degree functions have similar distributions whether inputs are Boolean or Gaussian — this bridges discrete and continuous analysis. The ''Majority is Stablest'' theorem (2005): among all balanced low-influence functions, majority is the most noise-stable.

''Connections to hardness.'' Fourier analysis underpins the proof of the PCP theorem and hardness of approximation. Hastad's 3-bit PCP (1997) uses the noise sensitivity of random parities. The ''Unique Games Conjecture'' connects to the ''Majority is Stablest'' theorem, implying optimal hardness for MAX-CUT.

''Beyond $\{-1,+1\}$.'' For functions on $\{0,1,\ldots,q-1\}^n$ (the $q$-ary cube), use characters of $\mathbb{Z}_q$. For the symmetric group, use representation theory (Young tableaux). The core idea — decomposing into orthogonal "frequency" components — generalizes widely.
