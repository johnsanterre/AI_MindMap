! Empirical Bayes

<img src="assets/empirical_bayes.svg" alt="Empirical Bayes diagram — left panel shows James-Stein shrinkage pulling extreme observations toward the grand mean, right panel shows the Robbins recipe for estimating priors from data" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are a teacher grading essays from hundreds of students. One student scores 98 on a single essay. Is she really a 98-level writer, or did she just have a great day? Your instinct says: probably somewhere between 98 and the class average. That instinct — pulling extreme observations toward a central value — is exactly what ''Empirical Bayes'' does, except it figures out //how much// to pull, and //toward what//, directly from the data. It is the statistical method that lets you be Bayesian without having to invent a prior from thin air, because the prior is estimated from the population of similar problems you are already looking at.

---

!! The Core Insight

Standard <<link "Bayesian inference" "Bayes' Theorem">><</link>> requires you to specify a prior distribution $p(\theta)$ //before// seeing any data. This is philosophically clean but practically awkward: where does the prior come from? Empirical Bayes answers with a pragmatic move — use the data itself to estimate the prior, then proceed with Bayes' theorem as usual.

The trick works when you have ''many related estimation problems'' at once. You want to estimate the true batting average of each of 500 baseball players, the true disease rate in each of 3,000 counties, or the true effect of each of 20,000 genes. Each individual problem has limited data, but the //collection// of problems reveals the shape of the population distribution. That population distribution becomes your estimated prior.

This is not circular reasoning. The prior for any single unit $\theta_i$ is estimated from all the //other// units — the individual observation $x_i$ contributes negligibly to the overall shape when the number of units is large. The result is a method that is neither fully Bayesian (the prior is data-driven, not subjective) nor fully frequentist (you still use Bayes' theorem for inference). It sits at the bridge between the two traditions, and it //works// — often dramatically better than treating each problem in isolation.

---

!! James-Stein and the Shrinkage Revolution

The intellectual roots of Empirical Bayes trace to one of the most surprising results in statistics. In 1961, Charles Stein and Willard James proved that when you estimate three or more means simultaneously, the obvious estimator — just use each sample mean — is ''inadmissible''. There exists a better estimator that dominates it in total mean squared error, and that estimator ''shrinks'' every observation toward a common value.

The ''James-Stein estimator'' for $p \geq 3$ independent normal means $x_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta_i, \sigma^2)$ is:

$$\hat{\theta}_i^{JS} = \bar{x} + \left(1 - \frac{(p-2)\sigma^2}{\sum_{j=1}^p (x_j - \bar{x})^2}\right)(x_i - \bar{x})$$

Every observation is pulled toward the grand mean $\bar{x}$. Extreme values get pulled the most. The shrinkage factor $B = (p-2)\sigma^2 / \sum(x_j - \bar{x})^2$ is estimated from the data — this is Empirical Bayes in action. The factor $(p-2)$ explains why the result requires $p \geq 3$: with fewer parameters, there is not enough ''borrowing of strength'' across problems to beat the individual estimates.

This result scandalized statisticians because it means that estimating a baseball player's batting average //improves// if you simultaneously estimate the temperature in Tokyo and the price of wheat — as long as you shrink all three toward a common mean. The philosophical discomfort is real, but the mathematics is unassailable.

Herbert Robbins took a different route in the 1950s, developing a ''nonparametric'' Empirical Bayes approach. Rather than assuming the prior belongs to a parametric family, Robbins showed you can estimate the Bayes-optimal decision rule directly from the marginal distribution of observations, without ever explicitly estimating the prior. His compound decision theory proved that as the number of problems grows, the Empirical Bayes procedure converges to the performance of the oracle Bayes procedure — the one that //knows// the true prior.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Suppose you observe $x_i | \theta_i \sim f(x_i | \theta_i)$ for $i = 1, \ldots, p$, and the parameters $\theta_i$ are drawn from an unknown prior $G(\theta)$. The marginal density of $x_i$ is:

$$m(x) = \int f(x | \theta) \, dG(\theta)$$

The Bayes posterior mean is:

$$E[\theta_i | x_i] = \frac{\int \theta \, f(x_i | \theta) \, dG(\theta)}{m(x_i)}$$

''Parametric Empirical Bayes:'' Assume $G$ belongs to a family $G_\eta$ indexed by hyperparameters $\eta$. Estimate $\eta$ by <<link "maximum likelihood" "Maximum Likelihood Estimation">><</link>> on the marginal:

$$\hat{\eta} = \arg\max_\eta \sum_{i=1}^p \log m_\eta(x_i)$$

Then plug $\hat{\eta}$ into the posterior formula. For the normal-normal model $x_i | \theta_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta_i, \sigma^2)$ and $\theta_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \tau^2)$, the posterior mean simplifies to:

$$\hat{\theta}_i^{EB} = \left(1 - \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma^2 + \hat{\tau}^2}\right) x_i + \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma^2 + \hat{\tau}^2} \hat{\mu}$$

This is a weighted average of the individual observation $x_i$ and the estimated grand mean $\hat{\mu}$. The shrinkage factor $B = \sigma^2 / (\sigma^2 + \hat{\tau}^2)$ depends on how much variation is due to noise ($\sigma^2$) versus true differences ($\tau^2$). When $\tau^2$ is large relative to $\sigma^2$, individual data is trusted; when $\tau^2$ is small, everyone gets pulled toward the mean — the data says the units are not really that different.

''Nonparametric Empirical Bayes (Robbins):'' For Poisson data $x_i | \theta_i \sim \text{Poisson}(\theta_i)$, Robbins showed:

$$E[\theta_i | x_i] = (x_i + 1) \frac{m(x_i + 1)}{m(x_i)}$$

The marginal $m(x)$ can be estimated by simple frequency counts — no parametric model for the prior is needed. This is the ''Robbins' formula'', and its elegance is remarkable: a fully nonparametric Bayes estimate using nothing but histogram bin counts.

!! Where Empirical Bayes Appears in Practice

''Large-scale hypothesis testing.'' When testing 20,000 genes for differential expression, the false discovery rate (FDR) framework of Benjamini-Hochberg can be understood as an Empirical Bayes procedure. The proportion of null hypotheses $\pi_0$ is estimated from the data, acting as a prior probability. Efron's ''local false discovery rate'' makes this connection explicit: it estimates the prior mixture $f(z) = \pi_0 f_0(z) + (1 - \pi_0) f_1(z)$ and computes posterior probabilities of being null.

''Insurance and actuarial science.'' The Buhlmann-Straub model estimates each policyholder's true risk by shrinking their claims history toward the portfolio average. This is parametric Empirical Bayes with a normal-normal model, and it has been standard practice since the 1960s.

''Sports analytics.'' Estimating true ability from limited performance data — batting averages, free-throw percentages, quarterback ratings — is a textbook application. The beta-binomial model treats each player's success probability as drawn from a Beta prior, estimates the Beta parameters from all players, and shrinks each player's observed rate toward the league average.

''<<link "Regularization" "Regularization">><</link>> as implicit Empirical Bayes.'' <<link "Ridge regression" "Ridge regression">><</link>> is the MAP estimate under a Gaussian prior on the weights. Choosing the regularization strength $\lambda$ by cross-validation is performing a kind of Empirical Bayes — selecting the prior hyperparameter that best predicts held-out data. Evidence maximization (Type-II MLE) makes this connection explicit, optimizing $\lambda$ by maximizing the marginal likelihood. The <<link "conjugate prior" "Conjugate Priors">><</link>> structure of linear models makes this tractable.

!! When X Breaks Down

''1. Too few groups to estimate the prior.'' Empirical Bayes borrows strength across many similar problems. With only 5 or 10 groups, the estimated prior is unstable — its variance is large, and the resulting shrinkage can be erratic. The method shines with hundreds or thousands of units. With a handful, you are better off using a genuine subjective prior or simply reporting the individual estimates with appropriate uncertainty.

''2. Heterogeneous populations.'' The method assumes all $\theta_i$ are drawn from the //same// prior $G$. If your 20,000 genes actually come from three distinct biological pathways with very different effect-size distributions, a single estimated prior will be a poor compromise. The shrinkage target will be wrong for all three subpopulations. You need mixture models or group-specific priors — which is possible but adds complexity and risks overfitting the prior itself.

''3. Ignoring the uncertainty in the estimated prior.'' Classical Empirical Bayes treats the estimated hyperparameters $\hat{\eta}$ as known, plugging them in without accounting for estimation error. This makes posterior intervals too narrow — they are calibrated for the //true// prior, not the estimated one. The fix is either hierarchical Bayes (put a hyperprior on $\eta$ and integrate it out) or bootstrap procedures, but both are more computationally expensive.

!! Technical Details

''Connection to shrinkage estimators.'' For $x_i | \theta_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta_i, 1)$ and $\theta_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \tau^2)$, the Bayes estimator is $\hat{\theta}_i^B = (1 - B) x_i$ where $B = 1/(1 + \tau^2)$. The James-Stein estimator uses $\hat{B} = (p-2)/\sum x_i^2$ as a data-driven estimate of $B$. This is asymptotically optimal but can overshoot: when $\hat{B} > 1$, the positive-part James-Stein estimator truncates at zero.

''Tweedie's formula.'' For the exponential family $x | \theta \sim p(x|\theta)$ with conjugate prior, the posterior mean takes the form $E[\theta|x] = x + \sigma^2 \frac{d}{dx} \log m(x)$ (for the normal case). This remarkable identity — ''Tweedie's formula'' — says the Bayes estimate equals the MLE plus a correction that depends only on the log-marginal density. You can estimate $\log m(x)$ nonparametrically, making this a general-purpose Empirical Bayes machine. Efron (2011) exploited this extensively.

''Stein's unbiased risk estimate (SURE).'' Rather than estimating the prior, you can directly minimize an unbiased estimate of the total MSE $\sum E[(\hat{\theta}_i - \theta_i)^2]$. SURE provides such an estimate without knowing the true $\theta_i$. Minimizing SURE over a class of shrinkage estimators yields an Empirical Bayes-like procedure with frequentist justification.

''Compound vs. parametric.'' Robbins' ''compound decision'' framework asks: what single decision rule $\delta(x)$, applied to all units, minimizes total risk? This is distinct from parametric Empirical Bayes but converges to the same solution as $p \to \infty$. The compound approach avoids misspecifying the prior family; the parametric approach is more efficient when the family is correct.

''False discovery rates.'' The local FDR $\text{lfdr}(x) = \pi_0 f_0(x) / m(x)$ is the posterior probability that a case is null, computed via Empirical Bayes. Efron's ''central matching'' estimates $f_0$ empirically rather than assuming a theoretical null, which is crucial when the null distribution is contaminated by unmodeled correlations.

''Bayesian regression connection.'' The <<link "Bayesian regression" "Bayesian Regression">><</link>> posterior mean under a Gaussian prior is an Empirical Bayes estimate when the prior variance is chosen by evidence maximization. The automatic relevance determination (ARD) prior — a separate variance per weight, optimized via marginal likelihood — is an Empirical Bayes approach to sparse regression.
