! Generating Functions

<img src="assets/generating_functions.svg" alt="Diagram showing ordinary, exponential, and moment generating functions as different encodings of sequences" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

A generating function is a clothesline: you hang an entire sequence of numbers $a_n$ on it, encoded as coefficients of a power series $G(x) = \sum_n a_n x^n$. The clothesline — the function — is easier to manipulate than the sequence directly. Multiply two clotheslines to convolve sequences, differentiate to shift indices, evaluate at special points to extract statistics. In probability, the ''moment generating function'' $M(t) = E[e^{tX}]$ encodes all moments of a distribution in one elegant object: differentiate $k$ times and evaluate at zero to get $E[X^k]$. In combinatorics, generating functions count labeled and unlabeled objects. In ML, //characteristic functions// are the bridge between distributions and their Fourier transforms — a tool powerful enough to prove the Central Limit Theorem in a few lines.

!! The Core Insight

Converting a sequence $\{a_n\}$ into a function $G(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x^n$ translates operations on sequences into algebraic operations on functions. ''Multiplying'' $G(x) \cdot H(x)$ computes the //convolution// of the two sequences: if $C_n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} A_k B_{n-k}$, then $G_C(x) = G_A(x) \cdot G_B(x)$. ''Differentiating'' $G(x)$ and multiplying by $x$ shifts the sequence and extracts moments. The key philosophical shift is that hard combinatorial or probabilistic identities often become simple algebraic facts once you move to the generating function world. ''Inversion'' — recovering individual coefficients $a_n$ from $G(x)$ — is accomplished via Cauchy's formula or by reading off Taylor coefficients.

!! The Zoo of Generating Functions

There are several standard flavors, each suited to a different domain:

- ''Ordinary Generating Function (OGF):'' $G(x) = \sum_{n \geq 0} a_n x^n$. Used in combinatorics for counting //unlabeled// structures. The Fibonacci numbers, Catalan numbers, and partition counts all have elegant OGFs.

- ''Exponential Generating Function (EGF):'' $G(x) = \sum_{n \geq 0} a_n \frac{x^n}{n!}$. Used for counting //labeled// structures (permutations, trees, set partitions). The $1/n!$ damping improves convergence and matches the natural scaling of labeled combinatorics.

- ''Probability Generating Function (PGF):'' $G(z) = E[z^X] = \sum_{n \geq 0} P(X = n)\, z^n$. Defined for non-negative integer-valued random variables. The PGF of a sum of independent random variables is the product of their individual PGFs.

- ''Moment Generating Function (MGF):'' $M(t) = E[e^{tX}]$. Moments are extracted via $M^{(k)}(0) = E[X^k]$. The MGF uniquely determines a distribution //when it exists// in a neighborhood of zero, but for heavy-tailed distributions it may be infinite for all $t > 0$.

- ''Characteristic Function:'' $\varphi(t) = E[e^{itX}]$. Always exists for any real-valued random variable. Equal to the Fourier transform of the density. Lévy's continuity theorem makes it the rigorous foundation for convergence-in-distribution arguments.

- ''Cumulant Generating Function (CGF):'' $K(t) = \log M(t)$. Its derivatives at zero give the //cumulants//: $K'(0) = \mu$ (mean), $K''(0) = \sigma^2$ (variance), $K^{(3)}(0) = $ skewness numerator, $K^{(4)}(0) = $ excess kurtosis numerator. For a Gaussian, all cumulants of order $\geq 3$ are exactly zero — this is a //characterization// of normality.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''Coefficient extraction'' via Cauchy's integral formula:

$$[x^n]\, G(x) = a_n = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint \frac{G(z)}{z^{n+1}}\, dz$$

This is the formal justification for reading off Taylor coefficients, and it connects generating functions to complex analysis.

''Convolution theorem:'' If $C_n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} A_k B_{n-k}$, then $G_C(x) = G_A(x) \cdot G_B(x)$. This is why the PGF of a sum of independent integer random variables is the product of their PGFs.

''Random walk:'' After $n$ steps of a symmetric $\pm 1$ walk, the PGF for position is $G_n(z) = \left(\frac{z + z^{-1}}{2}\right)^n$. Expanding recovers the binomial distribution of positions.

''Branching process extinction:'' The probability of eventual extinction $q$ satisfies $G(q) = q$, where $G$ is the PGF of the offspring distribution. The smallest root in $[0,1]$ gives the extinction probability.

''CLT via MGFs:'' Let $X_i$ be iid with $M(t)$. The MGF of the standardized sum $S_n = \frac{\sum X_i - n\mu}{\sqrt{n}\sigma}$ is:

$$M_{S_n}(t) = \left[M\!\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}\,\sigma}\right) e^{-\mu t / (\sqrt{n}\,\sigma)}\right]^n \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} e^{t^2/2}$$

which is the MGF of the standard Gaussian. This is the cleanest proof of the CLT when the MGF exists.

!! Generating Functions in ML

''Moment matching'' is a distribution-fitting strategy: fit parameters so that theoretical moments (from the MGF) match empirical moments. This underpins method-of-moments estimators and some variational inference approaches.

''The Gaussian and cumulants:'' The Gaussian is the //unique// distribution with only two nonzero cumulants (mean and variance). This characterization makes the CGF the right tool for understanding how far a distribution deviates from normality.

''Score matching'' (used to train energy-based models and diffusion models) relies on $\nabla_x \log p(x)$, the score function. The log-density is closely related to the CGF when $p$ is an exponential family.

''Kernel methods:'' The kernel function in a shift-invariant RKHS takes the form $K(x, y) = \varphi(x - y)$, where $\varphi$ is a characteristic function (Bochner's theorem). The RBF kernel is the characteristic function of a Gaussian.

''Mixture models and EM:'' The marginal likelihood of a mixture $p(x) = \sum_k \pi_k p_k(x)$ is a weighted sum of component likelihoods — the same linear structure that makes generating functions composable. See <<link "Expectation Maximization" "Expectation Maximization">><</link>>.

''Z-transforms'' in discrete signal processing are precisely ordinary generating functions for discrete-time signals, and the convolution theorem is the foundation of FIR filtering. See <<link "Signal Processing" "Signal Processing">><</link>>.

!! When Generating Functions Break Down

''Radius of convergence.'' The power series $\sum a_n x^n$ converges only for $|x| < R = 1/\limsup |a_n|^{1/n}$. If $a_n$ grows faster than exponentially, $R = 0$ and the OGF is formal only. More critically, the MGF $E[e^{tX}]$ is infinite for all $t > 0$ when the distribution has a heavy tail (e.g., Cauchy, Pareto, log-normal in extreme cases). In these situations the characteristic function $\varphi(t) = E[e^{itX}]$ must be used instead — it always exists since $|e^{itX}| = 1$.

''Non-uniqueness of moments.'' A distribution is //not// always uniquely determined by its moment sequence. The classic counterexample: the log-normal family contains two different distributions with identical moments to all orders (the Hamburger moment problem). This means moment matching is not a logically sound training objective in general — you may converge to the wrong distribution. Distributions are moment-determinate if $\sum_{n} \mu_{2n}^{-1/(2n)} = \infty$ (Carleman's condition).

''High-dimensional extensions.'' Generating functions are naturally scalar-valued for scalar distributions. Extending to multivariate distributions requires multi-variable formal power series $G(x_1, \ldots, x_d) = \sum_\alpha a_\alpha x^\alpha$. Convergence domains become polyhedra in $\mathbb{R}^d$, coefficient extraction requires iterated contour integrals, and combinatorial identities become far more intricate. The multivariate MGF $M(\mathbf{t}) = E[e^{\mathbf{t}^\top \mathbf{X}}]$ exists only in a neighborhood of $\mathbf{0}$, and the joint cumulant structure encodes covariances, co-skewnesses, and higher-order interactions.

!! Technical Details

''Historical notes:'' Laplace (1812) formalized generating functions in //Théorie analytique des probabilités//. Chebyshev developed the moment sequence approach. Lévy introduced characteristic functions as the universal tool for weak convergence.

''Lévy's continuity theorem:'' If $\varphi_n(t) \to \varphi(t)$ pointwise for all $t$ and $\varphi$ is continuous at 0, then $X_n \to X$ in distribution (and conversely). This is the rigorous foundation for CLT proofs via characteristic functions.

''Stein's method'' uses generating-function-style algebraic manipulation to obtain quantitative rates of convergence in distributional approximations (e.g., Poisson approximation, normal approximation).

''Saddlepoint approximation:'' For the density of a sum $S_n = \sum X_i$, the saddlepoint approximation $\hat{f}(x) \propto e^{n K(t^*) - t^* x}$ (where $K'(t^*) = x/n$) gives exponentially accurate tail approximations — far better than the CLT's Gaussian approximation.

''Exponential families:'' A distribution is in an exponential family if $p(x|\theta) = h(x)\exp(\eta(\theta) \cdot T(x) - A(\theta))$ where $A(\theta) = \log M_{T(x)}(\eta(\theta))$ is the log-partition function — itself the CGF of the sufficient statistic. See <<link "Exponential Family" "Exponential Family">><</link>>.
