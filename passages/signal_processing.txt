! Signal Processing

<img src="assets/signal_processing.svg" alt="Diagram showing time-domain to frequency-domain transform pipeline with FFT, filtering, and inverse transform" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine a radio receiver picking up every station at once — all frequencies superimposed into one messy, crackling signal. You hear everything and nothing at the same time. Signal processing is the mathematics of untangling that mess: decomposing signals into their component frequencies, removing the noise, extracting the patterns that matter, and reconstructing clean information from the chaos. Every audio app on your phone, every MRI scanner in a hospital, every speech recogniser listening for your voice — they all run on these ideas.

---

!! The Core Insight

The fundamental insight of signal processing is that any signal can be represented in two equivalent ways: as ''values over time'' (the time domain) and as ''amplitudes at different frequencies'' (the frequency domain). The [[Fourier Transform|FFT]] converts between them. This duality is not just a mathematical curiosity — it is the engine of the entire field. Many operations that are hopelessly complex in the time domain become simple multiplications in the frequency domain. ''Convolution in time equals multiplication in frequency.'' Filtering — removing unwanted components — reduces to zeroing out the frequencies you do not want. Want to remove a 60 Hz electrical hum from an audio recording? Transform to frequency domain, set the 60 Hz component to zero, transform back. Done.

This duality connects deeply to modern ML. [[CNN|CNN]] layers perform convolution, which is filtering. [[Transformers|Transformers]] use sinusoidal positional encodings — basis functions borrowed directly from Fourier analysis. [[Information Theory|Information Theory]] quantifies the information content that signals carry. And [[Auto-Correlation|Auto-Correlation]] measures how a signal relates to shifted copies of itself, a core tool in both classical DSP and modern sequence modeling.

---

!! The Fourier Transform and Its Variants

The family of Fourier-related transforms forms a hierarchy. The ''Continuous Fourier Transform'' handles analog signals mathematically — an integral over all time. The ''Discrete Fourier Transform'' (DFT) is the digital version, operating on $N$ sampled points. The ''Fast Fourier Transform'' ([[FFT]]) is not a different transform but a //brilliant algorithm// — Cooley and Tukey (1965) showed that the DFT can be computed in $O(N \log N)$ operations instead of $O(N^2)$, one of the most consequential algorithmic improvements in computing history.

But the standard Fourier transform has a limitation: it tells you //which// frequencies are present but not //when// they occur. For signals that change over time — speech, music, heartbeats — this is unacceptable. The ''Short-Time Fourier Transform'' (STFT) solves this by sliding a window across the signal and computing a local DFT within each window. The result is a ''spectrogram'': a 2D map of frequency versus time. ''Wavelets'' (Daubechies, 1988) go further with multi-resolution analysis, using short windows at high frequencies and long windows at low frequencies, capturing both time and frequency information simultaneously.

For audio and speech processing, the ''mel spectrogram'' rescales frequency according to the mel scale, which approximates human auditory perception — compressing high frequencies where our ears are less sensitive. This is the standard input representation for models like Whisper (OpenAI) and most modern speech systems.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The Discrete Fourier Transform decomposes a signal $x[n]$ into $N$ complex sinusoids:

$$X[k] = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n] \cdot e^{-i 2\pi k n / N}$$

Each $X[k]$ is a complex number whose magnitude $|X[k]|$ gives the amplitude of frequency $k$ and whose phase $\angle X[k]$ gives the shift. The inverse reconstructs the original signal exactly:

$$x[n] = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} X[k] \cdot e^{i 2\pi k n / N}$$

The ''Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem'' is the foundational constraint: to perfectly reconstruct a continuous signal with maximum frequency $f_{\max}$, you must sample at a rate $f_s \geq 2 f_{\max}$. The frequency $f_s / 2$ is the ''Nyquist frequency''. When you violate this — sampling too slowly — high frequencies masquerade as low frequencies, a phenomenon called ''aliasing''. Once aliased, information is irretrievably lost.

The ''convolution theorem'' is what makes frequency-domain processing powerful:

$$\mathcal{F}\{f * g\} = \mathcal{F}\{f\} \cdot \mathcal{F}\{g\}$$

Convolution in the time domain — an $O(N^2)$ operation — becomes pointwise multiplication in the frequency domain — $O(N)$ after the $O(N \log N)$ transform. This is why large convolutions are often computed via FFT: transform both signals, multiply, transform back.

---

!! Signal Processing in ML

Signal processing is not just a precursor to ML — it is woven into modern architectures. In ''audio and speech'', mel spectrograms are fed directly into [[CNN|CNN]] or [[Transformers|Transformers]] architectures. OpenAI's Whisper processes 80-channel mel spectrograms. WaveNet generates raw audio waveforms sample by sample. Music generation systems like Jukebox and MusicLM operate on spectral representations.

In ''time series analysis'', spectral features are used for anomaly detection, seasonal decomposition, and detrending. The periodogram — the squared magnitude of the DFT — estimates the power spectral density, revealing dominant cycles in data.

At a deeper level, ''CNNs are signal processing''. A 1D or 2D convolutional layer is a learnable filter bank. Each kernel extracts a specific pattern — an edge, a texture, a motif — exactly as a bandpass filter extracts a frequency band. The connection between [[CNN|convolutional layers]] and frequency-domain filtering is not metaphorical; it is mathematical identity. Some architectures (FNet, AFNO) replace attention entirely with Fourier transforms, achieving competitive performance at lower cost.

[[Attention|Attention]] itself can be interpreted as ''adaptive filtering'' — dynamically weighting different parts of the input based on relevance, rather than using fixed filter coefficients.

---

!! When Signal Processing Breaks Down

''Non-stationarity:'' The Fourier transform assumes the signal's frequency content does not change over time. Real signals — speech, stock prices, neural activity — are fundamentally non-stationary. A single FFT of a speech recording tells you which phonemes were spoken but not when. The STFT and wavelets mitigate this, but they introduce a fundamental trade-off: better time resolution means worse frequency resolution (the Heisenberg-Gabor uncertainty principle for signals). There is no free lunch.

''Finite window effects:'' Real signals have finite duration, which means the DFT operates on a truncated segment. Truncation is equivalent to multiplying by a rectangular window, which in the frequency domain convolves with a sinc function — spreading energy from sharp frequency peaks into neighbouring bins. This ''spectral leakage'' distorts the spectrum. Windowing functions (Hamming, Hann, Blackman) taper the signal edges to reduce leakage, but they widen the main lobe and reduce frequency resolution. Every window is a compromise.

''High dimensionality:'' Classical digital signal processing was designed for 1D signals (audio, communications) and 2D signals (images). Extending these techniques to the high-dimensional representations common in modern ML — 768-dimensional embeddings, 1024-channel feature maps — requires care. The curse of dimensionality means that naive spectral methods scale poorly, and the physical intuitions about frequency and filtering do not transfer cleanly to abstract representation spaces.

!! Technical Details

''Key historical contributions:'' Fourier (1807) — decomposition into sinusoids. Nyquist (1928) — sampling rate requirements. Shannon (1949) — sampling theorem and information theory. Gabor (1946) — STFT and time-frequency analysis. Cooley and Tukey (1965) — FFT algorithm. Daubechies (1988) — compactly supported wavelets.

''Z-transform:'' The discrete-time analogue of the Laplace transform, $X(z) = \sum_{n} x[n] z^{-n}$, is the standard tool for analysing discrete-time linear systems. Poles and zeros of $X(z)$ determine stability and frequency response.

''Filter types:'' ''FIR'' (Finite Impulse Response) filters have a finite number of coefficients, are always stable, and have linear phase. ''IIR'' (Infinite Impulse Response) filters use feedback, achieve sharper cutoffs with fewer coefficients, but can be unstable. CNN kernels are FIR filters.

''Hilbert transform:'' Produces the analytic signal $x_a(t) = x(t) + i \hat{x}(t)$, enabling extraction of instantaneous amplitude and frequency — used in communications and biomedical signal processing.

''Group theory connection:'' The DFT is the representation theory of the cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}/N\mathbb{Z}$. This perspective generalises to the ''Graph Fourier Transform'' (eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian) used in graph neural networks, and to spherical harmonics for signals on the sphere.

''Spectral methods in PDEs:'' Fourier transforms convert differential equations into algebraic equations — differentiation becomes multiplication by $i\omega$. Physics-informed neural networks sometimes leverage this for efficiency.
