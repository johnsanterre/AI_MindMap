! Wasserstein Distance
<img src="assets/wasserstein_distance.svg" alt="Wasserstein Distance" width="800">

Imagine you have two piles of sand shaped into different hills, and you need to reshape one pile to match the other. The ''Wasserstein distance'' measures the cheapest way to do this — accounting for both //how much// sand you move and //how far// you move it. Unlike other ways of comparing [[Probability Distributions]], it gives you a meaningful number even when the two distributions don't overlap at all, which is why it has become a cornerstone of modern generative modeling.

The name comes from Leonid Vaserstein, though the idea is also called the ''Earth Mover's Distance'' (EMD) in computer science — a name that makes the intuition immediately clear. You are literally computing the minimum effort to move earth from one shape to another.

!! The Core Insight

Most distance measures between distributions — like [[KL Divergence]] — work by comparing probability values //at the same points//. If one distribution assigns zero probability where the other assigns nonzero, KL divergence explodes to infinity. This is a real problem: in high dimensions, two randomly chosen distributions almost never overlap.

The Wasserstein distance sidesteps this entirely by asking a different question: instead of comparing densities point-by-point, it asks "what's the cheapest way to //transport// one distribution's mass to match the other?" This means it always gives a finite, geometrically meaningful answer — and it varies smoothly as distributions move around, providing useful gradients for optimization.

!! Moving Earth Between Distributions

Think of [[Optimal Transport]] as a logistics problem. You have warehouses (source distribution $P$) and stores (target distribution $Q$), and you need to decide how much product to ship from each warehouse to each store to satisfy demand at minimum total shipping cost.

The ''transport plan'' $\gamma$ specifies exactly how much mass moves from each source location to each target location. The ''cost'' of moving a unit of mass from point $x$ to point $y$ is the distance $\|x - y\|$, and the total cost is the sum of all shipments weighted by their distances.

Why is this better than KL divergence for non-overlapping distributions? Consider two point masses: one at $x = 0$ and one at $x = \epsilon$. KL divergence is infinite for //any// $\epsilon > 0$. But the Wasserstein distance is simply $\epsilon$ — it captures the geometric reality that these distributions are close when $\epsilon$ is small. This smoothness is what makes Wasserstein distance so valuable for training [[Generative Adversarial Networks]].

The coupling interpretation provides another lens: a ''coupling'' of $P$ and $Q$ is any joint distribution $\gamma(x, y)$ whose marginals are $P$ and $Q$. The Wasserstein distance minimizes the expected distance $\mathbb{E}[\|x - y\|]$ over all such couplings — it finds the joint distribution that keeps paired points as close together as possible.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ''$p$-Wasserstein distance'' between measures $\mu$ and $\nu$ on a metric space is:

$$W_p(\mu,\nu) = \inf_{\gamma \in \Gamma(\mu,\nu)} \left(\int \|x-y\|^p \, d\gamma(x,y)\right)^{1/p}$$

where $\Gamma(\mu,\nu)$ is the set of all couplings — joint distributions with marginals $\mu$ and $\nu$. The infimum searches over all valid transport plans to find the cheapest one.

For $p = 1$, the ''Kantorovich-Rubinstein duality'' provides an equivalent formulation:

$$W_1(\mu,\nu) = \sup_{\|f\|_L \leq 1} \left( \int f \, d\mu - \int f \, d\nu \right)$$

where the supremum is over all 1-Lipschitz functions $f$. This dual form says: find the smoothest function (slope bounded by 1) that maximally separates the two distributions. This is the formulation that makes Wasserstein GANs practical — you approximate the Lipschitz function with a neural network.

For discrete distributions with $n$ points each, the transport problem becomes a ''linear program'': minimize $\sum_{i,j} c_{ij} \gamma_{ij}$ subject to marginal constraints. The computational cost is $O(n^3 \log n)$ via network simplex methods.

!! Wasserstein in Machine Learning

The ''Wasserstein GAN'' (WGAN) replaced the Jensen-Shannon divergence in the original GAN objective with $W_1$, using the Kantorovich-Rubinstein dual. The critic network approximates the optimal 1-Lipschitz function $f$, and the Lipschitz constraint is enforced via weight clipping (WGAN) or gradient penalty (WGAN-GP). This stabilizes training because $W_1$ provides smooth gradients even when generated and real distributions have disjoint supports — a near-certainty in high-dimensional spaces. The result: less mode collapse, more stable training dynamics, and a loss that actually correlates with sample quality.

''Wasserstein barycenters'' generalize the concept of an average to distributions: given distributions $P_1, \ldots, P_k$, the barycenter minimizes $\sum_i w_i W_2^2(\cdot, P_i)$. This is used in texture mixing, shape interpolation, and multi-source [[Distribution Shift|domain adaptation]].

In ''fairness-aware ML'', the Wasserstein distance measures how different the model's output distributions are across protected groups. Regularizing with $W_1$ between group-conditional predictions encourages equalized outcome distributions while preserving the geometric structure of the prediction space.

Wasserstein distances also appear in ''distributional reinforcement learning'', where value functions are replaced by full return distributions, and distributional Bellman updates use Wasserstein projections onto a parametric family.

!! When Wasserstein Distance Breaks Down

* ''Computational cost is prohibitive in high dimensions.'' Exact computation scales as $O(n^3 \log n)$ for $n$ discrete samples, and the curse of dimensionality strikes hard: the sample complexity to estimate $W_p$ within error $\epsilon$ is $O(\epsilon^{-d})$ in $d$ dimensions. For images or other high-dimensional data, exact Wasserstein is essentially intractable without approximations like Sinkhorn or slicing.

* ''The Lipschitz constraint in WGANs is hard to enforce properly.'' Weight clipping biases the critic toward simple functions and can cause vanishing gradients. Gradient penalty (WGAN-GP) works better but adds computational overhead and can fail with batch normalization. Spectral normalization offers a different trade-off but doesn't perfectly enforce the Lipschitz condition either.

* ''Wasserstein distance can be overly sensitive to outliers.'' Because it accounts for the //distance// mass must travel, a single outlier point far from the bulk of the distribution can dominate the Wasserstein distance, making it less robust than $f$-divergences in settings with heavy-tailed noise or corrupted data.

!! Technical Details

The ''Sinkhorn divergence'' approximates $W_p$ by adding an entropic regularization term: $W_\epsilon(\mu,\nu) = \inf_\gamma \int \|x-y\|^p \, d\gamma + \epsilon \, \text{KL}(\gamma \| \mu \otimes \nu)$. This converts the linear program into a matrix scaling problem solvable by alternating row and column normalization of the kernel matrix $K_{ij} = e^{-c_{ij}/\epsilon}$. Complexity drops to $O(n^2 / \epsilon^2)$ iterations, and GPU-friendly batched implementations make it practical. As $\epsilon \to 0$, Sinkhorn converges to the true Wasserstein distance.

The ''sliced Wasserstein distance'' projects distributions onto random 1D lines, computes the (closed-form) 1D Wasserstein distance on each projection, and averages. In 1D, $W_p$ between distributions $F$ and $G$ is simply $\left(\int_0^1 |F^{-1}(t) - G^{-1}(t)|^p \, dt\right)^{1/p}$ — just the $L^p$ distance between quantile functions. Sliced Wasserstein costs $O(Ln \log n)$ for $L$ random projections and provides a valid [[Metric Graphs|metric]].

Sample complexity is a critical limitation. Estimating $W_1$ in $d$ dimensions from $n$ samples has bias $O(n^{-1/d})$ for $d \geq 3$, making it unreliable without many samples in high dimensions. The sliced variant has dimension-independent rates of $O(n^{-1/2})$.

Connections to [[Optimal Transport]] theory run deep: Wasserstein spaces are themselves metric spaces (the space of distributions with finite $p$-th moments, metrized by $W_p$), and geodesics in Wasserstein space correspond to displacement interpolations — smooth morphings between distributions. Villani's formalization shows $W_2$ geometry connects to the Monge-Amp&egrave;re equation, Ricci curvature, and the heat equation as gradient flow.
