! Wavelet Transform

<img src="assets/wavelet_transform.svg" alt="Diagram showing wavelet multi-resolution decomposition with filter bank structure and FFT vs wavelet comparison" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are listening to a symphony and someone asks you what instruments are playing. A Fourier transform is like answering "violin, oboe, timpani" without saying when each one enters or exits. Useful, but incomplete. A ''wavelet transform'' is like answering "the oboe enters at measure 12, the timpani hits at measure 30, and the violins sustain throughout" — it tells you //both// what frequencies are present //and// where in time they appear. It does this by breaking the signal apart at multiple resolutions simultaneously: coarse, sweeping views for slow trends and fine, detailed views for sharp transients. This multi-resolution trick is what makes wavelets indispensable anywhere signals change over time.

---

!! The Core Insight

The Fourier transform uses infinite sinusoids as its building blocks — waves that extend from negative infinity to positive infinity. That is why it captures frequency perfectly but loses all time information. The wavelet transform replaces those infinite sinusoids with short, localized oscillations called ''wavelets'' — little waves that start, wiggle, and die out. By stretching (''dilating'') a wavelet you match low frequencies; by compressing it you match high frequencies. By sliding (''translating'') it across the signal you locate //when// each frequency occurs.

The key innovation is ''multi-resolution analysis'': at low frequencies, the wavelet is stretched wide, giving excellent frequency resolution but coarse time resolution. At high frequencies, the wavelet is compressed narrow, giving excellent time resolution but coarser frequency resolution. This is not a flaw — it is exactly how physical signals behave. A bass drum resonance lasts a long time and needs frequency precision; a snare crack is instantaneous and needs time precision. Wavelets adapt automatically.

This connects directly to <<link "Signal Processing" "Signal Processing">><</link>> and the limitations of the <<link "FFT" "FFT">><</link>>. Where the Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) forces a fixed window size — a single compromise between time and frequency resolution — wavelets give you the best of both worlds by using different window sizes at different scales.

---

!! Wavelet Families and the Filter Bank

Not all wavelets are the same. A ''wavelet family'' is defined by the shape of the ''mother wavelet'' $\psi(t)$, from which all analysis functions are generated by dilation and translation. The most important families include:

''Haar wavelets'' — the simplest: a step function that is +1 for the first half and -1 for the second half. Discontinuous but computationally trivial. Good for detecting abrupt changes.

''Daubechies wavelets'' (db1 through dbN) — Ingrid Daubechies constructed these in 1988 to be compactly supported (finite length) with maximum smoothness for a given support width. They are the workhorse of practical wavelet analysis. The number N indicates the number of vanishing moments — higher N means the wavelet ignores polynomial trends of higher degree.

''Morlet wavelets'' — a Gaussian-windowed complex sinusoid, $\psi(t) = e^{i\omega_0 t} e^{-t^2/2}$. These closely resemble the STFT but with the multi-resolution property. Popular in neuroscience and geophysics.

In practice, the ''Discrete Wavelet Transform'' (DWT) is implemented as a ''filter bank'': the signal passes through a low-pass filter $h[n]$ and a high-pass filter $g[n]$, each followed by downsampling by 2. The low-pass output (''approximation coefficients'') captures the smooth trend; the high-pass output (''detail coefficients'') captures the sharp features at that scale. The approximation is then fed recursively into another filter pair, decomposing the next octave. This cascade produces a complete multi-resolution representation in $O(N)$ time — even faster than the FFT.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ''Continuous Wavelet Transform'' (CWT) of a signal $x(t)$ is defined as:

$$W(a, b) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{|a|}} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x(t) \, \psi^*\!\left(\frac{t - b}{a}\right) dt$$

Here $a$ is the ''scale'' parameter (dilation: large $a$ = stretched wavelet = low frequency), $b$ is the ''translation'' parameter (position in time), and $\psi^*$ is the complex conjugate of the mother wavelet. The factor $1/\sqrt{|a|}$ ensures energy normalization across scales.

The ''admissibility condition'' guarantees perfect reconstruction:

$$C_\psi = \int_0^{\infty} \frac{|\hat{\psi}(\omega)|^2}{\omega} \, d\omega < \infty$$

This requires that $\hat{\psi}(0) = 0$, meaning the wavelet must have zero mean — it must oscillate. This is why wavelets are "little waves" and not bumps.

For the ''Discrete Wavelet Transform'', scale and translation are sampled on a dyadic grid: $a = 2^j$, $b = k \cdot 2^j$. The DWT coefficients are:

$$d_{j,k} = \sum_n x[n] \, g_j[n - 2^j k]$$

$$a_{j,k} = \sum_n x[n] \, h_j[n - 2^j k]$$

where $g_j$ and $h_j$ are the high-pass and low-pass filters at level $j$. Mallat's algorithm (1989) computes these recursively via the filter bank, achieving $O(N)$ complexity. The original signal can be perfectly reconstructed from its wavelet coefficients using the ''inverse DWT'' — no information is lost.

The connection to <<link "Spectral Methods" "Spectral Methods">><</link>> is deep: both wavelets and Fourier methods expand signals in terms of basis functions, but wavelets use a basis that is localized in //both// time and frequency rather than only frequency.

---

!! Wavelets in Machine Learning and Applications

Wavelets have found broad application across engineering and increasingly in ML:

''Signal and image compression:'' JPEG 2000 uses the Cohen-Daubechies-Feauveau 9/7 wavelet instead of the DCT used in standard JPEG. Wavelet compression handles edges and textures better because wavelets are localized — they do not produce ringing artifacts around sharp transitions. The FBI fingerprint compression standard also uses wavelets.

''Denoising:'' Wavelet shrinkage (Donoho and Johnstone, 1994) is elegant: transform to wavelet domain, threshold small coefficients (which are mostly noise), transform back. This works because real signals tend to have sparse wavelet representations — a few large coefficients carry the signal, while noise spreads evenly across all coefficients. This connects to <<link "Feature Engineering" "Feature Engineering">><</link>> and the broader theme of finding representations where signal separates from noise.

''Time series and anomaly detection:'' Wavelets excel at detecting transients — sudden spikes, regime changes, discontinuities — because high-frequency detail coefficients respond sharply to abrupt changes while being insensitive to smooth trends.

''Neural network architectures:'' Several architectures incorporate wavelet ideas. Wavelet scattering networks (Mallat, 2012) use cascaded wavelet transforms with modulus nonlinearities to build translation-invariant, deformation-stable representations — achieving strong results on texture classification without learned parameters. Some <<link "Convolutional Neural Networks" "Convolutional Neural Networks">><</link>> replace pooling layers with wavelet decomposition to preserve both coarse and fine information during downsampling.

''Audio and speech:'' While mel spectrograms (STFT-based) dominate modern speech ML, wavelet representations are used for music information retrieval, sound event detection, and any task where transient resolution matters more than steady-state frequency resolution.

---

!! When Wavelets Break Down

''Shift variance:'' The critically-sampled DWT is ''not'' shift-invariant — shifting the input signal by one sample can produce completely different wavelet coefficients. This is problematic for pattern recognition tasks where features should not depend on exact alignment. The ''stationary wavelet transform'' (SWT) fixes this by omitting the downsampling step, but at the cost of $O(N \log N)$ redundancy. For ML applications, this lack of shift invariance means naive wavelet features can confuse classifiers.

''Directional blindness in 2D:'' The standard 2D DWT separates horizontal, vertical, and diagonal detail — only three directions. Real images contain edges and textures at arbitrary orientations. This limitation drove the development of ''curvelets'' (Candes and Donoho, 2000) and ''shearlets'' that capture directional information. When applying wavelets to image data for ML pipelines, this directional poverty can limit discriminative power compared to learned <<link "Convolutional Neural Networks" "Convolutional Neural Networks">><</link>> filters that adapt to any orientation.

''Fixed basis, no learning:'' Classical wavelets are hand-designed, not learned from data. A Daubechies wavelet is the same whether you are analyzing seismic data or birdsong. Modern deep learning has shown that learned representations — via CNNs, transformers, or autoencoders — consistently outperform fixed bases on complex pattern recognition tasks. Wavelets provide excellent general-purpose priors (locality, multi-resolution), but they cannot adapt to the statistical structure of a specific dataset the way a trained network can.

---

!! Technical Details

''Vanishing moments:'' A wavelet with $N$ vanishing moments satisfies $\int t^k \psi(t) \, dt = 0$ for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, N-1$. This means it is "blind" to polynomial trends of degree $< N$, concentrating its energy on true signal features. More vanishing moments = smoother wavelet = better frequency localization, but longer support = worse time localization.

''Multiresolution analysis (MRA):'' Formally, an MRA is a nested sequence of closed subspaces $\{V_j\}$ of $L^2(\mathbb{R})$ such that $\ldots \subset V_1 \subset V_0 \subset V_{-1} \subset \ldots$ with $\bigcup V_j$ dense and $\bigcap V_j = \{0\}$, and each $V_j$ has an orthonormal basis generated by translates of a scaling function $\phi(t)$. The wavelet $\psi(t)$ spans the orthogonal complement $W_j = V_{j-1} \setminus V_j$.

''Wavelet packets:'' Generalize the DWT by also decomposing the detail coefficients (not just the approximation). This produces a full binary tree of subbands, from which the best basis can be selected to minimize a cost function (entropy, sparsity). Coifman and Wickerhauser (1992) showed this can be done in $O(N \log N)$.

''Lifting scheme:'' Sweldens (1996) introduced a factorization of wavelet transforms into alternating predict and update steps, enabling in-place computation with half the memory, integer-to-integer transforms, and easy construction of wavelets on irregular grids. JPEG 2000 uses lifting internally.

''Heisenberg-Gabor uncertainty:'' No time-frequency representation can have arbitrarily fine resolution in both time and frequency simultaneously. The product $\Delta t \cdot \Delta \omega \geq 1/2$ is a hard lower bound. The Gabor (Morlet) wavelet achieves equality — it is ''optimally localized'' in the time-frequency plane. This is the fundamental constraint that wavelets navigate, not eliminate.

''Scattering transform:'' Mallat's scattering network cascades wavelet transforms with modulus nonlinearities: $S_1 x = |x * \psi_{\lambda_1}|$, $S_2 x = ||x * \psi_{\lambda_1}| * \psi_{\lambda_2}|$, etc. The result is provably stable to diffeomorphisms and translation-invariant — properties that CNNs achieve empirically but without theoretical guarantees.
