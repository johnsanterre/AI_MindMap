! Bayesian Regression

<img src="assets/bayesian_regression.svg" alt="Bayesian regression diagram — comparing a single OLS best-fit line to a posterior distribution of plausible regression lines with uncertainty bands" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are trying to predict house prices, and a colleague hands you a single equation: price equals some number times square footage plus some constant. That is ordinary regression — one answer, take it or leave it. Now imagine instead that your colleague says: "I am not sure of the exact slope, but I think it is somewhere around 200 dollars per square foot, give or take 50." That is ''Bayesian regression''. Instead of committing to a single best line, you maintain an entire distribution over possible lines, updating that distribution as you see data. The result is not just a prediction, but a prediction //with a built-in error bar// — a principled measure of how confident you should be.

---

!! The Core Insight

In classical (frequentist) regression, the model parameters $\mathbf{w}$ are treated as fixed but unknown numbers. You find the single best set of weights — the ''maximum likelihood estimate'' (MLE) — and call it a day. Bayesian regression flips the script: the weights themselves are ''random variables'' with their own probability distributions.

Before seeing any data, you specify a ''prior distribution'' $p(\mathbf{w})$ encoding what you believe about the weights. After observing data $\mathcal{D} = \{(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)\}$, you use <<link "Bayes' Theorem" "Bayes' Theorem">><</link>> to compute the ''posterior distribution'' $p(\mathbf{w} | \mathcal{D})$. This posterior combines your prior beliefs with the evidence from the data. More data means the posterior concentrates tightly; less data means it stays broad, honestly reflecting your uncertainty.

The key philosophical shift: you never claim to //know// the true parameters. You describe what you //believe// about them, and how strongly.

---

!! From Priors to Posteriors

The Bayesian regression workflow has three stages:

''1. Choose a prior.'' For linear regression, the most common choice is a Gaussian prior on the weight vector: $\mathbf{w} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \tau^2 \mathbf{I})$. This says you expect weights to be small and centered around zero — a built-in regularization effect identical in spirit to <<link "Ridge regression" "Ridge regression">><</link>>. You can also use informative priors if domain knowledge suggests certain weights should be large or positive.

''2. Specify the likelihood.'' The standard assumption is that observations are corrupted by Gaussian noise: $y_i = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}_i + \epsilon_i$ where $\epsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$. This gives the likelihood $p(\mathcal{D} | \mathbf{w}) = \prod_i \mathcal{N}(y_i | \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}_i, \sigma^2)$.

''3. Compute the posterior.'' Apply Bayes' theorem: $p(\mathbf{w} | \mathcal{D}) \propto p(\mathcal{D} | \mathbf{w}) \, p(\mathbf{w})$. With Gaussian prior and Gaussian likelihood, the posterior is also Gaussian — this is the magic of <<link "conjugate priors" "Conjugate Priors">><</link>>. You get a closed-form answer without any sampling or approximation.

The posterior mean gives you a point prediction (which turns out to equal the <<link "MAP estimate" "Maximum A Posteriori">><</link>> in this case), but crucially you also have the posterior //covariance//, which tells you how uncertain you are about each weight and how they covary.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Start with a linear model $y = \mathbf{X}\mathbf{w} + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ where $\boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \sigma^2 \mathbf{I})$ and a prior $\mathbf{w} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{m}_0, \mathbf{S}_0)$.

The posterior is:

$$p(\mathbf{w} | \mathcal{D}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{w} | \mathbf{m}_N, \mathbf{S}_N)$$

where

$$\mathbf{S}_N^{-1} = \mathbf{S}_0^{-1} + \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{X}$$

$$\mathbf{m}_N = \mathbf{S}_N \left( \mathbf{S}_0^{-1} \mathbf{m}_0 + \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{y} \right)$$

The posterior precision (inverse covariance) $\mathbf{S}_N^{-1}$ is the sum of the prior precision and the data precision. This is the Bayesian update rule in matrix form: certainty adds up.

For a new input $\mathbf{x}_*$, the ''predictive distribution'' is:

$$p(y_* | \mathbf{x}_*, \mathcal{D}) = \mathcal{N}\!\left(\mathbf{m}_N^\top \mathbf{x}_*, \; \sigma^2 + \mathbf{x}_*^\top \mathbf{S}_N \mathbf{x}_*\right)$$

The predictive variance has two terms: $\sigma^2$ is irreducible observation noise (''aleatoric uncertainty''), and $\mathbf{x}_*^\top \mathbf{S}_N \mathbf{x}_*$ is ''epistemic uncertainty'' — uncertainty about the weights that shrinks as you collect more data. This decomposition is one of the most practically valuable features of Bayesian regression.

With a zero-mean isotropic prior $\mathbf{S}_0 = \tau^2 \mathbf{I}$ and $\mathbf{m}_0 = \mathbf{0}$, the posterior mean simplifies to:

$$\mathbf{m}_N = \left(\mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{X} + \frac{\sigma^2}{\tau^2} \mathbf{I}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}^\top \mathbf{y}$$

This is exactly the Ridge regression solution with regularization parameter $\lambda = \sigma^2 / \tau^2$. Ridge regression is Bayesian regression's MAP estimate under a Gaussian prior — the Bayesian view //explains// why Ridge works.

---

!! Predictions with Uncertainty

The practical payoff of Bayesian regression is the ''predictive distribution''. Instead of a single predicted number, you get a full Gaussian distribution at every test point. This means:

''Confidence intervals are free.'' A 95% predictive interval is simply $\hat{y} \pm 1.96 \cdot \hat{\sigma}_*$ where $\hat{\sigma}_*^2$ is the predictive variance. These intervals are wider where data is sparse and narrower where data is dense — exactly the behavior you want.

''Model comparison via marginal likelihood.'' The ''evidence'' $p(\mathcal{D}) = \int p(\mathcal{D}|\mathbf{w}) \, p(\mathbf{w}) \, d\mathbf{w}$ scores how well the model (including the prior) predicted the data. Models that are too simple underfit; models that are too complex waste probability mass on data configurations that never appear. The evidence automatically balances fit against complexity — a principled alternative to cross-validation.

''Online learning is natural.'' Today's posterior becomes tomorrow's prior. When new data arrives, you update the posterior mean and covariance without reprocessing old data. This is exact for the linear-Gaussian case, making Bayesian regression well-suited for streaming settings.

The Bayesian approach connects to <<link "Gaussian Processes" "Gaussian Processes">><</link>>: a GP is essentially Bayesian regression lifted to an infinite-dimensional feature space, where the prior is placed directly over functions rather than finite weight vectors.

---

!! When X Breaks Down

''1. Prior misspecification.'' If your prior is too narrow and centered on the wrong values, the posterior can be slow to recover even with substantial data. For example, placing a tight prior that all weights are near zero when the true relationship involves large coefficients will bias predictions and produce overconfident uncertainty estimates. With enough data the likelihood eventually dominates, but in small-sample regimes a bad prior can be worse than no prior at all.

''2. Computational cost in high dimensions.'' The closed-form posterior requires inverting a $d \times d$ matrix, costing $O(d^3)$. When $d$ is in the tens of thousands (as in many modern feature spaces), this becomes impractical. You must resort to approximations: variational inference, stochastic gradient methods, or sparse priors — all of which sacrifice some of the exactness that makes Bayesian regression elegant.

''3. Model misspecification.'' Bayesian regression assumes the true data-generating process is linear with Gaussian noise. If the real relationship is nonlinear, heteroscedastic, or has heavy-tailed errors, the posterior can be well-calibrated //for the wrong model//. The uncertainty estimates will be mathematically consistent but practically misleading. In such cases you need <<link "Gaussian Processes" "Gaussian Processes">><</link>> or Bayesian neural networks.

---

!! Technical Details

''Posterior derivation:'' The Gaussian-Gaussian conjugacy means $p(\mathbf{w}|\mathcal{D}) \propto \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{m}_0)^\top \mathbf{S}_0^{-1}(\mathbf{w}-\mathbf{m}_0) - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\|\mathbf{y}-\mathbf{X}\mathbf{w}\|^2\right)$. Completing the square in $\mathbf{w}$ gives the posterior mean $\mathbf{m}_N$ and covariance $\mathbf{S}_N$ shown above.

''Marginal likelihood (evidence):'' For the conjugate linear model, $\log p(\mathbf{y}|\mathbf{X}) = -\frac{N}{2}\log(2\pi) - \frac{1}{2}\log|\mathbf{K}| - \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{y}^\top \mathbf{K}^{-1}\mathbf{y}$ where $\mathbf{K} = \sigma^2\mathbf{I} + \mathbf{X}\mathbf{S}_0\mathbf{X}^\top$. This is identical to the GP marginal likelihood with a linear kernel.

''Empirical Bayes (Type-II MLE):'' Rather than fixing hyperparameters $\sigma^2, \tau^2$, optimize them by maximizing the marginal likelihood. This gives an automatic, data-driven choice of regularization strength without cross-validation.

''Connection to Ridge:'' With prior $\mathbf{w} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \tau^2\mathbf{I})$, the MAP estimate is the Ridge solution with $\lambda = \sigma^2/\tau^2$. But Bayesian regression gives more: the full posterior covariance, predictive uncertainty, and evidence for model selection.

''Sparse Bayesian regression (Relevance Vector Machine):'' Using an ARD (automatic relevance determination) prior — a separate variance $\alpha_j^{-1}$ per weight — and optimizing the $\alpha_j$ via evidence maximization drives most $\alpha_j \to \infty$, zeroing the corresponding weights. This yields sparse solutions like <<link "Lasso" "Lasso">><</link>> but with calibrated uncertainty and no need to tune $\lambda$.

''Sequential update:'' Given posterior $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{m}_t, \mathbf{S}_t)$ and a new observation $(\mathbf{x}_{t+1}, y_{t+1})$: $\mathbf{S}_{t+1}^{-1} = \mathbf{S}_t^{-1} + \sigma^{-2}\mathbf{x}_{t+1}\mathbf{x}_{t+1}^\top$, $\mathbf{m}_{t+1} = \mathbf{S}_{t+1}(\mathbf{S}_t^{-1}\mathbf{m}_t + \sigma^{-2}y_{t+1}\mathbf{x}_{t+1})$. This is a rank-one update, computable in $O(d^2)$ via the matrix inversion lemma.

''Heteroscedastic extension:'' Replace fixed $\sigma^2$ with input-dependent noise $\sigma^2(\mathbf{x})$. The posterior is no longer conjugate; variational or MCMC methods are needed. This is important when noise varies across the input space (e.g., financial data with volatility clustering).
