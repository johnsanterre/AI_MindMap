! RSA Cryptography

<img src="assets/rsa_cryptography.svg" alt="RSA Cryptography" width="800">

Imagine you want to send a locked box to a friend, but you've never met in person to exchange keys. RSA solves this with a clever trick: your friend sends you an //open padlock// (the public key) that anyone can snap shut, but only your friend holds the private key that opens it. You lock the box and send it — even the person who handed you the padlock can't open it. ''RSA cryptography'' turns this physical intuition into mathematics by exploiting a deep asymmetry: multiplying two large prime numbers together is trivially fast, but reversing that multiplication — factoring the product back into its primes — is computationally devastating. This one-way trapdoor, rooted in [[number theory|Modular Arithmetic]], underpins much of modern secure communication.

!! The Core Insight

RSA rests on a single mathematical asymmetry: ''multiplication is easy, factoring is hard''. Given two 1024-bit primes $p$ and $q$, computing $n = p \times q$ takes microseconds. But given only $n$, no known classical algorithm can recover $p$ and $q$ in any reasonable time. This asymmetry creates a ''trapdoor function'' — easy to compute in one direction, practically impossible to reverse without secret information. The trapdoor here is knowledge of $p$ and $q$ individually, which lets you compute the private key. Without them, an attacker is stuck trying to factor a number with hundreds of digits — a problem that has resisted centuries of mathematical effort and currently has no efficient classical solution.

What makes RSA especially elegant is that encryption and decryption are //the same operation// — modular exponentiation — just with different exponents. The public key encrypts, the private key decrypts, and the mathematical guarantee that this works comes from [[Euler's theorem|Modular Arithmetic]], which itself flows from the structure of [[Finite Fields]] and the totient function.

!! From Number Theory to Encryption

The RSA algorithm has three phases. ''Key generation'' begins by selecting two large primes $p$ and $q$ (each typically 1024 bits or more, verified via [[Primality Testing]]). You compute $n = pq$ and the totient $\phi(n) = (p-1)(q-1)$. Next, choose a public exponent $e$ that is coprime to $\phi(n)$ — the standard choice is $e = 65537$ because it's prime, has only two bits set (making exponentiation fast), and is large enough to resist certain attacks. Finally, compute the private exponent $d$ such that $ed \equiv 1 \pmod{\phi(n)}$, using the extended Euclidean algorithm. The public key is $(e, n)$; the private key is $(d, n)$.

''Encryption'' takes a plaintext message $M$ (encoded as an integer less than $n$) and computes $C = M^e \bmod n$. ''Decryption'' recovers the message: $M = C^d \bmod n$. The magic is that these are true inverses — raising to the $e$-th power and then the $d$-th power (mod $n$) returns you to where you started.

RSA also enables ''digital signatures''. To sign a message, the sender computes $S = H(M)^d \bmod n$ where $H$ is a hash function. Anyone with the public key can verify: check that $S^e \bmod n = H(M)$. This proves the signer holds the private key without revealing it.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The correctness of RSA follows from ''Euler's theorem'': for any $M$ coprime to $n$,

$$M^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod{n}$$

Since $ed \equiv 1 \pmod{\phi(n)}$, we can write $ed = 1 + k\phi(n)$ for some integer $k$. Then:

$$C^d = (M^e)^d = M^{ed} = M^{1 + k\phi(n)} = M \cdot (M^{\phi(n)})^k \equiv M \cdot 1^k = M \pmod{n}$$

This works even when $\gcd(M, n) \neq 1$ (which happens only when $M$ is a multiple of $p$ or $q$) by applying the [[Chinese Remainder Theorem]] to handle each prime factor separately.

The totient $\phi(n) = (p-1)(q-1)$ counts the integers from $1$ to $n$ that are coprime to $n$. Knowing $\phi(n)$ is equivalent to knowing $p$ and $q$ — if you have $n$ and $\phi(n)$, you can solve a quadratic $x^2 - (n - \phi(n) + 1)x + n = 0$ to recover both primes. This is why $\phi(n)$ must remain secret.

''Modular exponentiation'' is computed efficiently via ''repeated squaring'' (also called binary exponentiation). To compute $M^e \bmod n$, you express $e$ in binary and square-and-multiply: this takes $O(\log e)$ multiplications rather than $e$ multiplications. Each multiplication operates on numbers up to $n$, so the total cost is $O(\log^2 n \cdot \log e)$ — fast even for 2048-bit keys.

In practice, decryption is accelerated using the [[Chinese Remainder Theorem]]. Instead of computing $C^d \bmod n$ directly, compute $C^d \bmod p$ and $C^d \bmod q$ separately (with smaller exponents $d_p = d \bmod (p-1)$ and $d_q = d \bmod (q-1)$), then combine the results using CRT. This gives roughly a ''4x speedup''.

!! RSA in the Real World

RSA was the backbone of internet security for decades, used in SSL/TLS to establish secure connections, in PGP for email encryption, and in SSH for server authentication. In practice, RSA is rarely used to encrypt data directly — it's too slow for bulk encryption. Instead, it encrypts a short ''session key'' for a fast symmetric cipher like AES. This ''hybrid encryption'' scheme combines RSA's key-distribution advantage with symmetric encryption's speed.

Key sizes have grown steadily as computing power increases. The original 512-bit keys were broken in 1999. Today, ''2048-bit'' keys are the minimum standard, and ''4096-bit'' keys are recommended for long-term security. Each doubling of key length makes factoring roughly $2^{30}$ times harder with the best known algorithms (the general number field sieve).

''Padding schemes'' are critical. Naive "textbook RSA" ($C = M^e \bmod n$) is deterministic and vulnerable to many attacks. ''OAEP'' (Optimal Asymmetric Encryption Padding) adds randomized padding before encryption, making the scheme provably secure under the RSA assumption. For signatures, ''PSS'' (Probabilistic Signature Scheme) serves the same role.

!! When RSA Cryptography Breaks Down

* ''Quantum computing threatens factoring.'' Shor's algorithm can factor $n$ in polynomial time on a quantum computer, completely breaking RSA. A sufficiently large quantum computer — estimated at a few thousand logical qubits — would render all current RSA key sizes insecure. This has driven the development of post-quantum cryptography standards (lattice-based, code-based) that resist quantum attacks. While such machines don't yet exist, adversaries may be harvesting encrypted data today to decrypt it later ("harvest now, decrypt later").

* ''Implementation side-channel attacks.'' Even with mathematically sound RSA, implementations leak information through timing variations, power consumption, electromagnetic emissions, or cache-access patterns during modular exponentiation. Timing attacks can recover the private key by measuring how long decryption takes for different ciphertexts. Defenses include constant-time implementations, blinding (multiplying by a random factor before exponentiation), and hardware security modules.

* ''Weak key generation.'' RSA's security assumes $p$ and $q$ are truly random, independent, and large. If the random number generator is flawed — producing predictable primes, reusing primes across keys, or choosing primes that are too close together — the system collapses. In 2012, researchers found that 0.2% of RSA public keys on the internet shared a prime factor, allowing trivial factorization via GCD computation. Fermat's factorization method can quickly break keys where $|p - q|$ is small.

!! Technical Details

The ''general number field sieve'' (GNFS) is the fastest known classical factoring algorithm, with heuristic complexity $L_n[1/3, (64/9)^{1/3}]$ where $L_n[a,c] = \exp(c (\ln n)^a (\ln \ln n)^{1-a})$. This sub-exponential (but super-polynomial) runtime is why RSA keys must be so large — 2048 bits gives roughly 112 bits of security.

The ''Carmichael function'' $\lambda(n) = \text{lcm}(p-1, q-1)$ can replace $\phi(n)$ in RSA and often produces a smaller private exponent $d$, since $\lambda(n)$ divides $\phi(n)$. Modern implementations prefer $\lambda(n)$.

''Multi-prime RSA'' uses $n = p_1 p_2 \cdots p_k$ with $k > 2$ primes. The CRT speedup scales as $k^2$, so three primes give roughly $9/4$ the speed of two primes. However, each prime is smaller, so factoring is easier — the total modulus must be correspondingly larger.

RSA key generation requires efficient [[Primality Testing]]. In practice, candidates are generated randomly and tested with the ''Miller-Rabin'' probabilistic test (typically 40+ rounds for negligible error probability), sometimes followed by a deterministic check.

The ''RSA problem'' (recovering $M$ from $C$, $e$, $n$) is believed equivalent to factoring, but this has never been proven. It's conceivable that RSA could be broken without factoring $n$, though no such method is known.

''Bleichenbacher's attack'' (1998) exploited PKCS#1 v1.5 padding to decrypt RSA ciphertexts via an adaptive chosen-ciphertext attack using roughly $2^{20}$ oracle queries. This motivated the transition to OAEP padding.
