! Operator Theory

<img src="assets/operator_theory.svg" alt="Diagram showing an operator T mapping one function space to another, with eigenfunction decomposition showing decreasing eigenvalues" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Think of a machine on a factory floor. You feed in a metal rod and it bends, stretches, or reshapes it into something new. Now replace the rod with an entire //function// — a curve, a signal, a probability density — and the machine with a mathematical rule that takes that function and produces a different one. That machine is an ''operator''. Operator theory studies what these machines can do, what they preserve, and when they break. In machine learning, every kernel method, every neural tangent kernel analysis, and every neural operator architecture is quietly relying on the properties these mathematical machines guarantee.

!! The Core Insight

A matrix multiplies a vector to produce a new vector. An ''operator'' does the same thing, but with functions instead of finite vectors. The operator $T: V \to W$ takes a function $f$ in one function space and produces a function $Tf$ in another. The deep question is: //which properties of matrices survive this jump to infinite dimensions?//

The answer: only some, and only under the right conditions. Eigenvalue decompositions still work — but only for ''compact'' or ''self-adjoint'' operators. Continuity and boundedness, which are synonymous for matrices, become genuinely different properties. The spectrum of an operator can contain values that are not eigenvalues at all. Operator theory identifies exactly which structural guarantees hold and when, giving machine learning a rigorous foundation for working in infinite-dimensional function spaces.

!! Bounded, Compact, and Self-Adjoint Operators

''Bounded operators'' are the well-behaved ones. An operator $T$ is bounded if there exists a constant $C$ such that $\|Tf\| \leq C\|f\|$ for all $f$. The smallest such $C$ is the ''operator norm'':

$$\|T\| = \sup_{\|f\|=1} \|Tf\|$$

This is the infinite-dimensional generalization of the largest singular value of a matrix. Bounded operators are automatically continuous — they do not blow up nearby inputs into wildly different outputs.

''Compact operators'' are even better behaved. They map bounded sets to sets with compact closure, meaning they "compress" the space. Intuitively, a compact operator is one that can be approximated arbitrarily well by finite-rank operators (operators whose range is finite-dimensional). Every compact operator on a Hilbert space has a ''singular value decomposition'':

$$Tf = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \sigma_n \langle f, u_n \rangle v_n$$

where $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \to 0$. The singular values decay to zero — this is what makes compact operators "nearly finite-dimensional."

''Self-adjoint operators'' satisfy $\langle Tf, g \rangle = \langle f, Tg \rangle$ for all $f, g$ — the infinite-dimensional analog of symmetric matrices. For a compact self-adjoint operator, the ''spectral theorem'' gives a clean eigendecomposition:

$$Tf = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \lambda_n \langle f, e_n \rangle e_n$$

where $\{e_n\}$ are orthonormal eigenfunctions and $\lambda_n \to 0$. This is the theoretical engine behind Mercer's theorem, [[Spectral Methods|Spectral Methods]], and functional PCA.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The most important operator in machine learning is the ''kernel integral operator''. Given a kernel $k(x,y)$, define:

$$(T_k f)(x) = \int k(x,y) f(y) \, dy$$

This operator takes a function $f$ and produces a new function by "smearing" $f$ against the kernel. If $k$ is continuous and symmetric on a compact domain, $T_k$ is compact and self-adjoint. The spectral theorem then yields ''Mercer's theorem'':

$$k(x,y) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \lambda_n e_n(x) e_n(y)$$

where $\lambda_n > 0$ and $\{e_n\}$ are orthonormal eigenfunctions of $T_k$. This decomposition reveals the implicit feature map of any kernel: $\phi(x) = (\sqrt{\lambda_1} e_1(x), \sqrt{\lambda_2} e_2(x), \ldots)$, and the kernel evaluation is the inner product $k(x,y) = \langle \phi(x), \phi(y) \rangle$.

The ''resolvent'' of an operator, $(T - \lambda I)^{-1}$, encodes the spectral structure. The ''spectrum'' $\sigma(T)$ is the set of $\lambda$ for which the resolvent fails to exist as a bounded operator. For compact operators, the spectrum consists of eigenvalues (which accumulate only at zero) plus zero itself. For general bounded operators, the spectrum can include ''continuous spectrum'' and ''residual spectrum'' — values where the resolvent is unbounded or not densely defined, even though no eigenfunction exists.

The ''Koopman operator'' takes a different perspective. Given a dynamical system $x_{t+1} = F(x_t)$, the Koopman operator $\mathcal{K}$ acts on //observable functions// $g$:

$$(\mathcal{K}g)(x) = g(F(x))$$

This is a linear operator, even when $F$ is nonlinear. The price: $\mathcal{K}$ is infinite-dimensional. Its eigenvalues and eigenfunctions capture the geometry of the dynamics — periodicities, decay rates, invariant structures — in a linear framework. The eigendecomposition:

$$g(x) = \sum_n c_n \phi_n(x), \quad \mathcal{K}\phi_n = \mu_n \phi_n$$

allows prediction: $g(F^t(x)) = \sum_n c_n \mu_n^t \phi_n(x)$.

!! Operators in Machine Learning

''Kernel integral operators and [[Gaussian Processes|Gaussian Processes]]'': The eigendecomposition of the kernel operator $T_k$ determines the effective dimensionality of the RKHS. Eigenvalues $\lambda_n$ that decay rapidly mean the kernel concentrates on a low-dimensional subspace. This controls the generalization behavior — kernels with fast spectral decay learn smooth functions well but struggle with rough ones.

''[[Neural Tangent Kernel|Neural Tangent Kernel]] (NTK)'': In the infinite-width limit, a neural network's training dynamics are governed by the kernel integral operator of the NTK. The eigenvalues of this operator determine which target functions are learned quickly (those aligned with top eigenfunctions) versus slowly (those aligned with trailing eigenfunctions). This spectral bias explains why wide networks learn low-frequency patterns first.

''Koopman operators for dynamical systems'': Rather than fitting nonlinear models directly, Koopman-based methods (DMD, EDMD, deep Koopman networks) approximate the linear Koopman operator from data. This turns nonlinear prediction into a linear eigenvalue problem at the cost of choosing the right observable functions — an active area where deep learning meets operator theory.

''Neural operators (DeepONet, Fourier Neural Operator)'': These architectures learn operators — mappings from input functions to output functions — directly from data. DeepONet parameterizes the operator using a branch network (encodes the input function) and a trunk network (encodes the evaluation point), motivated by the ''universal approximation theorem for operators'' (Chen & Chen, 1995). The Fourier Neural Operator parameterizes the integral kernel in Fourier space, exploiting the fact that convolution operators are diagonal in the Fourier basis.

!! When Operator Methods Break Down

''The spectrum is not just eigenvalues.'' For finite matrices, the spectrum consists entirely of eigenvalues. For infinite-dimensional operators, the spectrum can include continuous and residual components where no eigenvector exists. The shift operator on $\ell^2$ has no eigenvalues, yet its spectrum is the entire unit disk. Algorithms that search only for eigenvalue-eigenvector pairs (like naive power iteration) will miss essential spectral structure and can produce misleading results.

''Compact approximations can fail for non-compact operators.'' Many practical operators — differential operators, the Koopman operator for chaotic systems, unbounded multiplication operators — are not compact. Truncating their "spectrum" to finitely many eigenvalues introduces errors that do not diminish with more terms, because the operator does not have a convergent eigenfunction expansion in the first place. The Koopman operator for turbulent flows, for instance, has continuous spectrum that resists finite-rank approximation.

''Domain and regularity mismatches break everything.'' The spectral theorem requires specific structural conditions: self-adjointness, compactness, or at minimum, normality. Applying spectral methods to operators that fail these conditions — non-symmetric kernels, operators on mismatched function spaces, or unbounded operators without careful domain specification — produces mathematically meaningless decompositions. In neural operator architectures, choosing function spaces that do not match the problem's regularity leads to approximation errors that no amount of training data can fix.

!! Technical Details

The ''Fredholm alternative'' states that for a compact operator $T$ and scalar $\lambda \neq 0$, either $(T - \lambda I)$ is invertible, or $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue. There is no middle ground — unlike the general bounded case.

''Trace-class operators'' satisfy $\sum_n \sigma_n < \infty$. The trace $\text{tr}(T) = \sum_n \langle Te_n, e_n \rangle$ is well-defined and basis-independent. ''Hilbert-Schmidt operators'' satisfy $\sum_n \sigma_n^2 < \infty$, equivalently $\int\!\int |k(x,y)|^2 \, dx \, dy < \infty$. Every kernel integral operator with a square-integrable kernel is Hilbert-Schmidt.

The ''spectral mapping theorem'' states that for a polynomial $p$, $\sigma(p(T)) = p(\sigma(T))$. For the continuous functional calculus (self-adjoint $T$ in a Hilbert space), this extends to continuous functions: $f(T) = \int f(\lambda) \, dE(\lambda)$ where $E$ is the ''spectral measure'' (projection-valued measure).

The ''Riesz-Schauder theorem'' sharpens the spectral picture for compact operators: nonzero spectral values are isolated eigenvalues of finite multiplicity, and they can accumulate only at zero.

''Operator semigroups'': A strongly continuous semigroup $\{T(t)\}_{t \geq 0}$ satisfies $T(t+s) = T(t)T(s)$ and governs linear evolution equations $\frac{du}{dt} = Au$, where $A$ is the infinitesimal generator. The ''Hille-Yosida theorem'' characterizes when an operator $A$ generates such a semigroup. Neural ODEs implicitly learn semigroup generators.
