! CLIP
<img src="assets/clip.svg" alt="CLIP dual-encoder contrastive architecture" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine learning what "dog" means not from a dictionary definition but from seeing a million photos labeled "dog." You would quickly learn to connect the visual appearance of fur, four legs, and a wagging tail to the word — without anyone ever spelling out a rule. CLIP does exactly this at scale: 400 million image-text pairs scraped from the internet, training two encoders to agree on which image matches which caption.

---
!! The Core Insight

''CLIP'' (Contrastive Language–Image Pre-Training), introduced by Radford et al. at OpenAI in 2021, pairs a ''visual encoder'' with a ''text encoder'' and trains them jointly using contrastive loss. The image encoder (a ViT or ResNet) and the text encoder (a transformer) each project their input into a shared 512-dimensional embedding space. For a batch of N image-text pairs, the loss pushes the N correct (matched) pairs close together in that space while pushing the N²−N incorrect pairs apart. The result is a model that understands images and language in a unified representation — enabling ''zero-shot transfer'': to classify an image into any category, simply embed text descriptions of those categories and find the nearest image embedding. No fine-tuning required.

Key links: [[Transformers]], [[Embeddings]], [[Contrastive Learning]], [[Vision Transformer]], [[Word Embeddings]].

---
!! Zero-Shot Transfer and Prompt Engineering

CLIP's most striking capability is zero-shot image classification. Given a set of candidate categories, you construct text prompts such as //"a photo of a {class}"// for each, embed them with the text encoder, and rank them by cosine similarity to the image embedding. No labeled examples or gradient updates are needed.

''Prompt engineering'' matters significantly. The generic template //"a photo of a {class}"// already works well, but domain-specific prompts improve performance further: //"a satellite photo of a {class}"// for aerial imagery, //"a microscopy image of a {class}"// for biomedical tasks. ''Prompt ensembling'' — averaging embeddings from multiple prompt templates — further boosts accuracy.

On ImageNet zero-shot, CLIP ViT-L/14 achieves 76.2% top-1 accuracy, competitive with a supervised ResNet-50 trained on 1.28 million labeled images. This is remarkable: no ImageNet labels were used, and the model generalizes from internet text-image co-occurrence alone.

---
!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

CLIP optimizes a ''symmetric cross-entropy loss'' over the matrix of cosine similarities between all image embeddings $I_i$ and text embeddings $T_j$ in a batch of $N$ pairs. Embeddings are L2-normalized before computing similarities, so the dot product equals cosine similarity.

$$\mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{2N}\sum_{i=1}^N \left[\log\frac{\exp(I_i \cdot T_i / \tau)}{\sum_{j=1}^N \exp(I_i \cdot T_j / \tau)} + \log\frac{\exp(T_i \cdot I_i / \tau)}{\sum_{j=1}^N \exp(T_j \cdot I_i / \tau)}\right]$$

The first term treats each image as a query and each text in the batch as a candidate: it maximizes the probability that the correct text ranks first. The second term does the same with roles reversed. The symmetry ensures both encoders are trained equally.

$\tau$ is a ''learnable temperature'' parameter (initialized to 0.07) that controls the sharpness of the softmax distribution. A lower temperature makes the model more confident and the loss more discriminative.

''Large batches are critical:'' the original CLIP used $N = 32{,}768$. Each image faces 32,767 negative texts, forcing the encoders to produce highly discriminative embeddings. This is why CLIP is expensive to train but can be deployed zero-shot for virtually any downstream task.

---
!! Applications and Descendants

CLIP embeddings have become a foundational component across vision-language AI:

''Generative models:'' DALL-E 2 uses CLIP image embeddings as conditioning signals. Stable Diffusion uses CLIP's text encoder to condition the diffusion process. The quality of CLIP representations directly affects image generation fidelity.

''Open-vocabulary detection:'' Grounding DINO combines CLIP with a detection transformer to localize any object described in text, without training on those specific classes.

''Multimodal LLMs:'' LLaVA, GPT-4V, and similar models use CLIP (or its descendants) as a frozen visual backbone, projecting image features into the token space of a language model.

''CLIP descendants:'' OpenCLIP (open-source reproduction with LAION-400M/5B), SigLIP (sigmoid loss replacing softmax — removes the need for large batches), ALIGN (Google, 1.8B noisy pairs), and VideoCLIP (extending to video-text pairs).

---
!! When CLIP Breaks Down

''Compositional reasoning failure:'' CLIP encodes entire sentences into a single vector, losing fine-grained structural information. It struggles to distinguish "a red cube on a blue sphere" from "a blue cube on a red sphere" — it recognizes the objects well but not their spatial relationships or attribute bindings. Bag-of-words behavior persists despite the transformer text encoder.

''Fine-grained discrimination:'' Zero-shot CLIP underperforms supervised models on tasks requiring subtle visual distinctions, such as identifying bird species, car models, or medical imaging findings. Short text descriptions cannot capture the visual nuances that distinguish these classes, and the model has no mechanism to learn them without fine-tuning.

''Distribution shift and social bias:'' CLIP is trained on 400 million internet image-text pairs without curation. It inherits the biases present in that data — geographic and cultural under-representation, racial and gender stereotypes in word-image associations, and performance disparities across demographic groups. Downstream applications that use CLIP zero-shot transfer may propagate or amplify these biases.

---
!! Technical Details

''Original paper:'' Radford et al., "Learning Transferable Visual Models From Natural Language Supervision," ICML 2021 (OpenAI). Evaluated on 27 downstream datasets.

''Training:'' 400M image-text pairs from the internet. Largest model: ViT-L/14 at 336px resolution. Training required approximately 400 GPU-days on V100s. Batch size: 32,768.

''Architectures tested:'' ResNet-50, ResNet-101, ResNet-50x4/16/64 (EfficientNet-style scaling), ViT-B/32, ViT-B/16, ViT-L/14. ViT-L/14 is the strongest.

''Open reproductions:'' LAION-400M and LAION-5B provide open training data. OpenCLIP (Cherti et al. 2023) reproduces and scales CLIP with open data and code, matching or exceeding original performance.

''SigLIP'' (Zhai et al. 2023, Google): replaces softmax contrastive loss with sigmoid loss applied independently to each pair. Removes the large-batch requirement, enables more efficient training, and improves performance at smaller batch sizes.

''ALIGN'' (Jia et al. 2021, Google): same dual-encoder contrastive framework trained on 1.8 billion noisy image-text pairs from the web, demonstrating that data scale can compensate for noise.
