! Importance Sampling
<img src="assets/importance_sampling.svg" alt="Importance Sampling" width="800">

Imagine you want to estimate how often a rare bird visits a particular forest, but standing in that forest all day is impractical — the bird almost never shows up. A smarter approach: go to a nearby lake where the bird appears more frequently, count your sightings there, then //adjust// your counts to reflect the difference between the lake and the forest. That adjustment — the reweighting — is the heart of ''importance sampling''. Instead of drawing samples from the distribution you actually care about (which may be expensive or impossible to sample from directly), you sample from a more convenient ''proposal distribution'' and correct each sample by a weight that accounts for the mismatch.

This technique sits at the core of [[Monte Carlo Methods|Monte Carlo Methods]], [[Bayesian Inference|Bayesian Inference]], off-policy [[Reinforcement Learning|Reinforcement Learning]], and modern [[Variational Inference|Variational Inference]].

!! The Core Insight

The fundamental insight of importance sampling is that an expectation under one distribution can be ''exactly'' rewritten as an expectation under a completely different distribution, as long as you multiply by the right correction factor. You are not approximating — the identity is exact. The only requirement is that your proposal distribution assigns nonzero probability everywhere the target does. This means you have enormous freedom in choosing //where// to sample from, and the art lies in choosing a proposal that makes your estimator low-variance.

!! Sampling from the Wrong Distribution on Purpose

Why would you deliberately sample from the "wrong" distribution $q(x)$ instead of the target $p(x)$? Three compelling reasons:

'''1. The target is hard to sample from.''' Many distributions in [[Bayesian Inference|Bayesian Inference]] are known only up to a normalizing constant. You can evaluate $p(x)$ pointwise (up to proportionality), but you cannot draw independent samples from it. A simpler proposal $q(x)$ — perhaps a Gaussian or a mixture — lets you generate samples easily.

'''2. You want to focus samples where they matter.''' If you are estimating an expectation of a function $f(x)$ that is large only in the tails of $p(x)$, most samples from $p(x)$ will be wasted in regions where $f(x) \approx 0$. Choosing a $q(x)$ that places more mass in the tails concentrates your computational effort where the integrand is large.

'''3. You can reuse samples across distributions.''' In off-policy reinforcement learning, you have data collected under an old policy $q$ but need to evaluate a new policy $p$. Importance weights let you reuse that data without re-collecting it.

The ''importance weights'' $w(x) = \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}$ correct the bias introduced by sampling from $q$ instead of $p$. When $q(x)$ over-represents a region relative to $p(x)$, the weight $w(x) < 1$ downweights those samples. When $q(x)$ under-represents a region, $w(x) > 1$ amplifies them. On average, the reweighting is exact.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The importance sampling identity starts from the definition of an expectation:

$$E_p[f(X)] = \int f(x) p(x) \, dx = \int f(x) \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} q(x) \, dx = E_q\!\left[f(X) \frac{p(X)}{q(X)}\right]$$

Given $N$ samples $x_1, \ldots, x_N \sim q$, the ''importance sampling estimator'' is:

$$\hat{\mu}_{IS} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} f(x_i) \frac{p(x_i)}{q(x_i)} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} f(x_i) \, w(x_i)$$

This estimator is ''unbiased'': $E_q[\hat{\mu}_{IS}] = E_p[f(X)]$.

The ''variance'' of the IS estimator is:

$$\text{Var}_q[\hat{\mu}_{IS}] = \frac{1}{N} \text{Var}_q\!\left[f(X) \frac{p(X)}{q(X)}\right] = \frac{1}{N}\left(E_q\!\left[f(X)^2 \frac{p(X)^2}{q(X)^2}\right] - \mu^2\right)$$

The ''optimal proposal'' that minimizes variance is:

$$q^*(x) = \frac{|f(x)| \, p(x)}{\int |f(x')| \, p(x') \, dx'}$$

This is usually intractable (it requires the integral you are trying to estimate), but it guides intuition: a good proposal should be large where $|f(x)| p(x)$ is large.

When the normalizing constant of $p$ is unknown, ''self-normalized importance sampling'' uses:

$$\hat{\mu}_{SNIS} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} w(x_i) f(x_i)}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} w(x_i)}$$

This estimator is ''biased'' but ''consistent'' — the bias vanishes as $N \to \infty$. It is often more stable in practice because the weights are automatically normalized.

!! Importance Sampling in ML

''Off-policy reinforcement learning.'' An agent learns from trajectories collected under a behavior policy $\mu$ while evaluating a target policy $\pi$. The per-step importance ratio $\frac{\pi(a|s)}{\mu(a|s)}$ reweights returns. Over long trajectories, these ratios multiply together, creating the high-variance problem that motivates techniques like [[Reinforcement Learning|V-trace and PPO clipping]].

''VAE ELBO estimation.'' The evidence lower bound in variational autoencoders involves $E_{q(z|x)}[\log p(x|z)]$. Importance-weighted autoencoders (IWAE) tighten this bound by drawing $K$ samples from $q(z|x)$ and using importance weights $\frac{p(x,z)}{q(z|x)}$ inside a log-mean-exp, yielding the ''IWAE bound'' which approaches $\log p(x)$ as $K \to \infty$.

''Rare event simulation.'' Estimating the probability of catastrophic failure in safety-critical systems requires sampling from distributions tilted toward failure modes. Importance sampling with a shifted proposal makes these rare events common in the sample, then reweights to get the correct probability.

''Particle filtering.'' Sequential importance sampling (SIS) and sequential importance resampling (SIR) are the backbone of [[Monte Carlo Methods|particle filters]] for state estimation in nonlinear dynamical systems. At each time step, particles are weighted by the likelihood of the new observation, then optionally resampled to prevent weight degeneracy.

!! When Importance Sampling Breaks Down

''Weight degeneracy in high dimensions.'' As dimensionality grows, even small mismatches between $p$ and $q$ compound: most importance weights become negligibly small while a handful explode. In $d$ dimensions, the variance of the weights can grow //exponentially// with $d$. The effective sample size collapses to near 1, and your estimate is driven by a single lucky sample — essentially a random guess. This is why naive importance sampling is rarely used in spaces with more than about 20 dimensions.

''Insufficient tail coverage.'' If $q(x)$ has thinner tails than $p(x)$ — meaning there exist regions where $p(x) > 0$ but $q(x) \approx 0$ — the importance weights $p(x)/q(x)$ can become unbounded. Worse, you will rarely //sample// those regions, so the estimator appears stable but is quietly missing significant probability mass. The variance may technically be infinite, and the central limit theorem fails to apply. A safe rule: always choose $q$ with heavier tails than $p$.

''Compounding ratios in sequential settings.'' In off-policy RL or sequential Monte Carlo, the importance weight for a trajectory of length $T$ is the product of $T$ per-step ratios. Even if each individual ratio is well-behaved, the product can have astronomical variance. A trajectory of length 100 with per-step ratios averaging 1.05 yields a product around $1.05^{100} \approx 131$. Practical solutions include truncating or clipping weights (as in PPO), using V-trace's truncation operators, or periodic resampling in particle filters.

!! Technical Details

''Effective sample size (ESS).'' A diagnostic for weight degeneracy:

$$\text{ESS} = \frac{\left(\sum_{i=1}^N w_i\right)^2}{\sum_{i=1}^N w_i^2}$$

ESS ranges from 1 (one dominant weight) to $N$ (uniform weights). An ESS below $N/2$ signals poor proposal fit.

''Weight degeneracy and the curse of dimensionality.'' For two Gaussians in $d$ dimensions with unit variance and means separated by $\delta$, the $\chi^2$ divergence between them (which controls IS variance) grows as $\exp(d \cdot \delta^2)$. This exponential scaling is fundamental, not an artifact of poor proposal choice.

''Multiple importance sampling (MIS).'' Given $K$ proposals $q_1, \ldots, q_K$, the ''balance heuristic'' combines them:

$$w(x_i) = \frac{p(x_i)}{\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K q_k(x_i)}$$

Veach and Guibas (1995) proved this is provably close to optimal within a constant factor. MIS is widely used in [[Probability Distributions|computer graphics rendering]] and multi-modal posterior estimation.

''Annealed importance sampling (AIS).'' Bridges the gap between a tractable $q$ and a complex $p$ through a sequence of intermediate distributions $p_0 = q, p_1, \ldots, p_T = p$. At each stage, [[MCMC|MCMC]] transitions move samples toward the next intermediate, and importance weights accumulate the ratio of successive distributions. AIS provides an ''unbiased'' estimate of the normalizing constant ratio $Z_p / Z_q$, making it the gold standard for evaluating generative models.

''Defensive importance sampling.'' Mix a small fraction of the target into the proposal: $q_{\text{def}}(x) = (1-\epsilon) q(x) + \epsilon p(x)$. This guarantees bounded weights and protects against tail mismatch at the cost of slightly higher variance in well-covered regions.
