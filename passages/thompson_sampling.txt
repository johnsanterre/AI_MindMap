! Thompson Sampling

<img src="assets/thompson_sampling.svg" alt="Diagram showing Thompson Sampling drawing samples from three Beta posterior distributions and selecting the arm with the highest sample" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you moved to a new city with three coffee shops on your block. You have tried each once or twice, but you are still uncertain about which makes the best espresso. One morning you think: "Shop A has been good twice, Shop C was amazing once but I have only been there once — maybe I should go back and check." ''Thompson Sampling'' is the formal version of this reasoning. Instead of agonizing over how much to explore versus exploit, you maintain a probability distribution over how good each option is, draw a random number from each distribution, and simply pick whichever drew the highest number. Options you are uncertain about will occasionally draw high and get explored; options you are confident are good will draw high consistently and get exploited. No tuning parameter, no exploration schedule — just sample and act.

---

!! The Core Insight

The <<link "Explore-Exploit Tradeoff" "Explore-Exploit Tradeoff">><</link>> is one of the most fundamental tensions in sequential decision-making. You want to exploit the option that seems best so far, but you also need to explore other options that might turn out to be better. Algorithms like epsilon-greedy handle this crudely: explore randomly some fixed fraction of the time, exploit the rest. Upper confidence bound (UCB) methods explore deterministically by adding an optimism bonus. Both require tuning a parameter that controls the exploration rate.

Thompson Sampling, proposed by William R. Thompson in 1933, takes a completely different approach rooted in <<link "Bayes Theorem" "Bayes Theorem">><</link>>. For each option (or ''arm'' in the <<link "Multi-Armed Bandit" "Multi-Armed Bandit">><</link>> framing), you maintain a ''posterior distribution'' over its unknown reward probability. At each round, you draw one random sample from each arm's posterior, then play the arm whose sample is highest. That is the entire algorithm. The posterior encodes both what you know and how uncertain you are. Arms with wide posteriors (high uncertainty) will occasionally produce very high samples, guaranteeing they get tried. Arms with narrow posteriors centered on high values will produce consistently high samples, guaranteeing they get exploited. Exploration and exploitation emerge organically from the shape of the posteriors — no explicit balancing is needed.

The elegance is that Thompson Sampling is ''probability matching'': it selects each arm with probability equal to the posterior probability that that arm is optimal. This is a principled Bayesian strategy, and it achieves near-optimal regret bounds in a wide range of bandit problems without any parameter tuning.

---

!! How the Posterior Learns

The canonical setting is the Bernoulli bandit: each arm pays out 1 (success) or 0 (failure) with an unknown probability $\theta_k$. The natural Bayesian prior for a probability is the <<link "Beta Distribution" "Beta Distribution">><</link>>, $\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$, where $\alpha$ counts prior successes plus one and $\beta$ counts prior failures plus one. Starting with a uniform prior $\text{Beta}(1,1)$ for each arm, the update after observing a success on arm $k$ is:

$$\text{Beta}(\alpha_k, \beta_k) \;\to\; \text{Beta}(\alpha_k + 1, \beta_k)$$

After a failure:

$$\text{Beta}(\alpha_k, \beta_k) \;\to\; \text{Beta}(\alpha_k, \beta_k + 1)$$

This conjugate update is the simplest possible Bayesian learning: no matrix inversions, no MCMC, just increment a counter. Each arm's posterior is summarized by two integers. At every round you draw $\tilde{\theta}_k \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha_k, \beta_k)$ for each arm and play $\arg\max_k \tilde{\theta}_k$.

Early on, when all posteriors are wide and flat, the samples are nearly random — the algorithm explores broadly. As evidence accumulates, the posteriors narrow. The best arm's posterior concentrates near a high value, so its samples dominate. Inferior arms' posteriors concentrate near lower values, so they rarely win the max comparison. Exploration decays naturally, at exactly the rate justified by the evidence, without any explicit schedule.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The formal guarantee of Thompson Sampling is expressed in terms of ''Bayesian regret''. Define the regret after $T$ rounds as:

$$R_T = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \left(\theta^* - \theta_{A_t}\right)$$

where $\theta^*$ is the true mean of the best arm and $A_t$ is the arm selected at round $t$. The Bayesian regret is the expectation of $R_T$ over both the prior distribution on the problem instance and the algorithm's randomness. For Bernoulli bandits with $K$ arms, Thompson Sampling achieves:

$$\mathbb{E}[R_T] = O\!\left(\sqrt{KT \ln T}\right)$$

This is within a logarithmic factor of the theoretical lower bound $\Omega(\sqrt{KT})$. More refined analyses show that Thompson Sampling achieves the ''Lai-Robbins'' asymptotically optimal regret bound:

$$\liminf_{T \to \infty} \frac{\mathbb{E}[R_T]}{\ln T} \;\geq\; \sum_{k:\theta_k < \theta^*} \frac{\theta^* - \theta_k}{D_{\text{KL}}(\theta_k \| \theta^*)}$$

where $D_{\text{KL}}$ is the Kullback-Leibler divergence between the reward distributions of arm $k$ and the optimal arm. Thompson Sampling matches this bound, meaning it explores exactly the right amount in an information-theoretic sense.

The probability matching property can be stated precisely: at each round, the probability that Thompson Sampling selects arm $k$ equals the posterior probability that arm $k$ is optimal:

$$P(A_t = k \mid \mathcal{H}_t) = P\!\left(\theta_k = \max_j \theta_j \;\middle|\; \mathcal{H}_t\right)$$

where $\mathcal{H}_t$ is the history of observations. This is what makes the algorithm Bayes-optimal for one-step-ahead decisions and near-optimal over the full horizon.

---

!! Thompson Sampling in Practice

Beyond simple Bernoulli bandits, Thompson Sampling extends naturally to richer settings. For Gaussian rewards, use a Normal-Inverse-Gamma posterior. For contextual bandits (where you observe features before choosing an arm), maintain a posterior over a linear model: $r = x^T \beta + \epsilon$ and sample $\tilde{\beta}$ from the posterior at each round. This is called ''Linear Thompson Sampling'' and is widely deployed in recommendation systems, ad placement, and clinical trials.

Thompson Sampling is a core component of <<link "Bayesian Optimization" "Bayesian Optimization">><</link>>, where the arms are continuous design points and the posterior is maintained via a Gaussian process. Each iteration samples a function from the GP posterior and evaluates the point where the sampled function achieves its maximum. This is the ''Thompson Sampling acquisition function'', and it naturally balances exploration of uncertain regions with exploitation of promising ones.

In A/B testing, Thompson Sampling replaces the classical approach of running a fixed experiment and then analyzing results. Instead, traffic is allocated adaptively: the variant that appears to be winning receives more traffic in real time, while underperforming variants are automatically down-weighted. This reduces the opportunity cost of experimentation and converges to the winner faster than uniform random assignment.

One practical advantage over UCB methods is that Thompson Sampling handles ''delayed feedback'' and ''batched decisions'' more gracefully. When rewards arrive with a delay, the posterior is simply not updated until the reward is observed — the algorithm still functions correctly with a slightly stale posterior, whereas UCB bonuses can become poorly calibrated.

---

!! When Thompson Sampling Breaks Down

''Misspecified prior or likelihood:'' Thompson Sampling assumes the true reward-generating process is well-described by the chosen likelihood model, and that the prior is not pathologically wrong. If you use a Beta-Bernoulli model but rewards are actually heavy-tailed continuous values, the posterior concentrates on the wrong quantity. Similarly, a prior that assigns zero probability to the true parameter value will never recover. In practice this means you need to choose the right distributional family. When the model class is wrong, Thompson Sampling can converge to a suboptimal arm with full confidence and never self-correct.

''Non-stationary environments:'' The standard algorithm assumes arm reward distributions are fixed over time. If the best arm changes — for instance, user preferences shift seasonally — the posterior becomes anchored to stale data. The accumulated evidence from thousands of past observations overwhelms recent signals. Variants like ''discounted Thompson Sampling'' (downweighting old observations) or ''sliding window Thompson Sampling'' address this, but the vanilla algorithm fails to adapt.

''Computationally intractable posteriors:'' The elegant sample-and-act recipe requires that you can (a) maintain the posterior exactly and (b) draw samples from it efficiently. For simple conjugate models this is trivial, but for complex models — deep neural networks, non-conjugate likelihoods, structured action spaces — exact posterior inference is intractable. Approximate methods like MCMC, variational inference, or ensemble bootstrapping are used as surrogates, but they introduce approximation error that can degrade Thompson Sampling's theoretical guarantees. The gap between the clean theory and messy practice is widest when the posterior is hardest to compute.

---

!! Technical Details

''Historical origin:'' Thompson (1933), //On the Likelihood that One Unknown Probability Exceeds Another in View of the Evidence of Two Samples//, Biometrika. This paper predates UCB methods by decades and is arguably the earliest adaptive allocation algorithm. It was largely ignored until the 2010s when computational experiments and new theoretical analyses revived interest.

''Key theoretical results:'' Agrawal and Goyal (2012) proved the first near-optimal frequentist regret bounds for Thompson Sampling in Bernoulli bandits. Russo and Van Roy (2014) introduced the ''information ratio'' — the ratio of expected squared regret to information gained — and showed Thompson Sampling has a bounded information ratio, providing a unified regret analysis across many problem structures. Kaufmann, Korda, and Munos (2012) established the asymptotic optimality matching the Lai-Robbins lower bound.

''Connections to other algorithms:'' Thompson Sampling is equivalent to ''randomized probability matching'' and closely related to ''posterior sampling for reinforcement learning'' (PSRL), which extends the idea to MDPs: sample a complete MDP from the posterior, solve it optimally, execute the resulting policy for one episode, observe transitions, and update the posterior. PSRL achieves near-optimal Bayesian regret in tabular RL settings.

''Implementation detail:'' For Bernoulli bandits, the entire state is $K$ pairs of integers $(\alpha_k, \beta_k)$, and each round requires $K$ draws from Beta distributions — an $O(K)$ operation. This makes Thompson Sampling one of the most memory- and compute-efficient bandit algorithms. For Gaussian bandits, the state per arm is a mean, variance, and count, with draws from a Student-$t$ posterior.

''Optimistic Thompson Sampling'' resamples until the drawn value exceeds the posterior mean, combining Thompson Sampling's randomness with UCB's optimism. This variant can improve worst-case regret by a constant factor. ''Top-Two Thompson Sampling'' draws two samples and allocates between the top two arms to improve best-arm identification (pure exploration) performance.
