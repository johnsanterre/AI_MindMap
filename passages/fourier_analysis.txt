! Fourier Analysis

[img[assets/fourier_analysis.svg]]

Imagine walking into a crowded restaurant and hearing a wall of noise — dozens of conversations, clinking glasses, background music, a kitchen fan humming. Your brain somehow separates all of this into individual streams: you can focus on the person across from you, notice the song playing, or tune into the table behind you. Fourier analysis is the mathematical version of that trick. It takes any signal — no matter how tangled and complex — and decomposes it into a sum of pure sine and cosine waves, each with its own frequency, amplitude, and phase. What looks like chaos in the time domain becomes a neat inventory of components in the frequency domain.

---

!! The Core Insight

The central revelation of Fourier analysis is that ''any periodic signal can be perfectly reconstructed as a weighted sum of sinusoids''. A jagged square wave, a sharp sawtooth, an irregular heartbeat — each is secretly a choir of sine waves singing at different frequencies. The Fourier transform does not approximate or compress; it provides an ''exact, lossless'' change of representation. The signal in the time domain and its spectrum in the frequency domain contain identical information — they are two views of the same object.

This matters because many problems that are nearly impossible in the time domain become trivial in the frequency domain. Removing a 60 Hz electrical hum? Zero out one frequency bin. Detecting periodicity in a noisy dataset? Look for spikes in the spectrum. Computing a [[Convolution Theorem|convolution]]? Multiply two spectra pointwise instead of sliding a window through every position. The transform turns hard problems into easy ones.

---

!! From Time Domain to Frequency Domain

Think of the time domain as watching a movie frame by frame — you see everything that happens at each instant, but patterns that unfold over time are hard to spot. The frequency domain is like looking at the movie's soundtrack separated into individual instrument tracks. You can immediately see which notes are present, how loud each is, and how they combine.

The ''Fourier series'' handles periodic signals, expressing them as a (possibly infinite) sum of harmonics — integer multiples of a fundamental frequency. The ''Fourier transform'' generalises this to non-periodic signals by letting the set of frequencies become continuous rather than discrete. The ''Discrete Fourier Transform'' (DFT) adapts the idea to sampled digital data: given $N$ samples, it produces $N$ frequency coefficients. And the ''Fast Fourier Transform'' ([[FFT]]) computes the DFT in $O(N \log N)$ operations instead of $O(N^2)$, making spectral analysis practical for signals with millions of samples.

The relationship between these variants is a ladder of increasing practicality: pure mathematics (Fourier series) to continuous analysis (Fourier transform) to digital computation (DFT) to efficient implementation (FFT).

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The continuous Fourier transform takes a function $f(t)$ and produces its frequency representation $\hat{f}(\omega)$:

$$\hat{f}(\omega) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(t) \, e^{-i\omega t} \, dt$$

The exponential $e^{-i\omega t}$ is Euler's formula in action: $e^{-i\omega t} = \cos(\omega t) - i\sin(\omega t)$. The integral computes a ''correlation'' between the signal and each possible frequency — if the signal contains energy at frequency $\omega$, the integral accumulates a large value; if not, the contributions cancel out.

For digital signals, the ''Discrete Fourier Transform'' operates on $N$ samples:

$$X[k] = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x[n] \cdot e^{-i 2\pi k n / N}$$

Each coefficient $X[k]$ is a complex number. Its ''magnitude'' $|X[k]|$ tells you how much of frequency $k$ is present; its ''phase'' $\angle X[k]$ tells you where that sinusoid is shifted in time. The inverse DFT reconstructs the original signal exactly:

$$x[n] = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} X[k] \cdot e^{i 2\pi k n / N}$$

The ''Parseval's theorem'' connects energy across domains: $\sum |x[n]|^2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum |X[k]|^2$. Energy is conserved — nothing is created or destroyed by the transform. The ''power spectral density'' $|X[k]|^2$ shows how energy is distributed across frequencies.

---

!! Fourier Methods in ML

Fourier analysis appears throughout modern machine learning in both explicit and implicit forms.

''Audio and speech processing'' relies on spectrograms — the output of Short-Time Fourier Transforms — as the primary input representation. Models like Whisper convert raw audio into mel spectrograms before any learning begins. The mel scale compresses higher frequencies to match human perception, but the underlying decomposition is Fourier.

''Efficient architectures'' have begun replacing attention with Fourier transforms. ''FNet'' (Google, 2021) showed that replacing self-attention layers with simple FFTs across the sequence dimension achieves 92% of BERT's accuracy at much lower cost. The ''Adaptive Fourier Neural Operator'' (AFNO) uses Fourier layers for weather prediction and turbulence modeling. These work because the FFT provides global mixing of information — every output token depends on every input token — which is precisely what attention does, but in $O(N \log N)$ instead of $O(N^2)$.

''Positional encodings'' in [[Transformers|Transformers]] use sinusoidal functions at different frequencies — a direct application of Fourier basis functions. Each position is encoded as a vector of $\sin$ and $\cos$ values at geometrically spaced frequencies, enabling the model to represent relative positions through simple linear operations.

''[[Spectral Graph Theory]]'' extends Fourier analysis to graphs. The eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian serve as a Fourier basis for signals defined on graph nodes. Graph convolution in spectral domain — filtering by multiplying in the eigenspace — is the mathematical foundation of spectral graph neural networks like ChebNet and GCN.

''[[Signal Processing]]'' and the [[Wavelet Transform]] are closely related disciplines. Where Fourier analysis decomposes into global sinusoids, wavelets use localised, multi-scale basis functions — better suited for signals with sharp transients like edges in images or spikes in neural recordings.

---

!! When Fourier Analysis Breaks Down

''Non-stationary signals:'' The Fourier transform assumes that all frequencies are present for the entire duration of the signal. For signals whose frequency content changes over time — speech, music, stock prices — a single transform smears together information from different time periods. The Short-Time Fourier Transform mitigates this by windowing, but it introduces an inescapable trade-off: narrow windows give good time resolution but poor frequency resolution, and vice versa. This is the ''Heisenberg-Gabor uncertainty principle'' for signals — you cannot have perfect resolution in both time and frequency simultaneously.

''Spectral leakage:'' When the DFT is applied to a finite segment of a signal, the abrupt truncation at the edges acts as a rectangular window. In the frequency domain this window convolves with the true spectrum, spreading energy from sharp peaks into neighbouring bins — creating phantom frequency components that do not exist in the original signal. Windowing functions like Hann or Blackman reduce leakage but widen the main lobe, trading leakage for reduced frequency resolution. Every practical Fourier analysis is a compromise shaped by the choice of window.

''Nonlinearity and non-periodic structure:'' Fourier analysis excels at representing signals that are sums of sinusoids. But many real-world phenomena — sharp edges in images, impulse-like events in time series, chaotic dynamics — require very many Fourier coefficients to represent accurately. A single discontinuity produces the Gibbs phenomenon: the Fourier reconstruction overshoots and rings around the jump. For such signals, the [[Wavelet Transform]] or learned representations (like those in deep networks) often provide more compact, useful decompositions.

!! Technical Details

''Historical foundations:'' Joseph Fourier (1807) proposed that any function can be represented as a sum of sinusoids while studying heat conduction. Dirichlet (1829) provided the first rigorous convergence conditions. The Cooley-Tukey FFT algorithm (1965) made computation practical, though Gauss had discovered a similar method around 1805.

''Convergence conditions:'' The Fourier series of a function $f$ converges pointwise at every point where $f$ is continuous, provided $f$ is piecewise smooth. At discontinuities, it converges to the average of the left and right limits. In the $L^2$ sense, any square-integrable function has a convergent Fourier series — this is the completeness of the trigonometric system.

''Uncertainty principle:'' For any signal, the product of its time spread $\Delta t$ and frequency spread $\Delta \omega$ satisfies $\Delta t \cdot \Delta \omega \geq \frac{1}{2}$. Gaussian signals achieve equality — they are maximally localised in both domains simultaneously.

''Multidimensional DFT:'' The 2D DFT is separable — compute 1D DFTs along rows, then along columns. This extends to arbitrary dimensions and is the basis for spectral methods in image processing, PDE solving, and volumetric data analysis.

''Relationship to Laplace and Z-transforms:'' The Fourier transform is the Laplace transform evaluated on the imaginary axis ($s = i\omega$). The DFT is the Z-transform evaluated on the unit circle ($z = e^{i2\pi k/N}$). These connections unify continuous and discrete, time and frequency analysis.

''Computational notes:'' Modern FFT libraries (FFTW, cuFFT) achieve near-optimal performance on GPUs. Mixed-radix algorithms handle non-power-of-2 sizes. The FFT is one of the ten most important algorithms in scientific computing.
