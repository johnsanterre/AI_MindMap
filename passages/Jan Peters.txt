! Jan Peters
<img src="assets/jan_peters.svg" alt="Jan Peters" width="800">

Imagine you want to teach a person to throw darts. You would not make them memorise every muscle activation in sequence — instead, you would give them a rough motion template and let them refine it through practice. Jan Peters has spent two decades building the mathematical machinery that lets robots learn exactly this way: start with structured movement representations, then optimise them through principled ''policy search'' methods rather than brute-force exploration. His work sits at the crossroads of ''control theory'', ''reinforcement learning'', and ''motor neuroscience'', and has produced some of the most compelling demonstrations of robots acquiring dexterous skills — from playing table tennis to tossing a ball into a cup.

!! Key Contributions

Peters has shaped modern robot learning through several intertwined research threads:

* ''Robot Motor Skill Learning'' — Framing robot skill acquisition not as generic RL but as learning structured movement policies that respect the physics and geometry of the task.
* ''Policy Search Methods'' — Developing algorithms like ''Relative Entropy Policy Search (REPS)'' and ''Policy learning by Weighting Exploration with the Returns (PoWER)'' that update policies efficiently in high-dimensional continuous action spaces.
* ''Movement Primitives'' — Advancing ''Dynamic Movement Primitives (DMPs)'' and ''Probabilistic Movement Primitives (ProMPs)'' as compact, reusable building blocks for motor behaviours.
* ''Hierarchical RL for Robotics'' — Structuring learning into layers of abstraction so that complex tasks decompose into manageable sub-skills.
* ''TU Darmstadt Intelligent Autonomous Systems Lab'' — Building one of Europe's premier robot learning groups, training a generation of researchers who now lead labs worldwide.
* ''Max Planck Institute for Intelligent Systems'' — Holding a joint appointment that bridges fundamental research and applied robotics.

!! The Core Insight

The central thesis running through Peters' work is that ''robots need structured policy representations''. Generic [[Reinforcement Learning|Reinforcement Learning]] algorithms treat the agent as a blank slate — a //tabula rasa// learner that must discover everything from scratch. But a robot arm has known kinematics, tasks have temporal structure, and movements follow smooth trajectories. By encoding these priors into the policy representation — through movement primitives, contextual parameters, or hierarchical decompositions — the search space shrinks dramatically and learning becomes practical on real hardware with limited trials.

This is not a rejection of RL but a //refinement// of it: use the right inductive bias so that [[Policy Gradient|Policy Gradient]] methods and information-theoretic updates can operate in a tractable space.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''Policy search'' frames robot learning as direct optimisation of a parameterised policy $\pi_\theta(a \mid s)$ to maximise expected return:

$$J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}\left[\sum_{t=0}^{T} \gamma^t r(s_t, a_t)\right]$$

Standard policy gradient computes $\nabla_\theta J(\theta)$ and takes gradient steps, but this can be unstable in robotics. Peters' ''REPS'' algorithm instead constrains the policy update by bounding the ''Kullback-Leibler divergence'' between successive policies:

$$\max_{\pi} \mathbb{E}_\pi[R] \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \text{KL}(\pi \| \pi_{\text{old}}) \leq \epsilon$$

This information-theoretic bound prevents catastrophically large updates — critical when each trial runs on a real robot. The solution yields a closed-form weight for each sample trajectory:

$$w_i \propto \exp\left(\frac{R_i}{\eta}\right)$$

where $\eta$ is a temperature parameter found by solving a convex dual problem.

''Dynamic Movement Primitives'' represent a trajectory as the output of a damped spring-mass system driven by a learnable forcing function:

$$\tau \ddot{y} = \alpha_z(\beta_z(g - y) - \dot{y}) + f(x)$$

where $g$ is the goal, $\tau$ is a temporal scaling factor, and $f(x)$ is a weighted sum of basis functions that shapes the movement. Learning reduces to finding the basis function weights — a low-dimensional, well-conditioned regression problem.

!! Impact on the Field

Peters' influence extends well beyond his own publications:

* ''European Robotics Leadership'' — His lab at TU Darmstadt and the associated Max Planck group have been central to making Europe competitive in robot learning, an area historically dominated by US and Japanese labs.
* ''Bridging Control Theory and RL'' — By insisting that RL for robotics must respect the structure of dynamical systems, Peters helped create a productive middle ground between classical control and modern deep RL. His students, including [[Sergey Levine|Sergey Levine]], have carried this perspective into influential research programs worldwide.
* ''Compelling Demonstrations'' — The ''robot table tennis'' system and the ''ball-in-a-cup'' experiments became iconic benchmarks that showed policy search could acquire complex sensorimotor skills with remarkably few trials on real hardware.
* ''[[Imitation Learning|Imitation Learning]] Integration'' — His work on learning from demonstrations combined with subsequent RL refinement established a practical workflow: show the robot roughly what to do, then let it improve autonomously.

!! When Robot Skill Learning Breaks Down

''1. Reward specification collapse.'' When the reward function does not capture the true task objective, the robot optimises the wrong thing. A table tennis reward based purely on ball velocity may produce motions that destroy the paddle. Structured movement primitives mitigate but do not eliminate this — the reward must still be well-designed.

''2. Sim-to-real transfer gap.'' Policy search methods developed in simulation may exploit simulator artefacts (unrealistic friction, perfect sensing). Peters' emphasis on real-robot learning sidesteps this, but limits the number of trials available, making sample efficiency paramount. [[Model-Based RL|Model-Based RL]] and [[Bayesian Optimization|Bayesian Optimization]] can help stretch limited data further.

''3. Non-stationary task structure.'' Movement primitives assume a relatively stable task geometry. When the environment changes unpredictably between episodes — a moving target, a deformable object, an unstructured workspace — the fixed primitive parameterisation may lack the expressiveness to adapt, requiring hierarchical or contextual extensions that add complexity.

!! Technical Details

''Key Papers:''
* Peters &amp; Schaal, "Reinforcement Learning of Motor Skills with Policy Gradients" (Neural Networks, 2008) — foundational treatment of policy gradient methods for robotics.
* Peters, Muelling &amp; Altun, "Relative Entropy Policy Search" (AAAI, 2010) — introduced the REPS algorithm.
* Kober &amp; Peters, "Policy Search for Motor Primitives in Robotics" (NIPS, 2009) — PoWER algorithm for episodic RL.
* Paraschos, Daniel, Peters &amp; Neumann, "Probabilistic Movement Primitives" (NIPS, 2013) — ProMPs framework.

''Career:'' PhD at University of Southern California under Stefan Schaal; faculty at TU Darmstadt (Full Professor, Computer Science) and research group leader at Max Planck Institute for Intelligent Systems, Tuebingen.

''Awards &amp; Recognition:'' IEEE Robotics and Automation Society (RAS) Early Career Award; ERC Starting Grant; multiple best paper awards at ICRA and IROS. Active in the IEEE RAS community as associate editor and programme committee member.

''Research Group:'' The Intelligent Autonomous Systems (IAS) lab at TU Darmstadt has produced dozens of PhD graduates who now hold positions at major universities and industry labs across Europe and North America.
