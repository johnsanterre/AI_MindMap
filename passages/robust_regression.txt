! Robust Regression
<img src="assets/robust_regression.svg" alt="Robust regression loss functions and RANSAC vs OLS comparison" style="max-width:100%;margin:1em 0;">

Imagine you are measuring the heights of students in a classroom to find the average. One student stands on a chair as a joke and reports their height as ten feet. If you use the ordinary average, that single prankster drags the result up and misleads you about the real class. If instead you use the //median// or simply ignore measurements that are absurdly far from the rest, you recover the truth. Robust regression does the same thing for fitting lines and curves: it ensures that a handful of wild data points cannot hijack the entire model.

!! The Core Insight

Ordinary least squares (OLS) minimises the sum of //squared// residuals. Squaring means that a point ten times farther away than normal gets one hundred times more influence on the fit. ''Robust regression'' methods replace or modify this squared penalty so that extreme residuals receive bounded or diminishing influence. The three major families are:

- ''M-estimators'' — generalise OLS by replacing the squared loss with a less rapidly growing function (Huber, Tukey bisquare, etc.)
- ''RANSAC'' (Random Sample Consensus) — repeatedly fits the model to random subsets, then selects the fit with the most inlier support
- ''Least Trimmed Squares (LTS)'' — minimises the sum of the smallest $h$ squared residuals, explicitly discarding the rest

The result is a regression that tells you about the //majority// of your data, not the minority of rogues.

!! M-Estimators and Influence Functions

An ''M-estimator'' minimises $\sum_{i} \rho(r_i)$ where $r_i = y_i - \mathbf{x}_i^T \boldsymbol{\beta}$ and $\rho$ is a chosen loss function. The key tool for understanding robustness is the ''influence function'' $\psi(r) = \rho'(r)$, which tells you how much a single observation shifts the estimate.

For OLS, $\rho(r) = r^2$ so $\psi(r) = 2r$: influence grows without bound. For the ''Huber loss'', influence is linear up to a threshold $\delta$ and then constant — large residuals still matter but cannot dominate. For ''Tukey's bisquare'', $\psi(r) = 0$ for $|r| > c$: extreme outliers are completely ignored, making it a ''redescending'' estimator.

The ''breakdown point'' quantifies the maximum fraction of arbitrarily corrupted observations an estimator can tolerate before its output becomes meaningless. OLS has a breakdown point of $0\%$ (a single outlier can drag the fit to infinity). LTS and the Least Median of Squares have breakdown points of nearly $50\%$.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ''Huber loss'' combines quadratic and linear regimes:

$$\rho_{\delta}(r) = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{2}r^2 & |r| \le \delta \\ \delta\,|r| - \frac{1}{2}\delta^2 & |r| > \delta \end{cases}$$

The parameter $\delta$ controls the transition. Small residuals are treated as in OLS (efficient estimation); large residuals incur only linear cost (bounded influence).

''Tukey's bisquare'' goes further with a redescending $\psi$:

$$\rho_c(r) = \begin{cases} \frac{c^2}{6}\left[1 - \left(1 - \frac{r^2}{c^2}\right)^3\right] & |r| \le c \\ \frac{c^2}{6} & |r| > c \end{cases}$$

Points with $|r| > c$ contribute zero gradient and are effectively discarded.

''RANSAC'' operates differently. Given $n$ points and a model requiring $p$ parameters:

$$k = \frac{\log(1 - P)}{\log(1 - w^p)}$$

where $k$ is the number of random subsets to draw, $P$ is the desired probability of finding an outlier-free subset, and $w$ is the estimated inlier ratio. Each subset of $p$ points defines a candidate model; the model with the largest consensus set (points within threshold $\epsilon$) wins.

The ''Iteratively Reweighted Least Squares'' (IRLS) algorithm solves M-estimation iteratively:

$$\boldsymbol{\beta}^{(t+1)} = \left(\mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{W}^{(t)} \mathbf{X}\right)^{-1} \mathbf{X}^T \mathbf{W}^{(t)} \mathbf{y}$$

where the diagonal weight matrix $\mathbf{W}^{(t)}$ has entries $w_i^{(t)} = \psi(r_i^{(t)}) / r_i^{(t)}$, down-weighting large residuals at each step.

!! Practical Considerations

''Choosing the method:'' Use Huber when you expect a modest fraction of moderate outliers and want a convex, easy-to-optimise objective. Use RANSAC when outliers are severe (e.g., computer vision point matching with $>30\%$ outliers). Use Tukey bisquare or LTS when you need high breakdown but can tolerate non-convexity and the need for good initialisation.

''Tuning $\delta$ and $c$:'' For the Huber loss, $\delta = 1.345 \hat{\sigma}$ achieves $95\%$ asymptotic efficiency relative to OLS under Gaussian errors. For Tukey bisquare, $c = 4.685 \hat{\sigma}$ is the standard choice. The scale estimate $\hat{\sigma}$ is typically the ''Median Absolute Deviation'' (MAD) multiplied by $1.4826$.

''Computational cost:'' M-estimators via IRLS converge in a few iterations and scale like OLS. RANSAC can be expensive for high-dimensional models because the required number of subsets $k$ grows exponentially in $p$.

''High-dimensional settings:'' combining robust loss with <<link "Regularization" "Regularization">><</link>> (e.g., Huber + $\ell_1$ penalty) yields robust-sparse estimators, useful when both outliers and irrelevant features are present.

!! When Robust Regression Breaks Down

''Outliers in the feature space (leverage points):'' M-estimators down-weight residuals but not leverage. A point far from the data cloud in $x$-space can have a small residual while still distorting the fit. Generalised M-estimators (GM-estimators) or bounded-influence estimators are needed to handle leverage outliers.

''Non-convex loss landscapes:'' Redescending estimators (Tukey) and LTS have multiple local minima. Poor initialisation can leave the algorithm stuck at a suboptimal solution. Common practice is to initialise with a simpler robust method (e.g., Least Median of Squares) or to use multiple random starts.

''Very high outlier fractions approaching the breakdown point:'' When nearly half the data is corrupted, even high-breakdown estimators become unreliable, and distinguishing inliers from outliers becomes fundamentally ambiguous. No method can robustly estimate a regression when the majority of observations are adversarial.

!! Technical Details

- ''Huber (1964)'' — Robust estimation of a location parameter; introduced M-estimators and the Huber loss
- ''Fischler & Bolles (1981)'' — RANSAC: Random Sample Consensus for model fitting in computer vision
- ''Rousseeuw (1984)'' — Least Median of Squares and Least Trimmed Squares; $50\%$ breakdown point estimators
- ''Hampel et al. (1986)'' — formalised the influence function and breakdown point as core robustness measures
- ''Tukey's bisquare:'' standard tuning constant $c = 4.685$ at Gaussian model; $95\%$ efficiency
- ''Huber loss:'' standard $\delta = 1.345 \hat{\sigma}$; $95\%$ asymptotic relative efficiency under normality
- OLS breakdown point: $1/n$ (one observation can ruin the fit)
- LTS breakdown point: $\lfloor (n - p)/2 \rfloor / n \approx 50\%$
- IRLS typically converges in 5-15 iterations for Huber loss
- RANSAC complexity: $O(k \cdot p \cdot n)$ where $k$ grows exponentially in $p$
- Python: `sklearn.linear_model.HuberRegressor`, `sklearn.linear_model.RANSACRegressor`, `statsmodels.robust.norms`
