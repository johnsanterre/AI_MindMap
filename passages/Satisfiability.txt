! Satisfiability
<img src="assets/satisfiability.svg" alt="Satisfiability diagram showing CNF formula, DPLL search tree, and SAT/UNSAT outcomes">

Imagine you have a massive switchboard with hundreds of toggle switches, and someone hands you a list of rules: "at least one of switches 3, 7, or 12 must be on," "switch 5 or switch 9 must be off," and so on. The ''satisfiability problem'' (SAT) asks a deceptively simple question: is there //any// combination of switch positions that obeys every single rule simultaneously? This turns out to be one of the most important questions in all of computer science -- the first problem ever proven to be NP-complete, and a cornerstone of computational complexity theory.

!! The Core Insight

SAT is the canonical [[NP-Completeness|NP-Completeness]] problem. Every problem in NP can be reduced to SAT in polynomial time, which means a fast SAT solver would crack open //every// problem in NP. This is the essence of the ''Cook-Levin theorem'': Stephen Cook (1971) and Leonid Levin (independently) proved that Boolean satisfiability is NP-complete. If you can solve SAT efficiently, you can solve everything from scheduling to protein folding. Conversely, if SAT is truly hard, then thousands of practical problems are fundamentally hard too. This places SAT squarely at the heart of the [[P vs NP|P vs NP]] question.

!! Conjunctive Normal Form and the Language of SAT

A SAT instance is typically expressed in ''Conjunctive Normal Form'' (CNF): a conjunction (AND) of ''clauses'', where each clause is a disjunction (OR) of ''literals'' (a variable or its negation). For example:

$$(x_1 \lor \neg x_2 \lor x_3) \land (\neg x_1 \lor x_2 \lor x_4) \land (x_2 \lor \neg x_3 \lor \neg x_4)$$

A ''$k$-SAT'' instance restricts every clause to exactly $k$ literals. This distinction matters enormously:

* ''2-SAT'' is solvable in polynomial time using implication graphs and strongly connected components -- it lives comfortably in P.
* ''3-SAT'' is NP-complete. The jump from 2 to 3 literals per clause is where computational hardness explodes.

Any Boolean formula can be converted to an equisatisfiable CNF formula in polynomial time using the ''Tseitin transformation'', which introduces auxiliary variables to avoid exponential blowup.

This structural framework connects directly to [[Propositional Logic|Propositional Logic]], since SAT asks whether a propositional formula has a satisfying truth assignment.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Given a CNF formula $\varphi$ over $n$ Boolean variables $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n$ with $m$ clauses, we seek an assignment $\alpha: \{x_1, \ldots, x_n\} \to \{0, 1\}$ such that:

$$\alpha \models \varphi \iff \forall\, C_j \in \varphi,\; \exists\, l \in C_j \text{ such that } \alpha(l) = 1$$

The ''DPLL algorithm'' (Davis-Putnam-Logemann-Loveland, 1962) systematically searches the space of assignments using [[Backtracking|Backtracking]]:

# ''Unit propagation'': If a clause has exactly one unassigned literal, that literal //must// be true. Set it and simplify.
# ''Pure literal elimination'': If a variable appears only positively (or only negatively) across all clauses, assign it to satisfy those clauses.
# ''Branching'': Pick an unassigned variable $x_i$, try $x_i = 1$, recurse. If that fails, try $x_i = 0$.

The worst-case runtime is $O(2^n)$, but unit propagation prunes enormous portions of the search tree in practice.

Modern solvers use ''CDCL'' (Conflict-Driven Clause Learning), which extends DPLL with:

$$\text{When a conflict occurs at decision level } d: \text{ analyze the implication graph to learn a new clause } C_{\text{learn}}$$

$$\varphi' = \varphi \land C_{\text{learn}}$$

The learned clause prevents the solver from ever revisiting the same dead-end combination. CDCL also performs ''non-chronological backjumping'' -- instead of backing up one level at a time, it leaps directly to the decision level responsible for the conflict:

$$\text{Backjump level} = \max\{d(l) : l \in C_{\text{learn}},\; d(l) \neq d_{\text{conflict}}\}$$

!! Random SAT and Phase Transitions

One of the most striking discoveries in computational complexity is the ''phase transition'' in random $k$-SAT. For random 3-SAT instances with $n$ variables and $m$ clauses, define the clause-to-variable ratio $\alpha = m / n$. There exists a critical threshold:

$$\alpha_c \approx 4.267$$

* When $\alpha < \alpha_c$: instances are almost surely satisfiable (underconstrained).
* When $\alpha > \alpha_c$: instances are almost surely unsatisfiable (overconstrained).
* Near $\alpha \approx \alpha_c$: instances are hardest for solvers -- the "mushy middle" where satisfying assignments exist but are extremely difficult to find.

This phase transition mirrors phenomena in statistical physics, connecting SAT to the study of spin glasses and [[Complexity Classes|Complexity Classes]] at a deep structural level.

!! SAT in Machine Learning and Verification

SAT solvers have found surprising applications in ML:

* ''Neural network verification'': Encoding a neural network as a Boolean formula lets SAT/SMT solvers prove properties like robustness -- "no input within $\epsilon$ of this image changes the classification." This is critical for safety-critical AI.
* ''MaxSAT for optimization'': The ''Maximum Satisfiability'' problem asks: what is the largest number of clauses that can be simultaneously satisfied? Weighted MaxSAT naturally encodes soft constraints, making it useful for combinatorial optimization in ML pipelines.
* ''Constraint satisfaction'': Feature selection, Bayesian network structure learning, and hardware-aware model compression can all be formulated as SAT or constraint satisfaction problems.
* ''Interpretable ML'': SAT solvers have been used to find optimal decision trees and rule lists, producing models that are provably minimal and interpretable.

!! When Satisfiability Breaks Down

''Clause explosion from naive encoding.'' Real-world problems often have structure that, if encoded naively into CNF, produces an astronomical number of clauses. The Tseitin transformation helps, but choosing the right encoding remains an art. A poor encoding can turn a tractable problem into one that chokes even state-of-the-art solvers.

''Hardness at the phase transition.'' While random instances away from the critical ratio $\alpha_c$ are easy, instances near the threshold resist all known algorithms. This is not just a theoretical curiosity -- structured industrial instances can land in similarly hard regimes, and no amount of clever heuristics guarantees fast solutions.

''Scalability limits for ML verification.'' Encoding even a modest neural network into SAT produces millions of variables and clauses. Current solvers can verify small networks, but scaling to production-size deep learning models remains an open challenge. Approximation and abstraction techniques are active research areas.

!! Technical Details

''Complexity landscape:''
* SAT is NP-complete (Cook-Levin, 1971).
* 2-SAT $\in$ P, solvable in $O(n + m)$ via Tarjan's algorithm on the implication graph.
* 3-SAT is NP-complete; best known exact algorithm runs in $O(1.307^n)$ (Hertli, 2014).
* Horn-SAT (clauses with at most one positive literal) is solvable in linear time.
* MAX-2-SAT is NP-hard; MAX-3-SAT is NP-hard to approximate beyond ratio $7/8$ (Hastad).

''CDCL components:''
* ''Decision heuristics'': VSIDS (Variable State Independent Decaying Sum) scores variables by recent conflict participation.
* ''Restart strategies'': Luby sequence or geometric restarts prevent the solver from getting trapped in unproductive subtrees.
* ''Clause database management'': Learned clauses are periodically purged based on activity scores (LBD -- Literal Block Distance).
* ''Watched literals'': An efficient data structure that avoids scanning all clauses during propagation -- only two literals per clause are "watched."

''Key solvers'': MiniSat, CaDiCaL, Kissat, Glucose. Modern solvers routinely handle industrial instances with millions of variables.

''Resolution proof system'': DPLL and CDCL produce proofs in the resolution proof system. A resolution step derives $C_1 \lor C_2$ from $(C_1 \lor x)$ and $(C_2 \lor \neg x)$:

$$\frac{(A \lor x) \quad (B \lor \neg x)}{A \lor B}$$
