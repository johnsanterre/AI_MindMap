! Monte Carlo Methods

[img[assets/monte_carlo_methods.svg]]

Imagine you need to find the area of a strangely shaped pond in your backyard, but you have no measuring tools. So you stand on the roof and throw thousands of pebbles at random onto the rectangular yard below. Count how many land in the pond versus on dry ground, and the ratio gives you the pond's area as a fraction of the rectangle. That is the essence of ''Monte Carlo methods'': use randomness to estimate quantities that would be difficult or impossible to compute exactly.

!! The Core Insight

Monte Carlo methods replace exact computation with ''statistical estimation''. Instead of solving an integral analytically, you draw random samples and average the results. The law of large numbers guarantees that this average converges to the true answer as you draw more samples — and crucially, the convergence rate of $O(1/\sqrt{N})$ does //not// depend on the dimensionality of the problem. This dimension-independence is why Monte Carlo dominates high-dimensional computation where grid-based methods would require astronomically many evaluation points.

The name comes from the famous casino in Monaco — a fitting metaphor for a method that harnesses chance. The approach was formalized during the Manhattan Project by Stanislaw Ulam and John von Neumann, who used random sampling to simulate neutron diffusion through shielding materials when deterministic calculation was intractable.

!! Importance Sampling and Variance Reduction

The naive approach — sampling uniformly at random — works but can be inefficient. If the function you are integrating has most of its mass concentrated in a small region, uniform samples will waste effort on regions that contribute almost nothing. ''Importance sampling'' fixes this by sampling from a //proposal distribution// $q(x)$ that concentrates samples where the integrand is large, then reweighting each sample by the ratio $p(x)/q(x)$ to correct for the bias.

The ''importance-weighted estimator'' is:

$$\hat{I} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{f(x_i) \, p(x_i)}{q(x_i)}, \quad x_i \sim q$$

When $q$ is proportional to $|f(x)| \, p(x)$, the variance drops to zero — the //optimal// importance distribution. In practice you cannot achieve this exactly (it requires knowing the answer), but even rough approximations can reduce variance by orders of magnitude.

Other ''variance reduction'' techniques include:

* ''Stratified sampling'': divide the domain into strata, sample within each, and combine — guarantees every region is represented.
* ''Antithetic variates'': pair each sample $x$ with a negatively correlated counterpart $1-x$, so their errors partially cancel.
* ''Control variates'': subtract a function whose expectation you know analytically, reducing the variance of the remainder.

See <<link "Importance Sampling" "Importance Sampling">><</link>> for a deeper treatment.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The fundamental problem is evaluating an integral:

$$I = \int f(x) \, p(x) \, dx = \mathbb{E}_{p}[f(X)]$$

The ''Monte Carlo estimator'' draws $N$ independent samples $x_1, \ldots, x_N \sim p(x)$ and computes:

$$\hat{I}_N = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} f(x_i)$$

By the ''strong law of large numbers'', $\hat{I}_N \to I$ almost surely as $N \to \infty$. The ''central limit theorem'' gives the error distribution:

$$\sqrt{N}(\hat{I}_N - I) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\left(0, \text{Var}_p[f(X)]\right)$$

So the standard error is $\sigma / \sqrt{N}$, where $\sigma^2 = \text{Var}_p[f(X)]$. Halving the error requires quadrupling the samples — but this rate holds //regardless of dimension//, unlike deterministic quadrature which suffers the curse of dimensionality.

For importance sampling with proposal $q$:

$$\hat{I}_q = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} f(x_i) \frac{p(x_i)}{q(x_i)}, \quad x_i \sim q$$

The ''effective sample size'' (ESS) measures how many independent samples your importance-weighted samples are worth:

$$\text{ESS} = \frac{\left(\sum_i w_i\right)^2}{\sum_i w_i^2}$$

where $w_i = p(x_i)/q(x_i)$. When $q$ matches $p$ poorly, a few samples dominate the weights and ESS collapses.

!! Monte Carlo in ML

Monte Carlo methods are woven throughout modern machine learning:

''Bayesian inference'': Computing the posterior $p(\theta \mid D) \propto p(D \mid \theta) \, p(\theta)$ requires integrating over the parameter space. <<link "MCMC" "MCMC">><</link>> algorithms — including <<link "Metropolis-Hastings" "Metropolis-Hastings">><</link>>, Hamiltonian Monte Carlo, and Gibbs sampling — construct Markov chains whose stationary distribution is the posterior, letting you draw approximate samples without computing the normalizing constant.

''Variational inference'': The evidence lower bound (ELBO) involves an expectation under the variational distribution. The ''reparameterization trick'' rewrites samples as deterministic transformations of noise, enabling gradient-based optimization of Monte Carlo estimates of the ELBO.

''Reinforcement learning'': Policy gradient methods like REINFORCE estimate the gradient of expected reward using Monte Carlo rollouts of the policy. <<link "Monte Carlo Tree Search" "Monte Carlo Tree Search">><</link>> uses random simulations to evaluate game positions — it powered AlphaGo's superhuman Go play.

''Dropout as Monte Carlo'': Running a network with dropout at test time and averaging predictions approximates Bayesian model averaging — a technique called MC Dropout that provides uncertainty estimates for free.

''Generative models'': Diffusion models, variational autoencoders, and energy-based models all rely on Monte Carlo estimation of intractable likelihoods or gradients during training.

!! When Monte Carlo Methods Break Down

''High variance from bad proposals'': If your proposal distribution $q$ assigns low probability to regions where $f(x) \cdot p(x)$ is large, a few rare samples will receive enormous importance weights. The estimate becomes dominated by these outliers, and the effective sample size collapses. You might need millions of samples to get a reliable estimate — or worse, your confidence interval may //look// tight while systematically missing the true value because the rare important region was never sampled.

''Slow mixing in MCMC'': When the target distribution has well-separated modes (think a posterior with multiple peaks separated by valleys of near-zero probability), Markov chain samplers can get trapped in a single mode for extremely long runs. The chain appears to have converged — diagnostics look fine — but it has only explored one mode. This is the curse of multimodality, and it afflicts high-dimensional problems in Bayesian deep learning and statistical physics alike.

''Compounding error in sequential estimation'': In particle filters and sequential Monte Carlo, samples are reweighted and resampled at each time step. Small errors compound over time: the particle population progressively degenerates as a few particles accumulate all the weight. After enough steps, the effective sample size drops to one, and the filter has collapsed. Resampling strategies (systematic, residual) help but do not eliminate the fundamental issue in high dimensions or over long time horizons. See <<link "Random Variables" "Random Variables">><</link>> for foundational concepts.

!! Technical Details

''History'': The method was named by Nicholas Metropolis during the Manhattan Project (1940s). Ulam conceived the idea while playing solitaire and wondering about the probability of winning — he realized random simulation was more practical than combinatorial analysis.

''Convergence rate'': The Monte Carlo error is $O(N^{-1/2})$ regardless of dimension $d$. Compare with the trapezoidal rule at $O(N^{-2/d})$ — in $d = 20$ dimensions, Monte Carlo is vastly superior.

''Quasi-Monte Carlo'': Replacing pseudorandom sequences with low-discrepancy sequences (Sobol, Halton) can improve convergence to $O(N^{-1} (\log N)^d)$ in favorable cases, though the advantage diminishes in very high dimensions.

''Rao-Blackwellization'': If you can analytically integrate out some variables, do so — this always reduces variance. The resulting ''Rao-Blackwellized estimator'' uses the conditional expectation over the analytically tractable variables, keeping Monte Carlo only for the rest.

''Effective sample size'': A practical diagnostic. When ESS drops below $N/2$, importance sampling estimates become unreliable. For MCMC, ESS accounts for autocorrelation between successive samples.

''Bias vs. consistency'': Simple Monte Carlo estimators are unbiased. Self-normalized importance sampling (dividing by $\sum w_i$) introduces a small bias of order $O(1/N)$ but is often more stable in practice.

''Coupling and perfect simulation'': Propp and Wilson's ''coupling from the past'' algorithm produces exact samples from a Markov chain's stationary distribution, eliminating the need to worry about burn-in — though it requires a monotone coupling structure.
