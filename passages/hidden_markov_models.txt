! Hidden Markov Models
[img[assets/hidden_markov_models.svg]]

Imagine you are standing outside a room where a friend is flipping between different activities — reading, cooking, or exercising — but you cannot see them. All you can hear are the sounds coming through the door: page turning, pot clanging, or heavy breathing. From those sounds alone, you try to figure out what your friend is //actually doing// at each moment. That is a ''Hidden Markov Model'' (HMM): a system where the true states are invisible, but each state produces observable clues according to some probability, and the states themselves shift over time following their own probabilistic rules.

HMMs were developed in the late 1960s and became the backbone of speech recognition, gene finding, and part-of-speech tagging for decades. They remain one of the most elegant examples of how probability theory handles sequences of uncertain information.

!! The Core Insight

The key idea behind an HMM is a clean separation between two layers of randomness. There is a ''hidden process'' — a <<link "Markov chain" "Markov Chains">><</link>> of states you never observe directly — and an ''observation process'' where each hidden state emits a visible symbol according to its own probability distribution. Because the hidden states follow the ''Markov property'' (the next state depends only on the current state, not the full history), the entire model is tractable despite the uncertainty. You can think of it as a two-tier probabilistic machine: the top tier drives the dynamics, and the bottom tier generates the data you actually see.

!! The Three Problems: Evaluation, Decoding, Learning

Every HMM practitioner faces three fundamental problems, each with its own classic algorithm:

''1. Evaluation (Likelihood):'' Given a model and an observation sequence, what is the probability that the model produced those observations? This is solved by the ''Forward Algorithm'', a <<link "dynamic programming" "Dynamic Programming">><</link>> method that sums over all possible state paths efficiently.

''2. Decoding (Best State Sequence):'' Given a model and observations, what is the single most likely sequence of hidden states? This is solved by the ''<<link "Viterbi Algorithm" "Viterbi Algorithm">><</link>>'', which finds the optimal path through the state trellis using dynamic programming.

''3. Learning (Parameter Estimation):'' Given observations but unknown model parameters, how do you learn the transition and emission probabilities? This is solved by the ''Baum-Welch Algorithm'', a special case of <<link "Expectation Maximization" "Expectation Maximization">><</link>> that iteratively refines parameters until convergence.

These three problems and their solutions are sometimes called the "holy trinity" of HMMs.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

An HMM is defined by five components: a set of $N$ hidden states, a set of $M$ observation symbols, a ''transition matrix'' $A$, an ''emission matrix'' $B$, and an ''initial state distribution'' $\pi$.

The transition probability from state $i$ to state $j$ is:

$$a_{ij} = P(q_{t+1} = S_j \mid q_t = S_i)$$

The emission probability of observing symbol $v_k$ from state $j$ is:

$$b_j(k) = P(O_t = v_k \mid q_t = S_j)$$

The ''Forward Algorithm'' computes the forward variable $\alpha_t(i)$, the probability of seeing observations $O_1, O_2, \ldots, O_t$ and being in state $S_i$ at time $t$:

$$\alpha_1(i) = \pi_i \cdot b_i(O_1)$$

$$\alpha_{t+1}(j) = \left[\sum_{i=1}^{N} \alpha_t(i) \cdot a_{ij}\right] \cdot b_j(O_{t+1})$$

The total likelihood of the observation sequence is then:

$$P(O \mid \lambda) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \alpha_T(i)$$

The ''Viterbi Algorithm'' replaces the sum with a max to find the most probable state path:

$$\delta_{t+1}(j) = \max_{i} \left[\delta_t(i) \cdot a_{ij}\right] \cdot b_j(O_{t+1})$$

The ''Baum-Welch Algorithm'' uses both forward ($\alpha$) and backward ($\beta$) variables to compute expected transition and emission counts, then re-estimates:

$$\hat{a}_{ij} = \frac{\sum_{t=1}^{T-1} \xi_t(i,j)}{\sum_{t=1}^{T-1} \gamma_t(i)}$$

where $\gamma_t(i)$ is the probability of being in state $i$ at time $t$, and $\xi_t(i,j)$ is the probability of transitioning from $i$ to $j$ at time $t$.

!! HMMs in Practice

HMMs have been workhorses across many fields:

''Speech Recognition:'' For decades, HMMs modeled phoneme sequences. Each hidden state represented a phoneme or sub-phoneme, and observations were acoustic feature vectors. Systems like Sphinx and HTK built entire speech pipelines around HMMs.

''Bioinformatics:'' Profile HMMs identify protein families by modeling conserved sequence patterns. Gene-finding tools like GENSCAN use HMMs to locate exons, introns, and regulatory regions in DNA.

''Part-of-Speech Tagging:'' In natural language processing, HMMs tag words with grammatical categories (noun, verb, adjective) by treating tags as hidden states and words as observations.

''Finance:'' Regime-switching HMMs model market states (bull, bear, sideways) where each regime has different return distributions.

HMMs connect naturally to the broader world of <<link "probabilistic graphical models" "Probabilistic Graphical Models">><</link>>, where they appear as the simplest chain-structured directed model.

!! When HMMs Break Down

''1. The Markov assumption is too restrictive.'' Real sequences often have long-range dependencies — a word in a sentence may depend on context from many positions back, not just the immediately preceding state. When the single-step memory of HMMs is insufficient, models like LSTMs or Transformers that capture long-range context will outperform them dramatically.

''2. Discrete emission assumptions fail on complex data.'' Standard HMMs emit from a finite symbol set or a simple Gaussian. When observations are high-dimensional and have intricate structure (images, raw audio waveforms), the emission model cannot capture the richness of the data. Gaussian Mixture HMMs help somewhat, but deep generative models have largely replaced HMMs for complex observation spaces.

''3. Baum-Welch converges to local optima.'' The EM-based learning algorithm is only guaranteed to find a //local// maximum of the likelihood, not the global one. With poor initialization, the learned model may be far from optimal. Multiple random restarts or informed initialization (e.g., from domain knowledge) are necessary but not guaranteed to solve the problem.

!! Technical Details

An HMM with $N$ states and $M$ observation symbols has $N^2 + N \cdot M - 1$ free parameters (accounting for row-stochastic constraints on $A$, $B$, and $\pi$). The Forward and Viterbi algorithms both run in $O(N^2 T)$ time, where $T$ is the sequence length — a massive improvement over the naive $O(N^T)$ enumeration of all state paths. The Backward algorithm, used in Baum-Welch, has the same $O(N^2 T)$ complexity.

''Variants'' include ''left-right HMMs'' (states can only move forward, common in speech), ''ergodic HMMs'' (any state reachable from any other), ''hierarchical HMMs'' (states themselves contain sub-HMMs), and ''input-output HMMs'' where transitions depend on external input. ''Continuous-density HMMs'' replace discrete emission tables with Gaussian mixture models. The ''factorial HMM'' uses multiple independent hidden chains to model different factors simultaneously.

HMMs are special cases of ''state-space models'': when states and emissions are both continuous and linear with Gaussian noise, the HMM reduces to a Kalman filter. When the hidden structure is a general directed acyclic graph rather than a chain, you get a ''Bayesian network''.

The ''spectral learning'' method (Hsu, Kakade, Zhang 2009) provides a consistent, non-iterative alternative to Baum-Welch using observable operator models and singular value decomposition, avoiding the local-optima problem entirely.
