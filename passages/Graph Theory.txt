! Graph Theory

<img src="assets/graph_theory.svg" alt="Graph Theory" width="800">

Imagine a city''s subway map: stations are dots, and the lines connecting them are routes you can travel. You don''t care about the exact geography—only which stations connect to which. That stripped-down picture of //connections// is exactly what a ''graph'' is. Graph theory is the mathematics of relationships, giving us a universal language for anything that can be described as "things linked to other things"—from friendships on social media to atoms in a molecule to neurons in a brain.

!! The Core Insight

A graph captures the //structure of relationships// while discarding everything else. Two systems that look completely different on the surface—say, a power grid and a food web—can have identical graph structures, meaning the same theorems and algorithms apply to both. This abstraction is what makes graph theory so powerful: once you model a problem as vertices and edges, centuries of mathematical results become available to you instantly.

!! Graphs as Universal Models

A ''graph'' $G = (V, E)$ consists of a set of ''vertices'' (also called //nodes//) $V$ and a set of ''edges'' $E$ connecting pairs of vertices.

In an ''undirected graph'', edges have no direction—if vertex $A$ connects to vertex $B$, then $B$ also connects to $A$. Think of a handshake: it is mutual. In a ''directed graph'' (or //digraph//), each edge has a direction, like a one-way street. A ''weighted graph'' assigns a numerical value to each edge, representing cost, distance, capacity, or strength.

The ''degree'' of a vertex is the number of edges touching it. In directed graphs, we distinguish ''in-degree'' (edges arriving) from ''out-degree'' (edges leaving).

A ''path'' is a sequence of vertices connected by edges with no repeated vertices. A ''cycle'' is a path that returns to its starting vertex. A graph is ''connected'' if there exists a path between every pair of vertices.

Key structures include ''trees'' (connected graphs with no cycles), ''bipartite graphs'' (vertices split into two groups with edges only between groups—see [[Bipartite Graphs|Bipartite Graphs]]), and ''complete graphs'' $K_n$ where every pair of vertices is connected. These ideas extend naturally into [[Network Theory|Network Theory]] and [[Combinatorics|Combinatorics]].

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ''adjacency matrix'' $A$ of a graph with $n$ vertices is an $n \times n$ matrix where:

$$A_{ij} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if there is an edge between } v_i \text{ and } v_j \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

For weighted graphs, $A_{ij}$ equals the edge weight instead of 1. The ''degree matrix'' $D$ is a diagonal matrix where $D_{ii} = \deg(v_i)$.

The ''graph Laplacian'' is defined as:

$$L = D - A$$

This matrix is central to [[Graph Laplacian|Graph Laplacian]] analysis and [[Spectral Graph Theory|Spectral Graph Theory]]—its eigenvalues reveal deep structural properties like the number of connected components (the multiplicity of eigenvalue 0) and how well-connected the graph is.

The ''degree sequence'' is the sorted list of vertex degrees. A fundamental result, the ''Handshaking Lemma'', states:

$$\sum_{v \in V} \deg(v) = 2|E|$$

Every edge contributes exactly 2 to the total degree count—one for each endpoint. This means the number of odd-degree vertices is always even.

For ''planar graphs'' (graphs drawable on a plane without edge crossings), ''Euler''s formula'' relates vertices, edges, and faces:

$$V - E + F = 2$$

This elegant identity constrains planar graph structure and proves, for example, that $K_5$ and $K_{3,3}$ are non-planar.

!! Graphs in Machine Learning

''[[Graph Neural Networks|Graph Neural Networks]]'' (GNNs) extend deep learning to graph-structured data. In a GNN, each node aggregates information from its neighbors through learned functions, producing node embeddings that capture local and global structure. The message-passing update at layer $k$ is:

$$h_v^{(k)} = \text{UPDATE}\left(h_v^{(k-1)}, \text{AGGREGATE}\left(\{h_u^{(k-1)} : u \in \mathcal{N}(v)\}\right)\right)$$

''Knowledge graphs'' represent facts as (subject, predicate, object) triples—essentially directed labeled graphs—powering question answering and recommendation systems. ''Social network analysis'' uses graph metrics like betweenness centrality, clustering coefficients, and community detection to understand human behavior. ''Molecular graphs'' encode atoms as vertices and bonds as edges, letting GNNs predict chemical properties directly from structure.

Graph-based methods also appear in [[Graph Coloring|Graph Coloring]] for scheduling and register allocation, shortest-path algorithms for navigation, and flow networks for optimization.

!! When Graph Models Break Down

* ''Scalability walls.'' Real-world graphs like the web or social networks have billions of vertices and edges. Many classical graph algorithms are polynomial but still too slow at this scale, and storing the full adjacency matrix ($O(n^2)$) becomes impossible. Sparse representations and approximate algorithms are essential but sacrifice exactness.

* ''Expressiveness limits of GNNs.'' Standard message-passing GNNs cannot distinguish certain non-isomorphic graphs—they are bounded by the Weisfeiler-Leman graph isomorphism test. This means structurally different graphs can produce identical embeddings, limiting what GNNs can learn without architectural extensions like higher-order message passing.

* ''Static structure assumption.'' Most graph models assume a fixed topology, but many real systems—contact networks, financial transaction graphs, evolving knowledge bases—change over time. Temporal and dynamic graph models exist but add substantial complexity and remain an active research frontier.

!! Technical Details

''Graph isomorphism'' asks whether two graphs are structurally identical (a bijection between vertex sets that preserves adjacency). No polynomial-time algorithm is known in general, though Babai (2016) showed it is solvable in quasipolynomial time. ''Planarity'' can be tested in linear time using the Hopcroft-Tarjan algorithm.

''Treewidth'' measures how "tree-like" a graph is. Many NP-hard problems become polynomial on graphs of bounded treewidth via dynamic programming on tree decompositions—a key insight in parameterized complexity.

The ''spectral gap'' is the difference between the two largest eigenvalues of the adjacency matrix (or the smallest nonzero eigenvalue of the Laplacian). A large spectral gap indicates strong connectivity and rapid mixing of random walks, which connects to expander graphs and the convergence of PageRank.

''Ramsey numbers'' $R(s, t)$ answer: what is the minimum number of vertices guaranteeing that any 2-coloring of the edges contains a monochromatic $K_s$ or $K_t$? Despite decades of effort, only a handful of exact Ramsey numbers are known—$R(3,3) = 6$ is classic, but $R(5,5)$ remains open. Erdős famously suggested that computing $R(6,6)$ would be harder than fighting off an alien invasion.
