! Moment Generating Functions

<img src="assets/moment_generating_functions.svg" alt="Moment Generating Functions" width="800">

Imagine a locked box that contains every statistical fact about a random variable — its average, its spread, how lopsided it is, how fat its tails are. The ''moment generating function'' (MGF) is the key to that box. You turn the key (differentiate) once and get the mean; turn it again to get the second moment; keep turning and every moment spills out in order. What makes this remarkable is that a single, compact formula — $M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}]$ — //packages infinite information// about a distribution into one analytic object. Rather than computing each moment separately, you build the MGF once and extract whatever you need by evaluating derivatives at $t = 0$.

!! The Core Insight

The exponential function $e^{tX}$ acts as a ''universal encoder'' for the moments of $X$. Expanding the exponential as a Taylor series reveals why:

$$M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}] = E\!\left[\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(tX)^k}{k!}\right] = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{E[X^k]}{k!}\, t^k$$

Each coefficient of $t^k/k!$ is exactly the $k$-th moment $E[X^k]$. Differentiating $k$ times and setting $t=0$ isolates it: $M_X^{(k)}(0) = E[X^k]$. The MGF is therefore a //generating function for the moment sequence// — hence the name. The deep payoff is that complicated probabilistic operations (summing independent variables, proving convergence theorems) become simple algebra on the MGF.

!! Encoding All Moments

''One function, infinite data.'' The MGF literally stores the entire moment sequence $\{E[X^k]\}_{k=0}^{\infty}$ as Taylor coefficients. No finite collection of moments can achieve this; the MGF captures all of them simultaneously.

''The uniqueness theorem.'' If $M_X(t)$ exists and is finite in some open interval $(-\delta, \delta)$ around zero, then it //uniquely determines// the distribution of $X$. Two distributions with the same MGF in a neighborhood of zero must be identical. This is what makes MGF-based proofs rigorous — matching MGFs is equivalent to matching distributions.

''From moments to variance.'' Once you have $E[X]$ and $E[X^2]$, variance follows immediately: $\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 = M''(0) - (M'(0))^2$. Higher central moments and standardized quantities (skewness, kurtosis) are similarly extracted by combining raw moments from the MGF.

''Cumulant generating function.'' Taking the logarithm $K(t) = \log M(t)$ yields the ''cumulant generating function'' (CGF), whose derivatives at zero give //cumulants// rather than moments. Cumulants have a crucial advantage: for independent random variables, cumulants simply //add//. The first cumulant is the mean, the second is the variance, and all cumulants of order $\geq 3$ vanish if and only if the distribution is [[Gaussian Distribution|Gaussian Distribution]].

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''Definition and moment extraction:''

$$M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{tx}\, f_X(x)\, dx$$

Differentiating under the integral sign:

$$M_X^{(k)}(t) = E[X^k e^{tX}] \quad \Rightarrow \quad M_X^{(k)}(0) = E[X^k]$$

''MGF of a sum equals the product of MGFs.'' If $X$ and $Y$ are independent [[Random Variables|Random Variables]]:

$$M_{X+Y}(t) = E[e^{t(X+Y)}] = E[e^{tX}]\cdot E[e^{tY}] = M_X(t)\cdot M_Y(t)$$

This product rule extends to any finite sum of independent variables. It is the workhorse behind proofs involving sums of independent observations.

''Gaussian MGF:'' For $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$:

$$M_X(t) = \exp\!\left(\mu t + \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2}\right)$$

Differentiating: $M'(0) = \mu$, $M''(0) = \mu^2 + \sigma^2$, confirming $\text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2$.

''Poisson MGF:'' For $X \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$:

$$M_X(t) = \exp\!\left(\lambda(e^t - 1)\right)$$

$M'(0) = \lambda$, $M''(0) = \lambda^2 + \lambda$, giving $\text{Var}(X) = \lambda$.

''Bernoulli MGF:'' For $X \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$:

$$M_X(t) = 1 - p + pe^t$$

$M'(0) = p$, $M''(0) = p$, giving $\text{Var}(X) = p - p^2 = p(1-p)$.

!! MGFs in ML and Statistics

''Proving the Central Limit Theorem.'' The cleanest proof of the [[Central Limit Theorem|Central Limit Theorem]] uses MGFs. Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be iid with mean $\mu$, variance $\sigma^2$, and MGF $M(t)$. The standardized sum $Z_n = \frac{\sum X_i - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}$ has MGF:

$$M_{Z_n}(t) = \left[M\!\left(\frac{t}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}\right) \exp\!\left(\frac{-\mu t}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}\right)\right]^n \xrightarrow{n \to \infty} e^{t^2/2}$$

The limit is the standard Gaussian MGF, so $Z_n \to \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ in distribution.

''Chernoff bound via MGF.'' The ''Chernoff bound'' uses the MGF to obtain exponentially tight tail bounds. For any $t > 0$:

$$P(X \geq a) = P(e^{tX} \geq e^{ta}) \leq \frac{E[e^{tX}]}{e^{ta}} = \frac{M_X(t)}{e^{ta}}$$

Optimizing over $t$ gives the tightest possible exponential bound. This technique underpins many [[Concentration Inequalities|Concentration Inequalities]] — Hoeffding's inequality, Bennett's inequality, and Bernstein's inequality are all derived by bounding the MGF of centered random variables.

''Exponential families.'' In [[Exponential Family|Exponential Family]] distributions $p(x|\theta) = h(x)\exp(\eta \cdot T(x) - A(\theta))$, the log-partition function $A(\theta)$ is exactly the CGF of the sufficient statistic $T(x)$. Derivatives of $A$ yield the expected sufficient statistics and their covariance, making the MGF/CGF the backbone of exponential family theory.

''Moment matching in variational inference.'' Some approximate inference methods fit parameters by matching moments derived from the MGF to empirical moments, providing tractable approximations to intractable posteriors.

!! When MGFs Don't Exist

''Heavy-tailed distributions.'' The Cauchy distribution has density $f(x) = 1/(\pi(1+x^2))$, whose tails decay as $1/|x|^2$ — too slowly for $E[e^{tX}]$ to be finite for any $t \neq 0$. More generally, any distribution whose tails are not dominated by an exponential (Pareto, stable distributions with $\alpha < 2$, some log-normal regimes) has no MGF. In these cases, the //characteristic function// $\varphi(t) = E[e^{itX}]$ must be used instead, since $|e^{itX}| = 1$ guarantees it always exists.

''Moment indeterminacy.'' Even when all individual moments $E[X^k]$ exist, the moment sequence may fail to //uniquely determine// the distribution. The log-normal distribution provides the classic counterexample: infinitely many distinct distributions share its moment sequence. This is the ''Hamburger moment problem''. Carleman's condition — $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \mu_{2n}^{-1/(2n)} = \infty$ — guarantees moment determinacy when satisfied, but when it fails, no amount of moment matching can pin down the distribution.

''Multivariate complications.'' For a random vector $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^d$, the joint MGF $M(\mathbf{t}) = E[e^{\mathbf{t}^\top \mathbf{X}}]$ must exist in a neighborhood of $\mathbf{0} \in \mathbb{R}^d$. The domain of convergence becomes a convex region (not necessarily all of $\mathbb{R}^d$), and the joint moment/cumulant structure encodes not just marginal moments but all cross-moments and higher-order dependencies. Extracting useful information requires partial derivatives $\frac{\partial^{j+k}}{\partial t_1^j \partial t_2^k} M(\mathbf{0})$, and computational complexity grows rapidly with dimension.

!! Technical Details

''Characteristic functions as the universal alternative.'' The ''characteristic function'' $\varphi_X(t) = E[e^{itX}]$ always exists for any real-valued random variable because $|e^{itX}| = 1$. It is the Fourier transform of the distribution and uniquely determines it via the inversion formula. Lévy's continuity theorem — if $\varphi_n(t) \to \varphi(t)$ pointwise with $\varphi$ continuous at 0, then $X_n \to X$ in distribution — is the rigorous foundation for CLT proofs when the MGF may not exist.

''Cumulant generating function.'' The CGF $K(t) = \log M(t)$ has derivatives giving cumulants: $\kappa_1 = \mu$, $\kappa_2 = \sigma^2$, $\kappa_3$ relates to skewness, $\kappa_4$ to excess kurtosis. For independent variables, $K_{X+Y}(t) = K_X(t) + K_Y(t)$, so cumulants are additive — a property moments do not share. The Gaussian is characterized by $K(t) = \mu t + \sigma^2 t^2/2$ (all $\kappa_n = 0$ for $n \geq 3$).

''Laplace transform connection.'' For non-negative random variables, $M_X(t) = \mathcal{L}\{f_X\}(-t)$, where $\mathcal{L}$ denotes the Laplace transform. This connects MGF theory to the vast machinery of Laplace transform analysis — useful for survival analysis, queueing theory, and reliability engineering.

''Saddlepoint approximation.'' For sums $S_n = \sum X_i$, the saddlepoint approximation $\hat{f}(x) \propto \exp(n K(\hat{t}) - \hat{t} x)$ (where $K'(\hat{t}) = x/n$) gives exponentially accurate density approximations, far more precise than CLT-based Gaussian approximations, especially in the tails.
