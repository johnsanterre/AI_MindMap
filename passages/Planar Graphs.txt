! Planar Graphs

<img src="assets/planar_graphs.svg" alt="Diagram comparing a planar graph embedding with non-planar K5 and K3,3 graphs showing Kuratowski's theorem" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine drawing a subway map where no two lines cross except at actual stations. If you can do it, your network is ''planar'' — it can be laid flat on a sheet of paper with no edge crossings. This seemingly simple geometric constraint turns out to have deep mathematical consequences: it controls how many edges a graph can have, how many colours you need to colour a map, and whether a circuit board can be manufactured on a single layer. Planarity is one of the oldest questions in [[Graph Theory|Graph Theory]], and its modern applications stretch from chip design to mesh-based physics simulations to specialised graph neural networks.

!! The Core Insight

A graph is ''planar'' if it can be drawn in the plane so that its edges meet only at their endpoints — no crossings. Such a crossing-free drawing is called a ''planar embedding''. The same graph may have many different planar embeddings (think of rearranging the subway map without introducing crossings), but the key topological fact is binary: either //at least one// crossing-free drawing exists, or //none// does.

The central result is ''Euler's formula for planar graphs'': for any connected planar graph with $V$ vertices, $E$ edges, and $F$ faces (regions bounded by edges, including the outer infinite face),

$$V - E + F = 2$$

This deceptively simple equation has enormous power. It immediately implies that a simple planar graph satisfies $E \leq 3V - 6$, which means planar graphs are ''sparse'' — a planar graph on $n$ vertices can have at most $O(n)$ edges, never $O(n^2)$. This sparsity is precisely why many algorithms run faster on planar graphs than on general graphs.

!! Forbidden Subgraphs and Kuratowski's Theorem

How do you tell if a graph is //not// planar? ''Kuratowski's theorem'' (1930) gives a complete characterisation: a graph is planar if and only if it contains no ''subdivision'' of $K_5$ (the complete graph on 5 vertices) or $K_{3,3}$ (the complete bipartite graph with parts of size 3).

$K_5$ has 5 vertices and 10 edges — since $10 > 3(5) - 6 = 9$, it violates Euler's bound and cannot be planar. $K_{3,3}$ has 6 vertices and 9 edges — it satisfies $E \leq 3V - 6 = 12$, but it still fails planarity because it has no triangles, so the tighter bound $E \leq 2V - 4$ applies, and $9 > 2(6) - 4 = 8$.

The equivalent ''Wagner's theorem'' uses ''graph minors'' instead of subdivisions: a graph is planar if and only if it has no $K_5$ or $K_{3,3}$ //minor//. This connects to the deep Robertson-Seymour theorem, which shows that //every// minor-closed graph property can be characterised by a finite set of forbidden minors.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Euler's formula arises from a topological invariant — the ''Euler characteristic'' $\chi$ of the surface on which the graph is embedded. For the plane (or equivalently the sphere), $\chi = 2$:

$$V - E + F = \chi = 2$$

For a graph embedded on a surface of genus $g$ (a surface with $g$ handles, like a torus with $g = 1$), the generalised formula is:

$$V - E + F = 2 - 2g$$

This means graphs that are not planar may still embed on higher-genus surfaces. The ''genus'' of a graph is the minimum $g$ such that it can be embedded on a surface of genus $g$ without crossings. For $K_5$, the genus is 1 — it embeds on a torus.

The ''dual graph'' $G^*$ of a planar graph $G$ places a vertex in each face of $G$ and connects two vertices whenever the corresponding faces share an edge. Euler's formula guarantees the dual is well-defined for planar embeddings. The dual satisfies:

$$V^* = F, \quad E^* = E, \quad F^* = V$$

so $V^* - E^* + F^* = F - E + V = 2$. Duality swaps ''cuts'' and ''cycles'': a minimum cut in $G$ corresponds to a shortest cycle in $G^*$, enabling efficient algorithms for maximum flow in planar networks via shortest-path computations in the dual.

For planarity testing, the key algorithmic insight is that an embedding can be constructed incrementally. The ''left-right planarity test'' (de Fraysseix and Rosenstiehl) performs a DFS and tracks constraints on which side of the DFS tree each back-edge must lie. This yields a linear-time $O(V + E)$ decision algorithm.

!! Connections to Colouring and ML

The ''four colour theorem'' states that every planar graph can be properly coloured with at most 4 colours — no two adjacent vertices share a colour. This is equivalent to saying every map can be coloured with 4 colours. Proved by Appel and Haken (1976) using computer-assisted case analysis, it remains one of the most famous results connecting planarity to [[Graph Coloring|Graph Coloring]].

In ML and engineering, planarity appears in several contexts:

''Circuit layout (VLSI design):'' a single-layer circuit board requires a planar interconnection graph. When the graph is non-planar, the designer must add vias (layer crossings), and minimising crossings is an NP-hard problem. Planarity testing determines whether a single-layer layout is even possible.

''Mesh generation for physics simulation:'' finite element methods discretise 2D domains into triangular or polygonal meshes, which are planar graphs. The quality of the mesh (element shape, degree distribution) directly affects simulation accuracy. Delaunay triangulations produce planar graphs with good angle properties.

''Planar graph neural networks:'' standard GNNs treat all graphs uniformly, but planar graphs have special structure that can be exploited. Architectures like ''Planar Graph Networks'' use face information from the planar embedding as additional features. Since faces are only defined for planar graphs, these models encode topological structure that generic message-passing GNNs cannot access. Dual-graph convolutions — applying message passing on $G^*$ alongside $G$ — capture complementary information.

''Planar separators:'' the ''planar separator theorem'' (Lipton-Tarjan 1979) guarantees that every planar graph on $n$ vertices has a balanced separator of size $O(\sqrt{n})$. This enables divide-and-conquer algorithms and is the foundation for efficient nested dissection solvers for sparse linear systems arising from planar meshes.

!! When Planar Graphs Break Down

''(1) Most real-world graphs are not planar:'' social networks, citation graphs, protein interaction networks, and knowledge graphs are almost never planar. They contain dense subgraphs, long-range connections, and high-degree hubs that inevitably produce $K_5$ or $K_{3,3}$ minors. Algorithms and theory designed for planar graphs simply do not apply to the graphs most commonly encountered in ML. The planar separator theorem, dual graph constructions, and efficient flow algorithms all fail on non-planar inputs.

''(2) Planar embeddings are not unique:'' a planar graph can have exponentially many distinct planar embeddings (different orderings of edges around each vertex). Algorithms that depend on the embedding — face enumeration, dual construction, planar GNN features — must choose a specific embedding, and different choices can yield different results. For 3-connected graphs, Whitney's theorem guarantees a unique embedding (up to reflection), but many graphs of interest are not 3-connected.

''(3) Near-planarity is hard to exploit:'' a graph that becomes planar after removing just one edge is "almost planar," but this near-miss does not unlock planar algorithms. There is no smooth degradation: the crossing number (minimum crossings in any drawing) can jump from 0 to 1, and suddenly Euler's formula, the separator theorem, and dual-graph techniques no longer apply. Bounded-genus and bounded-treewidth generalizations exist but are substantially more complex.

!! Technical Details

''Historical roots:'' Euler's formula appears in his 1758 work on polyhedra. Kuratowski published his planarity characterization in 1930. The four colour conjecture was posed by Guthrie in 1852 and proved by Appel and Haken in 1976 — the first major theorem proved with essential computer assistance. A simpler proof was given by Robertson, Sanders, Seymour, and Thomas in 1997.

''Planarity testing algorithms:'' the first linear-time algorithm was by Hopcroft and Tarjan (1974). Modern implementations use the left-right planarity test (de Fraysseix, de Mendez, Rosenstiehl 2006) or the Boyer-Myrvold algorithm (2004). Both run in $O(V + E)$ time and either confirm planarity with a valid embedding or output a $K_5$ or $K_{3,3}$ subdivision as a certificate of non-planarity.

''Crossing number:'' for non-planar graphs, the ''crossing number'' $\text{cr}(G)$ is the minimum number of edge crossings in any drawing. Computing $\text{cr}(G)$ is NP-hard. The crossing lemma gives a lower bound: for $E > 4V$, $\text{cr}(G) \geq \frac{E^3}{64V^2}$.

''Minor theory:'' the Robertson-Seymour theorem (proved over 1983–2004 in 23 papers) shows that graphs are ''well-quasi-ordered'' by the minor relation — every minor-closed property has a finite forbidden minor characterisation. Planarity (forbidden: $K_5$, $K_{3,3}$) is the simplest nontrivial example. Linkless embeddability requires the ''Petersen family'' of 7 forbidden minors.

''Planar GNNs:'' Bouritsas et al. (2022) showed that incorporating structural features from planar embeddings (face cycles, dual-graph adjacency) into GNN architectures improves expressiveness beyond the Weisfeiler-Leman hierarchy on planar graph benchmarks. This connects to broader work on making GNNs more powerful than 1-WL.
