! Variational Autoencoder
[img[assets/variational_autoencoder.svg]]

Imagine you're a portrait artist who has painted thousands of faces. Over time, you develop an intuitive sense of the "space of all faces" — you know that tweaking a few mental dials (face width, nose length, expression) can smoothly generate new, realistic faces you've never seen before. A ''Variational Autoencoder'' (VAE) does exactly this: it learns a compressed, continuous "sketch pad" of possibilities from data, then draws new samples from that pad. Unlike a plain <<link "autoencoder" "Autoencoders">><</link>>, which simply memorizes compressed codes, a VAE learns a //probability distribution// over codes, so it can generate entirely new data by sampling from that distribution.

!! The Core Insight

The breakthrough idea behind VAEs is treating the encoding process as //probabilistic//. Instead of mapping an input to a single point in <<link "latent space" "Latent Space">><</link>>, the encoder maps it to a //distribution// — specifically, a mean vector $\mu$ and a standard deviation vector $\sigma$. During training, you sample from this distribution to get a latent code $z$, then decode it back. This forces the network to spread its representations across a smooth, continuous region rather than memorizing isolated points. The genius is that this smoothness means nearby points in latent space decode to similar outputs, giving you a meaningful space you can interpolate through and sample from.

!! The Encoder-Decoder Framework

The architecture has two halves. The ''encoder'' $q_\phi(z|x)$ takes an input $x$ (an image, a sentence, a molecule) and produces two vectors: $\mu$ and $\sigma$. These parameterize a Gaussian distribution in latent space. The ''decoder'' $p_\theta(x|z)$ takes a sampled latent code $z$ and reconstructs the original input. The whole system trains end-to-end, but there's a problem: you can't backpropagate through a random sampling step. The ''reparameterization trick'' solves this elegantly — instead of sampling $z \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ directly, you sample noise $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ and compute $z = \mu + \sigma \odot \epsilon$. This moves the randomness outside the computational graph, letting gradients flow through $\mu$ and $\sigma$ as usual.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The VAE objective maximizes the ''Evidence Lower Bound'' (<<link "ELBO" "ELBO">><</link>>), a lower bound on the log-likelihood of the data:

$$\text{ELBO} = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] - D_{KL}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p(z))$$

The first term is the ''reconstruction loss'' — it measures how well the decoder recreates the input from the sampled latent code. In practice this is often a pixel-wise binary cross-entropy or MSE loss.

The second term is the <<link "KL divergence" "KL Divergence">><</link>> between the encoder's learned posterior $q_\phi(z|x)$ and the prior $p(z) = \mathcal{N}(0, I)$. This acts as a regularizer, pulling the encoder's distributions toward a standard normal. For Gaussian distributions, the KL term has a closed-form solution:

$$D_{KL} = -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{j=1}^{J} \left(1 + \log(\sigma_j^2) - \mu_j^2 - \sigma_j^2\right)$$

The tension between these two terms is fundamental: reconstruction wants to spread codes apart for precision, while the KL term wants to pack them into a standard normal for smooth generation. The balance between them determines whether the model prioritizes faithful reconstruction or clean generation.

!! VAE Variants

Several important extensions address VAE limitations:

''Beta-VAE'' introduces a hyperparameter $\beta$ weighting the KL term: $\mathcal{L} = \text{Reconstruction} - \beta \cdot D_{KL}$. Setting $\beta > 1$ encourages disentangled latent representations where individual dimensions correspond to interpretable factors of variation.

''VQ-VAE'' (Vector Quantized VAE) replaces the continuous latent space with a discrete codebook. The encoder maps to the nearest codebook vector, enabling sharper reconstructions and serving as the backbone for modern audio and image generation systems.

''Conditional VAE'' (CVAE) conditions both encoder and decoder on a label $y$, learning class-specific latent spaces: $q_\phi(z|x,y)$ and $p_\theta(x|z,y)$. This enables controlled generation — "generate a face with glasses" or "generate a molecule with high solubility."

''Hierarchical VAEs'' stack multiple layers of latent variables $z_1, z_2, \ldots, z_L$, where each level captures structure at a different scale. Modern hierarchical VAEs like NVAE achieve image quality competitive with GANs.

VAEs also served as a key stepping stone toward <<link "diffusion models" "Diffusion Models">><</link>>, which can be understood as hierarchical VAEs with a fixed encoder process.

!! When VAEs Break Down

''1. Posterior collapse.'' The decoder becomes so powerful it ignores the latent code entirely, and the KL term drives $q_\phi(z|x)$ to match the prior exactly. The latent space becomes meaningless — the model degenerates into a standard autoregressive model. This is especially common with expressive decoders like LSTMs or Transformers. Mitigation strategies include KL annealing (gradually increasing the KL weight during training), free bits (setting a minimum information threshold per latent dimension), and using weaker decoders.

''2. Blurry outputs.'' Because the decoder must cover the full distribution $p_\theta(x|z)$ and typically uses a Gaussian likelihood, it tends to output the //mean// of all plausible reconstructions rather than a single sharp sample. This "hedging its bets" behavior produces blurry images — a fundamental limitation of the pixel-wise reconstruction loss. VQ-VAEs and adversarial training (VAE-GAN hybrids) partially address this.

''3. Over-regularization from the KL term.'' When $\beta$ or the KL weight is too high, the model compresses too aggressively. Latent codes become nearly identical for different inputs, and the decoder produces generic, low-quality outputs. The model generates "average" samples that lack detail or specificity. Tuning the reconstruction-KL balance is critical and often dataset-specific.

!! Technical Details

* ''Architecture:'' Encoder and decoder are typically symmetric CNNs (for images) or MLPs (for tabular data). Latent dimensions typically range from 2 (for visualization) to 512 (for complex data).

* ''Prior:'' Standard choice is $p(z) = \mathcal{N}(0, I)$, but richer priors (mixture of Gaussians, autoregressive flows, VampPrior) improve expressiveness.

* ''Training:'' Adam optimizer, learning rate $\sim 10^{-3}$ to $10^{-4}$. KL annealing over first 10–50 epochs is common practice. Batch sizes of 64–256.

* ''ELBO is a lower bound:'' The gap between ELBO and true log-likelihood equals $D_{KL}(q_\phi(z|x) \| p_\theta(z|x))$ — the mismatch between the approximate and true posterior. Better encoders tighten this bound.

* ''Latent traversals:'' To interpret what the model learned, fix all but one latent dimension and sweep it across a range. In disentangled models, each dimension controls a single factor (e.g., rotation, color, scale).

* ''Interpolation:'' Encode two inputs to $z_1, z_2$, then decode convex combinations $\alpha z_1 + (1 - \alpha) z_2$. Smooth transitions indicate a well-structured latent space.

* ''FID and reconstruction metrics:'' Generation quality is measured by Frechet Inception Distance; reconstruction quality by MSE or SSIM. VAEs typically achieve FID 50–150 on CIFAR-10 (vs. GANs at 10–30), but offer stable training and meaningful latent spaces.

* ''Seminal paper:'' Kingma & Welling, "Auto-Encoding Variational Bayes" (2013). Concurrent independent work by Rezende et al., "Stochastic Backpropagation and Approximate Inference in Deep Generative Models."
