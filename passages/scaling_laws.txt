! Scaling Laws

<img src="assets/scaling_laws.svg" alt="Diagram illustrating power-law scaling relationships between model size, data, compute, and loss" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are filling a bathtub with three faucets: one controls how big the tub is, one controls how much water you pour in, and one controls how fast you pour. If any single faucet is barely open while the others are wide open, the tub fills inefficiently â€” water spills over one edge while the other sides stay dry. ''Scaling laws'' are the empirical discovery that deep learning works the same way. Model performance (measured as loss) improves as a smooth, predictable power law in three resources â€” parameter count, training data, and compute â€” but only when all three grow in coordinated proportion. Crank up parameters without enough data and you waste compute. Flood the model with data it is too small to absorb and you waste tokens. The scaling laws tell you exactly how to turn all three faucets in harmony.

---

!! The Core Insight

In 2020, Kaplan et al. at OpenAI published //Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models//, showing that the cross-entropy loss of a <<link "Transformers" "Transformers">><</link>> language model follows clean power laws when plotted against model size $N$ (number of non-embedding parameters), dataset size $D$ (number of tokens), or compute budget $C$ (FLOPs). On a log-log plot, loss versus each resource traces a straight line â€” the signature of a power law. These relationships hold across seven orders of magnitude, from models with thousands of parameters to models with billions.

The critical observation was that these power laws are ''smooth and predictable''. There are no sudden jumps, plateaus, or diminishing returns at any scale tested. This means you can train a small model cheaply, measure where it falls on the scaling curve, and extrapolate to predict how a 100x larger model will perform â€” before spending the compute to train it. This predictability transformed large-scale model development from guesswork into engineering.

Two years later, Hoffmann et al. at DeepMind published the ''Chinchilla'' paper, which revised the Kaplan findings. Kaplan's original work suggested that model size should grow faster than dataset size â€” roughly, scaling parameters yields more bang per FLOP than scaling data. Chinchilla showed this was wrong: the compute-optimal frontier requires scaling parameters and data //equally//. A 70B-parameter model trained on 1.4 trillion tokens (Chinchilla) outperformed a 280B-parameter model trained on 300 billion tokens (Gopher), despite using the same compute budget. The lesson: most large models before 2022 were ''undertrained'' â€” too many parameters, too few tokens.

---

!! The Three Scaling Dimensions

''Parameter scaling'' ($N$): Holding data and compute fixed, increasing model size reduces loss as a power law $L(N) \propto N^{-\alpha_N}$. Kaplan estimated $\alpha_N \approx 0.076$ for language models. This means each 10x increase in parameters yields a roughly constant reduction in log-loss. The gains are real but expensive â€” a 10x larger model requires roughly 10x more memory and, depending on the training regime, 10x or more compute.

''Data scaling'' ($D$): Holding model size fixed, increasing the number of training tokens reduces loss as $L(D) \propto D^{-\alpha_D}$. Kaplan estimated $\alpha_D \approx 0.095$. The Chinchilla revision showed this exponent was underestimated because Kaplan's experiments used learning rate schedules tuned for fixed compute rather than fixed data. When training is done properly, data scaling is just as powerful as parameter scaling â€” a finding that launched the era of massive dataset curation.

''Compute scaling'' ($C$): The total compute budget $C \approx 6ND$ (for a transformer, roughly 6 FLOPs per parameter per token) governs the overall efficiency. Given a fixed compute budget, there is a specific $(N^*, D^*)$ pair that minimizes loss. Deviating from this optimum â€” by making the model too large or too small for the available data â€” wastes compute. The Chinchilla-optimal allocation roughly doubles both $N$ and $D$ for every 4x increase in compute.

These three dimensions interact. The ''irreducible loss'' $L_\infty$ represents the entropy of the data distribution itself â€” no model, however large, can beat it. The scaling laws describe the ''reducible loss'' $L - L_\infty$, which is the gap between current performance and perfection. Each resource (parameters, data, compute) chips away at this gap, but with diminishing returns governed by the power-law exponents.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The Kaplan scaling law for model size takes the form:

$$L(N) = \left(\frac{N_c}{N}\right)^{\alpha_N} + L_\infty$$

where $N_c$ is a characteristic scale, $\alpha_N$ is the power-law exponent, and $L_\infty$ is the irreducible loss. Similarly for data:

$$L(D) = \left(\frac{D_c}{D}\right)^{\alpha_D} + L_\infty$$

When both $N$ and $D$ vary simultaneously, Hoffmann et al. proposed a joint parametric form:

$$L(N, D) = \frac{A}{N^{\alpha}} + \frac{B}{D^{\beta}} + L_\infty$$

where $A$, $B$, $\alpha$, $\beta$, and $L_\infty$ are fitted constants. The first term captures the approximation error (how well the model class can represent the true distribution), the second captures the estimation error (how much the finite training sample deviates from the population), and $L_\infty$ is the Bayes-optimal loss.

To find the ''compute-optimal'' allocation, fix $C = 6ND$ and minimize $L(N, D)$ subject to this constraint. Using Lagrange multipliers:

$$\frac{\partial L}{\partial N} = -\frac{A\alpha}{N^{\alpha+1}}, \quad \frac{\partial L}{\partial D} = -\frac{B\beta}{D^{\beta+1}}$$

Setting the ratio of marginal returns equal to the constraint gradient yields the optimal allocation $N^* \propto C^a$ and $D^* \propto C^b$ where $a + b = 1$. Chinchilla found $a \approx b \approx 0.5$, meaning parameters and data should scale equally â€” in contrast to Kaplan's original finding that $a \approx 0.73$. The practical upshot: for a given FLOP budget, the compute-optimal model is ''smaller and trained longer'' than the Kaplan prescription suggested.

The power-law form itself is not derived from first principles â€” it is an empirical fit. However, theoretical work connecting <<link "Loss Functions" "Loss Functions">><</link>> on natural data to the structure of language has suggested that the power-law exponents may reflect the Zipfian distribution of natural language or the fractal-like structure of concept hierarchies in the training corpus.

---

!! Compute-Optimal Training in Practice

The Chinchilla result immediately changed how labs build models. Before Chinchilla, the prevailing wisdom (from the Kaplan paper) was to make models as large as possible for a given compute budget, even if this meant training on relatively few tokens. GPT-3 (175B parameters, 300B tokens) exemplified this approach. After Chinchilla, the industry shifted toward training smaller models on vastly more data: LLaMA (65B parameters, 1.4T tokens), and later LLaMA 2 (70B parameters, 2T tokens) followed the Chinchilla-optimal ratio.

The ''tokens-per-parameter'' rule of thumb from Chinchilla is roughly 20 tokens per parameter. A 7B model should see about 140B tokens; a 70B model about 1.4T tokens. Training beyond this ratio (''over-training'') can still improve performance, but with diminishing returns per FLOP. Some teams deliberately over-train smaller models because inference cost scales with $N$ but not $D$: a smaller model trained on extra data is cheaper to deploy than a larger compute-optimal model, even if the training itself is less FLOP-efficient.

Scaling laws also predict when to stop training. If you plot your training loss curve against the power-law prediction, any deviation signals a problem â€” a bug in the data pipeline, a <<link "Overfitting" "Overfitting">><</link>> model, or a learning rate that needs adjustment. Teams at <<link "Large Language Models" "Large Language Models">><</link>> labs routinely use scaling law extrapolations as a diagnostic tool: train a suite of small models, fit the scaling curve, and use it to predict the final loss of the large target model before committing to the full training run.

---

!! When Scaling Laws Break Down

''Task-specific emergence:'' Scaling laws predict //average loss// smoothly, but specific downstream capabilities can appear abruptly. A model may show zero performance on a task like multi-digit arithmetic until it crosses a critical size threshold, then jump to high accuracy. These <<link "Emergent behaviors" "Emergent behaviors">><</link>> mean that smooth loss improvement does not guarantee smooth capability improvement. You cannot reliably predict //when// a new capability will appear just by extrapolating the loss curve â€” the mapping from loss to task accuracy is nonlinear and task-dependent.

''Distribution shift and data quality:'' The scaling laws assume training data is drawn i.i.d. from a fixed distribution. In practice, data quality varies enormously â€” web scrapes contain duplicates, toxic content, and machine-generated text. Scaling data naively (by scraping more of the internet) often degrades data quality, changing the effective distribution and breaking the power-law fit. The scaling exponents measured on curated data do not transfer to uncurated data. Similarly, if the evaluation distribution differs from the training distribution, the loss predictions can be arbitrarily wrong. Scaling laws are //interpolation// tools within a fixed data regime, not extrapolation tools across regimes.

''Architecture and training recipe dependence:'' The power-law exponents are not universal constants â€” they depend on architecture (transformer vs. state-space model vs. mixture-of-experts), tokenization, optimizer, learning rate schedule, and batch size. A scaling law fitted on dense transformers does not predict the behavior of a mixture-of-experts model, because MoE models decouple parameter count from compute per token. New architectures or training innovations (like <<link "GPT Architecture" "GPT Architecture">><</link>> improvements in attention mechanisms) can shift the scaling frontier, meaning that a scaling law fitted today may underestimate performance achievable tomorrow with better methods.

---

!! Technical Details

''Key papers:'' Kaplan et al. (2020), //Scaling Laws for Neural Language Models// â€” established power-law relationships for transformer language models across $N$, $D$, and $C$, with exponents $\alpha_N \approx 0.076$, $\alpha_D \approx 0.095$, and $\alpha_C \approx 0.050$. Hoffmann et al. (2022), //Training Compute-Optimal Large Language Models// (Chinchilla) â€” revised the optimal $N$-to-$D$ ratio, showing equal scaling ($a \approx b \approx 0.5$) rather than Kaplan's parameter-heavy allocation. Henighan et al. (2020) extended scaling laws to other modalities (images, video, math, code). Clark et al. (2022) showed scaling laws for routing in mixture-of-experts models.

''The irreducible loss'' $L_\infty$ depends on the data distribution and tokenization. For natural language with byte-pair encoding, estimates range from roughly 1.0 to 1.7 nats depending on the corpus. This sets a hard floor: no amount of scaling can push loss below the entropy of the data-generating process.

''Broken neural scaling laws:'' Caballero et al. (2023) proposed fitting scaling curves with a sigmoidal functional form rather than pure power laws, capturing the eventual saturation as loss approaches $L_\infty$. This ''broken power law'' provides better extrapolation at the largest scales, where the pure power-law form systematically overestimates the remaining reducible loss.

''Scaling for downstream tasks:'' While pre-training loss follows clean power laws, downstream task performance (accuracy on benchmarks) does not. The relationship between loss and accuracy is typically sigmoidal: accuracy stays near random until loss drops below a task-specific threshold, then improves rapidly before saturating. This creates the appearance of <<link "Emergent behaviors" "Emergent behaviors">><</link>> even when the underlying loss curve is smooth. Wei et al. (2022) analyzed this phenomenon in //Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models//, while Schaeffer et al. (2023) argued that some apparent emergence is an artifact of the nonlinear metric, not the model's capabilities.

''Inference scaling:'' Recent work on <<link "Transformers" "Transformers">><</link>> has explored ''test-time compute scaling'' â€” spending more compute at inference (e.g., chain-of-thought, repeated sampling, search) to improve performance without retraining. This introduces a fourth scaling dimension orthogonal to the training-time scaling laws. The interaction between training-time and inference-time compute scaling is an active research frontier.

The <<link "Double Descent" "Double Descent">><</link>> phenomenon interacts with scaling laws in a subtle way: at any given model size, training too long can push through a double-descent spike before settling into a lower-loss regime. The Chinchilla-optimal stopping point avoids most double-descent effects because it terminates training well before the model memorizes the training set, but over-training regimes must account for these non-monotonic dynamics.
