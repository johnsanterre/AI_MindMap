! Probability Distributions
<img src="assets/probability_distributions.svg" alt="Family tree of probability distributions showing relationships from Bernoulli to Binomial to Normal, Exponential to Gamma, Beta to Dirichlet" style="max-width:100%;margin:1em 0;">

Imagine you run a factory that makes lightbulbs. You can't predict which //specific// bulb will fail, but you //can// say that about 3% fail per month, and failures arrive independently like raindrops. That single sentence — a rate, a shape, a story about independence — is a ''probability distribution''. It replaces the chaos of individual outcomes with a complete mathematical portrait: not just the average, but the spread, the skew, the tails, and every percentile in between. Every ML model, from [[Random Variables|random variable]] transformations to billion-parameter language models, ultimately outputs, consumes, or implicitly assumes a probability distribution. Choosing the right one is half the battle.

!! The Core Insight

A ''probability distribution'' assigns a probability (or probability density) to every possible outcome of a [[Random Variables|random variable]]. For ''discrete'' variables, a ''probability mass function'' (PMF) gives $P(X = x)$ directly. For ''continuous'' variables, a ''probability density function'' (PDF) $f(x)$ gives probability through integration: $P(a \le X \le b) = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx$. In both cases, the ''cumulative distribution function'' (CDF) $F(x) = P(X \le x)$ provides a unified view — it always exists, always increases from 0 to 1, and uniquely characterizes any distribution.

The reason we have a "zoo" of named distributions rather than one universal formula is that different //generative stories// — coin flips, waiting times, proportions, counts — produce different mathematical shapes. Each named distribution encodes a specific set of assumptions about how data is generated, and selecting a distribution is equivalent to stating those assumptions explicitly.

!! The Distribution Zoo

''Discrete distributions:''

''Bernoulli($p$):'' The simplest distribution — a single trial with two outcomes, success (1) with probability $p$ and failure (0) with probability $1-p$. Every [[Bernoulli Distribution|binary classifier's output]] is a Bernoulli random variable.

''Binomial($n, p$):'' The number of successes in $n$ independent Bernoulli trials. If you flip a coin 100 times, the count of heads follows $\text{Binomial}(100, 0.5)$. See [[Binomial Distribution]].

''Poisson($\lambda$):'' The count of rare, independent events in a fixed window — website clicks per minute, mutations per gene, typos per page. As a Binomial with $n \to \infty$ and $p \to 0$ while $np = \lambda$ stays fixed.

''Categorical($p_1, \ldots, p_k$):'' A single draw from $k$ categories — a generalization of Bernoulli to more than two outcomes. The output layer of a classifier with softmax produces a Categorical distribution.

''Multinomial($n, \mathbf{p}$):'' Counts from $n$ independent Categorical draws — the multi-class generalization of Binomial. The bag-of-words model treats a document as a Multinomial draw over the vocabulary.

''Continuous distributions:''

''Uniform($a, b$):'' Equal density everywhere on $[a, b]$. The maximum-entropy distribution when you know only the range. Used as an uninformative prior and as the starting point for inverse-CDF sampling.

''Gaussian (Normal) $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$:'' The bell curve — the most important distribution in all of statistics. Justified by the [[Central Limit Theorem]]: sums of many independent variables converge to it regardless of the original distribution. See [[Gaussian Distribution]].

''Exponential($\lambda$):'' The waiting time until the first event in a Poisson process. Memoryless: $P(X > s + t \mid X > s) = P(X > t)$. The only continuous memoryless distribution.

''Gamma($\alpha, \beta$):'' The waiting time for the $\alpha$-th event in a Poisson process — a sum of $\alpha$ independent Exponentials. When $\alpha$ is large, the Gamma approaches a Gaussian. Used as a prior for rate and precision parameters.

''Beta($\alpha, \beta$):'' A distribution //on probabilities// — values live in $[0, 1]$. The conjugate prior for [[Bernoulli Distribution|Bernoulli]] and [[Binomial Distribution|Binomial]] likelihoods, making Bayesian updating trivial: observe $s$ successes and $f$ failures, update $\text{Beta}(\alpha + s, \beta + f)$. See [[Beta Distribution]].

''Multivariate distributions:''

''Dirichlet($\boldsymbol{\alpha}$):'' A distribution over probability vectors (simplexes) — the multivariate generalization of Beta. The conjugate prior for Categorical and Multinomial likelihoods. Central to topic models like LDA. See [[Dirichlet Distribution]].

''Multivariate Normal $\mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})$:'' Joint Gaussian over $d$ dimensions, parameterized by a mean vector and covariance matrix. The foundation of [[Gaussian Processes|Gaussian process]] regression, Kalman filters, and VAE latent spaces.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''PMF (discrete):'' $P(X = x)$ directly, with $\sum_x P(X = x) = 1$.

''PDF (continuous):'' $f(x) \ge 0$ with $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x)\,dx = 1$. Note: $f(x)$ can exceed 1 — it is a //density//, not a probability.

''CDF (both):'' $F(x) = P(X \le x)$. For continuous variables, $F'(x) = f(x)$.

Key distribution formulas:

$$\text{Bernoulli: } P(X = k) = p^k (1-p)^{1-k}, \quad k \in \{0, 1\}$$

$$\text{Binomial: } P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$$

$$\text{Poisson: } P(X = k) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}$$

$$\text{Gaussian: } f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

$$\text{Exponential: } f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x \ge 0$$

$$\text{Gamma: } f(x) = \frac{\beta^\alpha}{\Gamma(\alpha)} x^{\alpha-1} e^{-\beta x}, \quad x \ge 0$$

$$\text{Beta: } f(p) = \frac{p^{\alpha-1}(1-p)^{\beta-1}}{B(\alpha, \beta)}, \quad 0 \le p \le 1$$

''Key relationships between distributions:''
* Bernoulli is Beta with $\alpha, \beta \in \{0,1\}$ degenerate — or equivalently, Binomial($1, p$).
* Binomial($n, p$) $\to$ Poisson($\lambda$) when $n \to \infty$, $p \to 0$, $np \to \lambda$.
* Binomial($n, p$) $\to$ $\mathcal{N}(np, np(1-p))$ when $n$ is large ([[Central Limit Theorem]]).
* Exponential($\lambda$) = Gamma($1, \lambda$).
* Sum of $k$ independent Exp($\lambda$) = Gamma($k, \lambda$).
* Beta is the univariate special case of Dirichlet (with $k=2$).

!! Distributions in ML

''Likelihood functions:'' Choosing a distribution for the data defines the likelihood. Gaussian noise gives MSE loss; Bernoulli likelihood gives binary [[Cross Entropy|cross-entropy]]; Categorical likelihood gives multi-class cross-entropy. The "loss function" is almost always the negative log-likelihood of an assumed distribution.

''Priors and posteriors:'' In [[Bayesian Methods]], the prior distribution over parameters encodes beliefs before seeing data. After observing data, Bayes' theorem yields the posterior. Conjugate priors (Beta-Bernoulli, Dirichlet-Categorical, Normal-Normal) make the posterior analytically tractable — the posterior is the same family as the prior with updated parameters.

''Noise models:'' Regression assumes Gaussian noise; count data assumes Poisson; survival analysis assumes Exponential or Weibull. Misspecifying the noise distribution biases estimates and distorts uncertainty.

''Output distributions:'' A sigmoid output is a Bernoulli parameter; a softmax output parameterizes a Categorical distribution; mixture density networks output Gaussian mixture parameters. Variational autoencoders place a multivariate Normal prior on the latent space and use the reparameterization trick to sample differentiably.

!! When Distribution Assumptions Break Down

''Heavy tails and outliers:'' The Gaussian assigns negligible probability to events more than $4\sigma$ from the mean, but financial returns, network traffic, and natural language token frequencies have //heavy tails// — extreme events are far more common than Gaussians predict. Using Gaussian likelihoods with heavy-tailed data produces fragile models that overreact to outliers. ''Fix:'' Use Student-$t$ distributions (heavier tails, controlled by degrees of freedom), Cauchy distributions, or robust loss functions like Huber loss.

''Independence violations:'' The Binomial assumes independent trials; the Poisson assumes independent events. When trials are correlated — users clicking in bursts, genes co-expressed on the same pathway — counts become ''overdispersed'' (variance exceeds the distribution's prediction). ''Fix:'' Use Beta-Binomial or Negative Binomial models that add a dispersion parameter, or hierarchical models that capture group-level correlation.

''Wrong support or shape:'' Applying a Gaussian to strictly positive data (incomes, durations) assigns non-zero probability to negative values — nonsensical predictions. Using a unimodal distribution for genuinely multimodal data (user heights in a mixed population of children and adults) hides structure. ''Fix:'' Use distributions matching the data's natural support (Log-Normal or Gamma for positive reals, Beta for $[0,1]$), or mixture models for multimodality.

!! Technical Details

''Exponential family unification:'' Most named distributions belong to the ''exponential family'', which has the canonical form:

$$p(x \mid \boldsymbol{\eta}) = h(x) \exp\!\left(\boldsymbol{\eta}^\top \mathbf{T}(x) - A(\boldsymbol{\eta})\right)$$

where $\boldsymbol{\eta}$ are ''natural parameters'', $\mathbf{T}(x)$ are ''sufficient statistics'', and $A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$ is the ''log-partition function''. Bernoulli, Binomial, Poisson, Gaussian, Exponential, Gamma, Beta, Dirichlet, and Categorical all belong to this family. The exponential family is special because: (1) sufficient statistics compress data without loss, (2) the log-partition function generates all cumulants via differentiation, (3) [[Maximum Likelihood Estimation|MLE]] reduces to moment matching on sufficient statistics. See [[Exponential Family]].

''Conjugacy table:'' Prior $\to$ Likelihood $\to$ Posterior — Beta $\to$ Bernoulli/Binomial $\to$ Beta; Dirichlet $\to$ Categorical/Multinomial $\to$ Dirichlet; Normal $\to$ Normal (known variance) $\to$ Normal; Gamma $\to$ Poisson $\to$ Gamma; Gamma $\to$ Exponential $\to$ Gamma. Conjugacy means the posterior has the same functional form as the prior, with parameters updated by simple addition of data statistics.

''Maximum entropy characterization:'' Each standard distribution is the //maximum entropy// distribution subject to specific constraints. Uniform: fixed support, no other constraints. Gaussian: fixed mean and variance. Exponential: fixed mean over positive reals. This means choosing a distribution is equivalent to stating //exactly which summary statistics you trust// and being maximally uncertain about everything else.

''Reproductive property:'' Some distributions are ''closed under convolution'': the sum of independent Gaussians is Gaussian; the sum of independent Poissons is Poisson; the sum of independent Gammas (same rate) is Gamma. This closure is why the [[Central Limit Theorem]] drives sums toward Gaussians — the Gaussian is the //fixed point// of the convolution operation in the space of finite-variance distributions.
