! Random Variables

<img src="assets/random_variables.svg" alt="Random Variables" width="800">

Imagine you run an experiment — rolling dice, measuring a patient's blood pressure, or sampling a sentence from the internet. The outcome itself might be complicated: a sequence of die faces, a physiological state, a string of words. A ''random variable'' is a translator that converts each messy outcome into a number you can actually compute with. It is the bridge between the abstract world of "things that could happen" and the concrete world of arithmetic, algebra, and calculus. Every feature column in your dataset, every loss value during training, every gradient update in SGD — all of these are random variables, whether you call them that or not.

!! The Core Insight

A ''random variable'' $X$ is not random, and it is not a variable. It is a //function//. Specifically, it is a function from a sample space $\Omega$ (the set of all possible outcomes of some experiment) to the real numbers $\mathbb{R}$:

$$X: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$$

Each outcome $\omega \in \Omega$ gets mapped to a number $X(\omega)$. Roll two dice and let $X$ be the sum: if $\omega = (3,5)$, then $X(\omega) = 8$. The "randomness" comes entirely from //which $\omega$ nature selects// — once that is determined, $X$ mechanically produces a number.

The key requirement is ''measurability'': $X$ must be structured so that questions like "what is the probability that $X$ falls in the interval $[2, 5]$?" have well-defined answers. Formally, $X$ must be a measurable function from $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ to $(\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$, meaning the preimage of every Borel set is an event in your $\sigma$-algebra.

!! Discrete vs Continuous

Random variables come in two fundamental flavors, unified by a single object:

''Discrete random variables'' take on countably many values — integers, categories, counts. Their behavior is fully described by the ''probability mass function'' (PMF):

$$p(x) = P(X = x)$$

The PMF assigns a probability to each possible value. All probabilities are non-negative and sum to 1: $\sum_x p(x) = 1$. Examples: Bernoulli (coin flip), Binomial (number of successes), Poisson (count of rare events).

''Continuous random variables'' take values in an interval or all of $\mathbb{R}$. No single point has positive probability — $P(X = x) = 0$ for every $x$. Instead, probabilities come from the ''probability density function'' (PDF):

$$P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_a^b f(x) \, dx$$

The PDF $f(x)$ can exceed 1 (it is a density, not a probability), but it integrates to 1: $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) \, dx = 1$. Examples: [[Gaussian Distribution|Gaussian Distribution]], Exponential, Uniform.

The ''cumulative distribution function'' (CDF) unifies both:

$$F(x) = P(X \leq x)$$

Every random variable has a CDF. It is always non-decreasing, right-continuous, with $\lim_{x \to -\infty} F(x) = 0$ and $\lim_{x \to \infty} F(x) = 1$. For discrete variables, the CDF is a staircase. For continuous variables, it is a smooth curve. For a PDF, $f(x) = F'(x)$. The CDF is the true unifying object of [[Probability Distributions|Probability Distributions]].

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''Formal definition.'' A random variable is a measurable function $X: (\Omega, \mathcal{F}) \to (\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$. Measurability means: for every Borel set $B \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$, the preimage $X^{-1}(B) = \{\omega \in \Omega : X(\omega) \in B\} \in \mathcal{F}$. This ensures that $P(X \in B)$ is well-defined through the probability measure $P$ on $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$. See [[Measure Theory|Measure Theory]] for the full scaffolding.

''Expectation'' is the probability-weighted average value:

$$E[X] = \int_\Omega X(\omega) \, dP(\omega) = \begin{cases} \sum_x x \, p(x) & \text{discrete} \\ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x \, f(x) \, dx & \text{continuous} \end{cases}$$

The expectation is a Lebesgue integral with respect to the probability measure $P$. Linearity always holds: $E[aX + bY] = aE[X] + bE[Y]$, regardless of dependence between $X$ and $Y$.

''Variance'' measures spread around the mean:

$$\text{Var}(X) = E[(X - E[X])^2] = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2$$

The standard deviation $\sigma = \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)}$ shares units with $X$. For independent random variables: $\text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y)$.

''Joint, marginal, and conditional distributions.'' For two random variables $X$ and $Y$, the joint distribution describes their simultaneous behavior. The ''marginal'' recovers one variable by integrating out the other:

$$f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x,y) \, dy$$

The ''conditional distribution'' of $Y$ given $X = x$ is:

$$f_{Y|X}(y|x) = \frac{f_{X,Y}(x,y)}{f_X(x)}$$

This is the continuous version of [[Conditional Probability|Conditional Probability]] and the foundation of [[Bayesian Inference|Bayesian Inference]]. Independence means the joint factors: $f_{X,Y}(x,y) = f_X(x) f_Y(y)$.

!! Random Variables in ML

''Features as random variables.'' Each column in your dataset — pixel intensity, word count, age — is a random variable. The joint distribution of all features defines the data-generating process. A model learns $P(Y | X_1, \ldots, X_d)$, which is a conditional distribution of one random variable (the target) given others (the features).

''Loss as a random variable.'' The loss $\ell(f(X), Y)$ on a randomly drawn sample $(X, Y)$ is itself a random variable. The //expected risk// $E[\ell(f(X), Y)]$ is its expectation — the quantity we truly want to minimize. The //empirical risk// $\frac{1}{n}\sum_i \ell(f(x_i), y_i)$ is a sample average estimating this expectation. Generalization theory studies when the sample average converges to the true expectation.

''Stochastic gradient descent.'' At each step, SGD computes $\nabla \ell(f(x_i), y_i)$ on a random minibatch. This gradient is a random variable whose expectation equals the full-batch gradient: $E[\nabla \ell_i] = \nabla E[\ell]$. The variance of this gradient estimator controls convergence speed — high variance means noisy updates and slower progress. Techniques like momentum, variance reduction, and learning rate schedules all manage properties of this random variable.

''The [[Central Limit Theorem|Central Limit Theorem]]'' explains why averaging works: the mean of $n$ i.i.d. random variables converges to a [[Gaussian Distribution|Gaussian Distribution]] as $n$ grows, regardless of the original distribution. This underpins confidence intervals, hypothesis testing, and the statistical foundations of [[Statistics|Statistics]].

!! When Random Variable Assumptions Break Down

''The independence assumption fails.'' Most ML theory assumes training samples are i.i.d. draws from some distribution. In reality, time-series data is auto-correlated, user data has group structure, and web-scraped text has complex dependencies. When samples are dependent, variance estimates are wrong (typically underestimated), standard errors shrink artificially, and confidence intervals lie. Ignoring dependence leads to overconfident models that look excellent on paper and fail in deployment.

''The distribution shifts.'' A random variable's distribution is defined relative to a data-generating process. When that process changes — new user demographics, sensor drift, adversarial manipulation — the random variable's distribution changes too. A model trained under distribution $P_{\text{train}}$ makes guarantees about $E_{P_{\text{train}}}[\ell]$, but you deploy it under $P_{\text{deploy}} \neq P_{\text{train}}$. The expected loss under the new distribution can be arbitrarily worse, and no amount of training data from the old distribution helps.

''Moments may not exist.'' Not all random variables have finite mean or variance. Heavy-tailed distributions (Cauchy, Pareto with small $\alpha$) break the law of large numbers in practice — sample means wander instead of converging, sample variances explode, and standard confidence intervals become meaningless. Financial returns, network degree distributions, and loss spikes in training can exhibit heavy tails that invalidate moment-based reasoning.

!! Technical Details

''Borel measurability.'' The Borel $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ is the smallest $\sigma$-algebra containing all open intervals. A function $X: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ is Borel measurable (i.e., a valid random variable) if $X^{-1}((-\infty, a]) \in \mathcal{F}$ for all $a \in \mathbb{R}$. Equivalently, $\{X \leq a\} \in \mathcal{F}$ for all $a$. Continuous functions are always measurable; so are limits of measurable functions.

''Transformations of random variables.'' If $X$ has density $f_X$ and $Y = g(X)$ for a monotone differentiable $g$, then:

$$f_Y(y) = f_X(g^{-1}(y)) \cdot \left|\frac{d}{dy} g^{-1}(y)\right|$$

For multivariate transformations, the derivative becomes the Jacobian determinant. This is the foundation of normalizing flows and the reparameterization trick in variational autoencoders.

''Moment generating function'' (MGF): $M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}]$, defined for $t$ in a neighborhood of 0. The $n$-th moment is $E[X^n] = M_X^{(n)}(0)$. If two random variables have the same MGF in a neighborhood of 0, they have the same distribution. The MGF of a sum of independent RVs is the product of their MGFs.

''Characteristic function'': $\varphi_X(t) = E[e^{itX}]$, which always exists (unlike the MGF). It uniquely determines the distribution and is the Fourier transform of the density. The [[Central Limit Theorem|Central Limit Theorem]] is most elegantly proved via characteristic functions: if $S_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum_i (X_i - \mu)/\sigma$, then $\varphi_{S_n}(t) \to e^{-t^2/2}$, the characteristic function of the standard normal.

''Convergence of random variables'' comes in multiple modes: almost sure ($X_n \to X$ with probability 1), in probability ($P(|X_n - X| > \epsilon) \to 0$), in $L^p$ ($E[|X_n - X|^p] \to 0$), and in distribution ($F_{X_n}(x) \to F_X(x)$ at continuity points). Each mode is strictly weaker than the previous in this list.
