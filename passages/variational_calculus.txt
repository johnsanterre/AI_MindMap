! Variational Calculus

<img src="assets/variational_calculus.svg" alt="Diagram showing the brachistochrone problem and Euler-Lagrange equation optimizing over functions" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Ordinary calculus finds the input $x$ that minimizes a function $f(x)$. ''Variational calculus'' finds the entire //function// $y(x)$ that minimizes a ''functional'' $J[y] = \int F(x, y, y')\,dx$. The classic question: what is the fastest slide between two points under gravity — the ''brachistochrone''? The answer — a cycloid — cannot be found by ordinary calculus, because you are optimizing over an infinite-dimensional space of curves, not a single number. Variational calculus is the calculus of infinite-dimensional spaces.

!! The Core Insight

The ''Euler-Lagrange equation'' is the analog of "derivative = 0" for functionals:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\!\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial y'}\right) - \frac{\partial F}{\partial y} = 0$$

Any function $y^*$ that extremizes $J[y]$ must satisfy this ODE. This single equation is the bridge between physics (Lagrangian mechanics), control theory (Pontryagin's maximum principle), and modern ML (variational inference, neural ODEs). Just as setting $f'(x) = 0$ finds stationary points of a scalar function, the Euler-Lagrange equation finds stationary //functions// of a functional — the stationarity condition lifted into infinite dimensions.

!! The Euler-Lagrange Equation

The derivation proceeds by ''perturbation''. Let $y = y^* + \varepsilon\eta$ where $\eta(x)$ is any smooth test function satisfying $\eta = 0$ at the boundary points. Then $J[y^* + \varepsilon\eta]$ is an ordinary function of $\varepsilon$, and stationarity requires:

$$\left.\frac{dJ}{d\varepsilon}\right|_{\varepsilon=0} = \int \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial y}\eta + \frac{\partial F}{\partial y'}\eta'\right)dx = 0$$

Integrating the second term by parts and using the boundary conditions $\eta = 0$ at the endpoints collapses this to:

$$\int \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial y} - \frac{d}{dx}\frac{\partial F}{\partial y'}\right)\eta\,dx = 0$$

Since $\eta$ is arbitrary, the integrand must vanish — giving the Euler-Lagrange ODE. ''Key examples:''

- ''Shortest path (geodesics):'' $F = \sqrt{1 + y'^2}$, giving $y'' = 0$ (straight lines in flat space)
- ''Brachistochrone:'' $F = \sqrt{(1+y'^2)/y}$, giving the cycloid
- ''Minimal surfaces (soap films):'' $F = \sqrt{1 + y_x^2 + y_z^2}$, giving the minimal surface equation

''Noether's theorem'' is perhaps the deepest result in variational calculus: every continuous symmetry of the Lagrangian corresponds to a conserved quantity. Time-translation symmetry → conservation of energy. Spatial-translation symmetry → conservation of momentum. Rotational symmetry → conservation of angular momentum. The conservation laws of physics are not postulated; they are consequences of symmetry acting through the variational structure.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ''functional derivative'' $\delta J / \delta y(x)$ is the gradient of $J$ in function space — a function, not a number. Setting it to zero:

$$\frac{\delta J}{\delta y(x)} = \frac{\partial F}{\partial y} - \frac{d}{dx}\frac{\partial F}{\partial y'} = 0$$

recovers the Euler-Lagrange equation. This is gradient descent lifted to infinite dimensions.

''Hamilton's principle'' states that physical trajectories extremize the ''action'':

$$S[q] = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} L(q, \dot{q}, t)\,dt, \qquad L = T - V$$

where $T$ is kinetic energy and $V$ is potential energy. The Euler-Lagrange equations applied to $S$ yield Newton's second law.

The ''Hamiltonian formulation'' introduces conjugate momenta $p = \partial L/\partial \dot{q}$ and defines:

$$H(q, p, t) = p \cdot \dot{q} - L(q, \dot{q}, t)$$

This ''Legendre transform'' exchanges the velocity variable $\dot{q}$ for the momentum $p$, converting the second-order Euler-Lagrange ODE into Hamilton's equations — a pair of first-order ODEs:

$$\dot{q} = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p}, \qquad \dot{p} = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q}$$

For ''constrained optimization'', Lagrange multipliers become //multiplier fields// $\lambda(x)$. The constrained functional $J[y] + \int \lambda(x) g(y, y')\,dx$ is varied freely, and the Euler-Lagrange equations for both $y$ and $\lambda$ give the solution and the constraint simultaneously.

!! Variational Calculus in ML

Variational calculus is not merely historical — it is the mathematical backbone of several active ML research areas:

''Variational Inference:'' The goal is to approximate an intractable posterior $p(z|x)$ with a tractable family $q(z)$. The ''ELBO'' (Evidence Lower Bound) is:

$$\mathcal{L}[q] = \mathbb{E}_{q}[\log p(x, z)] - \mathrm{KL}(q \,||\, p)$$

Maximizing $\mathcal{L}[q]$ over the distribution $q$ is a functional optimization problem — precisely variational calculus. The name ''variational autoencoder'' (VAE) comes directly from this heritage.

''Neural ODEs:'' The dynamics function $f_\theta$ in $\dot{z}(t) = f_\theta(z(t), t)$ plays the role of the Lagrangian. Training via the adjoint method is derived from Pontryagin's maximum principle, a variational result for optimal control.

''Continuous Normalizing Flows:'' Change of variables is computed by integrating along trajectories of a learned vector field — a continuous-time variational structure.

''Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs):'' The loss is the residual of a PDE treated as a functional — the network minimizes a discretized variational problem.

''Optimal Control (Pontryagin's Maximum Principle):'' For a controlled system $\dot{x} = f(x, u)$, the optimal control $u^*(t)$ maximizes the Hamiltonian pointwise. This is the variational stationarity condition applied to the control functional.

!! When Variational Calculus Breaks Down

''1. Non-existence of minimizers.'' Not every functional has a minimizer in the chosen function space. A minimizing sequence $\{y_n\}$ may converge to a function outside the space (e.g., a discontinuous limit when $F$ requires smoothness). Resolving this requires careful choice of Sobolev spaces $W^{k,p}$ and compactness arguments — the modern "direct method" of the calculus of variations. In ML, this manifests as variational problems whose true minimizer is not representable by the chosen network architecture.

''2. Local vs. global extrema.'' The Euler-Lagrange equation gives only //necessary// conditions, not sufficient ones. A solution $y^*$ may be a saddle point, a local minimum, or a local maximum rather than the global minimizer. The ''second variation'' $\delta^2 J$ (analogous to the Hessian) must be positive definite for a true local minimum (Jacobi condition). In variational inference, the ELBO landscape has many local optima, and gradient-based methods find only local solutions.

''3. Discretization errors.'' In practice, functionals are discretized: finite mini-batches approximate integrals, and finite neural networks approximate function spaces. The discrete solution can differ significantly from the true continuous variational solution — particularly when the functional is sensitive to high-frequency components of $y$ that the discretization misses. This is a fundamental tension in physics-informed ML: the PDE residual loss is a functional, but it is evaluated only at finite collocation points.

!! Technical Details

''Historical milestones:'' Euler (1744) — first systematic treatment of the brachistochrone class. Lagrange (1755) — first complete formulation of the variational principle via perturbation (the letter to Euler that founded the field). Hamilton (1833) — action principle and Hamiltonian mechanics. Noether (1915, published 1918) — symmetry-conservation law correspondence.

''Modern analysis:'' The rigorous treatment uses ''Sobolev spaces'' $H^1(\Omega) = W^{1,2}(\Omega)$, where weak derivatives are square-integrable. The direct method of the calculus of variations (Tonelli, early 20th century) establishes existence via coercivity and lower semicontinuity of the functional.

''Optimal transport'' (Monge–Kantorovich) is a variational problem: find the transport map $T$ that minimizes $\int c(x, T(x))\,d\mu(x)$. This connects to Wasserstein distances used in generative modeling.

''Mean-field game theory'' combines variational calculus with game theory: each agent solves a control problem (variational), and the equilibrium is a fixed point over the population distribution (functional equation).
