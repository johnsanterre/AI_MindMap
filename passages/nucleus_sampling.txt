! Nucleus Sampling
<img src="assets/nucleus_sampling.svg" alt="Nucleus Sampling top-p truncation diagram" style="max-width:100%; margin:0.5em 0 1em 0;">

Imagine you are ordering at a restaurant. Sometimes you know exactly what you want — only one or two dishes even cross your mind. Other times the menu is wide open and a dozen options seem equally appealing. A smart ordering strategy would consider fewer choices when you are confident and more choices when you are not. ''Nucleus sampling'' (also called ''top-p sampling'') does exactly this for language generation: instead of always picking from a fixed number of candidate words, it adaptively selects the smallest set of tokens whose cumulative probability exceeds a threshold $p$, then samples from that set after renormalizing. The result is text that stays coherent when the model is confident and remains creative when it is uncertain.

!! The Core Insight

Holtzman et al. (2020) observed that the shape of a language model's output distribution changes drastically from token to token. At some positions, probability mass concentrates on one or two tokens; at others, it spreads across hundreds. Fixed strategies like ''top-k'' always consider the same number of candidates regardless of this shape, which either cuts off valid options (when the distribution is flat) or includes junk tokens (when it is peaked). Nucleus sampling solves this by letting the distribution itself determine how many tokens to consider. You set a single parameter $p \in (0, 1]$ — typically 0.9 or 0.95 — and the algorithm keeps adding tokens in descending probability order until their cumulative mass reaches $p$. This set is the ''nucleus''. Sampling then occurs only within this nucleus after renormalization.

!! How the Algorithm Works

Given a probability distribution over the vocabulary at a single decoding step:

# Sort all tokens by probability in descending order: $p_1 \geq p_2 \geq \cdots \geq p_V$.
# Find the smallest integer $k^*$ such that $\sum_{i=1}^{k^*} p_i \geq p$.
# The nucleus is $\{w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_{k^*}\}$.
# Zero out all tokens outside the nucleus.
# Renormalize the remaining probabilities so they sum to 1.
# Sample from this renormalized distribution.

The nucleus size $k^*$ is not fixed — it adapts at every single generation step. When the model is confident, $k^*$ might be 2 or 3. When uncertainty is high, $k^*$ might be 50 or 200.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Let $V$ be the vocabulary and let $\pi$ be a permutation sorting tokens by descending probability. The nucleus $\mathcal{N}_p$ is defined as:

$$\mathcal{N}_p = \arg\min_{S \subseteq V} |S| \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \sum_{w \in S} P(w \mid \text{context}) \geq p$$

The sampling distribution becomes:

$$P'(w) = \begin{cases} \frac{P(w)}{\sum_{w' \in \mathcal{N}_p} P(w')} & \text{if } w \in \mathcal{N}_p \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

When combined with <<link "Temperature" "Temperature">><</link>> scaling, temperature is applied //first// to the logits $z_i$:

$$P(w_i) = \frac{\exp(z_i / T)}{\sum_j \exp(z_j / T)}$$

and then nucleus truncation is applied to the resulting distribution. Low temperature ($T < 1$) sharpens the distribution, shrinking the nucleus; high temperature ($T > 1$) flattens it, expanding the nucleus.

The information-theoretic interpretation: nucleus sampling approximately bounds the ''surprisal'' of each generated token. Tokens outside the nucleus would carry very high information content (low probability = high surprisal), and removing them prevents the model from making implausible jumps. This connects directly to the concept of perplexity in <<link "Information Theory" "Information Theory">><</link>>.

!! Nucleus vs. Top-k vs. Greedy

||Strategy||Mechanism||Strength||Weakness||
|Greedy|Always pick highest-probability token|Deterministic, coherent|Repetitive, degenerate loops|
|Top-k ($k$ fixed)|Sample from $k$ most probable tokens|Simple to implement|$k$ is distribution-agnostic — too many or too few candidates|
|Nucleus (top-$p$)|Sample from smallest set exceeding $p$ mass|Adapts to distribution shape|One more hyperparameter to tune; sorting overhead|
|Typical sampling|Keep tokens near expected information content|Theoretically grounded|More complex; less widely adopted|

In practice, many systems combine nucleus sampling with temperature scaling: temperature controls the //overall sharpness// of the distribution, while $p$ provides a //safety net// that removes the long tail of low-probability tokens. A common production configuration is $T = 0.7$, $p = 0.95$.

!! When Nucleus Sampling Breaks Down

''Degenerate $p$ values:'' setting $p = 1.0$ recovers unrestricted sampling from the full <<link "Softmax" "Softmax">><</link>> distribution, reintroducing all the incoherence problems nucleus sampling was designed to fix. Setting $p$ too low (e.g., 0.5) makes the model overly conservative and repetitive — approaching greedy decoding. The sweet spot ($0.9$–$0.95$) is empirical, not principled.

''Interaction with temperature:'' when temperature is already very low ($T \ll 1$), the distribution is so peaked that the nucleus contains only 1–2 tokens regardless of $p$. Conversely, very high temperature can flatten the distribution so much that the nucleus includes nearly the entire vocabulary, defeating the purpose. Users who set both parameters without understanding their interaction often get unexpected behavior.

''Structured generation tasks:'' nucleus sampling assumes that //any// token in the nucleus is a reasonable next token. For tasks requiring precise outputs — code generation, arithmetic, structured data — the model may need to produce a specific low-probability token that falls outside the nucleus. In these cases, constrained decoding or beam search is more appropriate than any sampling strategy.

!! Technical Details

''Original paper:'' Holtzman, Buys, Du, Forbes & Choi (2020), "The Curious Case of Neural Text Degeneration" — introduced nucleus sampling and documented the failure modes of greedy and beam-search decoding for open-ended generation.

''Implementation detail:'' sorting the full vocabulary ($V \approx 50\text{K}$–$100\text{K}$) at every token is $O(V \log V)$. In practice, libraries use partial sort or top-k filtering followed by cumulative sum on the GPU, making the overhead negligible compared to the forward pass of a <<link "Language Models" "Language Models">><</link>>.

''Relation to top-k:'' top-$p$ and top-$k$ can be combined — first apply top-$k$ to limit the candidate set, then apply top-$p$ within that set. The HuggingFace `generate()` API supports both simultaneously.

''Typical sampling'' (Meister et al., 2023) is a newer alternative that keeps tokens whose information content (negative log probability) is close to the conditional entropy of the distribution — a more information-theoretically motivated truncation.

''Epsilon sampling'' clips any token with probability below a fixed $\epsilon$, providing a simpler lower bound on token quality.

The <<link "Tokenizer" "Tokenizer">><</link>> determines the granularity at which nucleus sampling operates — subword tokens from BPE or SentencePiece define the atoms of the probability distribution.
