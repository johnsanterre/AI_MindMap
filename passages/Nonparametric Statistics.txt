! Nonparametric Statistics

<img src="assets/nonparametric_statistics.svg" alt="Nonparametric Statistics" width="800">

Imagine you are a tailor. A ''parametric'' tailor stocks only three suit sizes — small, medium, large — and forces every customer into the closest one. A ''nonparametric'' tailor takes fresh measurements for every customer and cuts the fabric to fit. Nonparametric statistics takes the second approach: instead of assuming your data was generated by a bell curve or any other pre-specified shape, it lets the data itself reveal its own structure. The price is that you need more fabric (more data), but the fit can be dramatically better when reality refuses to match the textbook shapes.

!! The Core Insight

Most classical [[Statistics|Statistics]] begins by assuming data comes from a known family of [[Probability Distributions|Probability Distributions]] — a Gaussian, a Poisson, an exponential — and then estimates the parameters of that family. This is the [[Parametric Approaches|Parametric Approaches]] paradigm. ''Nonparametric statistics'' drops that assumption entirely. Instead of asking "what are the best parameters for this assumed shape?", it asks "what can we conclude directly from the ranks, counts, or empirical distribution of the data?"

This is not the same as having //no// parameters. In fact, nonparametric models often have //more// effective parameters than parametric ones — the number of parameters can grow with the amount of data. The name really means "not restricted to a fixed parametric form." The data speaks for itself, and the model's complexity adapts to however much evidence is available.

!! Freedom from Assumptions

Parametric methods are powerful when their assumptions hold. A $t$-test comparing two group means is optimally efficient //if// both groups are normally distributed. But what happens when the assumptions fail?

Consider these real-world situations where parametric assumptions crumble:

* ''Heavy-tailed data'' — income distributions, insurance claims, and network traffic all have extreme outliers that violate normality. A single billionaire can wreck a $t$-test on household income.
* ''Skewed data'' — reaction times, survival durations, and many biological measurements are strongly right-skewed. The mean and standard deviation are poor summaries.
* ''Ordinal data'' — Likert scales ("strongly agree" to "strongly disagree"), pain ratings, and rankings have a natural order but no meaningful numerical spacing. Computing a mean of ordinal categories is conceptually dubious.
* ''Small samples with unknown distributions'' — with $n = 12$ observations, you cannot reliably verify whether data is normally distributed, yet parametric tests //assume// you know the distribution.
* ''Multimodal data'' — mixtures of subpopulations create bumpy distributions that no single parametric family captures.

In every one of these cases, nonparametric methods provide valid inference without pretending the data has a shape it does not. They achieve this by working with ''ranks'' (the relative ordering of observations), ''permutations'' (shuffling labels to build null distributions), or ''empirical distribution functions'' (treating the observed data as its own distributional estimate).

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The three pillars of nonparametric methodology each have elegant mathematical machinery behind them.

''Kernel Density Estimation (KDE)'' builds a smooth estimate of the probability density function by placing a small bump (kernel) at each data point and summing them. For $n$ observations $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ with bandwidth $h$ and kernel function $K$:

$$\hat{f}(x) = \frac{1}{nh}\sum_{i=1}^{n} K\!\left(\frac{x - x_i}{h}\right)$$

The kernel $K$ is typically a Gaussian or Epanechnikov function, and $h$ controls the smoothness. Too small and the estimate is spiky; too large and genuine features get blurred. The optimal bandwidth under mean integrated squared error for a Gaussian kernel is Silverman's rule: $h^* = 1.06\,\hat{\sigma}\, n^{-1/5}$. See [[Kernel Methods|Kernel Methods]] for the broader story of kernels in ML.

''Rank-based tests'' replace raw data values with their ranks and compute test statistics from those ranks. The ''Wilcoxon rank-sum test'' (equivalent to the ''Mann-Whitney U test'') compares two groups. Given combined ranks $R_1, R_2, \ldots, R_{n_1}$ assigned to the first group, the test statistic is:

$$U = \sum_{i=1}^{n_1} R_i - \frac{n_1(n_1 + 1)}{2}$$

Under the null hypothesis that both groups come from the same distribution, $U$ has a known distribution that converges to a normal by the [[Central Limit Theorem|Central Limit Theorem]]. Crucially, this test is valid for //any// continuous distribution — it tests whether one group tends to produce larger values than the other.

''The [[Bootstrap|Bootstrap]]'' resamples the observed data //with replacement// to approximate the sampling distribution of any statistic $\hat{\theta}$. Draw $B$ bootstrap samples, compute $\hat{\theta}^{*}_1, \ldots, \hat{\theta}^{*}_B$, and use their spread as a standard error estimate:

$$\widehat{\text{SE}}_{\text{boot}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{B-1}\sum_{b=1}^{B}\left(\hat{\theta}^{*}_b - \bar{\theta}^{*}\right)^2}$$

This requires no distributional assumptions — the empirical distribution itself serves as the population model.

!! The Nonparametric Toolbox

Choosing the right nonparametric method depends on what you are testing. Here is a practical mapping from common parametric tests to their nonparametric counterparts:

| ''Parametric Test'' | ''Nonparametric Equivalent'' | ''Use When'' |
| One-sample $t$-test | Wilcoxon signed-rank test | Testing if a median equals a hypothesized value |
| Two-sample $t$-test | Mann-Whitney $U$ test | Comparing two independent groups |
| Paired $t$-test | Wilcoxon signed-rank (paired) | Comparing matched pairs |
| One-way ANOVA | Kruskal-Wallis $H$ test | Comparing 3+ independent groups |
| Pearson correlation | Spearman rank correlation | Measuring monotonic association |
| Linear regression | Theil-Sen estimator | Robust slope estimation |

''Permutation tests'' offer maximum flexibility for [[Hypothesis Testing|Hypothesis Testing]]. To test whether two groups differ: (1) compute a test statistic on the real data, (2) randomly shuffle group labels thousands of times, (3) compute the statistic each time, (4) the [[P-Values|P-Values]] is the fraction of permuted statistics as extreme as the observed one. This directly constructs the null distribution without any formulas — pure computational brute force.

For ''KDE bandwidth selection'', beyond Silverman's rule, cross-validation methods (leave-one-out likelihood) adapt to multimodal and non-Gaussian shapes. The ''Sheather-Jones'' plug-in method is often considered the best automatic selector for univariate data.

!! When Nonparametric Methods Break Down

* ''The curse of dimensionality destroys KDE.'' In $d$ dimensions, the number of data points needed for a good density estimate grows exponentially — roughly $n \propto h^{-d}$. With 10 features, kernel density estimation becomes essentially useless without millions of observations. This connects directly to the challenges discussed in [[Statistical Learning Theory|Statistical Learning Theory]].

* ''Lower statistical power with small samples.'' Nonparametric tests throw away information by converting values to ranks. When the parametric assumptions //actually hold//, this costs you roughly 5% efficiency (asymptotic relative efficiency of the Wilcoxon test vs the $t$-test is $3/\pi \approx 0.955$ under normality). With very small samples, this efficiency loss can mean the difference between detecting a real effect and missing it.

* ''Bandwidth and hyperparameter sensitivity.'' KDE, local regression, and other smoothing methods depend critically on tuning parameters. A poorly chosen bandwidth can produce wildly misleading density estimates — either overfitting every bump in the data or smoothing away genuine multimodality. Unlike parametric models where misspecification is at least //visible//, a bad bandwidth silently distorts results.

!! Technical Details

The ''asymptotic relative efficiency (ARE)'' of the Wilcoxon test relative to the $t$-test is $3/\pi \approx 0.955$ under normality, meaning you need only ~5% more data. But under heavy-tailed distributions, the ARE can exceed 1.0 — the nonparametric test becomes //more// efficient than the parametric one. For the logistic distribution, ARE $= \pi^2/9 \approx 1.097$.

''U-statistics'' provide a unifying framework for many nonparametric estimators. A U-statistic of degree $m$ based on a symmetric kernel $h$ is:

$$U_n = \binom{n}{m}^{-1} \sum_{1 \le i_1 < \cdots < i_m \le n} h(X_{i_1}, \ldots, X_{i_m})$$

The Wilcoxon statistic is a U-statistic of degree 2. The theory of U-statistics gives variance bounds via the Hoeffding decomposition and connects to [[Concentration Inequalities|Concentration Inequalities]] for finite-sample guarantees.

''Empirical process theory'' studies the behavior of the empirical CDF $\hat{F}_n(x) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\mathbf{1}(X_i \le x)$ as a [[Random Variables|Random Variables]] process indexed by $x$. The Glivenko-Cantelli theorem guarantees $\sup_x |\hat{F}_n(x) - F(x)| \to 0$ almost surely, while the Donsker theorem gives the rate: $\sqrt{n}(\hat{F}_n - F)$ converges to a Brownian bridge. The Kolmogorov-Smirnov test exploits this directly.

For KDE, the ''mean integrated squared error'' (MISE) decomposes into bias$^2$ + variance, with optimal rate $O(n^{-4/5})$ in one dimension versus the parametric rate $O(n^{-1})$. In $d$ dimensions the rate becomes $O(n^{-4/(4+d)})$, illustrating the curse of dimensionality.

''[[Bayesian Nonparametrics|Bayesian Nonparametrics]]'' extends this philosophy into the Bayesian framework by placing priors over infinite-dimensional function spaces (Dirichlet processes, Gaussian processes), allowing model complexity to grow with data while maintaining coherent uncertainty quantification.
