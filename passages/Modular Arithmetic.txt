! Modular Arithmetic

<img src="assets/modular_arithmetic.svg" alt="Modular Arithmetic" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Every twelve-year-old already understands modular arithmetic — they just call it "telling time." If it is 10 o'clock and you add five hours, you do not get 15 o'clock; you get 3 o'clock. The numbers ''wrap around'' after reaching 12, and the only thing that matters is the remainder. ''Modular arithmetic'' formalizes this wrapping: instead of working on the infinite number line, you work on a finite loop where numbers that differ by a fixed amount are treated as identical. This simple idea — "only the remainder matters" — underpins virtually all of modern cryptography, powers hash functions in every computer, gives neural networks a testbed for studying [[Grokking]], and provides the algebraic scaffolding for error-correcting codes that keep your data intact.

!! The Core Insight

We say that two integers $a$ and $b$ are ''congruent modulo $n$'' — written $a \equiv b \pmod{n}$ — if $n$ divides their difference. The number $n$ is called the ''modulus''. This is not a vague analogy; it is a precise equivalence relation that partitions //all// integers into $n$ non-overlapping buckets called ''congruence classes'' or ''residue classes''. Every integer belongs to exactly one class: $[0], [1], [2], \ldots, [n-1]$.

The critical property is that arithmetic //respects// these classes. If $a \equiv a' \pmod{n}$ and $b \equiv b' \pmod{n}$, then:

$$a + b \equiv a' + b' \pmod{n}$$
$$a \cdot b \equiv a' \cdot b' \pmod{n}$$

This means you can add, subtract, and multiply residues without ever leaving the system. You have a self-contained arithmetic universe with exactly $n$ elements. When $n$ is prime, something remarkable happens: every nonzero element has a ''multiplicative inverse'', and this tiny universe becomes a ''finite field'' $\mathbb{F}_p$ — the most important algebraic structure in modern cryptography and coding theory.

!! Rings, Fields, and the Role of the Modulus

The set $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ (integers mod $n$) always forms a ''ring'' — you can add, subtract, and multiply freely. But whether it forms a ''field'' (where division is also possible) depends entirely on $n$.

''When $n$ is prime:'' $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ is a field. Every nonzero element $a$ has a unique inverse $a^{-1}$ such that $a \cdot a^{-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$. For example, in $\mathbb{Z}/7\mathbb{Z}$: $3 \cdot 5 = 15 \equiv 1 \pmod{7}$, so $3^{-1} = 5$. This is what makes prime moduli so special and so ubiquitous in cryptography.

''When $n$ is composite:'' $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ has ''zero divisors'' — nonzero elements whose product is zero. In $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$: $2 \cdot 3 = 6 \equiv 0 \pmod{6}$. Elements that share a common factor with $n$ have no inverse. Only elements coprime to $n$ are invertible, and there are exactly $\phi(n)$ of them, where $\phi$ is ''Euler's totient function''.

''Euler's theorem'' generalizes Fermat's little theorem: for any $a$ coprime to $n$:

$$a^{\phi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod{n}$$

When $n = p$ is prime, $\phi(p) = p - 1$, recovering ''Fermat's little theorem'': $a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$. This result is the mathematical engine behind RSA encryption.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''The extended Euclidean algorithm'' is the workhorse for computing modular inverses. Given $a$ and $n$ with $\gcd(a, n) = 1$, it finds integers $x, y$ such that:

$$ax + ny = 1$$

Reducing modulo $n$ gives $ax \equiv 1 \pmod{n}$, so $x = a^{-1} \bmod n$. This runs in $O(\log n)$ steps — fast even for numbers with hundreds of digits.

''Modular exponentiation'' computes $a^k \bmod n$ efficiently via ''repeated squaring''. Rather than computing $a^k$ (which could have millions of digits) and then reducing, you reduce at every step:

$$a^{2m} \equiv (a^m)^2 \pmod{n}$$

This keeps intermediate values bounded by $n^2$ and runs in $O(\log k)$ multiplications. It is the computational core of RSA, Diffie-Hellman, and essentially all public-key cryptography.

''The Chinese Remainder Theorem (CRT)'' states that if $n_1, n_2, \ldots, n_k$ are pairwise coprime, the system of simultaneous congruences:

$$x \equiv a_1 \pmod{n_1}, \quad x \equiv a_2 \pmod{n_2}, \quad \ldots, \quad x \equiv a_k \pmod{n_k}$$

has a unique solution modulo $N = n_1 n_2 \cdots n_k$. This isomorphism $\mathbb{Z}/N\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/n_1\mathbb{Z} \times \cdots \times \mathbb{Z}/n_k\mathbb{Z}$ lets you decompose a hard problem mod $N$ into easier problems mod each $n_i$ — a technique used in RSA-CRT to speed up decryption by a factor of 4.

!! Applications in Computing and ML

''Cryptography:'' RSA encryption rests on the difficulty of factoring $N = pq$ (a product of two large primes). Encryption computes $c \equiv m^e \pmod{N}$; decryption computes $m \equiv c^d \pmod{N}$, where $ed \equiv 1 \pmod{\phi(N)}$. The entire security model is modular arithmetic over a carefully chosen composite modulus.

''Hash functions:'' Universal hashing uses $h(x) = (ax + b) \bmod p$ to distribute keys uniformly. Polynomial hashing, used in Rabin-Karp string matching, computes rolling hash values modulo a prime for $O(1)$ substring comparisons.

''Positional encodings:'' Some positional encoding schemes for [[Transformers]] use modular or periodic functions to represent token positions. The sinusoidal encodings in the original Transformer paper create patterns that repeat with different frequencies — a continuous analog of modular structure.

''Grokking:'' The landmark [[Grokking]] paper (Power et al., 2022) trained small transformers on modular addition $(a + b) \bmod 97$. The models first memorized the training set, then — after vastly more training — suddenly generalized. Mechanistic interpretability studies found that the networks learned to implement modular arithmetic via discrete Fourier transforms over $\mathbb{Z}/97\mathbb{Z}$, with neurons encoding circular representations of residue classes.

''Error-correcting codes:'' Reed-Solomon codes, used in QR codes, CDs, and deep-space communication, operate over finite fields $\mathbb{F}_{p^k}$ built from modular polynomial arithmetic. [[Combinatorics]] and modular structure together guarantee that corrupted symbols can be recovered.

!! When Modular Arithmetic Breaks Down

''Non-prime modulus kills division.'' When the modulus $n$ is composite, zero divisors appear and not every nonzero element has an inverse. Algorithms that assume field structure (like Gaussian elimination mod $n$, or naive polynomial interpolation) will silently produce garbage if $n$ is not prime. The fix is either to ensure $n$ is prime, or to explicitly check $\gcd(a, n) = 1$ before attempting inversion — and to use the extended Euclidean algorithm rather than Fermat's little theorem (which requires primality).

''Overflow in implementation.'' Computing $a \cdot b \bmod n$ naively requires an intermediate product $a \cdot b$ that can overflow fixed-width integers. If $a$ and $b$ are both close to $2^{63}$, their product exceeds $2^{126}$ — far beyond 64-bit range. The result is silently wrong, not an error. The fix is ''Montgomery multiplication'' or explicit 128-bit intermediate arithmetic. Every serious cryptographic library handles this; naive implementations routinely do not.

''Timing side channels.'' Modular exponentiation that branches on secret bits (e.g., "if this bit of $d$ is 1, multiply by $a$") leaks the secret key through execution time differences. An attacker measuring microsecond variations can recover RSA private keys. ''Constant-time'' implementations — where every operation takes the same number of cycles regardless of the secret value — are mandatory for cryptographic code. This is a case where mathematically correct code is still catastrophically insecure.

!! Technical Details

''Foundational concepts:'' The ring $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ is the quotient of $\mathbb{Z}$ by the ideal $n\mathbb{Z}$. It is the prototypical example in abstract algebra, motivating the general theory of quotient rings, ideals, and homomorphisms.

''Euler's totient function:'' $\phi(n) = n \prod_{p | n}(1 - 1/p)$ counts integers in $[1, n]$ coprime to $n$. For $n = p^a$: $\phi(p^a) = p^{a-1}(p-1)$. For $n = p q$: $\phi(pq) = (p-1)(q-1)$.

''Primitive roots:'' If $\gcd(g, n) = 1$ and the order of $g$ modulo $n$ is $\phi(n)$, then $g$ is a ''primitive root'' mod $n$. Primitive roots exist for $n = 1, 2, 4, p^k, 2p^k$ (where $p$ is an odd prime). The multiplicative group $(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^*$ is cyclic of order $p - 1$, generated by any primitive root.

''Quadratic residues:'' An integer $a$ is a quadratic residue mod $p$ if $x^2 \equiv a \pmod{p}$ has a solution. Exactly half the nonzero residues mod an odd prime are quadratic residues. The ''Legendre symbol'' $\left(\frac{a}{p}\right)$ encodes this, and ''quadratic reciprocity'' (Gauss's "golden theorem") relates $\left(\frac{p}{q}\right)$ to $\left(\frac{q}{p}\right)$ for distinct odd primes.
