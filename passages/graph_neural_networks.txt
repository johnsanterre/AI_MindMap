! Graph Neural Networks
<img src="assets/graph_neural_networks.svg" style="width:100%;max-width:800px;display:block;margin:0.5em auto 1em;">

Imagine a group of friends, each holding a different piece of a jigsaw puzzle. Nobody can see the full picture, but everyone can whisper to their immediate neighbors about what they see. After a few rounds of whispering, each person has a surprisingly good idea of what the whole puzzle looks like — even though they only ever talked to the people sitting next to them. That is the essential idea behind ''graph neural networks'' (GNNs): data lives on a graph, and nodes learn by exchanging messages with their neighbors.

!! The Core Insight

Many real-world datasets are not grids (images) or sequences (text) — they are ''graphs'': molecules where atoms are nodes and bonds are edges, social networks where people are nodes and friendships are edges, citation networks, knowledge bases, 3D meshes. Standard neural networks assume a fixed input shape, but graphs come in wildly different sizes and topologies. GNNs solve this by defining computation //relative to graph structure//: each node updates its representation by gathering information from its neighbors, so the same learned parameters work regardless of graph size or shape. The foundational unifying framework is the ''message passing neural network'' (MPNN) introduced by Gilmer et al. (2017), which showed that most existing GNN variants are special cases of a single message-passing template. !! Message Passing and Aggregation

A GNN operates in rounds (layers). At each round $k$, every node $v$ does three things:

''1. Message.'' Each neighbor $u \in \mathcal{N}(v)$ constructs a message from its current hidden state: $\mathbf{m}_{u \to v}^{(k)} = \text{MSG}^{(k)}(\mathbf{h}_u^{(k-1)}, \mathbf{h}_v^{(k-1)}, \mathbf{e}_{uv})$, where $\mathbf{e}_{uv}$ is an optional edge feature.

''2. Aggregate.'' Node $v$ combines all incoming messages using a permutation-invariant function (because neighbor ordering is arbitrary): $\mathbf{M}_v^{(k)} = \text{AGG}(\{\mathbf{m}_{u \to v}^{(k)} : u \in \mathcal{N}(v)\})$. Common choices are sum, mean, or max.

''3. Update.'' The node combines the aggregated message with its own state to produce a new embedding: $\mathbf{h}_v^{(k)} = \text{UPDATE}^{(k)}(\mathbf{h}_v^{(k-1)}, \mathbf{M}_v^{(k)})$.

After $K$ rounds, each node's embedding encodes structural information from its ''$K$-hop neighborhood'' — nodes up to $K$ edges away. This is the ''receptive field'' of a GNN, directly analogous to the receptive field in a CNN. For graph-level tasks (e.g., predicting a molecular property), a final ''readout'' function pools all node embeddings into a single graph vector: $\mathbf{h}_G = \text{READOUT}(\{\mathbf{h}_v^{(K)} : v \in G\})$. See also: <<link "Message Passing Neural Networks" "Message Passing Neural Networks">><</link>>.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The simplest and most influential instantiation is the ''Graph Convolutional Network'' (GCN) of Kipf and Welling (2017). Its layer-wise update rule is:

$$\mathbf{H}^{(k)} = \sigma\!\left(\tilde{D}^{-1/2}\,\tilde{A}\,\tilde{D}^{-1/2}\,\mathbf{H}^{(k-1)}\,\mathbf{W}^{(k)}\right)$$

where $\tilde{A} = A + I$ is the adjacency matrix with added self-loops, $\tilde{D}$ is its degree matrix, $\mathbf{W}^{(k)}$ is a learnable weight matrix, and $\sigma$ is a non-linearity (typically ReLU). The symmetric normalization $\tilde{D}^{-1/2}\tilde{A}\tilde{D}^{-1/2}$ computes a ''normalized mean'' of neighbor features, preventing high-degree nodes from dominating.

The ''Graph Attention Network'' (GAT) replaces the fixed normalization with learned attention weights:

$$\alpha_{uv} = \frac{\exp\!\left(\text{LeakyReLU}(\mathbf{a}^T[\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_u \| \mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_v])\right)}{\sum_{w \in \mathcal{N}(v)} \exp\!\left(\text{LeakyReLU}(\mathbf{a}^T[\mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_w \| \mathbf{W}\mathbf{h}_v])\right)}$$

so different neighbors contribute different amounts based on content, not just degree.

The ''Graph Isomorphism Network'' (GIN) uses sum aggregation with an injective update to maximize expressive power:

$$\mathbf{h}_v^{(k)} = \text{MLP}^{(k)}\!\left((1 + \epsilon^{(k)}) \cdot \mathbf{h}_v^{(k-1)} + \sum_{u \in \mathcal{N}(v)} \mathbf{h}_u^{(k-1)}\right)$$

Xu et al. (2019) proved that GIN is //as powerful as the Weisfeiler-Leman graph isomorphism test//, establishing a theoretical ceiling for standard message-passing GNNs. See: <<link "Graph Isomorphism Network" "Graph Isomorphism Network">><</link>>.

!! Applications and Architectures

''Molecular property prediction:'' atoms as nodes, bonds as edges. GNNs predict toxicity, solubility, binding affinity — the original motivation for MPNNs. ''Protein structure:'' residues as nodes, spatial proximity as edges. AlphaFold's Evoformer uses message-passing ideas on pair representations. ''Recommendation systems:'' users and items as a bipartite graph; GNNs propagate preference signals (PinSage at Pinterest). ''Combinatorial optimization:'' GNNs learn heuristics for NP-hard graph problems like TSP, graph coloring, and maximum independent set. ''Physics simulation:'' particles as nodes, interactions as edges; GNNs learn to simulate fluid dynamics and rigid-body physics (Interaction Networks, Graph Network Simulators). ''Point clouds and meshes:'' 3D points connected by k-nearest-neighbor graphs; GNNs perform 3D shape classification and segmentation.

Beyond the core GCN/GAT/GIN trio, notable architectures include ''GraphSAGE'' (Hamilton et al. 2017), which samples a fixed-size neighborhood for scalability; ''SchNet'' and ''DimeNet'' for 3D molecular graphs with distance and angle features; and ''equivariant GNNs'' (EGNN, SE(3)-Transformers) that respect 3D rotational symmetry. The broader framework of <<link "Geometric Deep Learning" "Geometric Deep Learning">><</link>> situates GNNs within a unified theory of neural networks on structured domains.

!! When GNNs Break Down

''Over-smoothing:'' as the number of message-passing layers increases, all node embeddings converge to the same vector. After many rounds of averaging, local structure is washed out. Empirically, most GNNs perform best with 2-4 layers — far fewer than the dozens of layers standard in CNNs or Transformers. Techniques like residual connections, DropEdge, and jumping knowledge mitigate but do not fully solve this.

''Limited expressiveness (the WL barrier):'' standard message-passing GNNs cannot distinguish certain non-isomorphic graphs that the 1-dimensional Weisfeiler-Leman test also fails on. For example, they cannot count cycles or detect certain substructures. Higher-order GNNs (k-WL), subgraph GNNs, and random feature augmentation push beyond this limit at increased computational cost.

''Scalability to large graphs:'' full-batch GCN requires storing the entire adjacency matrix and all node features in memory, which is infeasible for graphs with millions of nodes (e.g., social networks). Mini-batch training with neighborhood sampling (GraphSAGE) or cluster-based methods (ClusterGCN) address this, but introduce variance and complexity.

!! Technical Details

''Key papers:'' Kipf and Welling (ICLR 2017) — GCN; Velickovic et al. (ICLR 2018) — GAT; Gilmer et al. (ICML 2017) — MPNN framework; Xu et al. (ICLR 2019) — GIN and WL expressiveness analysis; Hamilton, Ying, Leskovec (NeurIPS 2017) — GraphSAGE. ''Typical hidden dimensions:'' 64-256 per node. ''Layers:'' 2-4 for most tasks; deeper requires residual connections. ''Aggregation functions:'' sum (most expressive for multisets), mean (normalized, stable), max (captures salient features). ''Readout:'' sum or mean pooling for graph classification; hierarchical pooling (DiffPool, TopKPool) for structured coarsening. ''Training:'' node classification uses cross-entropy on labeled nodes with the full graph as context; link prediction uses contrastive or binary cross-entropy losses on node pairs; graph classification pools node embeddings and feeds to an MLP head. ''Over-squashing:'' distinct from over-smoothing — information from distant nodes is compressed through bottleneck edges, losing fidelity. Graph rewiring and curvature-based methods address this. ''Spectral perspective:'' GCN can be derived as a first-order Chebyshev approximation of spectral graph convolution, connecting it to graph Fourier analysis and the graph Laplacian. ''Software:'' PyTorch Geometric (PyG), Deep Graph Library (DGL), and JAX-based frameworks (Jraph) are the primary implementations.
