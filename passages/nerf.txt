! NeRF
<img src="assets/nerf.svg" alt="NeRF pipeline diagram: camera ray sampling, MLP query, and volume rendering" style="max-width:100%;margin:1em 0;">

Take 20 photos of a sculpture from different angles and you can mentally visualize it in 3D. NeRF does the same computationally — given a set of photographs of a scene, it learns an implicit 3D model stored entirely in a neural network's weights, then renders that scene from any new viewpoint you choose.

!! The Core Insight

''Neural Radiance Fields'' (Mildenhall et al., 2020) represent a 3D scene not as a mesh or voxel grid, but as a function: an MLP $f_\theta : (x, y, z, \theta, \phi) \to (r, g, b, \sigma)$. Given any 3D position and a viewing direction, the network outputs an RGB color and a volume density $\sigma$. Novel views are synthesized by casting camera rays through every pixel, sampling 3D points along each ray, querying the MLP at every point, and compositing the results with volume rendering. No explicit 3D structure is ever constructed — the entire scene is implicit in the weights $\theta$. Related topics: [[CNN]], [[Deep Learning]], [[Embeddings]], [[Autoencoders]].

!! Volume Rendering

The expected color $C(\mathbf{r})$ along a ray $\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{o} + t\mathbf{d}$ is:

$$C(\mathbf{r}) = \int_{t_n}^{t_f} T(t)\,\sigma\!\left(\mathbf{r}(t)\right)\mathbf{c}\!\left(\mathbf{r}(t), \mathbf{d}\right) dt$$

where $T(t) = \exp\!\left(-\int_{t_n}^{t} \sigma(\mathbf{r}(s))\,ds\right)$ is the ''accumulated transmittance'' — the probability that the ray travels from $t_n$ to $t$ without hitting any surface. In practice the integral is discretized: the ray is divided into $N$ stratified bins and colors and densities are summed with quadrature weights. Training minimizes the mean-squared error between rendered pixel colors and ground-truth pixel colors across all training views and rays.

!! Positional Encoding

Raw $(x, y, z)$ coordinates fed directly to an MLP produce blurry results. MLPs have a ''spectral bias'': they preferentially learn low-frequency functions, missing fine geometric and texture detail. The fix is ''positional encoding'', which lifts each coordinate to a higher-dimensional space via sinusoids:

$$\gamma(p) = \left(\sin(2^0\pi p),\, \cos(2^0\pi p),\, \ldots,\, \sin(2^{L-1}\pi p),\, \cos(2^{L-1}\pi p)\right)$$

NeRF uses $L=10$ for position (adding 60 dimensions per coordinate) and $L=4$ for viewing direction. This encoding lets the MLP represent high-frequency geometry and view-dependent shading such as specular highlights.

!! Extensions and Speed

Vanilla NeRF is a proof of concept, not a practical system: training takes 1–2 days per scene, and rendering a single image takes ~30 seconds.

''Instant NGP'' (Müller et al., 2022) replaces the MLP's input with a multi-resolution hash encoding over a compact lookup table, reducing training to minutes and rendering to 60 FPS.

''3D Gaussian Splatting'' (Kerbl et al., 2023) abandons the implicit MLP entirely in favor of explicit 3D Gaussians, achieving real-time rendering at high quality.

Further extensions address other limitations: ''NeRF-W'' handles unconstrained in-the-wild photos with varying lighting and transient occluders; ''D-NeRF'' extends the framework to dynamic scenes with a deformation field; ''Mip-NeRF 360'' handles unbounded 360° scenes with anti-aliasing via cone casting. Downstream applications include AR/VR asset creation, robotic scene understanding, SLAM, and text-to-3D generation (DreamFusion, which distills a NeRF from a 2D diffusion model).

!! When NeRF Breaks Down

''Training time and per-scene optimization:'' vanilla NeRF must train a separate MLP for every new scene, taking hours. Generalizable NeRFs (IBRNet, pixelNeRF) amortize this with a learned prior but sacrifice quality.

''Dynamic and moving scenes:'' the core formulation assumes a static world. A person walking through the frame or a flag waving in the wind violates the model's assumptions, causing blurring or ghost artifacts.

''Extrapolation beyond the training cameras:'' NeRF interpolates well but does not extrapolate. Attempting to render from a viewpoint far outside the convex hull of training cameras produces hallucinated, incoherent geometry.

!! Technical Details

''Key papers:'' Mildenhall et al., "NeRF: Representing Scenes as Neural Radiance Fields for View Synthesis," ECCV 2020. Müller et al., "Instant Neural Graphics Primitives," SIGGRAPH 2022. Kerbl et al., "3D Gaussian Splatting for Real-Time Radiance Field Rendering," SIGGRAPH 2023. Barron et al., "Mip-NeRF 360: Unbounded Anti-Aliased Neural Radiance Fields," CVPR 2022.

''Architecture:'' two-stage MLP — a density network taking only $(x,y,z)$ (view-independent), followed by a color network that additionally takes $\mathbf{d}$ (view-dependent). Encourages geometry to be consistent across views.

''Hierarchical sampling:'' two networks trained simultaneously — a coarse network sampled uniformly, and a fine network sampled proportionally to the coarse density estimate (importance sampling).

''Datasets:'' Realistic Synthetic 360 (Blender objects), Local Light Field Fusion (LLFF, forward-facing scenes), Tanks and Temples.

''Representations compared:'' NeRF vs voxel grids (explicit, memory-heavy, fixed resolution) vs triangle meshes (efficient but hard to optimize from images) vs point clouds (no surface normals or density).
