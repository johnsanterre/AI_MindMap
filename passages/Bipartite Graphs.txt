! Bipartite Graphs

<img src="assets/bipartite_graphs.svg" alt="Bipartite graph with two vertex sets and a maximum matching highlighted">

Imagine a school dance where students stand on opposite sides of the room — one side all leads, the other all follows. Every dance partnership crosses the room; nobody pairs up with someone on their own side. That is a ''bipartite graph'' in a nutshell: a graph whose vertices split into two groups such that every edge connects a vertex in one group to a vertex in the other. This deceptively simple constraint unlocks a remarkable family of algorithms and theorems that power everything from recommendation engines to organ-donor matching programs.

!! The Core Insight

A graph is bipartite //if and only if// it contains no odd-length cycle. You can verify this with a two-coloring test: pick any vertex, color it red, color all its neighbors blue, continue alternating. If you ever need to assign the same color to both endpoints of an edge, the graph is not bipartite — and the path back to the conflict forms an odd cycle. This equivalence between "2-colorable" and "no odd cycle" is the foundational characterization from which almost every other bipartite result flows.

!! Two Sides of Every Connection

'''2-Colorability and the Odd-Cycle Test.''' The breadth-first two-coloring procedure runs in $O(|V| + |E|)$ time. For each connected component, start from an arbitrary vertex, assign it to set $U$, and place every neighbor in set $V$. If a conflict arises, the graph fails the bipartite test. This is the standard proof technique: bipartiteness is //equivalent// to 2-colorability, which is //equivalent// to the absence of odd cycles.

'''König's Theorem.''' In any bipartite graph, the size of a ''maximum matching'' equals the size of a ''minimum vertex cover''. This duality does //not// hold for general graphs (where the relationship is only an inequality via the Gallai theorem). König's theorem connects bipartite matching to the theory of [[Maximum Flow|Maximum Flow]] through linear programming duality — the matching polytope of a bipartite graph has integer vertices, so the LP relaxation solves the combinatorial problem exactly.

'''Hall's Marriage Theorem.''' A bipartite graph with parts $U$ and $V$ contains a matching that saturates every vertex in $U$ if and only if for every subset $S \subseteq U$, the neighborhood $N(S)$ satisfies $|N(S)| \ge |S|$. This "marriage condition" is the combinatorial gateway to existence proofs in [[Combinatorics|Combinatorics]]: if every group of applicants collectively qualifies for at least as many positions, a perfect assignment exists.

'''Relation to Graph Theory.''' Bipartite graphs sit at the core of [[Graph Theory|Graph Theory]] as one of the most tractable graph families. Many NP-hard problems on general graphs — chromatic number, maximum independent set, edge coloring — become polynomial-time solvable on bipartite graphs.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ''adjacency matrix'' of a bipartite graph with parts $U = \{u_1, \dots, u_m\}$ and $V = \{v_1, \dots, v_n\}$ has a characteristic block structure. If vertices are ordered by partition:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & B \\ B^T & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

where $B$ is the $m \times n$ ''biadjacency matrix'' with $B_{ij} = 1$ if $u_i$ is adjacent to $v_j$. This block form means the eigenvalues of $A$ are symmetric about zero: if $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue, so is $-\lambda$.

The ''matching polytope'' for a bipartite graph is defined as the convex hull of incidence vectors of matchings. By the Birkhoff–von Neumann theorem, for complete bipartite graphs this equals the set of doubly stochastic matrices — every extreme point is a permutation matrix, i.e., a perfect matching. The LP relaxation:

$$\max \sum_{e \in E} w_e x_e \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \sum_{e \ni v} x_e \le 1 \;\; \forall v, \quad x_e \ge 0$$

always yields integer solutions for bipartite graphs, a consequence of the total unimodularity of the constraint matrix.

The ''Hungarian algorithm'' solves the minimum-cost perfect matching (assignment problem) in $O(n^3)$ time. It maintains a feasible dual solution (vertex potentials) and iteratively finds augmenting paths to grow the matching, adjusting potentials to reveal new tight edges. The Hopcroft–Karp algorithm finds a maximum cardinality matching in $O(\sqrt{|V|} \cdot |E|)$ time by finding shortest augmenting paths in batches.

!! Bipartite Graphs in ML

'''Recommendation Systems.''' User–item interaction data is naturally bipartite: users on one side, items on the other, edges weighted by ratings or clicks. Collaborative filtering decomposes the biadjacency matrix $B$ via techniques like [[Nonnegative Matrix Factorization|Nonnegative Matrix Factorization]] or SVD to learn latent factors for users and items.

'''Bipartite Graph Neural Networks.''' Standard [[Graph Neural Networks|Graph Neural Networks]] can be specialized for bipartite structure. Message passing alternates between the two vertex sets, and the block structure of $A$ means two-hop neighbors always land in the same partition. LightGCN and PinSage exploit this for scalable recommendation.

'''Knowledge Graphs.''' Many [[Knowledge Graphs|Knowledge Graphs]] contain bipartite substructures — for example, the relation "authored_by" induces a bipartite graph between papers and researchers. Link prediction on such subgraphs benefits from bipartite-specific embeddings.

'''Assignment Problems.''' Task allocation, resource scheduling, and bipartite b-matching all reduce to weighted bipartite matching. In ad auctions, advertisers and ad slots form a bipartite graph, and the platform solves a real-time matching problem to maximize revenue.

!! When Bipartite Models Break Down

* ''Forced bipartition on non-bipartite data.'' Real-world interaction graphs often have within-group edges (user–user friendships, item–item similarity). Forcing a bipartite model discards these signals and can degrade performance compared to heterogeneous graph models.

* ''Extreme sparsity.'' When the biadjacency matrix $B$ is very sparse (most users rate very few items), matrix factorization and matching algorithms struggle with poor coverage. Cold-start users or items have no edges at all, making bipartite reasoning vacuous for them.

* ''Dynamic and streaming settings.'' Classical bipartite matching assumes the full graph is known upfront. In online bipartite matching (e.g., real-time ad allocation), vertices on one side arrive sequentially, and irrevocable decisions must be made without future knowledge — the competitive ratio drops to $1 - 1/e$ even with optimal algorithms.

!! Technical Details

''Ramsey-type bounds:'' Every bipartite graph on $n$ vertices in each part has at most $n^2/4$ edges (attained by $K_{n/2,n/2}$), which is the bipartite Turán number.

''Edge coloring:'' By König's edge-coloring theorem, the chromatic index of a bipartite graph equals its maximum degree $\Delta$, unlike general graphs where it can be $\Delta + 1$.

''Permanents and counting:'' The number of perfect matchings in a bipartite graph equals the permanent of its biadjacency matrix. Computing the permanent is #P-complete, but Jerrum, Sinclair, and Vigoda gave an FPRAS via Markov chain Monte Carlo.

''Spectral gap:'' The algebraic connectivity (second-smallest Laplacian eigenvalue) of a connected bipartite graph governs mixing time of random walks. The spectrum's symmetry about zero means bipartite graphs are //not// expanders in the spectral sense unless carefully constructed (e.g., Ramanujan bipartite graphs).

''Total unimodularity:'' The incidence matrix of a bipartite graph is totally unimodular (every square submatrix has determinant $0$, $1$, or $-1$), which is //why// LP relaxations of matching and covering problems yield integer optima.
