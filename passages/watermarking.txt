! Watermarking

<img src="assets/watermarking.svg" alt="Diagram showing the AI watermarking pipeline: token partition into green and red lists, biased sampling, and z-score detection" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Think of how a papermaker in the 1200s pressed a wire design into wet pulp — the resulting watermark was invisible in normal use but appeared when you held the sheet to light. AI watermarking works the same way: during text generation, the model embeds a faint statistical pattern that is invisible to a casual reader but lights up under the right test. Anyone with the secret key can check whether a piece of text was produced by a particular model, enabling provenance tracking and detection of machine-generated content at scale.

---

!! The Core Insight

''AI watermarking'' solves a fundamental attribution problem: once a language model emits a sequence of tokens, nothing inherent to the text marks it as machine-generated. A watermark changes that by //subtly biasing which tokens the model selects// during generation, creating a statistical fingerprint that a detector can recover later.

The dominant approach, introduced by Kirchenbauer et al. (2023), partitions the vocabulary into a ''green list'' and a ''red list'' at each generation step. The partition is determined by hashing the preceding token with a secret key, so the split looks random to anyone without the key. During sampling, green-list tokens receive a small logit boost $\delta$, making them slightly more likely to be chosen. Over a passage of reasonable length, the excess of green tokens becomes statistically detectable — a simple z-test is enough to distinguish watermarked text from unwatermarked text with high confidence.

The beauty is that this bias is //tiny per token// — the quality of any single sentence is barely affected — but //cumulative across the passage//. This is a direct application of the law of large numbers: a small per-token signal becomes overwhelmingly detectable when summed over hundreds of tokens. The approach connects deeply to <<link "Information Theory" "Information Theory">><</link>> and <<link "Shannon Entropy" "Shannon Entropy">><</link>>, because the watermark works by injecting a controlled amount of mutual information between the secret key and the generated text.

---

!! How Green-List Watermarking Works

At each generation step $t$, the model has produced tokens $w_1, \ldots, w_{t-1}$ and is about to sample $w_t$. The watermarking algorithm proceeds in three stages:

''1. Partition the vocabulary.'' A pseudorandom function seeded by a hash of the preceding token $w_{t-1}$ and the secret key $k$ splits the full vocabulary $V$ into two disjoint sets: a green list $G_t$ and a red list $R_t$, typically of equal size. Because the hash depends on $w_{t-1}$ and $k$, the partition changes at every step and is unpredictable without $k$.

''2. Bias the logits.'' Before applying softmax to produce the sampling distribution, the model adds a constant $\delta > 0$ to the logits of every green-list token. This is equivalent to multiplying the probability of each green token by $e^\delta$ before renormalization. The parameter $\delta$ controls the tradeoff between watermark strength and text quality — larger $\delta$ means stronger signal but more distortion of the original distribution.

''3. Sample as usual.'' After the logit bias, standard sampling (greedy, top-$k$, nucleus) proceeds normally. The token selected at step $t$ is more likely to be green than it would have been without the bias.

''Detection'' requires only the secret key $k$ and the suspect text. The detector replays the hashing process to reconstruct $G_t$ at each step, counts how many tokens in the text are green, and performs a one-proportion z-test. Under the null hypothesis (human-written text), about half the tokens should be green by chance. A watermarked passage will have significantly more, and the z-score grows with $\sqrt{T}$ where $T$ is the text length.

This mechanism is closely related to how a <<link "Tokenizer" "Tokenizer">><</link>> segments text — the watermark operates at the token level, not the word level, so the specific tokenization scheme directly affects the partition and detectability.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Let $T$ be the total number of tokens in the suspect text and $|G|$ the count of green-list tokens. Under the null hypothesis $H_0$ (no watermark), each token independently falls in the green list with probability $\gamma$ (typically $\gamma = 0.5$ for an equal split). The test statistic is:

$$z = \frac{|G| - \gamma T}{\sqrt{\gamma(1-\gamma)\,T}}$$

For $\gamma = 0.5$ this simplifies to:

$$z = \frac{|G| - T/2}{\sqrt{T/4}}$$

Under $H_0$, $z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ by the central limit theorem. A threshold such as $z > 4$ gives a false positive rate below $3 \times 10^{-5}$. This is a direct application of <<link "Hypothesis Testing" "Hypothesis Testing">><</link>>.

The logit bias $\delta$ shifts the per-token green probability from $\gamma$ to an effective value $\gamma' > \gamma$. If the original distribution is approximately uniform over $V$, the expected green probability becomes:

$$\gamma' = \frac{\gamma \, e^\delta}{\gamma \, e^\delta + (1 - \gamma)}$$

For $\delta = 2$ and $\gamma = 0.5$, this gives $\gamma' \approx 0.88$. The expected z-score for a watermarked text of length $T$ then scales as:

$$\mathbb{E}[z] = \frac{(\gamma' - \gamma)\,T}{\sqrt{\gamma(1-\gamma)\,T}} = \frac{(\gamma' - \gamma)\sqrt{T}}{\sqrt{\gamma(1-\gamma)}}$$

This grows as $\sqrt{T}$, meaning watermark detectability improves with text length — a passage of 200 tokens is far more reliably classified than one of 25 tokens.

The ''quality cost'' of watermarking can be quantified as the KL divergence between the original distribution $p$ and the watermarked distribution $\tilde{p}$:

$$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p \,\|\, \tilde{p}) = \sum_{w \in V} p(w) \log \frac{p(w)}{\tilde{p}(w)}$$

When the model is highly confident (one token dominates the distribution), the bias $\delta$ barely changes the output and $D_{\mathrm{KL}}$ is near zero. When the model is uncertain and many tokens have similar probability (high entropy), the bias has more effect on the actual choice. This means watermarks distort text quality primarily in ''high-entropy'' generation contexts — creative writing, paraphrasing — and minimally in ''low-entropy'' contexts like factual recall or code.

---

!! Practical Considerations and Variants

''Distortion-free watermarking'' (Christ, Gunn & Zamir, 2023; Kuditipudi et al., 2023) uses a different strategy: instead of biasing logits, it couples the randomness of the sampling process to a pseudorandom sequence derived from the secret key. From the model's perspective, the marginal distribution of each token is unchanged — so there is //zero distortion// on any single sample. Detection works by checking whether the generated tokens are correlated with the pseudorandom sequence. This approach preserves text quality perfectly in expectation but requires longer texts for the same detection power.

''Multi-bit watermarks'' encode not just a binary "watermarked or not" signal but a payload — an identifier for the model version, user, or deployment context. This is analogous to steganography: hiding a message inside the generated text. Multi-bit schemes sacrifice some detection power to embed richer provenance information.

''Robustness attacks'' attempt to remove watermarks by paraphrasing, synonym substitution, or passing text through a second model. Robust watermarking schemes use wider context windows (hashing on multiple preceding tokens rather than just one) or embed redundancy so that partial edits do not erase the signal entirely. The tension between robustness and imperceptibility mirrors the classic information-theoretic tradeoff in channel coding.

''Connection to <<link "Differential Privacy" "Differential Privacy">><</link>>:'' watermarking and differential privacy sit at opposite ends of a spectrum. Differential privacy hides information about individuals by adding noise; watermarking //injects// detectable information by adding structured bias. Both manipulate the randomness of an algorithm's output for a specific trust goal.

---

!! When Watermarking Breaks Down

''Short texts defeat detection.'' Because detectability scales as $\sqrt{T}$, short passages — tweets, one-line answers, brief code snippets — do not accumulate enough green-token excess to produce a statistically significant z-score. For a 25-token passage with $\delta = 2$, the expected z-score may be around 3.8, which is borderline. This is a fundamental information-theoretic limit: the watermark channel has finite capacity per token, and short messages carry too few bits.

''Paraphrase and editing attacks.'' If an adversary runs watermarked text through a second model or manually rephrases enough tokens, the green-list bias is diluted because the replacement tokens are not biased toward the original green partition. Even 20–30% token replacement can push the z-score below the detection threshold. Robust watermarking mitigates this with longer context windows and error-correcting codes, but a sufficiently aggressive rewrite will always destroy the signal — at some point the text is no longer the model's output in any meaningful sense.

''Spoofing and false accusations.'' If the watermarking scheme is reverse-engineered or the secret key leaks, an adversary can //add// a watermark to human-written text, framing it as AI-generated. This is the dual of the robustness problem: not only must watermarks resist removal, they must also resist //injection//. Asymmetric schemes (where only the model provider can embed but anyone can detect) partially address this, but key management and scheme secrecy remain open challenges.

---

!! Technical Details

''Key papers:'' Kirchenbauer et al. (2023), "A Watermark for Large Language Models" (U. Maryland) — introduced green-list/red-list logit biasing. Christ, Gunn & Zamir (2023), "Undetectable Watermarks for Language Models" — distortion-free scheme using coupled randomness. Kuditipudi et al. (2023), "Robust Distortion-free Watermarks for Language Models" (Stanford). Zhao et al. (2023), "Provable Robust Watermarking for AI-Generated Text."

''Detection parameters:'' Typical $\delta$ values range from 1.0 to 2.5. Typical z-score thresholds range from 4.0 to 6.0 (corresponding to false positive rates of $\sim10^{-5}$ to $\sim10^{-9}$). Minimum reliable text length is roughly 50–200 tokens depending on $\delta$.

''Image watermarking:'' Parallel work (Fernandez et al., 2023; Wen et al., 2023) embeds watermarks in diffusion model outputs by modifying the initial noise vector or latent representation. The detection principle is analogous — a statistical test checks for correlation between the image's latent representation and a secret key — but the embedding domain is continuous rather than discrete.

''Regulatory relevance:'' The EU AI Act and the White House Executive Order on AI Safety (Oct 2023) both reference AI-generated content identification, making watermarking a leading technical candidate for compliance. Several major model providers (Google DeepMind SynthID, OpenAI) have deployed watermarking or content credentials in production systems.

''Limitations:'' No watermarking scheme is simultaneously perfectly robust, perfectly imperceptible, and perfectly efficient. This impossibility result echoes the rate-distortion tradeoff in information theory. Open-source models can have watermarking removed by anyone with access to model weights, making watermarking most effective for API-served models.
