! Optimal Transport Theory

<img src="assets/optimal_transport_theory.svg" alt="Diagram showing optimal transport between source and target distributions with cost arrows and Kantorovich duality" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are a logistics company with warehouses full of goods spread across a city, and stores across town that need those goods. Each warehouse has a known supply, each store has a known demand, and shipping between any warehouse-store pair has a cost proportional to distance. Your job: move all the goods so every store is satisfied, at minimum total shipping cost. ''Optimal transport theory'' solves exactly this problem — except the "warehouses" and "stores" are probability distributions, and the "goods" are probability mass. Born in 1781 with Gaspard Monge's problem of moving dirt between piles, and reformulated by Leonid Kantorovich in 1942 using linear programming, optimal transport provides the mathematical foundation for ''Wasserstein distances'' — the metrics that revolutionized training of <<link "GANs" "GANs">><</link>> and now underpin generative modeling, domain adaptation, and distribution comparison across machine learning.

!! The Core Insight

Classical divergences like <<link "KL Divergence" "KL Divergence">><</link>> compare distributions pointwise: they ask "at each point, how different are the densities?" This breaks catastrophically when distributions have non-overlapping support — KL divergence becomes infinite, providing zero gradient signal. Optimal transport instead asks: "what is the //cheapest way// to reshape one distribution into the other?" This geometric perspective respects the underlying metric structure of the space. Two distributions that are nearby in space but have disjoint support still have a small transport cost, because moving mass a short distance is cheap.

The ''Wasserstein distance'' (or ''Earth Mover's Distance'') measures exactly this minimum reshaping cost. Unlike KL divergence, it is a true metric — symmetric, satisfies the triangle inequality, and is finite whenever the distributions have finite moments. This is why Wasserstein GANs train more stably: the critic provides smooth, informative gradients even when the generator distribution and the real data distribution do not overlap.

!! The Monge and Kantorovich Formulations

Monge's original problem (1781) seeks a //deterministic map// $T: \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Y}$ that pushes the source measure $\mu$ onto the target measure $\nu$ — written $T_{\#}\mu = \nu$ — while minimizing transportation cost:

$$\inf_{T:\, T_{\#}\mu = \nu} \int_{\mathcal{X}} c(x, T(x)) \, d\mu(x)$$

where $c(x, y)$ is the cost of moving a unit of mass from $x$ to $y$. The constraint $T_{\#}\mu = \nu$ means that for any measurable set $B$, the total mass that $T$ sends into $B$ equals $\nu(B)$.

Monge's formulation has a fatal flaw: a deterministic map cannot split mass. If $\mu$ is a single point mass and $\nu$ is spread across two locations, no valid $T$ exists. ''Kantorovich's relaxation'' (1942) solves this by allowing mass to split. Instead of a map, seek a ''coupling'' (or ''transport plan'') — a joint measure $\gamma$ on $\mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{Y}$ with marginals $\mu$ and $\nu$:

$$\inf_{\gamma \in \Pi(\mu, \nu)} \int_{\mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{Y}} c(x, y) \, d\gamma(x, y)$$

where $\Pi(\mu, \nu)$ is the set of all joint distributions with the correct marginals. This is an infinite-dimensional <<link "Linear Programming" "Linear Programming">><</link>> problem — and it always has a solution (by compactness of $\Pi(\mu, \nu)$ in the weak topology).

When $c(x,y) = \|x - y\|^p$, the optimal cost defines the $p$-Wasserstein distance:

$$W_p(\mu, \nu) = \left( \inf_{\gamma \in \Pi(\mu, \nu)} \int \|x - y\|^p \, d\gamma(x, y) \right)^{1/p}$$

The most common choices are $p=1$ (Earth Mover's Distance) and $p=2$ (squared Euclidean transport cost, with rich geometric structure).

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''Kantorovich duality'' transforms the primal (minimization over couplings) into a dual (maximization over functions). For $W_1$:

$$W_1(\mu, \nu) = \sup_{\|f\|_L \leq 1} \left[ \int f \, d\mu - \int f \, d\nu \right]$$

where the supremum is over all 1-Lipschitz functions $f$ (i.e., $|f(x) - f(y)| \leq \|x - y\|$). This is the formulation that Wasserstein GANs exploit: the critic network approximates the optimal dual variable $f$, and the difference $\mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mu}[f(x)] - \mathbb{E}_{y \sim \nu}[f(y)]$ estimates $W_1$.

For general $p$, the dual takes the form:

$$W_p^p(\mu, \nu) = \sup_{\phi, \psi} \left[ \int \phi \, d\mu + \int \psi \, d\nu \right]$$

subject to $\phi(x) + \psi(y) \leq c(x, y)$ for all $x, y$. The optimal $\phi$ and $\psi$ are called ''Kantorovich potentials'', and they encode the shadow prices of the transport constraints.

''Brenier's theorem'' (1991) provides the beautiful result that when $c(x,y) = \|x-y\|^2$ and $\mu$ is absolutely continuous, the optimal transport map exists, is unique, and equals the gradient of a convex function: $T(x) = \nabla \varphi(x)$. This connects optimal transport to <<link "Convex Optimization" "Convex Optimization">><</link>> and the Monge-Ampère PDE.

''Entropic regularization'' adds a penalty $\varepsilon H(\gamma)$ (negative entropy of the coupling) to the transport cost, yielding the ''Sinkhorn distance'':

$$W_\varepsilon(\mu, \nu) = \inf_{\gamma \in \Pi(\mu, \nu)} \int c \, d\gamma - \varepsilon H(\gamma)$$

This transforms the linear program into a strictly convex problem solvable by the ''Sinkhorn algorithm'' — alternating row and column normalizations of the kernel matrix $K_{ij} = e^{-c_{ij}/\varepsilon}$. Complexity drops from $O(n^3 \log n)$ (network simplex) to $O(n^2 / \varepsilon^2)$, making optimal transport practical in high dimensions.

!! Optimal Transport in Machine Learning

''Wasserstein GANs'' (WGAN): The original GAN training minimizes Jensen-Shannon divergence between generator and real distributions, which saturates when supports don't overlap — causing vanishing gradients and mode collapse. WGANs instead minimize $W_1$, using a critic constrained to be 1-Lipschitz (via weight clipping or gradient penalty). The Wasserstein loss provides meaningful gradients everywhere, correlating with sample quality.

''Domain adaptation'': Optimal transport aligns source and target feature distributions by learning a transport map between domains. The ''joint distribution optimal transport'' (JDOT) framework simultaneously learns the classifier and the transport, outperforming moment-matching approaches.

''Generative modeling'': Sliced Wasserstein distances project distributions onto random 1D directions (where $W_p$ has a closed-form solution via sorting), enabling scalable generative training. Diffusion models implicitly perform transport: the score function $\nabla \log p_t(x)$ relates to the velocity field of the optimal transport between noise and data distributions.

''Barycenters'': The Wasserstein barycenter of distributions $\mu_1, \ldots, \mu_K$ is $\arg\min_\nu \sum_k w_k W_2^2(\mu_k, \nu)$. This provides a principled way to average distributions — used in clustering, multi-task learning, and Bayesian model averaging.

!! When Optimal Transport Breaks Down

''The curse of dimensionality hits hard.'' The sample complexity of estimating $W_p$ from $n$ samples scales as $O(n^{-1/d})$ where $d$ is the dimension — exponentially worse as $d$ grows. In 100 dimensions, you need astronomical sample sizes for accurate Wasserstein estimates. This is why practical applications use either entropic regularization (which biases the estimate but reduces variance), sliced Wasserstein projections (reducing to 1D), or mini-batch approximations. Raw Wasserstein distances are unreliable in high-dimensional settings without these modifications.

''Computational cost remains prohibitive at scale.'' Even with Sinkhorn regularization, computing transport between $n$-point distributions requires $O(n^2)$ memory for the cost matrix and $O(n^2)$ per Sinkhorn iteration. For $n = 100{,}000$ samples, this means storing and iterating over a $10^{10}$-entry matrix. Mini-batch optimal transport, tree-based approximations, and low-rank factorizations partially address this, but there is always a tension between statistical fidelity and computational tractability.

''The choice of ground cost $c(x,y)$ is critical and often arbitrary.'' Optimal transport requires a meaningful cost function on the underlying space, but in feature spaces of neural networks, Euclidean distance may not reflect semantic similarity. Two images that differ by a single pixel shift have small Euclidean distance but may have wildly different feature representations. If the ground metric does not capture the geometry you care about, the Wasserstein distance measures the wrong thing — and no amount of algorithmic sophistication can fix a misspecified cost.

!! Technical Details

''Monge problem'': posed 1781 for moving soil between excavation sites. ''Kantorovich relaxation'': 1942, introduced linear programming duality. Kantorovich received the 1975 Nobel Prize in Economics for this work (shared with Koopmans).

''Wasserstein metrics'': $W_p$ for $p \geq 1$. The space of probability measures with finite $p$-th moment equipped with $W_p$ is itself a complete metric space (the ''Wasserstein space'' $\mathcal{P}_p(\mathcal{X})$). Convergence in $W_p$ is equivalent to weak convergence plus convergence of $p$-th moments.

''Benamou-Brenier formula'' (2000): dynamic reformulation as a fluid mechanics problem — $W_2^2(\mu, \nu) = \inf_{(\rho_t, v_t)} \int_0^1 \int \|v_t\|^2 \rho_t \, dx \, dt$ subject to the continuity equation $\partial_t \rho + \nabla \cdot (\rho v) = 0$. This connects optimal transport to fluid dynamics and is the basis for continuous normalizing flows.

''Sinkhorn algorithm'': converges linearly at rate $O(e^{-\varepsilon^{-1}})$. GPU-friendly due to matrix operations. The regularized problem converges to the true $W_p$ as $\varepsilon \to 0$ but becomes numerically unstable — log-domain stabilization is essential.

''Sliced Wasserstein'': $SW_p(\mu, \nu) = \left(\int_{S^{d-1}} W_p^p(\theta_{\#}\mu, \theta_{\#}\nu) \, d\sigma(\theta)\right)^{1/p}$, where $\theta_{\#}$ projects onto direction $\theta$. In 1D, $W_p$ is computed via quantile functions: $W_p^p = \int_0^1 |F_\mu^{-1}(t) - F_\nu^{-1}(t)|^p \, dt$.
