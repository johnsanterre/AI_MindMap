! Riemannian Geometry

<img src="assets/riemannian_geometry.svg" alt="Diagram showing a curved manifold with tangent plane, geodesic path between two points, and parallel transport of vectors along a curve" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are an ant living on the surface of a bowling ball. You have never seen flat ground. When you walk "straight ahead," you trace a great circle — the shortest path between two points on the sphere. You can measure angles between directions you might walk, and you notice something strange: if you carry a compass needle along a closed loop, it comes back rotated. That rotation is //curvature//, and everything the ant experiences — distances, shortest paths, how directions twist — is governed by a single object called the ''metric tensor''. Riemannian geometry is the mathematics of measuring things on curved spaces, and it turns out that much of modern machine learning quietly lives on curved spaces rather than flat ones.

!! The Core Insight

A ''Riemannian manifold'' $(M, g)$ is a smooth manifold $M$ equipped with a ''metric tensor'' $g$ — a smoothly varying inner product on each tangent space $T_pM$. The metric tensor is the master object: from $g$ alone, you can derive distances between points, angles between tangent vectors, volumes of regions, shortest paths (geodesics), and curvature.

On flat Euclidean space $\mathbb{R}^n$, the metric is just the identity matrix $g_{ij} = \delta_{ij}$, and everything reduces to familiar dot products and straight lines. On a curved manifold, $g_{ij}(p)$ varies from point to point, and "straight lines" become curved geodesics that follow the surface's shape. The key insight is that all geometric notions — distance, angle, curvature, volume — are //intrinsic// to the surface and depend only on $g$, not on how the surface sits inside some ambient space.

!! Metrics, Geodesics, and Curvature

The ''metric tensor'' $g$ at a point $p$ takes two tangent vectors $u, v \in T_pM$ and returns a real number $g_p(u, v)$ — their inner product. In local coordinates $(x^1, \ldots, x^n)$, the metric is an $n \times n$ positive definite matrix $g_{ij}(x)$, and the squared infinitesimal distance is:
$$ds^2 = g_{ij}\, dx^i\, dx^j$$

A ''geodesic'' is a curve that locally minimises length — the curved-space analogue of a straight line. On a sphere, geodesics are great circles. On a saddle surface, they curve in unexpected ways. Geodesics are the paths a particle would follow if no forces acted on it except the constraint of staying on the manifold.

''Parallel transport'' moves a tangent vector along a curve while keeping it "as constant as possible" given the curvature. On a flat surface, the transported vector returns to its original orientation after a closed loop. On a curved surface, it comes back rotated — and the amount of rotation is precisely the ''curvature'' enclosed by the loop.

The ''Riemann curvature tensor'' $R^i_{\ jkl}$ is the master curvature object. It measures how much parallel transport around an infinitesimal loop rotates a vector. From it, one derives the ''Ricci curvature'' $R_{ij}$ (an average over directions) and the ''scalar curvature'' $R$ (a single number summarising curvature at a point).

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

In local coordinates, the metric tensor is a symmetric positive definite matrix field:
$$g = [g_{ij}(x)], \quad g_{ij} = g_{ji}, \quad g_{ij}v^i v^j > 0 \text{ for all } v \neq 0$$

The ''Christoffel symbols'' encode how the coordinate basis vectors change from point to point, derived entirely from $g$:
$$\Gamma^k_{ij} = \frac{1}{2} g^{kl}\left(\frac{\partial g_{il}}{\partial x^j} + \frac{\partial g_{jl}}{\partial x^i} - \frac{\partial g_{ij}}{\partial x^l}\right)$$

The ''geodesic equation'' describes shortest paths:
$$\frac{d^2 x^k}{dt^2} + \Gamma^k_{ij} \frac{dx^i}{dt} \frac{dx^j}{dt} = 0$$

This is a second-order ODE: given an initial point $p$ and initial velocity $v$, there is a unique geodesic. The ''exponential map'' $\text{Exp}_p: T_pM \to M$ maps a tangent vector $v$ to the point reached by following the geodesic from $p$ in direction $v$ for unit time. Its inverse, the ''logarithmic map'' $\text{Log}_p: M \to T_pM$, maps a point $q$ back to the tangent vector whose geodesic reaches $q$.

The ''Riemann curvature tensor'' is:
$$R^i_{\ jkl} = \frac{\partial \Gamma^i_{jl}}{\partial x^k} - \frac{\partial \Gamma^i_{jk}}{\partial x^l} + \Gamma^i_{mk}\Gamma^m_{jl} - \Gamma^i_{ml}\Gamma^m_{jk}$$

The ''Ricci tensor'' contracts one index: $R_{jl} = R^i_{\ jil}$. The ''scalar curvature'' contracts again: $R = g^{jl} R_{jl}$.

The ''geodesic distance'' between two points is:
$$d(p, q) = \inf_{\gamma} \int_0^1 \sqrt{g_{\gamma(t)}(\dot{\gamma}(t), \dot{\gamma}(t))}\, dt$$

where the infimum is over all smooth curves $\gamma$ connecting $p$ to $q$.

!! Riemannian Geometry in Machine Learning

''Natural gradient descent:'' the parameter space of a statistical model is not flat — the [[Fisher information matrix|Information Geometry]] $F(\theta)$ defines a Riemannian metric on it. Standard [[gradient descent|Gradient Descent]] treats parameter space as Euclidean and updates $\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \nabla L$. The ''[[natural gradient|Natural Gradient]]'' corrects for the curvature:
$$\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta\, F(\theta)^{-1} \nabla L(\theta)$$
This is a Riemannian gradient step: it moves in the steepest direction as measured by the Fisher metric, not the Euclidean metric. The natural gradient is invariant to reparameterisation — a fundamental advantage.

''Optimisation on manifolds:'' many ML parameters are naturally constrained to manifolds: orthogonal matrices ($O(n)$), rotation matrices ($SO(3)$), low-rank matrices, positive definite matrices (the ''SPD manifold''). Rather than optimising in Euclidean space with constraints, Riemannian optimisation performs gradient descent directly on the manifold using the exponential map for updates and parallel transport for momentum:
$$\theta_{t+1} = \text{Exp}_{\theta_t}(-\eta\, \text{grad}\, f(\theta_t))$$

''Hyperbolic embeddings:'' hierarchical data (taxonomies, social networks, parse trees) embeds more naturally in ''hyperbolic space'' — a Riemannian manifold with constant negative curvature — than in Euclidean space. The Poincar&#233; ball model uses the metric $g_{ij} = \frac{4}{(1 - \|x\|^2)^2}\delta_{ij}$, where distances grow exponentially near the boundary, providing room for exponentially branching trees.

''SPD matrix manifolds:'' covariance matrices, diffusion tensors in brain imaging, and kernel matrices all live on the manifold of symmetric positive definite matrices. The standard Riemannian metric is the affine-invariant metric: $\langle A, B \rangle_P = \text{tr}(P^{-1}A P^{-1}B)$. Computing means and performing classification on this manifold (rather than in Euclidean space) respects the geometry and gives better results.

''The [[loss landscape|Loss Landscape]]:'' the Hessian of the loss function at a critical point defines a local metric on parameter space. Understanding the Riemannian geometry of the loss landscape — its geodesics, curvature, and connected components of low-loss regions — illuminates why neural networks train and generalise.

''[[Geometric deep learning|Geometric Deep Learning]]:'' neural networks on meshes, point clouds, and graphs must respect the geometry of the underlying domain. The Laplace-Beltrami operator, a Riemannian generalisation of the Laplacian, is the basis of spectral graph convolutions.

!! When Riemannian Methods Break Down

''(1) Computational cost of geometric operations:'' every Riemannian gradient step requires computing the metric tensor (or its inverse), Christoffel symbols, exponential maps, and parallel transport. For the Fisher information matrix in neural networks with millions of parameters, even storing the full $n \times n$ matrix is infeasible ($n = 10^8$ means $10^{16}$ entries). Approximations like K-FAC or diagonal Fisher reduce cost but sacrifice the geometric faithfulness that motivated Riemannian methods in the first place. The exponential map rarely has a closed-form expression outside well-studied manifolds (spheres, hyperbolic spaces, SPD matrices), requiring expensive numerical ODE solvers.

''(2) Choosing the right metric:'' the power of Riemannian geometry depends entirely on choosing a metric that captures the problem's structure. For parameter spaces, the Fisher information metric is theoretically motivated but alternatives exist (Wasserstein metric, Hessian of the loss). For data manifolds, the metric is unknown and must be estimated from finite, noisy samples. A poor metric choice can make Riemannian methods perform worse than flat Euclidean baselines, and there is no universal procedure for selecting the correct metric from data.

''(3) Local nature of Riemannian tools:'' the exponential map, geodesics, and curvature are all //local// concepts — they describe the manifold near a point. For globally complex manifolds (those with nontrivial [[topology|Topology]], multiple connected components, or high genus), local Riemannian tools may miss important global structure. The exponential map is only a diffeomorphism within the ''injectivity radius'' — beyond that, geodesics may cross and the map becomes ill-defined. In high-dimensional manifolds typical of ML, the injectivity radius can be very small relative to the manifold's diameter, severely limiting the useful range of local Riemannian coordinates.

!! Technical Details

''Historical roots:'' Bernhard Riemann introduced the concept in his 1854 habilitation lecture //Ueber die Hypothesen, welche der Geometrie zu Grunde liegen//. The formalism was developed by Christoffel (1869), Ricci-Curbastro and Levi-Civita (1900), and became central to Einstein's general relativity (1915). Riemannian optimisation entered ML through Amari's ''information geometry'' (1985) and the natural gradient (1998).

''Key manifolds in ML:'' the sphere $S^{n-1}$ (normalised embeddings, directional statistics); the Stiefel manifold $V_k(\mathbb{R}^n)$ (orthonormal $k$-frames, used in subspace learning); the Grassmann manifold $\text{Gr}(k, n)$ (k-dimensional subspaces); the hyperbolic space $\mathbb{H}^n$ (hierarchical embeddings); the SPD manifold $\mathcal{S}^n_{++}$ (covariance matrices, diffusion tensors).

''Software:'' Geomstats (Python library for Riemannian geometry in ML), Geoopt (Riemannian optimisation in PyTorch), Manopt (MATLAB/Python manifold optimisation), McTorch (manifold optimisation in PyTorch).

''Fr&#233;chet mean:'' the Riemannian analogue of the Euclidean mean. For points $\{p_1, \ldots, p_N\}$ on a manifold $(M, g)$:
$$\bar{p} = \arg\min_{q \in M} \sum_{i=1}^N d(q, p_i)^2$$
Existence and uniqueness depend on curvature bounds and the spread of the data.

''Sectional curvature:'' for a 2-plane $\sigma$ spanned by $u, v$ in $T_pM$:
$$K(\sigma) = \frac{g(R(u,v)v, u)}{g(u,u)g(v,v) - g(u,v)^2}$$
Positive sectional curvature (spheres) makes geodesics converge; negative (hyperbolic space) makes them diverge; zero (flat space) keeps them parallel.
