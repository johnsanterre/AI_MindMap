! Extreme Value Theory
<img src="assets/extreme_value_theory.svg" alt="Extreme Value Theory" width="800">

Imagine you run a chain of warehouses and need to know: what is the //worst// flood your facility might face in a hundred years? You don't care about the average river level — you care about the single most extreme event. ''Extreme Value Theory'' (EVT) is the branch of [[Statistics|Statistics]] that provides rigorous answers to exactly this kind of question. Where the [[Central Limit Theorem|Central Limit Theorem]] tells you what happens when you //average// many observations, EVT tells you what happens when you take the //maximum// (or minimum). And just as the CLT yields a universal limiting distribution (the Gaussian), EVT yields its own small family of universal limits — no matter what the original data looked like.

!! The Core Insight

The deep surprise of EVT is that ''extreme values are not lawless''. Regardless of whether your data comes from a Gaussian, an exponential, a Pareto, or almost any other well-behaved distribution, the distribution of the maximum of $n$ independent samples, properly rescaled, converges to one of only //three// possible shapes. This is the ''Fisher-Tippett-Gnedenko theorem'' — the CLT for extremes. The shape is governed by a single parameter $\xi$ (xi), which controls the tail: light, heavy, or bounded. You don't need to know the parent distribution to model extremes; you only need to estimate $\xi$.

!! The CLT for Extremes

The ''Fisher-Tippett theorem'' states that if sequences $a_n > 0$ and $b_n$ exist such that the rescaled maximum $(M_n - b_n)/a_n$ converges in distribution, the limit must be the ''Generalized Extreme Value'' (GEV) distribution. This is the universal attractor for block maxima, just as the Gaussian is the universal attractor for sums. The GEV unifies three classical types:

* ''Gumbel'' ($\xi = 0$): the domain of attraction for light-tailed distributions — Gaussian, exponential, log-normal. Tails decay exponentially.
* ''Fréchet'' ($\xi > 0$): the domain of attraction for heavy-tailed distributions — Pareto, Cauchy, Student-$t$. Tails decay polynomially, meaning catastrophic outliers are far more likely.
* ''Weibull'' ($\xi < 0$): the domain of attraction for distributions with a bounded upper tail — uniform, beta. The maximum has a finite upper endpoint.

Every [[Probability Distributions|probability distribution]] that satisfies mild regularity conditions belongs to exactly one of these three domains of attraction. This classification is one of the most powerful results in all of statistics.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The GEV cumulative distribution function is:

$$G(x;\,\mu,\sigma,\xi) = \exp\!\left\{-\left[1 + \xi\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)\right]^{-1/\xi}\right\}$$

for $1 + \xi(x-\mu)/\sigma > 0$, where $\mu$ is the location parameter, $\sigma > 0$ is the scale parameter, and $\xi$ is the ''shape parameter'' that determines the tail type. When $\xi = 0$ the expression is interpreted as the limit $G(x) = \exp(-e^{-(x-\mu)/\sigma})$, the Gumbel distribution.

For threshold exceedances (the ''peaks-over-threshold'' approach), the natural companion is the ''Generalized Pareto Distribution'' (GPD):

$$H(y;\,\sigma,\xi) = 1 - \left(1 + \xi\,\frac{y}{\sigma}\right)^{-1/\xi}$$

for $y > 0$ and $1 + \xi\,y/\sigma > 0$. The Pickands-Balkema-de Haan theorem guarantees that exceedances over a sufficiently high threshold $u$ converge to the GPD, with the //same// shape parameter $\xi$ as the corresponding GEV. This duality means you can either split data into blocks and fit the GEV to block maxima, or set a high threshold and fit the GPD to exceedances — both estimate the same tail behavior.

The [[Random Variables|random variable]] $X$ belongs to the domain of attraction of the Fréchet distribution if and only if $\bar{F}(x) = x^{-1/\xi}L(x)$ for a slowly varying function $L$, connecting EVT directly to the theory of regularly varying functions and heavy tails.

!! EVT in ML

EVT is quietly becoming essential across machine learning:

* ''Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection'': Fit a GEV or GPD to the tail of in-distribution activation scores. A new sample whose score falls deep into the extreme tail is flagged as OOD. This is more principled than ad hoc thresholds because EVT provides a //statistical model// for the tail — see [[Distribution Shift|Distribution Shift]].
* ''[[Anomaly Detection|Anomaly Detection]]'': The same tail-modeling logic applies. Instead of choosing an arbitrary percentile cutoff, EVT gives a probability that an observation is more extreme than anything in the training set.
* ''Tail risk in finance'': Value-at-Risk (VaR) and Expected Shortfall (ES) at extreme quantiles (99.9%) require accurate tail models. Fitting a Gaussian grossly underestimates losses; EVT's GPD fit to losses above a threshold captures the true heavy-tailed risk.
* ''Robustness certification'': Bounding the //worst-case// perturbation an adversary might find uses EVT to estimate the distribution of maximum adversarial loss over input neighborhoods.
* ''Calibration of extreme predictions'': Weather, climate, and engineering models need calibrated probabilities for rare events (Category 5 hurricanes, 500-year floods). EVT provides the principled extrapolation framework.

!! When EVT Breaks Down

'''1. Not enough tail data.''' EVT is asymptotic theory — it describes what happens as $n \to \infty$. With small samples the block maxima are noisy, the GPD threshold choice is fragile, and $\xi$ estimates have high variance. A mean excess plot may be unreadable with fewer than a few hundred exceedances, and misestimating $\xi$ by even $0.1$ changes tail probabilities by orders of magnitude.

'''2. Violations of independence and stationarity.''' The Fisher-Tippett theorem assumes i.i.d. data. Time series with clusters of extremes (volatility bursts in finance, heatwave sequences in climate) violate this. Naively applying EVT to dependent data inflates the effective sample size, yielding overconfident tail estimates. Remedies like the ''extremal index'' or declustering exist but add complexity and assumptions.

'''3. Choosing the wrong approach or threshold.''' Block maxima (GEV) wastes data by keeping only one extreme per block. POT (GPD) is more data-efficient but requires picking a threshold $u$: too low and the GPD approximation fails; too high and you have too few exceedances. There is no universally correct choice, and results can be sensitive. The mean excess plot and parameter stability plots guide the choice but are subjective.

!! Technical Details

* ''Return levels'': The $T$-year return level $x_T$ satisfies $G(x_T) = 1 - 1/T$. Under the GEV: $x_T = \mu - \frac{\sigma}{\xi}\left[1 - (-\log(1-1/T))^{-\xi}\right]$ for $\xi \neq 0$.
* ''Mean excess plot'': Plot of $e(u) = E[X - u \mid X > u]$ vs. $u$. A linear mean excess plot signals GPD-type tails. Upward slope indicates $\xi > 0$ (heavy tail); flat indicates $\xi = 0$ (Gumbel); downward slope indicates $\xi < 0$ (bounded tail).
* ''Hill estimator'': For $\xi > 0$, estimates the tail index from the $k$ largest order statistics: $\hat{\xi}_{\text{Hill}} = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{i=1}^{k}\log X_{(n-i+1)} - \log X_{(n-k)}$. Simple but only valid for positive $\xi$ and sensitive to choice of $k$.
* ''Pickands estimator'': Non-parametric, works for all $\xi$: $\hat{\xi}_{\text{Pickands}} = \frac{1}{\log 2}\log\frac{X_{(n-k+1)} - X_{(n-2k+1)}}{X_{(n-2k+1)} - X_{(n-4k+1)}}$. More general than Hill but higher variance.
* ''Block maxima vs. POT'': Block maxima fits the GEV to the maximum of each block (e.g., annual maxima). POT fits the GPD to all exceedances above threshold $u$. POT is generally preferred for efficiency, but block maxima avoids threshold selection issues.
* ''Domains of attraction'': A distribution $F$ is in the domain of attraction of $G_\xi$ (written $F \in \text{MDA}(G_\xi)$) if the rescaled maximum converges to $G_\xi$. The ''von Mises conditions'' provide sufficient conditions: e.g., $F \in \text{MDA}(\text{Gumbel})$ if the hazard rate $\frac{f(x)}{1-F(x)} \to c > 0$ as $x \to x^*$.
* ''Profile likelihood'': For GEV/GPD fitting, profile likelihood confidence intervals for $\xi$ and return levels are preferred over normal approximations because the likelihood surface is often asymmetric, especially when $\xi$ is near zero.
