! Stochastic Variance Reduction
<img src="assets/stochastic_variance_reduction.svg" alt="Diagram comparing SGD's noisy path vs SVRG's smooth variance-reduced path, with the control variate correction formula and method variants SVRG, SAGA, SARAH, Katyusha" style="max-width:100%; margin:1em 0;">

Imagine you are trying to estimate the average height of trees in a forest. You could randomly sample trees, but each measurement is noisy — some trees are unusually tall, others stunted. Now suppose a surveyor already measured every tree last month. You can take your new random sample and ''correct'' it using the surveyor's old data: subtract the old measurement of the same tree and add back the old average. Your estimate barely wobbles. That is exactly what variance reduction does to stochastic gradients.

!! The Core Insight

<<link "Stochastic Gradient Descent" "Stochastic Gradient Descent">><</link>> uses a single data point (or mini-batch) to estimate the full gradient, which introduces ''variance'' that forces the learning rate to shrink over time and limits convergence to a sub-linear $O(1/T)$ rate. Johnson & Zhang (2013) showed that by periodically computing the ''full gradient'' at a reference point and using it as a ''control variate'', you can construct a stochastic gradient estimator whose variance shrinks to zero as the iterates approach the optimum. The payoff: ''linear convergence'' — exponentially fast — for strongly convex problems, using only $O(n)$ gradient evaluations per epoch (where $n$ is dataset size), matching full <<link "Gradient Descent" "Gradient Descent">><</link>> in rate while keeping the per-iteration cost of SGD.

!! How Control Variates Tame the Noise

The key mechanism is borrowed from Monte Carlo simulation. A ''control variate'' is a random variable with known expectation that is correlated with your noisy estimate. You subtract the control variate and add its known mean, cancelling much of the noise.

In SVRG, at the start of each ''epoch'', you compute the full gradient $\tilde{\mu} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \nabla f_i(\tilde{w})$ at a snapshot point $\tilde{w}$. Then for each stochastic step with random index $i$:

$$v_t = \nabla f_i(w_t) - \nabla f_i(\tilde{w}) + \tilde{\mu}$$

The term $\nabla f_i(\tilde{w})$ acts as the control variate. Because $\mathbb{E}_i[\nabla f_i(\tilde{w})] = \tilde{\mu}$, the estimator $v_t$ is ''unbiased'': $\mathbb{E}[v_t] = \nabla F(w_t)$. But the variance is dramatically reduced: the two stochastic terms $\nabla f_i(w_t)$ and $\nabla f_i(\tilde{w})$ are highly correlated (same data point $i$, nearby parameters), so their difference has small variance. As $w_t \to w^*$ and $\tilde{w} \to w^*$, the variance goes to zero.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

For a finite-sum problem $F(w) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n f_i(w)$ with each $f_i$ being $L$-smooth and $F$ being $\mu$-strongly convex (condition number $\kappa = L/\mu$):

''SGD convergence:'' With decreasing step size $\eta_t = O(1/t)$:
$$\mathbb{E}[F(w_T) - F(w^*)] = O\!\left(\frac{1}{T}\right)$$

''SVRG convergence:'' With constant step size $\eta = \frac{1}{2L(1 + 4L/\mu)}$ and inner loop length $m = O(\kappa)$:
$$\mathbb{E}[\|w_s - w^*\|^2] \leq \rho^s \|w_0 - w^*\|^2, \quad \rho < 1$$

The convergence rate $\rho$ depends on the condition number: $\rho = \frac{1}{\mu \eta (1 - 2L\eta) m} + \frac{2L\eta}{1 - 2L\eta}$. Setting $m = O(n)$ and $\eta = O(1/L)$ gives $\rho = O(n/(n + \kappa))$, so the total gradient evaluations to reach $\epsilon$-accuracy is $O((n + \kappa)\log(1/\epsilon))$ — compared to $O(n\kappa\log(1/\epsilon))$ for full gradient descent and $O(1/\epsilon)$ for SGD.

''Variance of the corrected estimator:''
$$\text{Var}(v_t) = \mathbb{E}[\|\nabla f_i(w_t) - \nabla f_i(\tilde{w})\|^2] \leq 2L(F(w_t) - F(w^*) + F(\tilde{w}) - F(w^*))$$

This bound (using smoothness) shows variance vanishes as both $w_t$ and $\tilde{w}$ approach $w^*$.

!! Method Variants and Practical Usage

''SVRG (Johnson & Zhang, 2013):'' Periodic full gradient at a snapshot point. Simple, low memory ($O(d)$ extra storage for $\tilde{\mu}$ and $\tilde{w}$). Must choose inner loop length $m$.

''SAGA (Defazio et al., 2014):'' Stores one gradient per data point ($O(nd)$ memory). Updates the table incrementally — no periodic full gradient pass. Better for streaming or online settings. Update:
$$v_t = \nabla f_i(w_t) - g_i^{\text{stored}} + \bar{g}, \quad \text{then } g_i^{\text{stored}} \leftarrow \nabla f_i(w_t)$$

''SARAH (Nguyen et al., 2017):'' Uses recursive variance reduction:
$$v_t = \nabla f_i(w_t) - \nabla f_i(w_{t-1}) + v_{t-1}$$
Biased estimator but with inner product property $\langle v_t, \nabla F(w_t)\rangle \geq 0$ that guarantees descent. Tighter variance bounds than SVRG.

''Katyusha (Allen-Zhu, 2017):'' Combines SVRG with Nesterov <<link "Momentum" "Momentum">><</link>>, achieving the optimal rate $O((n + n^{3/4}\sqrt{\kappa})\log(1/\epsilon))$ — provably faster than SVRG alone for ill-conditioned problems.

''Practical considerations:'' These methods shine for convex problems (logistic regression, SVMs, structured prediction) where $n$ is large and $\kappa$ is moderate. For deep learning, the non-convexity and the dominance of mini-batch parallelism on GPUs have limited their adoption, though variance-reduced methods appear in reinforcement learning (SVRPG) and federated learning (SCAFFOLD). <<link "Learning Rate Scheduling" "Learning Rate Scheduling">><</link>> becomes simpler with variance reduction because a ''constant'' step size suffices.

!! When Variance Reduction Breaks Down

''Non-convex landscapes:'' The linear convergence guarantee requires strong convexity. In deep learning, the loss surface has saddle points, flat regions, and multiple minima. SVRG-style methods can still help empirically, but lose their theoretical edge. The periodic full gradient computation becomes expensive for large models, and the snapshot point $\tilde{w}$ may be far from useful in a rapidly changing loss landscape.

''Memory-constrained settings:'' SAGA requires storing one gradient vector per training example — $O(nd)$ memory, which is prohibitive for high-dimensional problems (e.g., $n = 10^6$ images, $d = 10^6$ parameters). SVRG avoids this but requires the full-gradient pass. In practice, neither is free: you either pay in memory or in a periodic expensive computation.

''GPU-parallel training:'' Modern deep learning relies on large mini-batches processed in parallel on GPUs. The variance of mini-batch SGD already decreases as $O(1/B)$ with batch size $B$, partially solving the variance problem by brute force. Variance reduction methods add computational overhead (two gradient evaluations per step in SVRG) that does not parallelize as naturally, making them less attractive when hardware parallelism is abundant.

!! Technical Details

''Foundational papers:''
- Schmidt, Le Roux & Bach (2013) — SAG: the first incremental gradient method with linear convergence for finite sums.
- Johnson & Zhang (2013) — SVRG: introduced the periodic full-gradient control variate approach.
- Defazio, Bach & Lacoste-Julien (2014) — SAGA: unified incremental gradient framework.
- Nguyen, Liu, Scheinberg & Takac (2017) — SARAH: recursive variance reduction.
- Allen-Zhu (2017) — Katyusha: optimal accelerated variance reduction.

''Relationship to <<link "Convex Optimization" "Convex Optimization">><</link>>:'' Variance reduction bridges the gap between stochastic and deterministic optimization. For strongly convex finite-sum problems, SVRG achieves the same linear rate as full gradient descent but with $O(n + \kappa)$ gradient evaluations per log-factor of accuracy improvement, vs. $O(n\kappa)$ for GD.

''Proximal extensions:'' All major VR methods have proximal variants (Prox-SVRG, Prox-SAGA) that handle $\ell_1$ regularization and other non-smooth penalties by replacing the gradient step with a proximal operator.

''Non-convex variants:'' Reddi et al. (2016) and Allen-Zhu & Hazan (2016) extended SVRG to non-convex settings, proving convergence to stationary points at rate $O(n^{2/3}/\epsilon)$ — faster than SGD's $O(1/\epsilon^2)$.

''Connection to federated learning:'' SCAFFOLD (Karimireddy et al., 2020) applies the SVRG control variate idea across distributed clients, using local vs. global gradient differences to correct for client drift — a direct descendant of variance reduction theory.
