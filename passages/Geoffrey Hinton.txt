! Geoffrey Hinton
<img src="assets/geoffrey_hinton.svg" alt="Geoffrey Hinton" width="800">

Imagine a scientist who bets his entire career on an idea the rest of the field considers a dead end — and turns out to be right. Geoffrey Hinton spent decades championing [[Neural Networks|Neural Networks]] when most of AI had moved on to symbolic approaches and rule-based [[Expert Systems|Expert Systems]]. He kept insisting that networks of simple, neuron-like units could learn from data, surviving two AI winters with nothing but stubbornness and good math. The field eventually came around, and the resulting revolution — [[Deep Learning|Deep Learning]] — reshaped technology, science, and society.

!! Key Contributions

Hinton''s fingerprints are on virtually every foundational idea in modern deep learning:

* ''Backpropagation popularization'' (1986): With David Rumelhart and Ronald Williams, he demonstrated that [[Backpropagation|Backpropagation]] could train multi-layer networks effectively, turning a theoretical curiosity into the workhorse algorithm of neural network training.
* ''Boltzmann machines'' (1985): Together with Terry Sejnowski, he introduced [[Restricted Boltzmann Machines|Restricted Boltzmann Machines]] — stochastic generative models grounded in statistical physics that could learn internal representations of data.
* ''[[Deep Belief Networks|Deep Belief Networks]]'' (2006): He showed that deep networks could be trained layer-by-layer using unsupervised pre-training, shattering the conventional wisdom that deep architectures were untrainable. This paper reignited interest in deep learning.
* ''[[Dropout|Dropout]]'' (2012, 2014): A deceptively simple regularization technique — randomly zeroing out neurons during training — that dramatically reduced overfitting and became standard practice across the field.
* ''[[AlexNet|AlexNet]] supervision'' (2012): His students Alex Krizhevsky and Ilya Sutskever built AlexNet under his guidance, winning the ImageNet competition by a historic margin and launching the modern deep learning era.
* ''Capsule networks'' (2017): A bold rethinking of how networks represent spatial hierarchies, using groups of neurons ("capsules") to encode both the presence and the pose of features.
* ''[[Knowledge Distillation|Knowledge Distillation]]'' (2015): A method for compressing the knowledge of a large model into a smaller one by training the student on the teacher''s soft output probabilities.
* ''Nobel Prize in Physics'' (2024): Awarded jointly with John Hopfield for foundational discoveries enabling machine learning with artificial neural networks — the first Nobel in physics for AI research.

!! The Core Insight

Hinton''s career rests on one deep conviction: //neural networks can learn distributed representations//. Rather than storing a concept in a single symbolic token, the network spreads it across many neurons, and each neuron participates in representing many concepts. This idea — borrowed from how the brain might actually work — means that similar concepts naturally end up with similar representations, giving networks the ability to generalize.

What made Hinton exceptional was not just the insight but the ''persistence''. Through the AI winters of the 1980s and 1990s, when funding dried up and conferences rejected neural network papers, he continued refining the mathematics of learning in multi-layer systems. When compute and data finally caught up to the theory, the frameworks he had built were ready.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ''Boltzmann machine energy function'' defines a probability distribution over binary states. For visible units $\mathbf{v}$ and hidden units $\mathbf{h}$:

$$E(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{h}) = -\sum_i a_i v_i - \sum_j b_j h_j - \sum_{i,j} v_i w_{ij} h_j$$

The probability of a visible configuration is $P(\mathbf{v}) = \frac{1}{Z}\sum_{\mathbf{h}} e^{-E(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{h})}$, where $Z$ is the partition function. Training maximizes the log-likelihood of the data, but computing the gradient exactly requires summing over all possible states — intractable for large networks.

''Contrastive divergence'' (CD-$k$) sidesteps this by approximating the negative phase gradient. Instead of running a Markov chain to equilibrium, CD-$k$ runs just $k$ steps of Gibbs sampling starting from the data, giving a biased but practically effective gradient estimate:

$$\Delta w_{ij} \approx \epsilon \left( \langle v_i h_j \rangle_{\text{data}} - \langle v_i h_j \rangle_{\text{recon}} \right)$$

''Dropout as implicit ensemble'': During training, each forward pass randomly sets each neuron to zero with probability $p$. This means the network trains an exponential number of "thinned" sub-networks simultaneously. At test time, weights are scaled by $(1-p)$, approximating the geometric mean of all sub-network predictions — a form of model averaging that provides powerful regularization.

!! Impact on the Field

* ''Turing Award'' (2018): Shared with [[Yoshua Bengio|Yoshua Bengio]] and [[Yann LeCun|Yann LeCun]] — the three "Godfathers of AI" — for conceptual and engineering breakthroughs in deep learning.
* ''Nobel Prize in Physics'' (2024): Shared with John Hopfield, recognizing that the statistical mechanics of neural networks constitutes fundamental physics.
* ''Institutional influence'': From the University of Toronto he built one of the world''s premier deep learning research groups. His move to Google Brain (2013–2023) brought neural network research into industrial scale. His students — including Yann LeCun, Ilya Sutskever, Radford Neal, Brendan Frey, and Ruslan Salakhutdinov — went on to lead AI labs worldwide.
* ''Public voice on AI safety'': After leaving Google in 2023, Hinton became one of the most prominent voices warning about existential risks from advanced AI, lending scientific credibility to safety concerns.

!! When Neural Networks Face Challenges

* ''The credit assignment problem in deep architectures'': Backpropagation works, but it requires differentiable operations end-to-end and struggles with very long credit assignment chains. Hinton himself has explored alternatives (target propagation, forward-forward algorithm) because he views backprop as biologically implausible — a tool that works despite not matching how brains learn.
* ''Representation brittleness'': Standard convolutional networks discard spatial relationships between parts. This is precisely what capsule networks were designed to fix, but capsule architectures have not yet scaled to compete with transformers on large benchmarks.
* ''Catastrophic forgetting'': Neural networks trained on new tasks tend to overwrite what they learned before. Distributed representations are powerful but fragile when the data distribution shifts, a problem that remains only partially solved by continual learning methods.

!! Technical Details

''Key papers'':
* Rumelhart, Hinton &amp; Williams, "Learning representations by back-propagating errors," //Nature//, 1986.
* Hinton &amp; Salakhutdinov, "Reducing the dimensionality of data with neural networks," //Science//, 2006.
* Krizhevsky, Sutskever &amp; Hinton, "ImageNet classification with deep convolutional neural networks," //NeurIPS//, 2012.
* Srivastava, Hinton, et al., "Dropout: A simple way to prevent neural networks from overfitting," //JMLR//, 2014.
* Hinton, Vinyals &amp; Dean, "Distilling the knowledge in a neural network," //NeurIPS Workshop//, 2015.
* Sabour, Frosst &amp; Hinton, "Dynamic routing between capsules," //NeurIPS//, 2017.

''Career'': Born 1947, London. PhD from University of Edinburgh (1978) under Christopher Longuet-Higgins. Faculty at Carnegie Mellon, then University of Toronto (1987–present emeritus). VP &amp; Engineering Fellow at Google (2013–2023). Fellow of the Royal Society. Officer of the Order of Canada.

''Notable students and postdocs'': Yann LeCun, Ilya Sutskever, Radford Neal, Brendan Frey, Ruslan Salakhutdinov, Tijmen Tieleman, Alex Krizhevsky, Jimmy Ba, Sara Sabour.
