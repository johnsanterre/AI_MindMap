! Low-Rank Approximation
<img src="assets/low_rank_approximation.svg" alt="Low-Rank Approximation" width="800">

Imagine you have a massive spreadsheet with thousands of rows and columns tracking every movie rating from every user on a streaming service. Most of those numbers are not independent&mdash;a handful of hidden taste dimensions (action lover, rom-com fan, arthouse devotee) explain almost all the variation. ''Low-rank approximation'' is the mathematical machinery that discovers those few hidden dimensions and reconstructs the full table from them, throwing away only noise. It is the reason Netflix can predict what you will watch next, the reason large language models can be fine-tuned on a laptop, and the foundation of nearly every dimensionality-reduction technique in machine learning.

!! The Core Insight

A matrix has ''rank'' $r$ if its columns span an $r$-dimensional space&mdash;equivalently, if it can be written as the sum of $r$ outer products. Real-world data matrices are often //effectively// low-rank: a small number of singular values dominate while the rest are negligible. Low-rank approximation formalizes the idea that you can replace a big, expensive matrix with a much smaller factored representation and lose almost nothing that matters.

The key guarantee comes from the ''Eckart-Young theorem'': among every possible matrix of rank $k$ or less, the one obtained by keeping the top-$k$ singular values of the [[Singular Value Decomposition|Singular Value Decomposition]] is //provably the closest// to the original in both the Frobenius and spectral norms. There is no cleverer compression&mdash;truncated SVD is optimal.

!! Compressing Matrices Without Losing Much

The ''Eckart-Young-Mirsky theorem'' (1936) is the theoretical bedrock. Given any matrix $A$ with singular value decomposition $A = U \Sigma V^T$, the best rank-$k$ approximation is obtained by zeroing out all but the $k$ largest singular values:

$$A_k = U_k \Sigma_k V_k^T$$

where $U_k$ keeps only the first $k$ columns of $U$, $\Sigma_k$ is the $k \times k$ upper-left block of $\Sigma$, and $V_k^T$ keeps the first $k$ rows of $V^T$.

//Why is this optimal?// Because singular values encode how much "energy" (variance) each component carries. The first singular value captures the single direction of greatest variation; the second captures the next-most, orthogonal to the first, and so on. Truncation discards the directions that contribute the least, and the theorem proves no other rank-$k$ matrix can do better.

Storage drops from $mn$ entries to $k(m + n + 1)$&mdash;a massive saving when $k \ll \min(m,n)$. For a $10{,}000 \times 10{,}000$ matrix with effective rank 50, that is roughly a 100$\times$ compression.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The rank-$k$ truncated SVD rewrites the matrix as a sum of $k$ rank-one outer products, each scaled by a singular value:

$$A_k = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sigma_i \, u_i \, v_i^T$$

The ''Eckart-Young optimality'' statement is:

$$A_k = \arg\min_{\text{rank}(B) \leq k} \|A - B\|_F$$

The reconstruction error is exactly the energy in the discarded singular values:

$$\|A - A_k\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_{i=k+1}^{r} \sigma_i^2}$$

where $r = \text{rank}(A)$. In the spectral (operator) norm, the error is simply $\sigma_{k+1}$, the first discarded singular value.

The ''relative'' error is often more informative:

$$\frac{\|A - A_k\|_F}{\|A\|_F} = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i>k} \sigma_i^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{r} \sigma_i^2}}$$

This ratio tells you what fraction of the matrix's total energy you are throwing away. In practice, if the singular values decay rapidly (exponentially or polynomially), even a very small $k$ captures $> 99\%$ of the energy.

!! Low-Rank Methods in ML

''LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation)'' has become the dominant method for [[Fine-Tuning|Fine-Tuning]] large language models. Instead of updating a full $d \times d$ weight matrix $W$, LoRA freezes $W$ and learns a low-rank update $\Delta W = B A$ where $B$ is $d \times r$ and $A$ is $r \times d$ with $r \ll d$ (often $r = 4$ or $8$). This slashes trainable parameters by $100\times$ or more while matching full fine-tuning performance&mdash;empirical evidence that weight updates are effectively low-rank.

''Matrix completion'' (the Netflix Prize problem) assumes the user-item rating matrix is approximately low-rank. Given a sparse set of observed entries, algorithms recover the full matrix by finding the lowest-rank matrix consistent with the observations, typically via nuclear norm minimization or alternating least squares.

[[Principal Component Analysis|Principal Component Analysis]] is low-rank approximation applied to the data covariance matrix. Keeping the top $k$ principal components is exactly computing the best rank-$k$ approximation of the centered data matrix, projecting into the subspace of maximum variance.

''Compressed sensing'' and ''random projections'' exploit low-rank (or sparse) structure to reconstruct signals from far fewer measurements than traditional Nyquist sampling would require, connecting to the Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma in [[Dimensionality Reduction|Dimensionality Reduction]].

!! When Low-Rank Assumptions Break Down

//1. The spectrum is flat.// When singular values do not decay&mdash;when every direction carries roughly equal energy&mdash;there is no good low-rank approximation. This happens with truly random or encrypted data. Truncating to rank $k$ discards a large fraction of the energy, and the reconstruction is poor.

//2. The structure is local, not global.// Low-rank models capture global patterns (broad user preferences, dominant image modes). If the important structure is //sparse and local//&mdash;a few critical entries scattered unpredictably&mdash;a low-rank model will smear that information across all entries. Sparse + low-rank decompositions (robust PCA) attempt to handle this hybrid case.

//3. Non-linear manifold structure.// Low-rank approximation is inherently //linear//: it finds the best linear subspace. When data lies on a curved manifold (e.g., images of a face rotating in 3D), the intrinsic dimensionality is low but a linear subspace cannot capture the curvature. Kernel methods, autoencoders, and manifold learning techniques are needed instead.

!! Technical Details

''Randomized SVD'' (Halko, Martinsson, Tropp, 2011) avoids computing the full SVD of an $m \times n$ matrix, which costs $O(mn \min(m,n))$. Instead, it projects $A$ onto a random low-dimensional subspace of dimension $k + p$ (where $p \approx 5{-}10$ is oversampling), computes the SVD of the small projected matrix, and lifts back. Total cost: $O(mn(k+p))$, with high-probability error bounds close to optimal. This is the workhorse behind scikit-learn's `TruncatedSVD` and Facebook's PCA implementations.

The ''Nystr&ouml;m approximation'' targets symmetric positive semidefinite matrices (kernel matrices). It samples a subset of $k$ columns, computes their interactions, and extrapolates to the full $n \times n$ matrix at cost $O(nk^2)$ instead of $O(n^3)$. Widely used in kernel methods and Gaussian process approximations.

''Nuclear norm minimization'' is the convex relaxation of rank minimization. Since $\text{rank}(X)$ is non-convex and NP-hard to minimize directly, replacing it with $\|X\|_* = \sum_i \sigma_i$ yields a semidefinite program that often recovers the exact low-rank solution under incoherence conditions&mdash;the theoretical foundation of matrix completion.

The ''Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma'' guarantees that $n$ points in high-dimensional space can be projected to $O(\log n / \epsilon^2)$ dimensions while preserving all pairwise distances within a factor $(1 \pm \epsilon)$. Random Gaussian or sparse projection matrices achieve this, connecting low-rank projection to distance preservation.

Key relationships: low-rank approximation via SVD is the backbone of [[Principal Component Analysis|Principal Component Analysis]], connects to [[Matrix Multiplication|Matrix Multiplication]] efficiency, and feeds directly into modern [[Dimensionality Reduction|Dimensionality Reduction]] pipelines. The topic lives within [[Core Linear Algebra|Core Linear Algebra]] as one of its most practically impactful results.
