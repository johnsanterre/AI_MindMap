! Inductive Bias
[img[assets/inductive_bias.svg]]

Imagine you're trying to guess what someone drew on the other side of a card. You could guess literally anything -- a dragon, a tax return, a fractal. But if someone tells you "it's a household object," suddenly you can make much better guesses. That constraint -- that prior assumption narrowing down the possibilities before you even see evidence -- is exactly what ''inductive bias'' does for a learning algorithm. Every machine learning model carries assumptions about the world baked into its architecture, its loss function, or its training procedure. These assumptions determine which patterns it finds easy to learn and which it will never discover, no matter how much data you throw at it.

!! The Core Insight

Without inductive bias, learning is //impossible//. This isn't hyperbole -- it's a mathematical fact. The <<link "No Free Lunch Theorem" "No Free Lunch Theorem">><</link>> proves that no learning algorithm can outperform random guessing across all possible problems. The only way to excel at //some// problems is to embed assumptions that match the structure of those problems. Inductive bias is the reason a model generalizes rather than merely memorizing. It is the bridge between finite training data and infinite possible functions.

Think of it this way: the space of all possible input-output mappings is unimaginably vast. A model's inductive bias carves out a //hypothesis space// -- a manageable subset of functions the model can even represent. The right bias means the true function lives inside that subset. The wrong bias means you're searching in entirely the wrong neighborhood.

!! Types of Inductive Bias

''Architectural bias'' is the most visible form. <<link "Convolutional Neural Networks" "Convolutional Neural Networks">><</link>> assume that useful patterns are local and translation-invariant -- a cat's ear looks the same whether it's in the top-left or bottom-right of an image. <<link "Transformers" "Transformers">><</link>> assume that relationships between elements matter regardless of their position, embedding a bias toward permutation <<link "equivariance" "Equivariance">><</link>> in the attention mechanism. Recurrent networks assume that sequences have temporal dependencies.

''Regularization bias'' nudges models toward simpler hypotheses. L2 regularization prefers small weights, implicitly favoring smoother functions. Dropout biases the network toward redundant representations that survive random ablation. Early stopping biases toward functions that are learnable in fewer gradient steps.

''Procedural bias'' comes from the learning algorithm itself. Stochastic gradient descent has an implicit bias toward flat minima in the loss landscape, which tend to generalize better. The order of training examples, the learning rate schedule, and even the random initialization all introduce subtle biases.

''Representational bias'' determines what a model //can// express. A linear model can only represent hyperplanes, making it impossible to learn XOR without feature engineering. A decision tree partitions space along axis-aligned splits. Each representation carves out a different hypothesis space.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Formally, a learning algorithm $\mathcal{A}$ maps a training set $S = \{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^n$ to a hypothesis $h \in \mathcal{H}$, where $\mathcal{H}$ is the hypothesis space. The inductive bias determines $\mathcal{H}$ and the preference ordering over hypotheses within it.

For a hypothesis space of VC dimension $d$, the generalization bound gives:

$$R(h) \leq \hat{R}(h) + \sqrt{\frac{d \ln(2n/d) + \ln(2/\delta)}{n}}$$

where $R(h)$ is the true risk, $\hat{R}(h)$ is the empirical risk, $n$ is the sample size, and $\delta$ is the confidence parameter. Smaller hypothesis spaces (stronger inductive bias) yield tighter bounds -- you need less data to generalize, //provided the true function is in $\mathcal{H}$//.

The <<link "bias-variance tradeoff" "Bias-Variance Tradeoff">><</link>> captures this tension directly:

$$\text{Error} = \text{Bias}^2 + \text{Variance} + \text{Noise}$$

Strong inductive bias increases the bias term (risk of systematic error) but decreases variance (sensitivity to the particular training set). Weak inductive bias does the opposite. The optimal point depends on the amount of training data and the true complexity of the problem.

In Bayesian terms, inductive bias corresponds to the prior $P(\theta)$. The posterior is:

$$P(\theta | D) \propto P(D | \theta) \cdot P(\theta)$$

A strong prior concentrates probability mass on a small region of parameter space. With little data, the prior dominates; with abundant data, the likelihood overwhelms it.

!! Inductive Bias in Modern Architectures

<<link "Convolutional Neural Networks" "Convolutional Neural Networks">><</link>> embed three key biases: //locality// (each neuron sees only a small patch), //weight sharing// (the same filter is applied everywhere), and //translation equivariance// (shifting the input shifts the output). These biases make CNNs extraordinarily data-efficient for vision tasks -- they don't need to relearn "what is an edge" at every pixel location.

<<link "Transformers" "Transformers">><</link>> have a weaker but different bias. Self-attention allows every token to attend to every other token, imposing no locality constraint. The primary bias is toward //pairwise interactions// -- attention computes relevance between pairs of elements. Positional encodings add a mild bias toward position-awareness, but the architecture itself is fundamentally permutation-equivariant.

This difference in inductive bias explains why CNNs dominated vision for years with limited data, while Transformers (Vision Transformers) only surpassed them when massive datasets became available. Weaker bias requires more data to compensate, but ultimately allows learning more general patterns.

Graph neural networks bias toward relational structure -- they assume the topology of connections matters. Physics-informed neural networks embed conservation laws and symmetries directly. Each architecture encodes domain knowledge as structural constraints.

!! When Inductive Biases Fail

''1. Bias mismatch.'' When your inductive bias contradicts the true data structure, no amount of data will save you. Applying a CNN to a problem where spatial locality is irrelevant -- like predicting stock prices from a randomly shuffled feature vector -- wastes the model's capacity enforcing translation invariance over meaningless spatial relationships. The model systematically ignores the actual signal.

''2. Excessive restriction.'' An overly strong inductive bias can make the hypothesis space too small to contain a good approximation of the true function. A linear model applied to a highly nonlinear problem will underfit permanently. This manifests as high training error that doesn't decrease with more capacity or data -- a hallmark of the bias being fundamentally wrong, not just insufficiently flexible.

''3. Implicit bias invisibility.'' The most dangerous inductive biases are the ones you don't know about. SGD's implicit bias toward certain solutions is still not fully understood theoretically. Batch normalization introduces biases toward particular feature scale distributions. When these hidden biases interact with data distribution shifts at deployment time, models fail in ways that are extremely difficult to diagnose because the bias was never made explicit.

!! Technical Details

The ''language of thought hypothesis'' in inductive bias: Mitchell (1980) formally defined inductive bias as the set of additional assumptions $B$ such that for all training sets $S$ and instances $x$: $\mathcal{A}(S) \vdash y$ if and only if $B \cup S \vdash y$, where $\vdash$ denotes logical entailment.

''Restriction bias'' limits the hypothesis space entirely (e.g., "only linear functions"), while ''preference bias'' orders hypotheses within a space (e.g., "prefer simpler functions"). Occam's razor is a preference bias. Most practical algorithms combine both.

The ''PAC-Bayes bound'' unifies inductive bias with generalization:

$$R(h) \leq \hat{R}(h) + \sqrt{\frac{KL(Q \| P) + \ln(n/\delta)}{2n}}$$

where $Q$ is the learned posterior over hypotheses, $P$ is the prior (inductive bias), and $KL$ is the Kullback-Leibler divergence. Models whose learned solutions stay close to the prior (low $KL$) generalize better -- the bias was appropriate.

''Double descent'' reveals that the classical bias-variance tradeoff is incomplete. In the overparameterized regime, increasing model capacity //past// the interpolation threshold actually //decreases// test error. This suggests that the implicit inductive bias of SGD in overparameterized models acts as a powerful regularizer, preferring minimum-norm solutions.

<<link "Equivariance" "Equivariance">><</link>> as formalized inductive bias: if a group $G$ acts on input space $\mathcal{X}$ and output space $\mathcal{Y}$, an equivariant model satisfies $f(g \cdot x) = g \cdot f(x)$ for all $g \in G$. This constrains $\mathcal{H}$ to only functions respecting the symmetry, dramatically reducing the effective hypothesis space.
