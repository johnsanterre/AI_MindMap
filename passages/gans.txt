! GANs

[img[assets/gans.svg]]

Imagine two artists locked in an eternal duel. One is a forger who paints fake masterpieces; the other is a detective who inspects each canvas and declares it real or counterfeit. Every time the detective catches a fake, the forger studies what gave it away and paints a better one. Every time a fake slips through, the detective sharpens her eye. Over thousands of rounds, the forger becomes so skilled that even the detective cannot tell real from fake — and at that moment, the forger has learned to produce perfect imitations. This is the core idea behind ''Generative Adversarial Networks'' (GANs), introduced by Ian Goodfellow and collaborators in 2014. Two neural networks — a ''generator'' and a ''discriminator'' — play a minimax game against each other, and the generator learns to produce synthetic data that is statistically indistinguishable from real data.

---

!! The Core Insight

A GAN reframes the problem of generative modeling as a two-player game from <<link "Game Theory" "Game Theory">><</link>>. Instead of explicitly modeling a probability distribution (as a <<link "Variational Autoencoder" "Variational Autoencoder">><</link>> does), the generator $G$ learns a mapping from a simple latent distribution $z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ to the data space. It never sees the real data directly — it only receives gradient signals from the discriminator $D$, which is simultaneously trained to distinguish real samples $x$ from generated samples $G(z)$.

The objective is a minimax game:

$$\min_G \max_D \; \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}}[\log D(x)] + \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}[\log(1 - D(G(z)))]$$

The discriminator wants to maximize this expression by assigning high probability to real data and low probability to fakes. The generator wants to minimize it by making $D(G(z))$ close to 1. In theory, this game has a unique <<link "Nash Equilibrium" "Nash Equilibrium">><</link>> at which $G$ perfectly reproduces $p_{\text{data}}$ and $D$ outputs $\frac{1}{2}$ everywhere — it literally cannot tell real from fake.

The revolutionary aspect is that the generator never explicitly computes a likelihood or a density function. It learns to sample from the data distribution purely through adversarial feedback. This sidesteps the intractable density estimation problem that plagues many other generative models.

---

!! Training Dynamics and Mode Collapse

Training a GAN is notoriously difficult because you are optimizing two networks with opposing objectives simultaneously. The training alternates: first update $D$ for $k$ steps to better classify real vs. fake, then update $G$ for one step to better fool $D$. The balance between these updates is critical.

''Mode collapse'' is the most infamous failure mode. The generator discovers that producing a small set of outputs — or even a single output — reliably fools the discriminator, and it collapses to generating only those samples. Mathematically, instead of learning the full data distribution $p_{\text{data}}$, the generator converges to a distribution $p_G$ that covers only a few modes of $p_{\text{data}}$. The discriminator eventually catches on, but the generator may then jump to a //different// small set of modes rather than learning all of them.

''Training instability'' arises because the generator and discriminator losses are coupled. If the discriminator becomes too strong too quickly, the generator receives vanishing gradients — the discriminator is so confident that $\log(1 - D(G(z))) \approx \log(1) = 0$ and provides almost no learning signal. Conversely, if the generator outpaces the discriminator, it receives no useful corrective feedback. The ideal training trajectory maintains a delicate equilibrium between the two.

''Non-convergence and oscillation'' occur because the minimax objective is not a simple minimization problem. Gradient descent on minimax games does not guarantee convergence to the Nash equilibrium. The training dynamics can orbit the equilibrium indefinitely, with the generator and discriminator chasing each other in parameter space without settling down.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The generator defines an implicit distribution $p_G$ over data space. For a fixed generator, the optimal discriminator is:

$$D^*(x) = \frac{p_{\text{data}}(x)}{p_{\text{data}}(x) + p_G(x)}$$

Substituting this optimal discriminator back into the minimax objective yields the ''virtual training criterion'' for the generator:

$$C(G) = -\log 4 + 2 \cdot D_{JS}(p_{\text{data}} \| p_G)$$

where $D_{JS}$ is the Jensen-Shannon divergence. The generator therefore minimizes the JS divergence between $p_G$ and $p_{\text{data}}$. Since $D_{JS} \geq 0$ with equality iff $p_G = p_{\text{data}}$, the global optimum is achieved when the generator exactly reproduces the data distribution.

However, the JS divergence has a critical flaw: when $p_{\text{data}}$ and $p_G$ have non-overlapping supports (which is common early in training when the generator produces unrealistic outputs), $D_{JS}$ saturates at $\log 2$ and provides zero useful gradient. This is why the <<link "Wasserstein Distance" "Wasserstein Distance">><</link>> was proposed as an alternative — the Wasserstein-1 (Earth Mover's) distance provides meaningful gradients even when distributions do not overlap:

$$W_1(p_{\text{data}}, p_G) = \inf_{\gamma \in \Pi(p_{\text{data}}, p_G)} \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim \gamma}[\|x - y\|]$$

The Wasserstein GAN (WGAN) replaces the discriminator with a ''critic'' that estimates this distance, subject to a Lipschitz constraint enforced by weight clipping or gradient penalty.

---

!! GAN Variants

The original GAN spawned an enormous family of architectures and training strategies:

''DCGAN'' (Deep Convolutional GAN, Radford et al. 2016) established the architectural template: strided convolutions in the discriminator, fractional-strided (transposed) convolutions in the generator, batch normalization throughout, and the removal of fully connected layers. Most subsequent image GANs follow this blueprint.

''WGAN and WGAN-GP'' replaced the JS divergence with the Wasserstein distance, dramatically improving training stability. WGAN-GP (Gulrajani et al. 2017) enforced the Lipschitz constraint via a gradient penalty rather than weight clipping, becoming the default for stable GAN training.

''Conditional GANs'' (cGAN, Mirza & Osindero 2014) condition both the generator and discriminator on auxiliary information such as class labels, enabling controlled generation: "generate a cat" vs. "generate a dog."

''Progressive GAN'' (Karras et al. 2018) grows both networks layer by layer from low resolution to high resolution during training, enabling the first photorealistic face generation at 1024x1024.

''StyleGAN'' and ''StyleGAN2'' (Karras et al. 2019, 2020) introduced a style-based generator architecture with adaptive instance normalization, enabling unprecedented control over generated image attributes and producing the famous "This Person Does Not Exist" faces.

''CycleGAN'' (Zhu et al. 2017) introduced unpaired image-to-image translation using cycle consistency loss — translating horses to zebras without paired training examples.

More recently, <<link "Diffusion Models" "Diffusion Models">><</link>> have largely supplanted GANs for image generation tasks, offering more stable training and better mode coverage, though GANs retain advantages in inference speed and certain real-time applications.

---

!! When GANs Break Down

''Mode collapse and missing diversity:'' The generator may produce high-quality but low-diversity outputs, covering only a fraction of the true data distribution. This is particularly dangerous when diversity matters — for example, generating synthetic training data that omits entire subpopulations. Metrics like Frechet Inception Distance (FID) can partially detect mode collapse, but no metric perfectly captures distributional coverage. Techniques like minibatch discrimination and unrolled GANs mitigate but do not eliminate this failure.

''Training instability and non-convergence:'' Unlike supervised learning where the loss monotonically decreases, GAN training involves a moving target. The discriminator's loss landscape changes every time the generator updates, and vice versa. This can produce oscillations, divergence, or sudden quality collapses late in training. No general-purpose convergence guarantee exists for GAN training with gradient descent on neural networks. Practitioners rely on heuristics: learning rate tuning, spectral normalization, progressive growing, and careful monitoring of generated sample quality throughout training.

''Evaluation difficulty:'' There is no single reliable metric for GAN quality. FID measures distributional similarity using Inception network features but is blind to certain artifacts. Inception Score (IS) measures quality and diversity but only within the ImageNet class taxonomy. Precision and Recall metrics for generative models help but require a reference model. The lack of a tractable likelihood function — the very feature that makes GANs attractive — also makes it impossible to compute $p_G(x)$ for a given sample, unlike a <<link "Variational Autoencoder" "Variational Autoencoder">><</link>> which provides an evidence lower bound.

!! Technical Details

''Original paper:'' Goodfellow et al. (2014), //Generative Adversarial Nets//, NeurIPS — introduced the GAN framework, proved that the global optimum corresponds to $p_G = p_{\text{data}}$, and demonstrated the concept on MNIST and CIFAR-10.

''Theoretical foundations:'' The GAN objective minimizes the Jensen-Shannon divergence $D_{JS}(p_{\text{data}} \| p_G) = \frac{1}{2}D_{KL}(p_{\text{data}} \| m) + \frac{1}{2}D_{KL}(p_G \| m)$ where $m = \frac{1}{2}(p_{\text{data}} + p_G)$. The optimal discriminator theorem gives $D^*(x) = p_{\text{data}}(x) / (p_{\text{data}}(x) + p_G(x))$. Convergence of the training procedure is guaranteed only when the discriminator is optimal at each generator update step — a condition never met in practice with finite-capacity networks and finite training steps.

''Spectral normalization'' (Miyato et al. 2018) constrains the Lipschitz constant of the discriminator by normalizing each weight matrix by its spectral norm (largest singular value). This provides a computationally cheap alternative to the gradient penalty and has become standard in modern GAN architectures.

''Architecture details for typical image GANs:'' Generator uses transposed convolutions (stride 2) with batch normalization and ReLU activations, ending with tanh. Discriminator uses strided convolutions (stride 2) with spectral normalization and LeakyReLU (slope 0.2), ending with a linear layer (for WGAN) or sigmoid (for original GAN). Typical latent dimension is 128 or 512. Adam optimizer with $\beta_1 = 0.0$, $\beta_2 = 0.9$, learning rate $\sim 2 \times 10^{-4}$.

''FID computation:'' Pass real and generated images through a pretrained Inception v3 network, extract activations from the pool3 layer, fit multivariate Gaussians $(\mu_r, \Sigma_r)$ and $(\mu_g, \Sigma_g)$, compute $\text{FID} = \|\mu_r - \mu_g\|^2 + \text{Tr}(\Sigma_r + \Sigma_g - 2(\Sigma_r \Sigma_g)^{1/2})$. Lower is better. State-of-the-art image GANs achieve FID below 5 on standard benchmarks.

''Two-timescale update rule'' (TTUR, Heusel et al. 2017): using different learning rates for generator and discriminator (typically higher for the discriminator) provides convergence guarantees under certain assumptions and often improves stability in practice.
