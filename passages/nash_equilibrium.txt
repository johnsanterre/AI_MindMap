! Nash Equilibrium
[img[assets/nash_equilibrium.svg]]

Imagine two food trucks parked on the same long beach. Each morning, both drivers independently choose where to set up. If one parks in the middle and the other at the far end, the middle truck steals most of the customers. After enough mornings of jockeying, both trucks end up parked right next to each other at the center of the beach — not because they coordinated, but because neither one can do better by moving alone. That resting point, where no player can improve their outcome by changing only their own strategy, is a ''Nash Equilibrium''.

Named after mathematician John Nash, this concept is the cornerstone of non-cooperative <<link "Game Theory" "Game Theory">><</link>>. It tells us not what players //should// do in some moral sense, but what they //will// do when each is acting rationally in their own self-interest and anticipates that everyone else is doing the same.

!! The Core Insight

A Nash Equilibrium is a set of strategies — one for each player — where no single player can gain a higher payoff by unilaterally changing their strategy, assuming all other players keep theirs fixed.

This does not mean the outcome is //optimal// for the group. The famous ''Prisoner's Dilemma'' shows exactly this tension: two suspects, interrogated separately, each have an incentive to betray the other. Both defecting is the Nash Equilibrium, even though both cooperating would leave them collectively better off. The equilibrium is //stable//, not //ideal//.

Key properties of Nash Equilibrium:

* ''Stability'' — no player has a profitable deviation
* ''Self-enforcing'' — if players announce their strategies in advance, nobody wants to change
* ''Existence'' — every finite game has at least one Nash Equilibrium (possibly in mixed strategies), as proven by Nash in 1950

!! Pure and Mixed Strategies

A ''pure strategy'' Nash Equilibrium is one where each player picks a single definite action. In Rock-Paper-Scissors, no pure strategy equilibrium exists — for any fixed choice, the opponent can beat it by switching.

A ''mixed strategy'' Nash Equilibrium allows players to randomize. In Rock-Paper-Scissors, the equilibrium is for each player to play Rock, Paper, and Scissors each with probability $\frac{1}{3}$. No opponent can exploit this randomization.

The distinction matters in practice:

* ''Pure equilibria'' are easy to describe and implement. The Prisoner's Dilemma has one: (Defect, Defect).
* ''Mixed equilibria'' require players to be indifferent among the actions they randomize over. Each action in the mix must yield the same expected payoff.
* ''Multiple equilibria'' can exist simultaneously. A coordination game like "which side of the road to drive on" has two pure equilibria (both drive left, both drive right) and one mixed equilibrium.

When a game has multiple Nash Equilibria, predicting which one players will reach requires additional concepts like ''focal points'' (Schelling points), evolutionary stability, or communication.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Consider a two-player game where player $i$ has strategy set $S_i$ and payoff function $u_i(s_1, s_2)$. A strategy profile $(s_1^*, s_2^*)$ is a Nash Equilibrium if:

$$u_1(s_1^*, s_2^*) \geq u_1(s_1, s_2^*) \quad \forall \, s_1 \in S_1$$

$$u_2(s_1^*, s_2^*) \geq u_2(s_1^*, s_2) \quad \forall \, s_2 \in S_2$$

For ''mixed strategies'', let $\sigma_i$ be a probability distribution over $S_i$. The expected payoff is:

$$U_i(\sigma_1, \sigma_2) = \sum_{s_1 \in S_1} \sum_{s_2 \in S_2} \sigma_1(s_1) \cdot \sigma_2(s_2) \cdot u_i(s_1, s_2)$$

A mixed strategy Nash Equilibrium $(\sigma_1^*, \sigma_2^*)$ satisfies:

$$U_i(\sigma_i^*, \sigma_{-i}^*) \geq U_i(\sigma_i, \sigma_{-i}^*) \quad \forall \, \sigma_i$$

Nash's existence theorem relies on ''Brouwer's fixed point theorem''. Define the best-response correspondence $BR_i(\sigma_{-i})$ as the set of strategies maximizing $U_i$ given opponents' strategies. A Nash Equilibrium is a fixed point of the joint best-response mapping:

$$\sigma^* \in BR_1(\sigma_2^*) \times BR_2(\sigma_1^*)$$

For ''computing'' equilibria, the Lemke-Howson algorithm solves two-player games in exponential worst-case time. The general problem of finding a Nash Equilibrium is PPAD-complete — believed to be computationally hard but not NP-hard.

!! Nash Equilibrium in ML

Nash Equilibrium is not just a textbook concept — it sits at the heart of several active areas in machine learning:

''Generative Adversarial Networks'' — <<link "GANs" "GANs">><</link>> frame image generation as a two-player zero-sum game between a generator and a discriminator. Training seeks a Nash Equilibrium where the generator produces perfect samples and the discriminator is reduced to random guessing. In practice, GAN training often //fails// to converge to this equilibrium, oscillating or collapsing instead. Understanding why requires the full theory of continuous games and <<link "Minimax" "Minimax">><</link>> optimization.

''<<link "Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning" "Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning">><</link>>'' — when multiple RL agents interact in the same environment, the solution concept shifts from single-agent optimality to Nash Equilibrium. Algorithms like Nash Q-learning and Nash-DQN explicitly compute equilibria at each step. The challenge is that the strategy space is enormous and equilibria may be non-unique.

''<<link "Mechanism Design" "Mechanism Design">><</link>>'' — the "inverse" of game theory: designing the rules of a game so that the Nash Equilibrium produces a desired outcome. Auction design (e.g., ad auctions at Google) uses this principle — the rules are crafted so that bidding truthfully is a Nash Equilibrium.

''Adversarial robustness'' — defending neural networks against adversarial examples can be modeled as a game between an attacker and a defender, where robustness corresponds to equilibrium strategies.

!! When Nash Equilibrium Breaks Down

''1. Computational intractability'' — finding a Nash Equilibrium is PPAD-complete. For large games — like those arising in multi-agent RL with continuous state spaces — exact computation is infeasible. Players in practice use heuristics, learning algorithms, or bounded rationality, and the resulting behavior may not correspond to any equilibrium at all.

''2. Multiplicity and selection'' — many games have multiple Nash Equilibria with very different payoffs. The theory alone does not tell you which equilibrium will be reached. In a coordination game, players might end up miscoordinated (each picking a different equilibrium), resulting in outcomes //worse// than any single equilibrium. Refinements like subgame perfection, trembling hand perfection, and correlated equilibrium attempt to narrow the set but add complexity.

''3. Rationality and common knowledge assumptions'' — Nash Equilibrium assumes every player is perfectly rational and knows that every other player is perfectly rational, and knows that they know, ad infinitum. Real agents — whether humans or RL policies — violate this. Behavioral game theory documents systematic deviations. In ML, agents trained by gradient descent may cycle, diverge, or settle on non-equilibrium strategies, as famously observed in GAN training instability.

!! Technical Details

''Formal definition (n-player):'' A game $\Gamma = (N, (S_i)_{i \in N}, (u_i)_{i \in N})$ has Nash Equilibrium $s^* = (s_1^*, \ldots, s_n^*)$ if $u_i(s_i^*, s_{-i}^*) \geq u_i(s_i, s_{-i}^*) \; \forall \, s_i \in S_i, \; \forall \, i \in N$.

''Nash's theorem (1950):'' Every finite game (finite players, finite strategy sets) has at least one Nash Equilibrium in mixed strategies. The proof uses Kakutani's fixed-point theorem (a generalization of Brouwer's).

''Support enumeration:'' In a $2 \times 2$ game, mixed strategy equilibrium is found by setting each player indifferent over the opponent's mixture. If Player A plays Up with probability $p$ and Player B plays Left with probability $q$, solve:
$$q \cdot u_B(\text{Up, Left}) + (1-q) \cdot u_B(\text{Up, Right}) = q \cdot u_B(\text{Down, Left}) + (1-q) \cdot u_B(\text{Down, Right})$$

''Complexity:'' 2-player Nash is PPAD-complete (Daskalakis, Goldberg, Papadimitriou, 2009). Correlated equilibrium, by contrast, is computable in polynomial time via linear programming.

''Refinements hierarchy:'' Nash Equilibrium $\supset$ Subgame Perfect Equilibrium $\supset$ Sequential Equilibrium $\supset$ Trembling Hand Perfect Equilibrium.

''Connection to fixed points:'' Best-response dynamics iterate $\sigma_i^{t+1} \in BR_i(\sigma_{-i}^t)$. Convergence is guaranteed in certain game classes (potential games, zero-sum games with minimax) but not in general.

''Price of Anarchy:'' Measures inefficiency of Nash Equilibrium relative to the social optimum: $\text{PoA} = \frac{\text{worst NE social cost}}{\text{optimal social cost}}$. In routing games (Braess's paradox), the PoA can be arbitrarily bad.
