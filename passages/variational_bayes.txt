! Variational Bayes

<img src="assets/variational_bayes.svg" alt="Variational Bayes diagram — intractable true posterior approximated by a tractable distribution family, with ELBO maximization and mean-field factorization" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Suppose you have a jar full of thousands of differently weighted dice, and someone tells you the total they rolled but not which dice they used. Figuring out the exact mix of dice that produced that total is impossibly hard — there are too many combinations to check. So instead you say: "Let me find the single //simplest// bag of dice whose behavior most closely matches what I see." That is ''Variational Bayes''. When the true posterior distribution $p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x})$ is too complex to compute exactly, you pick a tractable family of distributions $q(\mathbf{z}; \boldsymbol{\phi})$ and tune the parameters $\boldsymbol{\phi}$ until $q$ is as close to the true posterior as possible. The intractable integration problem becomes an optimization problem — and optimization is something we know how to do.

---

!! The Core Insight

<<link "Bayes' Theorem" "Bayes Theorem">><</link>> tells us that the posterior is $p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x}) = p(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z})\,p(\mathbf{z})\,/\,p(\mathbf{x})$. The denominator — the ''marginal likelihood'' or ''evidence'' $p(\mathbf{x}) = \int p(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z})\,p(\mathbf{z})\,d\mathbf{z}$ — requires integrating over every possible configuration of latent variables $\mathbf{z}$. For most interesting models (mixture models, topic models, deep generative models), this integral has no closed form and is exponentially expensive to compute exactly.

Variational Bayes sidesteps this by reframing inference as optimization. You choose a ''variational family'' $\mathcal{Q} = \{q(\mathbf{z};\boldsymbol{\phi})\}$ of tractable distributions, then find the member that minimizes the <<link "KL Divergence" "KL Divergence">><</link>> from $q$ to the true posterior: $\boldsymbol{\phi}^* = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\phi}} \mathrm{KL}(q(\mathbf{z};\boldsymbol{\phi}) \,\|\, p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x}))$. Since this KL divergence itself involves the intractable $p(\mathbf{x})$, you maximize an equivalent quantity called the <<link "ELBO" "ELBO">><</link>> instead.

The key trade-off: you sacrifice exactness for speed. The approximation $q^*$ will never be //exactly// the true posterior, but it can be remarkably close — and it is computable.

---

!! Mean-Field, CAVI, and Stochastic Variational Inference

The most common variational family is the ''mean-field'' approximation, which assumes the latent variables are independent under $q$:

$$q(\mathbf{z};\boldsymbol{\phi}) = \prod_{j=1}^{J} q_j(z_j;\phi_j)$$

Each factor $q_j$ has its own parameters $\phi_j$. This factorization is almost certainly wrong — the true posterior usually has complex dependencies — but it makes the math tractable and often captures the marginal distributions surprisingly well.

''CAVI (Coordinate Ascent Variational Inference)'' optimizes one factor $q_j$ at a time while holding the others fixed. The optimal update for each factor has a beautiful closed-form expression: $\log q_j^*(z_j) = \mathbb{E}_{-j}[\log p(\mathbf{z}, \mathbf{x})] + \text{const}$, where $\mathbb{E}_{-j}$ denotes the expectation under all factors except the $j$-th. You cycle through all factors, updating each in turn, and the <<link "ELBO" "ELBO">><</link>> is guaranteed to increase at every step.

CAVI works well for conjugate models, but it requires full passes over the dataset and model-specific derivations. ''Stochastic Variational Inference (SVI)'' scales variational methods to massive datasets by using stochastic gradient ascent on the ELBO. Instead of processing all data points per update, SVI subsamples minibatches and takes noisy gradient steps — exactly the same idea that makes deep learning scale. SVI opened the door to applying variational methods to millions of documents, images, and genomic sequences.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ''Evidence Lower Bound'' (ELBO) is the central objective:

$$\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\phi}) = \mathbb{E}_{q(\mathbf{z};\boldsymbol{\phi})}[\log p(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{z})] - \mathbb{E}_{q(\mathbf{z};\boldsymbol{\phi})}[\log q(\mathbf{z};\boldsymbol{\phi})]$$

This can be rearranged to show:

$$\log p(\mathbf{x}) = \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\phi}) + \mathrm{KL}(q(\mathbf{z};\boldsymbol{\phi}) \,\|\, p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x}))$$

Since KL divergence is non-negative, the ELBO is always a lower bound on $\log p(\mathbf{x})$. Maximizing the ELBO is equivalent to minimizing the KL divergence to the true posterior.

An equivalent decomposition separates the ELBO into ''reconstruction'' and ''regularization'' terms:

$$\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\phi}) = \mathbb{E}_{q}[\log p(\mathbf{x}|\mathbf{z})] - \mathrm{KL}(q(\mathbf{z};\boldsymbol{\phi}) \,\|\, p(\mathbf{z}))$$

The first term rewards configurations of $\mathbf{z}$ that explain the data well. The second term penalizes $q$ for deviating from the prior — a built-in Occam's razor.

For the mean-field CAVI update, the optimal $q_j$ satisfies:

$$q_j^*(z_j) \propto \exp\!\left(\mathbb{E}_{-j}[\log p(\mathbf{z}, \mathbf{x})]\right)$$

When the model is in the conjugate exponential family, this expectation yields a recognizable distribution (e.g., Gaussian, Dirichlet), and the update reduces to updating natural parameters — closely related to <<link "Expectation Maximization" "Expectation Maximization">><</link>> but maintaining full distributions rather than point estimates.

---

!! Variational Bayes in Practice

''Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)'' are the most prominent application. A neural encoder maps data to variational parameters $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$, defining $q(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x}) = \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \text{diag}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}^2))$. A neural decoder maps $\mathbf{z}$ back to data. The entire system is trained end-to-end by maximizing the ELBO using the ''reparameterization trick'': instead of sampling $z \sim q$, write $z = \mu + \sigma \odot \epsilon$ with $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, making the gradient flow through the sampling step.

''Topic models'' like Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) were among the first large-scale successes of variational Bayes. Mean-field variational inference with CAVI replaced the slow Gibbs sampling that was previously standard, enabling topic modeling on millions of documents.

''Bayesian neural networks'' use variational inference to maintain distributions over weights rather than point estimates. This provides uncertainty quantification but is computationally expensive — the variational parameters double the number of learnable quantities.

''Model selection'' comes free: the ELBO approximates $\log p(\mathbf{x})$, so you can compare models by comparing their ELBOs, bypassing cross-validation.

---

!! When X Breaks Down

''1. Mean-field underestimates uncertainty.'' Because the mean-field factorization forces $q$ to be fully factored, it cannot capture posterior correlations. This systematically underestimates the variance of the true posterior — the approximation is overconfident. In extreme cases, a multimodal posterior gets squashed into a single mode, losing critical structure. Normalizing flows and structured variational families partially address this but add complexity.

''2. The KL direction causes mode-seeking behavior.'' Minimizing $\mathrm{KL}(q\|p)$ (the "reverse KL") makes $q$ avoid placing mass where $p$ is small. For a multimodal posterior, $q$ tends to collapse onto a single mode rather than spread across all modes. This is the opposite of the "forward KL" $\mathrm{KL}(p\|q)$, which would be mode-covering but requires samples from the true posterior. The choice of KL direction is baked into the variational framework and cannot be changed without fundamentally altering the algorithm.

''3. ELBO optimization gets stuck in local optima.'' The ELBO landscape is generally non-convex, especially for non-conjugate models. CAVI can converge to poor local optima depending on initialization. SVI adds stochastic noise that can help escape shallow optima but introduces its own challenges: learning rate tuning, variance reduction, and the risk of divergence. There is no guarantee that the converged solution is close to the global optimum.

!! Technical Details

''ELBO gradient for SVI:'' Using the score function estimator (REINFORCE), $\nabla_\phi \mathcal{L} = \mathbb{E}_q[\nabla_\phi \log q(\mathbf{z};\boldsymbol{\phi})(\log p(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{z}) - \log q(\mathbf{z};\boldsymbol{\phi}))]$. This is unbiased but high-variance. The reparameterization trick (when applicable) gives lower-variance gradients: $\nabla_\phi \mathcal{L} = \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon}[\nabla_\phi \log p(\mathbf{x}, g(\epsilon,\boldsymbol{\phi})) - \nabla_\phi \log q(g(\epsilon,\boldsymbol{\phi});\boldsymbol{\phi})]$ where $\mathbf{z} = g(\epsilon, \boldsymbol{\phi})$.

''Conjugate exponential family:'' If $p(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{z})$ belongs to the exponential family, the CAVI updates reduce to updating expected sufficient statistics. For example, in a Gaussian mixture model, the variational distribution over cluster assignments is Categorical, and the variational distribution over means is Gaussian — both updated in closed form.

''Structured variational families:'' Beyond mean-field, one can use structured approximations that preserve key dependencies: $q(\mathbf{z}) = \prod_k q_k(\mathbf{z}_{C_k})$ where $\{C_k\}$ are groups of variables. This retains intra-group correlations while factoring across groups. Normalizing flows $q(\mathbf{z}) = q_0(\mathbf{z}_0)\prod_t |\det \frac{\partial f_t}{\partial \mathbf{z}_{t-1}}|^{-1}$ allow arbitrarily flexible variational distributions.

''Variational EM:'' When the model has both latent variables $\mathbf{z}$ and parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, alternate between (E-step) updating $q(\mathbf{z})$ to maximize ELBO with $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ fixed, and (M-step) updating $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ to maximize ELBO with $q$ fixed. This generalizes <<link "Expectation Maximization" "Expectation Maximization">><</link>> — classical EM is the special case where $q(\mathbf{z}) = p(\mathbf{z}|\mathbf{x},\boldsymbol{\theta})$ exactly.

''Amortized inference:'' Instead of optimizing $\boldsymbol{\phi}$ per data point, train a neural network $f_\psi(\mathbf{x}) \to \boldsymbol{\phi}$. This is the encoder in a VAE. Amortization sacrifices per-instance optimality for speed: one forward pass replaces iterative optimization.

''Black-box variational inference (BBVI):'' Uses only the ability to evaluate $\log p(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{z})$ and sample from $q$. No model-specific derivations needed. Combines the score function estimator with control variates for variance reduction.
