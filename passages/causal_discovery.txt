! Causal Discovery

<img src="assets/causal_discovery.svg" alt="Diagram showing three paradigms for causal discovery: constraint-based PC algorithm, score-based GES, and continuous optimization NOTEARS" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

You can see that sales of ice cream and drownings both rise in summer. A correlation matrix will dutifully report this. But no statistician would recommend banning ice cream to save swimmers. ''Causal discovery'' is the family of algorithms that takes a dataset — no experiments, no interventions, just passive observations — and tries to recover the directed acyclic graph (DAG) of cause-and-effect relationships that generated it. Where ordinary statistics asks "what co-occurs?", causal discovery asks "what causes what?" It is the machine that tries to draw the arrows, not just the lines, between variables.

---

!! The Core Insight

The central promise of causal discovery rests on a bridge between probability and graph structure. Under the ''faithfulness'' assumption, every conditional independence in the data corresponds to a separation in the underlying causal DAG, and every separation in the DAG produces a conditional independence in the data. This means the pattern of independences you can test statistically is a fingerprint of the causal structure. Two different DAGs that imply different independence patterns will produce statistically distinguishable data — so you can, at least in principle, narrow down which DAG generated your observations.

The limitation is equally fundamental: some DAGs are ''Markov equivalent'' — they encode exactly the same set of conditional independences. For example, $X \to Y$ and $X \leftarrow Y$ both say that $X$ and $Y$ are dependent, and no observational data can distinguish them without further assumptions. Causal discovery algorithms therefore typically recover a ''Markov equivalence class'' rather than a unique DAG. This equivalence class is represented by a ''completed partially directed acyclic graph'' (CPDAG), where some edges have definite directions and others remain ambiguous. Getting from equivalence class to unique DAG requires either interventional data or additional assumptions like linearity or non-Gaussianity.

This insight connects deeply to <<link "Conditional Independence" "Conditional Independence">><</link>> testing, to the theory of <<link "Bayesian Networks" "Bayesian Networks">><</link>>, and to the broader framework of <<link "Graph Theory" "Graph Theory">><</link>> — specifically the d-separation criterion that links graphical structure to probabilistic independence.

---

!! Constraint-Based, Score-Based, and Hybrid Methods

''Constraint-based methods'' work by systematically testing conditional independences. The ''PC algorithm'' (named after its creators Peter Spirtes and Clark Glymour) starts with a complete undirected graph over all variables, then removes edges: if $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y \mid S$ for some conditioning set $S$, the edge between $X$ and $Y$ is deleted. After the skeleton is determined, orientation rules identify ''v-structures'' — patterns like $X \to Z \leftarrow Y$ where $X$ and $Y$ are not adjacent but both cause $Z$. The ''FCI algorithm'' extends PC to handle latent confounders, producing a ''partial ancestral graph'' (PAG) that explicitly represents the possibility of hidden common causes.

''Score-based methods'' treat structure learning as an optimization problem. They define a scoring function — typically the ''Bayesian Information Criterion'' (BIC) — that balances data fit against model complexity, then search the space of DAGs for the highest-scoring structure. The ''Greedy Equivalence Search'' (GES) algorithm operates in two phases: a forward phase that greedily adds edges to increase the score, followed by a backward phase that greedily removes edges. GES is provably consistent: with enough data from a faithful distribution, it recovers the true equivalence class.

''Continuous optimization methods'' recast the combinatorial DAG search as a continuous optimization problem. ''NOTEARS'' (Zheng et al., 2018) parameterizes the DAG as a weighted adjacency matrix $W$ and enforces acyclicity through the differentiable constraint $\text{tr}(e^{W \circ W}) - d = 0$, where $d$ is the number of variables. This allows standard gradient-based optimizers to search over DAG structures, dramatically scaling causal discovery to hundreds of variables. Hybrid methods like ''MMHC'' combine constraint-based skeleton learning with score-based orientation.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The mathematical core of causal discovery connects <<link "Structural Equation Models" "Structural Equation Models">><</link>> to observable statistical patterns. A linear structural equation model has the form:

$$X = BX + \epsilon$$

where $B$ is a $d \times d$ matrix of causal coefficients with zeros on the diagonal and $\epsilon$ is a vector of independent noise terms. The entry $B_{ij}$ is nonzero if and only if variable $j$ is a direct cause of variable $i$. The matrix $B$ must be ''acyclic'' — no variable can be its own ancestor — which means $B$ can be permuted into a strictly lower-triangular form.

The observable covariance matrix $\Sigma$ relates to the causal structure through:

$$\Sigma = (I - B)^{-1} \Omega ((I - B)^{-1})^T$$

where $\Omega = \text{diag}(\sigma_1^2, \ldots, \sigma_d^2)$ is the noise covariance. This equation shows why the problem is hard: many different $B$ matrices (different causal structures) can produce the same $\Sigma$ (the same observational distribution). These are the Markov-equivalent DAGs.

The PC algorithm exploits the fact that $X_i \perp\!\!\!\perp X_j \mid X_S$ if and only if $X_i$ and $X_j$ are d-separated given $X_S$ in the true DAG. Each conditional independence test effectively checks whether a partial correlation is zero:

$$\rho_{ij \cdot S} = 0 \iff X_i \perp\!\!\!\perp X_j \mid X_S$$

in the linear Gaussian case. The BIC score for a DAG $G$ decomposes as:

$$\text{BIC}(G) = \sum_{i=1}^{d} \left[ -\frac{n}{2} \log \hat{\sigma}_i^2 - \frac{|\text{pa}_i|}{2} \log n \right]$$

where $\hat{\sigma}_i^2$ is the residual variance from regressing $X_i$ on its parents $\text{pa}_i$ in $G$. The $\log n$ penalty controls complexity, ensuring that spurious edges are pruned.

!! Practical Pipeline and Software

A typical causal discovery pipeline begins with data preprocessing: checking for missing values, detecting outliers, and assessing whether variables are approximately continuous or discrete. The choice of algorithm depends on domain constraints. If sample size is large relative to the number of variables and Gaussianity is reasonable, the PC algorithm with Fisher's z-test for conditional independence is a solid default. For mixed data or nonlinear relationships, kernel-based independence tests (HSIC) or conditional mutual information estimators are used instead.

Software ecosystems have matured considerably. The ''causal-learn'' library (Python, successor to the Tetrad project) implements PC, FCI, GES, NOTEARS, and many variants. ''gCastle'' provides GPU-accelerated continuous optimization methods. ''bnlearn'' (R) offers a comprehensive suite of constraint-based and score-based algorithms for <<link "Bayesian Networks" "Bayesian Networks">><</link>>. The ''DoWhy'' library integrates discovery with downstream <<link "Causal Inference" "Causal Inference">><</link>> tasks like treatment effect estimation.

Validation is the hardest practical challenge. Without ground-truth DAGs, practitioners rely on: (1) checking whether the discovered graph is consistent with known domain relationships, (2) stability analysis — running the algorithm on bootstrap resamples and retaining only edges that appear in most runs, and (3) held-out predictive checks using the discovered structure for conditional density estimation.

---

!! When Causal Discovery Breaks Down

''Faithfulness violations:'' The entire constraint-based framework assumes that every d-separation implies a conditional independence and vice versa. But in practice, ''unfaithful'' distributions arise when causal effects cancel exactly — for example, when the direct effect of $X$ on $Y$ is precisely negated by the indirect effect through $Z$. In such cases, the data shows $X \perp\!\!\!\perp Y$ even though a causal path exists, and the algorithm will incorrectly remove that edge. Faithfulness violations are measure-zero in theory but can be approximately satisfied in finite samples, especially in highly structured systems like feedback control loops.

''Insufficient sample size:'' Conditional independence tests require enough data to reliably detect (or fail to detect) associations. When the number of variables is large relative to the sample size, tests become unreliable: they miss weak but real dependencies (false negatives) or hallucinate dependencies from sampling noise (false positives). This is especially severe for high-order conditional independence tests, where conditioning on many variables fragments the data. The PC algorithm's output can change dramatically with small perturbations to the data in this regime, making the discovered structure unreliable without bootstrap stability analysis.

''Latent confounders and selection bias:'' The PC algorithm assumes ''causal sufficiency'' — that all common causes of measured variables are themselves measured. If an unmeasured confounder $U$ causes both $X$ and $Y$, the algorithm may incorrectly place a direct edge $X \to Y$ or $Y \to X$. The FCI algorithm relaxes this assumption but pays a price in identifiability: the output PAG contains more ambiguous edges. Selection bias (where inclusion in the dataset depends on a collider descendant) can create spurious associations that no standard algorithm accounts for, leading to entirely wrong graph structures.

!! Technical Details

''Identifiability beyond Markov equivalence:'' The LiNGAM (Linear Non-Gaussian Acyclic Model) framework by Shimizu et al. (2006) showed that if noise terms are non-Gaussian, the full DAG is identifiable from observational data — not just the equivalence class. The key insight is that independent component analysis (ICA) can recover the causal ordering when noise distributions are asymmetric or heavy-tailed. This exploits the Darmois-Skitovitch theorem: if $Y = aX + \epsilon$ with $X$ and $\epsilon$ independent and non-Gaussian, then the causal direction ($X \to Y$ vs $Y \to X$) is identifiable because the residual in the wrong direction will not be independent of the predictor. Extensions include nonlinear additive noise models (ANMs) where $Y = f(X) + \epsilon$ with $f$ nonlinear and $\epsilon$ independent of $X$.

''Complexity:'' The number of DAGs on $d$ nodes grows super-exponentially — approximately $2^{d^2/4}$ for large $d$. Exact search is NP-hard. The PC algorithm has worst-case complexity $O(d^q)$ where $q$ is the maximum conditioning set size (bounded by the maximum degree of the true graph). For sparse graphs, this is practical up to thousands of variables. GES has polynomial-time guarantees per step but may require exponentially many steps in adversarial cases. NOTEARS converts the combinatorial problem to continuous optimization with $O(d^2)$ parameters, but the augmented Lagrangian procedure is not guaranteed to find the global optimum.

''The NOTEARS acyclicity constraint:'' The function $h(W) = \text{tr}(e^{W \circ W}) - d$ equals zero if and only if $W$ encodes a DAG. The matrix exponential $e^{W \circ W} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(W \circ W)^k}{k!}$ counts weighted walks of all lengths; the trace sums the diagonal, which counts cycles. If no cycles exist, the diagonal entries of $(W \circ W)^k$ are all zero for $k \geq 1$, so the trace equals $d$ (from the $k=0$ identity term). This constraint is smooth, allowing gradient computation, and has spawned a family of methods including DAGMA, GOLEM, and GraN-DAG that extend it to nonlinear models and different loss functions.

''Interventional causal discovery:'' When experimental data is available (even partially), algorithms like GIES (Greedy Interventional Equivalence Search) and JCI (Joint Causal Inference) can exploit interventional targets to break Markov equivalences and recover more edge orientations. This connects causal discovery to experimental design: choosing which variables to intervene on to maximally reduce structural ambiguity is itself an active research area in <<link "Causal Inference" "Causal Inference">><</link>>.
