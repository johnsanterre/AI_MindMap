! Bayes Theorem

[img[assets/bayes_theorem.svg]]

Imagine you are a doctor who knows that a certain disease affects 1 in 1,000 people. A patient walks in with a positive test result, and the test is 99% accurate. Your gut screams "this patient almost certainly has the disease!" But your gut is wrong. Because the disease is so rare, even a highly accurate test produces far more false positives than true positives — and the actual chance the patient is sick is only about 9%. ''Bayes' theorem'' is the mathematical machinery that corrects this intuition. It tells you how to //update// what you believe about the world when you encounter new evidence, combining what you already knew (the prior) with what the data is telling you (the likelihood) to arrive at a revised belief (the posterior).

---

!! The Core Insight

Most of everyday reasoning runs on a flawed shortcut: we see evidence and immediately jump to conclusions, ignoring how likely those conclusions were //before// we saw the evidence. Bayes' theorem forces you to account for both pieces of information.

The theorem says: the probability of a hypothesis $H$ given observed data $D$ is proportional to how well the hypothesis explains the data, //weighted by how plausible the hypothesis was in the first place//. In symbols:

$$P(H|D) = \frac{P(D|H) \cdot P(H)}{P(D)}$$

Each piece has a name and a role:
* ''$P(H)$ — the prior:'' your belief about $H$ before seeing data.
* ''$P(D|H)$ — the likelihood:'' how probable the observed data would be //if// $H$ were true.
* ''$P(D)$ — the evidence (marginal likelihood):'' the total probability of seeing this data under all possible hypotheses. It acts as a normalizing constant.
* ''$P(H|D)$ — the posterior:'' your updated belief after seeing the data.

The insight is that beliefs should not be binary — they are //degrees of confidence// that shift smoothly as evidence arrives.

---

!! Prior, Likelihood, and Posterior

Think of these three components as the three acts of a reasoning story.

''Act 1: The Prior.'' Before any data arrives, you have some background expectation. In the medical example, the prior is $P(\text{sick}) = 0.001$. In machine learning, the prior might encode a preference for simpler models, smaller weights, or smoother functions. A ''strong prior'' concentrates probability on a narrow range of hypotheses; a ''weak prior'' (also called ''uninformative'') spreads probability broadly, letting the data speak for itself. The choice of prior is where domain knowledge enters the model — and also where subjectivity lives, which is both Bayesian reasoning's greatest strength and its most debated feature.

''Act 2: The Likelihood.'' This is the data's voice. Given a specific hypothesis, the likelihood tells you how expected or surprising the data would be. A hypothesis that makes the observed data very probable earns a high likelihood score. In the disease example, $P(\text{positive test} | \text{sick}) = 0.99$ — high. But $P(\text{positive test} | \text{healthy}) = 0.01$ is also nonzero, and because there are so many healthy people, this small number produces a flood of false positives. The likelihood alone is //not// the answer; it must be combined with the prior.

''Act 3: The Posterior.'' Multiply the prior by the likelihood, normalize by $P(D)$, and you get the posterior — your revised belief. The posterior is a compromise between prior and likelihood. With little data, the prior dominates. With mountains of data, the likelihood overwhelms the prior and the posterior converges to the truth (assuming the true hypothesis is in your model). This tug-of-war between prior and likelihood is the engine of Bayesian learning.

The posterior from one round of evidence becomes the prior for the next. This is the Bayesian update cycle: observe, update, repeat. Each new data point sharpens your beliefs, and the math guarantees that this process is //coherent// — it cannot produce self-contradictory beliefs.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Start from the definition of <<link "conditional probability" "Conditional Probability">><</link>>:

$$P(H|D) = \frac{P(H \cap D)}{P(D)}$$

By symmetry, $P(H \cap D) = P(D|H) \cdot P(H)$, so:

$$P(H|D) = \frac{P(D|H) \cdot P(H)}{P(D)}$$

The denominator $P(D)$ is computed by summing (or integrating) over all hypotheses:

$$P(D) = \sum_{i} P(D|H_i) \cdot P(H_i)$$

or in the continuous case:

$$P(D) = \int P(D|H) \cdot P(H) \, dH$$

For the medical test example with $P(\text{sick}) = 0.001$, $P(+|\text{sick}) = 0.99$, $P(+|\text{healthy}) = 0.01$:

$$P(\text{sick}|+) = \frac{0.99 \times 0.001}{0.99 \times 0.001 + 0.01 \times 0.999} = \frac{0.00099}{0.01098} \approx 0.09$$

Only about 9% — the low prior overwhelms the high likelihood.

In the continuous parameter setting used throughout machine learning, Bayes' theorem becomes:

$$p(\theta | \mathcal{D}) = \frac{p(\mathcal{D}|\theta) \, p(\theta)}{p(\mathcal{D})}$$

where $\theta$ is a parameter vector. The <<link "maximum likelihood" "Maximum Likelihood">><</link>> estimate ignores the prior and maximizes $p(\mathcal{D}|\theta)$ alone. The ''MAP estimate'' (maximum a posteriori) finds the mode of the posterior $p(\theta|\mathcal{D})$. Full <<link "Bayesian inference" "Bayesian Inference">><</link>> goes further: instead of a point estimate, it integrates over the entire posterior to make predictions.

---

!! Bayesian Reasoning in ML

Bayes' theorem is not just a formula — it is a //framework// that shapes how many ML systems are designed.

''Regularization as a prior.'' When you add an L2 penalty to a loss function (Ridge regression), you are implicitly placing a Gaussian prior on the weights: $p(\mathbf{w}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \tau^2\mathbf{I})$. The MAP estimate under this prior is exactly the Ridge solution. Similarly, an L1 penalty corresponds to a Laplace prior, encouraging sparsity. Bayesian reasoning //explains// regularization rather than treating it as an ad hoc trick.

''<<link "Bayesian networks" "Bayesian Networks">><</link>>.'' These graphical models represent joint probability distributions over many variables using conditional dependencies. Every inference query in a Bayesian network — computing the probability of one variable given observations of others — is an application of Bayes' theorem propagated through the graph.

''Naive Bayes classifiers.'' One of the simplest and most effective classifiers applies Bayes' theorem directly: $P(\text{class}|x) \propto P(x|\text{class}) \cdot P(\text{class})$, with the "naive" assumption that features are conditionally independent given the class. Despite this strong assumption, Naive Bayes works surprisingly well for text classification, spam filtering, and medical diagnosis.

''<<link "Prior distributions" "Prior Distribution">><</link>> in deep learning.'' Bayesian deep learning places distributions over neural network weights. While exact inference is intractable, techniques like variational inference and Monte Carlo dropout approximate the posterior, providing uncertainty estimates that are critical for safety-critical applications like autonomous driving and medical imaging.

---

!! When Bayesian Reasoning Breaks Down

''1. Intractable posteriors.'' For complex models — deep neural networks, large graphical models — the integral $P(D) = \int P(D|\theta)P(\theta) d\theta$ has no closed-form solution and the posterior landscape is high-dimensional and multimodal. Approximate methods (MCMC, variational inference) are needed, but they introduce their own biases. Variational methods tend to underestimate uncertainty; MCMC can be prohibitively slow. The elegant formula on paper becomes a computational nightmare in practice.

''2. Sensitivity to prior choice.'' In low-data regimes, the posterior is dominated by the prior. If the prior is wrong — too narrow, centered on implausible values, or structurally mismatched to the problem — the posterior will be confidently wrong. Two analysts with different priors can reach different conclusions from the same data, which critics argue makes Bayesian reasoning subjective. With enough data the prior washes out, but "enough" can mean thousands or millions of observations depending on the problem.

''3. Model misspecification.'' Bayes' theorem tells you the best hypothesis //within your model//, but if the true data-generating process is not in your hypothesis space, the posterior concentrates on the least-wrong option rather than the truth. Worse, the posterior can become extremely confident in this wrong answer, giving well-calibrated uncertainty //for the wrong model//. Bayesian reasoning does not protect you from asking the wrong question.

---

!! Technical Details

''Derivation from axioms:'' Bayes' theorem follows directly from the product rule of probability. If $P(A,B) = P(A|B)P(B) = P(B|A)P(A)$, then dividing both sides by $P(B)$ gives $P(A|B) = P(B|A)P(A)/P(B)$.

''Odds form:'' An alternative formulation uses odds ratios. The ''posterior odds'' equal the ''prior odds'' times the ''likelihood ratio'' (Bayes factor): $\frac{P(H|D)}{P(\neg H|D)} = \frac{P(H)}{P(\neg H)} \cdot \frac{P(D|H)}{P(D|\neg H)}$. This form is often more intuitive: a Bayes factor of 10 means the data is 10 times more likely under $H$ than under $\neg H$.

''Conjugate priors:'' When the prior and posterior belong to the same distribution family, the prior is called ''conjugate'' to the likelihood. Examples: Beta-Binomial, Gaussian-Gaussian, Dirichlet-Multinomial. Conjugacy enables closed-form updates and is the foundation of tractable Bayesian models.

''Sequential updating:'' If data arrives in batches $D_1, D_2, \ldots$, the posterior after $D_1$ becomes the prior for $D_2$: $p(\theta|D_1,D_2) \propto p(D_2|\theta) \cdot p(\theta|D_1)$. This gives identical results to processing all data at once — order does not matter.

''Bayesian model comparison:'' Given two models $M_1$ and $M_2$, their posterior ratio is $\frac{P(M_1|D)}{P(M_2|D)} = \frac{P(D|M_1)}{P(D|M_2)} \cdot \frac{P(M_1)}{P(M_2)}$. The ratio $P(D|M_1)/P(D|M_2)$ is the ''Bayes factor'' and implements an automatic Occam's razor: complex models that spread probability over many data patterns are penalized relative to simpler models that concentrate probability on the observed data.

''Connection to information theory:'' The KL divergence $D_\text{KL}(p(\theta|D) \| p(\theta))$ measures how much information the data provides about the parameters. A large KL divergence means the data was highly informative; a small one means the prior was already close to the posterior.
