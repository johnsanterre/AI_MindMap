! Hopfield Networks
<img src="assets/hopfield_networks.svg" alt="Hopfield Networks diagram showing classical fully-connected network with energy landscape and modern continuous Hopfield as attention" style="max-width:100%;margin:1em 0;">

Think of memory as an energy landscape covered in valleys. Each valley is a stored memory. A corrupted or partial input is like a ball rolled onto the hillside — it slides downhill under gravity, settling into the nearest valley. That valley is the retrieved memory. Hopfield networks make this physics metaphor mathematically precise: neurons evolve by gradient descent on a defined energy function, converging to stored patterns even from noisy starting points.

!! The Core Insight

Hopfield 1982: a recurrent network with ''symmetric weights'' $W_{ij} = W_{ji}$ and binary neurons $x_i \in \{-1, +1\}$. The key is an energy function $E = -\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{x}^T W \mathbf{x}$ that decreases monotonically under the update rule: flip each neuron to $\text{sign}(\sum_j W_{ij} x_j)$. Network dynamics are guaranteed to converge to a fixed point — a local energy minimum — which corresponds to a stored memory. Classical capacity is roughly $0.14N$ patterns for $N$ neurons. The ''modern continuous Hopfield network'' (Ramsauer et al. 2020) dramatically increases this capacity and reveals that self-attention in Transformers is a special case of Hopfield retrieval.

!! Classical Hopfield Networks

''Hebbian learning rule'' stores $P$ patterns $\{\xi^\mu\}$ via:

$$W = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{\mu=1}^{P} \xi^\mu (\xi^\mu)^T$$

with zeros on the diagonal ($W_{ii} = 0$). The energy function:

$$E(\mathbf{x}) = -\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{x}^T W \mathbf{x}$$

decreases at every asynchronous neuron update, so the network always converges. Each stored pattern $\xi^\mu$ is a local minimum of $E$. ''Retrieval'' works by initializing the network with a corrupted version of a pattern and iterating updates until convergence — typically within a few steps.

Problems: ''spurious memories'' (stable states that aren't stored patterns, arising from interference), ''capacity limit'' of $\approx 0.14N$ before retrieval degrades badly, binary-only patterns, and slow dynamics for large networks.

!! Modern Continuous Hopfield Networks

Ramsauer et al. 2020 reformulated Hopfield networks with continuous states and a new energy function:

$$E = -\text{lse}(\beta, X^T \xi) + \frac{1}{2}\xi^T \xi + \frac{1}{\beta}\log N + \frac{1}{2}M^2$$

where $\text{lse}(\beta, \mathbf{z}) = \frac{1}{\beta}\log\sum_i e^{\beta z_i}$ is log-sum-exp, $X$ is the matrix of stored patterns (columns), $\xi$ is the current state (query), $\beta$ is an inverse temperature, and $M = \max_i \|\mathbf{x}^i\|$.

The update rule derived from this energy is:

$$\xi^{\text{new}} = X \cdot \text{softmax}(\beta X^T \xi)$$

This is ''exactly one step of the attention mechanism'' used in Transformers. Capacity grows exponentially: up to $\sim \exp(d/2)$ patterns can be stored without interference for pattern dimension $d$. With high $\beta$ (low temperature), retrieval converges in a single step.

!! Hopfield Networks as Attention

The correspondence to attention is precise. In standard scaled dot-product attention, queries $Q$, keys $K$, and values $V$ produce:

$$\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V$$

In the Hopfield update, $\xi$ plays the role of the query, and $X$ plays the role of both keys and values. Setting $\beta = 1/\sqrt{d}$ recovers standard attention scaling. Multiple Hopfield update steps correspond to stacking attention layers or using multiple heads. This provides a ''theoretical foundation'' for why attention works: each head is performing content-addressable memory retrieval from an associative memory with exponential capacity.

!! When Hopfield Networks Break Down

''Spurious memories (classical):'' when stored patterns interfere, the energy landscape develops false minima — stable states that don't correspond to any stored pattern. This limits practical capacity to $\ll 0.14N$ for correlated patterns.

''Temperature sensitivity:'' $\beta$ too high causes the update rule to become a hard nearest-neighbor lookup, which is unstable near decision boundaries; $\beta$ too low causes the network to average over multiple memories, returning a blurry superposition rather than a clean retrieval.

''Correlated patterns:'' highly similar stored patterns create merged or distorted energy wells, degrading retrieval for both classical and modern networks.

!! Technical Details

''Key papers:'' Hopfield (1982) original network; Amit, Gutfreund, and Sompolinsky (1985) statistical mechanics capacity analysis; Ramsauer et al. (2020) "Hopfield Networks is All You Need" (modern continuous formulation and Transformer connection); Millidge, Salvatori et al. (2022) universal approximation via predictive coding Hopfield networks.

''Storage capacity:'' classical $\approx 0.14N$; modern continuous $\sim \exp(d/2)$ patterns of dimension $d$.

''Update convergence:'' classical guaranteed by Lyapunov function (energy); modern guaranteed by bounded energy decrease at each step.

''Applications:'' drug discovery (DeepMind's AlphaFold-adjacent models), multiple-instance learning (drug activity prediction from molecular sets), interpretable attention analysis, few-shot learning with external memory.

''Relationship to Boltzmann machines:'' classical Hopfield is a zero-temperature Boltzmann machine; adding stochastic updates (probabilistic flip) recovers the Boltzmann machine, enabling learning via contrastive divergence.
