! Random Walk

<img src="assets/random_walk.svg" alt="Random walk diagram showing a 2D wandering path and Gaussian spread of position" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine a pollen grain suspended in water, buffeted by invisible molecular collisions, drifting unpredictably — left, right, up, down — with no memory of where it has been. This is Brownian motion, and it looks like pure chaos. Yet the //statistics// of this chaos are perfectly predictable: after $n$ collisions, the grain will have spread an average distance of $\sqrt{n}$ from its starting point. The mathematics of ''random walks'' governs everything from option pricing on Wall Street to the way diffusion models generate photorealistic images from noise.

!! The Core Insight

A simple random walk proceeds by the humblest of rules: at each discrete time step, move $+1$ or $-1$ with equal probability $\frac{1}{2}$. Yet from this single rule, a rich structure emerges.

After $n$ steps the expected position is $E[X_n] = 0$ — the walk is ''unbiased'', with no preferred direction. But the variance grows linearly: $\text{Var}[X_n] = n$. This means the typical displacement is $\sqrt{n}$, not $n$. The walk ''diffuses'' rather than drifts — it spreads, but slowly.

The ''Central Limit Theorem'' explains why: $X_n$ is the sum of $n$ independent, identically distributed steps, so by CLT the distribution of $X_n / \sqrt{n}$ converges to a standard Gaussian as $n \to \infty$. Position is approximately normally distributed regardless of the step distribution (provided finite variance).

Taking the step size $\delta x \to 0$ and step duration $\delta t \to 0$ while holding $(\delta x)^2 / \delta t = \sigma^2$ constant yields ''Brownian motion'' (or the Wiener process) — the continuous-time limit of the random walk, foundational to stochastic calculus.

!! Variants and Generalizations

''Self-avoiding walks'' forbid revisiting any lattice site. They model polymer chains in solution, where a physical chain cannot occupy the same point in space twice. The statistics differ significantly from ordinary walks: the end-to-end distance scales as $n^\nu$ with $\nu \approx 0.588$ in 3D rather than $\sqrt{n}$.

''Biased random walks'' assign unequal probabilities to $+1$ and $-1$ steps, introducing a ''drift'' term. The position after $n$ steps has mean $\mu n$ (drift) and variance $\sigma^2 n$ (diffusion) — a combination central to Langevin dynamics.

''Random walks on graphs'' generalize the 1D walk to arbitrary network topologies. At each step, the walker moves to a uniformly random neighbor of the current node. This underlies ''PageRank'' (the stationary distribution of a random walk on the web graph), graph-based clustering, and embedding methods. <<link "Page Rank" "Page Rank">><</link>> uses the stationary distribution of such a walk to rank web pages. ''Node2vec'' (Grover & Leskovec 2016) uses ''biased'' random walks on graphs — interpolating between depth-first and breadth-first traversal — to generate node sequences for skip-gram embeddings.

''Lévy flights'' use heavy-tailed step distributions: instead of finite-variance steps, step lengths follow a power law $P(|Z| > x) \sim x^{-\alpha}$ for $\alpha \in (0,2)$. The CLT no longer applies; the distribution of $X_n$ converges to a ''stable distribution'' rather than a Gaussian. Lévy flights appear in animal foraging patterns, financial returns, and anomalous diffusion in disordered media.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Let $Z_1, Z_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. with $P(Z_i = +1) = P(Z_i = -1) = \frac{1}{2}$. The position after $n$ steps is:

$$X_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} Z_i$$

Since $E[Z_i] = 0$ and $E[Z_i^2] = 1$, we have $E[X_n] = 0$ and $\text{Var}[X_n] = n$.

''Polya's recurrence theorem'' (1921) establishes a stunning dimension-dependence: the probability of the walk returning to the origin is ''1'' in 1D and 2D (the walk is recurrent — it will return infinitely often), but ''strictly less than 1'' in 3D and higher (the walk is transient — it eventually escapes to infinity). This is why a drunk man will find his way home, but a drunk bird may not.

The ''first-passage time'' $T_0$ — the first time the walk returns to 0 — has a heavy-tailed distribution in 1D with $P(T_0 = 2k) = \binom{2k}{k} / (k \cdot 4^k)$, and its expectation is infinite.

The ''heat equation'' (diffusion equation) is the PDE limit of the random walk. Let $p(x,t)$ be the probability density of the walker's position at time $t$:

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = \frac{1}{2} \frac{\partial^2 p}{\partial x^2}$$

This is the same equation governing heat diffusion in a rod, making random walks and thermodynamics deeply connected.

The ''reflection principle'' counts lattice paths by reflecting paths that cross a barrier, enabling exact computation of first-passage and maximum-position distributions.

The random walk $X_n$ is a ''martingale'': $E[X_{n+1} \mid X_1, \ldots, X_n] = X_n$. So is $X_n^2 - n$ (the centered second moment). Martingale theory (Doob's optional stopping theorem) enables powerful results: the expected stopping time of a gambler's ruin equals the product of the two boundary distances.

!! Random Walks in ML

''Gradient descent'' on a stochastic loss landscape is a biased random walk. The gradient provides the drift (toward a minimum); the stochasticity of mini-batch gradients provides the diffusion. SGD's noise helps escape sharp minima and find flatter (better-generalizing) solutions — a phenomenon analyzed through the lens of Langevin dynamics.

''Dropout'' can be interpreted as sampling a random subnetwork at each training step — a random walk through the ensemble of all possible subarchitectures, regularizing by preventing co-adaptation of units.

''Node2vec'' generates training data for node embeddings by running biased random walks on the input graph, then applying Word2Vec (skip-gram) to the resulting sequences. The bias parameters $p$ and $q$ control exploration breadth vs. depth.

''Diffusion models'' are the most direct application: the ''forward process'' adds i.i.d. Gaussian noise at each of $T$ steps, exactly a discrete random walk toward a standard normal distribution. The ''reverse process'' learns to denoise — to walk backward through the noise — recovering the data distribution. See <<link "Diffusion Models" "Diffusion Models">><</link>> for the full treatment.

''MCMC'' methods, especially Metropolis-Hastings, construct a random walk on the parameter space designed so that its stationary distribution equals the target posterior. The chain proposes a random step; the acceptance criterion corrects for the target density. See <<link "MCMC" "MCMC">><</link>> for details.

<<link "Markov Chains" "Markov Chains">><</link>> are the natural generalization: a random walk is a Markov chain on the integers (or a graph), and Markov chain theory — mixing times, spectral gaps, stationarity — applies in full.

!! When Random Walk Models Break Down

''Heavy-tailed steps violate the CLT.'' The standard random walk assumes finite variance, so position distributions converge to Gaussian by CLT. Financial returns, gradient updates during instability, and many natural phenomena exhibit ''fat tails'' — occasional very large steps. When step variance is infinite ($\alpha \leq 2$ in Lévy flight notation), the Gaussian approximation fails entirely, the spread grows faster than $\sqrt{n}$, and Lévy process or stable-distribution models are required.

''Long-range dependence breaks the i.i.d. assumption.'' Standard random walks assume steps are independent. Real processes — stock prices, network traffic, seismic activity — exhibit ''long memory'': knowing the past tells you something about the future, even far into the future. ''Fractional Brownian motion'' with Hurst exponent $H \neq \frac{1}{2}$ models such processes: $H > \frac{1}{2}$ gives persistent (trending) walks, $H < \frac{1}{2}$ gives anti-persistent (mean-reverting) walks. The simple $\sqrt{n}$ scaling is replaced by $n^H$.

''Boundary effects alter walk statistics near constraints.'' Free random walk theory assumes unbounded space. When a walk operates near a hard boundary — parameter clipping in optimization, safety constraints in reinforcement learning, reflecting barriers in physics — the distribution of positions and hitting times changes fundamentally. Naive application of free-walk results (e.g., for mixing time bounds) can give badly wrong predictions near boundaries or in bounded domains.

!! Technical Details

''Historical milestones:'' Karl Pearson coined the term "random walk" in a 1905 letter to //Nature//, framing it as a model for mosquito infestation. In the same year, Albert Einstein's paper on Brownian motion derived the diffusion equation from random molecular impacts. George Polya proved the recurrence/transience dichotomy in 1921.

''Martingale connections:'' $X_n$ is a martingale; $X_n^2 - n$ is a martingale; $e^{\theta X_n} / (cosh\,\theta)^n$ is an exponential martingale. Doob's optional stopping theorem applies to all three.

''Gambler's ruin:'' Starting at position $k$ with absorbing barriers at 0 and $N$, the probability of ruin (reaching 0 before $N$) is exactly $1 - k/N$, and the expected game duration is $k(N-k)$.

''Spectral theory:'' On a finite graph, the mixing time of a random walk is governed by the spectral gap of the adjacency/transition matrix — the difference between the largest and second-largest eigenvalues.

''Key papers:'' Pearson (1905), Einstein (1905), Smoluchowski (1906), Polya (1921), Doob (1953), Grover & Leskovec — Node2vec (2016), Ho et al. — DDPM (2020).
