! Information Geometry

<img src="assets/information_geometry.svg" alt="Diagram showing the statistical manifold of probability distributions with Fisher metric and geodesics" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Ordinary geometry studies shapes in flat space using the Pythagorean theorem to measure distances. But probability distributions live on a curved surface — the "statistical manifold." The natural distance between two distributions is not Euclidean but is determined by the ''Fisher information metric''. Information geometry studies this curved space, and it reveals why natural gradient descent is fundamentally different from ordinary gradient descent and why KL divergence is not symmetric.

!! The Core Insight

Probability distributions form a ''Riemannian manifold'' with the Fisher information matrix as the metric tensor. When you parameterize a family of distributions — say, Gaussians with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$ — each point in the parameter space $\theta = (\mu, \sigma^2)$ corresponds to a distribution. The Fisher information matrix $I(\theta)$ defines how to measure distances on this manifold: two parameter vectors that are close in Euclidean space may correspond to very different distributions, and the Fisher metric accounts for this.

The ''natural gradient'' preconditions gradients by the inverse Fisher matrix, following steepest descent on this curved manifold rather than in flat parameter space. The result is an update that is invariant to how you parameterize the distribution — a property ordinary gradient descent lacks. This geometric perspective unifies many ideas in statistics and machine learning: the Cramer-Rao bound, sufficient statistics, exponential families, and trust-region reinforcement learning all have clean interpretations on the statistical manifold.

!! The Fisher Information Metric

The ''Fisher information matrix'' is defined entry-by-entry as:

$$I(\theta)_{ij} = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial \log p(x;\theta)}{\partial \theta_i} \cdot \frac{\partial \log p(x;\theta)}{\partial \theta_j}\right]$$

It measures how fast the distribution changes as parameters change — more precisely, how much information the data carries about the parameters. A large Fisher information means small parameter changes cause large distribution changes; a small Fisher information means the data is nearly uninformative about those parameters.

The ''natural gradient update'' is:

$$\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \, I(\theta)^{-1} \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta)$$

This is steepest descent on the statistical manifold, not in flat parameter space. The KL divergence locally approximates the squared Fisher geodesic distance:

$$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p_\theta \,\|\, p_{\theta + d\theta}) \approx \frac{1}{2} d\theta^\top I(\theta) \, d\theta$$

''Exponential families'' — Gaussians, Bernoulli, Poisson, and most distributions used in practice — have a special structure: they are ''e-flat'' (straight lines in natural parameters) and ''m-flat'' (straight lines in mean parameters). This duality makes their geometry especially tractable.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The Fisher information matrix is the ''Riemannian metric tensor'' $g = I(\theta)$. On a Riemannian manifold, the metric tensor tells you how to compute distances and angles in a coordinate-dependent way. For the statistical manifold, this metric is the unique one (up to a scalar) that is invariant under sufficient statistics and reparameterization.

''Geodesics'' are the shortest paths on the statistical manifold — the statistical analog of straight lines. For exponential families, geodesics in natural parameters are straight lines (e-geodesics), and geodesics in mean parameters are also straight lines (m-geodesics). These are the two ''dual connections'' introduced by Amari: the $+1$ connection (exponential) and $-1$ connection (mixture).

The ''Pythagorean theorem for KL divergence'' holds in exponential families: for three distributions $p$, $q$, $r$ where the e-geodesic from $p$ to $q$ is orthogonal (in the Fisher metric) to the m-geodesic from $q$ to $r$,

$$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p \,\|\, r) = D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p \,\|\, q) + D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q \,\|\, r)$$

The ''Cramer-Rao bound'' becomes a geometric statement. The variance of any unbiased estimator $\hat{\theta}$ satisfies:

$$\mathrm{Var}(\hat{\theta}) \geq \frac{1}{I(\theta)}$$

This is the statement that you cannot estimate parameters more precisely than the inverse metric allows — the Fisher metric is a fundamental limit on estimation.

!! Information Geometry in ML

''Natural gradient descent'' (Amari 1998) corrects for the curvature of the parameter space, achieving invariance to reparameterization. A model reparameterized from $\theta$ to $\phi = f(\theta)$ gives the same natural gradient updates — ordinary gradient descent does not have this property. In practice, the natural gradient often converges in far fewer steps than SGD, especially in early training when the loss surface is highly curved.

''K-FAC'' (Kronecker-Factored Approximate Curvature) makes the natural gradient practical for neural networks by approximating the Fisher matrix as a block-diagonal matrix with Kronecker-product blocks. Each block corresponds to a layer, and the Kronecker structure allows efficient inversion. K-FAC has been used to train ResNets and language models with faster convergence than Adam.

''TRPO and PPO'' in reinforcement learning constrain the KL divergence between consecutive policies:

$$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}} \,\|\, \pi_\theta) \leq \delta$$

This is a geodesic trust region on the policy manifold. The KL constraint ensures that consecutive policies are not too far apart //in distribution space//, regardless of how far apart they are in parameter space. PPO approximates this constraint with a clipped objective.

''Variational inference'' minimizes $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi \,\|\, p)$ over a family of approximate posteriors. This is projection onto a submanifold of the full distribution space. The ELBO is the variational objective, and natural gradient methods for variational inference (like natural evolution strategies) follow geodesics on the variational family's manifold.

The ''Neural Tangent Kernel'' (NTK) in the infinite-width limit of neural networks connects to information geometry: the NTK is the Fisher information matrix of the network outputs with respect to parameters, and the NTK regime corresponds to a linearized manifold where natural and ordinary gradient descent coincide.

!! When Information Geometry Breaks Down

''Intractable Fisher matrix for neural networks.'' The Fisher matrix $I(\theta)$ is $n_{\mathrm{params}} \times n_{\mathrm{params}}$ — for a network with millions of parameters, this is a matrix with trillions of entries. K-FAC and other approximations introduce structural errors (the true Fisher is not block-diagonal), and the Fisher matrix changes rapidly during training. Maintaining a good approximation requires frequent recomputation, making the per-step cost much higher than Adam or SGD. In practice, Adam's diagonal second-moment estimate is a crude but cheaper substitute.

''Geometric intuition fails in high dimensions.'' Geodesics and curvature are well-defined mathematical objects in millions of dimensions, but they are geometrically unintuitive. The statistical manifold of a deep network is highly non-convex, may have many saddle points and flat regions, and has no useful global geometric structure analogous to the simple curved surfaces that motivate information geometry. The local metric is well-defined, but local geometry may not reflect global optimization behavior.

''Misspecified models.'' Information geometry assumes the true data distribution is in the parametric family — that $p_{\mathrm{data}}$ is actually a member of $\{p_\theta\}$. If you fit a Gaussian to non-Gaussian data, the Fisher metric for Gaussians measures curvature of the Gaussian manifold, not curvature relevant to fitting $p_{\mathrm{data}}$. The geodesics and natural gradient follow the geometry of the //model family//, not the geometry of the problem. In high-dimensional, overparameterized models where the true distribution is unknown and likely outside any simple family, this assumption is almost never satisfied.

!! Technical Details

''Historical development:'' Rao (1945) first identified the Fisher information matrix as a Riemannian metric. Efron (1975) introduced statistical curvature as a measure of how non-exponential a family is. Amari (1985) systematized differential geometry of statistical models, introducing $\alpha$-connections and the dually flat structure of exponential families. Amari (1998) introduced natural gradient descent and analyzed its convergence properties.

''Connections to core statistics:'' Exponential families are characterized by sufficient statistics, and the Fisher metric on an exponential family has a particularly clean form in natural parameters: $I(\theta) = \nabla^2 A(\theta)$ where $A(\theta)$ is the log-partition function. The Cramer-Rao bound, Stein's lemma, and Bartlett identities all have geometric interpretations.

''Alternative geometries:'' Wasserstein geometry (optimal transport) provides a different metric on distributions, better suited for distributions with disjoint support (where KL divergence is infinite). The Wasserstein metric respects the geometry of the sample space, while the Fisher metric respects the geometry of the parameter space.

''Key references:'' Amari, S. (1985). //Differential-Geometrical Methods in Statistics//. Amari, S. & Nagaoka, H. (2000). //Methods of Information Geometry//. Martens, J. (2014). New insights and perspectives on the natural gradient method.
