! Hallucination
<img src="assets/hallucination.svg" style="max-width:100%;height:auto;display:block;margin:1em auto;" alt="Taxonomy of LLM hallucination types">

Imagine a student who writes a confident, well-structured essay full of made-up citations and plausible-sounding but false facts. The prose is fluent; the content is wrong. LLM hallucination is the same failure: the model produces text that sounds authoritative and reads naturally while being factually incorrect, logically inconsistent, or directly contradictory to provided source material. Fluency and accuracy are independent axes — a model can score perfectly on one while failing completely on the other.

!! The Core Insight

''Hallucination'' in LLMs occurs when a model generates content that is fluent and confident but false, unsupported, or contradictory. Unlike human confabulation, LLM hallucination is a structural feature of how these models work: next-token prediction optimizes for //plausibility of continuation//, not factual accuracy. There is no internal truth-checking step — only a learned distribution over likely next tokens. Three canonical types emerge: //factual hallucination// (inventing facts not supported by training data or world knowledge), //faithfulness hallucination// (contradicting facts explicitly present in a provided document), and //reasoning hallucination// (producing logically invalid steps in chain-of-thought). All three share the same surface symptom: confident, fluent output.

Related topics: [[Large Language Models]], [[Chain of Thought]], [[RAG]], [[Reward Modeling]], [[Calibration]], [[Model Calibration]].

!! Why Hallucination Happens

Four contributing causes:

(1) ''Knowledge gaps:'' the correct fact was absent or extremely rare in training data. Long-tail entities — obscure researchers, regional events, minority languages — are systematically under-represented.

(2) ''Pattern completion:'' next-token prediction rewards the most plausible continuation. "The capital of Australia is..." → "Sydney" is wrong but plausible; the model has no mechanism to prefer the less-probable correct answer "Canberra" unless trained explicitly to do so.

(3) ''Sycophancy:'' RLHF reward models are often trained on human preference ratings. Humans tend to prefer confident, fluent responses — rewarding the //style// of correctness rather than correctness itself. The reward signal teaches the model to sound certain even when uncertain.

(4) ''Attention dilution (lost-in-the-middle):'' in long contexts, transformers attend unevenly to the document. Facts stated far from the beginning or end of a long context are frequently ignored or contradicted in the output, producing faithfulness hallucinations even when the correct answer is literally in the input.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

A language model assigns probability $p(x_t \mid x_{<t})$ at each step. Hallucination occurs when greedy or nucleus sampling selects a token sequence that is high-probability but false. The model is ''not calibrated'' in the sense required for reliability: it assigns high probability to false statements.

Formally, a well-calibrated model would satisfy:

$$P(\text{answer is correct} \mid p_{\text{model}}(\text{answer}) = q) = q$$

for all confidence levels $q$. Empirically, LLMs are overconfident — their stated probability of being correct exceeds actual accuracy, especially for rare facts.

''Semantic entropy'' (Kuhn et al. 2023) clusters sampled answers by meaning and computes entropy over meaning clusters. High semantic entropy signals genuine uncertainty; low semantic entropy with wrong answers signals confident hallucination.

''Self-consistency'' (Wang et al. 2022) samples $k$ answers and takes a majority vote. Disagreement across samples is a signal of unreliability.

!! Detection and Mitigation

''Detection methods:''
- ''Self-consistency:'' sample multiple answers at nonzero temperature; flag low agreement as uncertain
- ''Semantic entropy:'' cluster answers by meaning; high entropy = model is uncertain
- ''SelfCheckGPT'' (Manakul et al. 2023): check whether sentences in a generation are supported by other independent samples
- ''Retrieval-based fact verification:'' retrieve supporting documents and compare claims

''Mitigation methods:''
- ''RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation):'' ground answers in retrieved documents, reducing reliance on parametric memory
- ''Citation generation:'' require the model to cite specific spans; unsupported claims become detectable
- ''Chain-of-Verification:'' generate verification questions from the draft answer, then answer them independently and check for contradictions
- ''Abstention:'' train models to say "I don't know" when uncertain rather than confabulate
- ''Factuality RLHF:'' use a reward model trained to prefer factually accurate responses over merely fluent ones

!! When Hallucination Is Worst

''Long-tail facts:'' obscure facts appear rarely in training data. Accuracy drops sharply as entity frequency falls (Mallen et al. 2023, PopQA benchmark).

''Multi-hop reasoning:'' each reasoning step compounds error probability. If each hop is 90% accurate, three hops yield only 73% end-to-end accuracy.

''Instruction following with hard constraints:'' the model may violate an explicit constraint (e.g., "do not include X") while maintaining fluency that masks the violation.

''Persona or roleplay:'' the model may blend a character's fictional "knowledge" with real-world facts, producing confident fictions delivered in the persona's voice.

!! Technical Details

Key references:
- Ji et al. 2023 — survey of hallucination in NLG across task types
- Lin et al. 2022 — TruthfulQA benchmark; GPT-4 ~77%, GPT-3 ~58% accuracy
- Manakul et al. 2023 — SelfCheckGPT, sampling-based detection without external knowledge
- Kuhn et al. 2023 — semantic entropy as a principled uncertainty measure
- Mallen et al. 2023 — PopQA; strong correlation between entity frequency in training data and factual accuracy
- Needle-in-a-haystack test: measures faithfulness by hiding a fact deep in a long document and asking the model to retrieve it; performance degrades for middle positions
- RAGAS metrics: automated evaluation suite for RAG faithfulness and answer relevance
