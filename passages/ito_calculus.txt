! Itô Calculus

<img src="assets/ito_calculus.svg" alt="Itô's lemma formula and applications diagram" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Ordinary calculus works on smooth curves — functions that, when you zoom in far enough, look like straight lines. But ''Brownian motion'' is not smooth: it jiggles infinitely fast at every scale, and no amount of zooming reveals a tangent. If you try to apply ordinary calculus to a function of Brownian motion, you get the //wrong answer// — systematically, not randomly. Kiyosi Itô in 1944 invented the correction: an extra term that accounts for the roughness, transforming stochastic differential equations from a formal trick into a rigorous mathematical tool. This correction — ''Itô's lemma'' — is what makes diffusion models mathematically principled.

!! The Core Insight

The heart of Itô calculus is ''Itô's lemma'', the stochastic analogue of the chain rule. For a smooth function $f(W(t), t)$ of Brownian motion $W(t)$, the ordinary chain rule would give $df = \frac{\partial f}{\partial t} dt + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} dW$. But this is wrong. The correct answer is:

$$df = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}\right) dt + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\, dW$$

The extra $\frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}\, dt$ is the ''Itô correction''. It arises because Brownian motion has nonzero ''quadratic variation'': informally, $(dW)^2 = dt$. In ordinary calculus, $(dx)^2 \approx 0$ and we discard it. In stochastic calculus, $(dW)^2$ is //first-order in time// and cannot be discarded. Without this correction, every stochastic calculation that involves a change of variables is systematically wrong. The correction is not small — for the Black-Scholes equation, omitting it produces wildly incorrect option prices.

!! Stochastic Differential Equations

''Stochastic differential equations'' (SDEs) describe the evolution of a process $X(t)$ driven by randomness:

$$dX = \mu(X, t)\, dt + \sigma(X, t)\, dW$$

The ''drift'' $\mu$ is the deterministic part — the average direction of motion. The ''diffusion coefficient'' $\sigma$ controls the noise amplitude. Together they specify a process that is simultaneously predictable on average and uncertain at each step.

Key examples:

- ''Geometric Brownian Motion'' (GBM): $dS = \mu S\, dt + \sigma S\, dW$. Used in Black-Scholes. Prices stay positive and grow exponentially on average, but with log-normal fluctuations.

- ''Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process'': $dX = \theta(\mu - X)\, dt + \sigma\, dW$. Mean-reverting: the drift pulls $X$ back toward $\mu$. Used in interest rate models and as a building block for diffusion model noise schedules.

- ''Langevin dynamics'': $dX = \frac{1}{2}\nabla \log p(X)\, dt + dW$. Gradient ascent on a log-density plus noise. At equilibrium, the stationary distribution is exactly $p(X)$ — making this the engine of <<link "MCMC" "MCMC">><</link>> samplers.

The ''Fokker-Planck equation'' describes how the probability density $p(x, t)$ of a diffusion process evolves over time, translating the SDE into a deterministic PDE for the distribution. This PDE connection is what allows mathematical analysis of where diffusion processes end up.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ''Itô integral'' $\int_0^T H(t)\, dW(t)$ is defined as a limit of left-endpoint Riemann sums:

$$\int_0^T H(t)\, dW(t) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} H(t_k)\left[W(t_{k+1}) - W(t_k)\right]$$

The left-endpoint convention is crucial: $H(t_k)$ is evaluated //before// the increment $dW$ is observed, so $H(t)$ is adapted to the current information — it cannot peek into the future. This is what makes the Itô integral a ''martingale'', which has clean probabilistic properties.

The ''Itô isometry'' is the fundamental tool for computing variances of stochastic integrals:

$$\mathbb{E}\!\left[\left|\int_0^T H\, dW\right|^2\right] = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\int_0^T H^2\, dt\right]$$

This converts the hard problem of computing a variance of a stochastic integral into the easier problem of computing an ordinary expectation. For a general SDE $dX = \mu\, dt + \sigma\, dW$, Itô's lemma for $f(X(t), t)$ is:

$$df = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\mu + \frac{\partial f}{\partial t} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}\right)dt + \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\sigma\right)dW$$

Applying this to $f(S) = \log S$ with GBM $dS = \mu S\, dt + \sigma S\, dW$ yields $d(\log S) = (\mu - \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2)\, dt + \sigma\, dW$ — the Itô correction produces the $-\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2$ term that shifts the drift of log-price. This is how Black-Scholes derives its option pricing formula. The same connection yields the ''Feynman-Kac formula'', which represents the solution of certain PDEs as expectations over diffusion processes.

!! Itô Calculus in ML and Diffusion Models

The modern resurgence of Itô calculus in ML was catalyzed by the ''score-based diffusion model'' framework (Song et al. 2021). The key insight: the forward process that gradually destroys data by adding noise is an SDE:

$$dX = f(X, t)\, dt + g(t)\, dW$$

The ''reverse-time SDE'' (Anderson 1982) removes noise and reconstructs data:

$$dX = \left[f(X, t) - g(t)^2 \nabla_x \log p_t(X)\right]dt + g(t)\, d\bar{W}$$

The reversal formula requires Itô's lemma and Girsanov's theorem to derive rigorously. The term $\nabla_x \log p_t(X)$ is the ''score function'' — learned by a neural network in <<link "Score Matching" "Score Matching">><</link>>. This is why <<link "Diffusion Models" "Diffusion Models">><</link>> are not just heuristic: the entire generative process rests on a mathematically exact time-reversal of a well-defined SDE.

''Langevin MCMC'' uses the Langevin SDE directly:

$$dX = \frac{1}{2}\nabla \log p(X)\, dt + dW$$

At convergence, the stationary distribution is $p(X)$. Discretizing this SDE gives the unadjusted Langevin algorithm (ULA). With a Metropolis-Hastings correction step it becomes MALA — a practical <<link "MCMC" "MCMC">><</link>> sampler for Bayesian inference.

''Neural SDEs'' parameterize the drift and diffusion of an SDE with neural networks, producing a continuous-time analog of recurrent networks with rigorous uncertainty quantification for time-series data.

''Continuous normalizing flows'' (CNFs) use the ODE limit of SDEs (setting $\sigma = 0$) to construct invertible transformations with exact log-likelihoods, computed via the instantaneous change-of-variables formula — itself a consequence of Itô's lemma.

!! When Itô Calculus Breaks Down

''Stratonovich vs. Itô convention.'' There are two different ways to define the stochastic integral, giving two different calculi. Itô uses left endpoints; Stratonovich uses midpoints. The Stratonovich integral obeys the ordinary chain rule (no correction term), which is natural for physical systems derived as limits of smooth noise. Financial models use Itô because it gives martingale properties. Converting between conventions requires adding a correction drift term $\frac{1}{2}\sigma \frac{\partial \sigma}{\partial x}$. Confusing the two — using an Itô-derived formula in a Stratonovich context or vice versa — produces wrong answers with no obvious error signal.

''Non-Markovian diffusions.'' Itô calculus is built for Markov processes: the future depends only on the present state, not the history. ''Fractional Brownian motion'' (with Hurst exponent $H \neq \frac{1}{2}$) has long-range memory and is not Markovian. Extending stochastic calculus to this setting requires fractional calculus, and many of Itô's clean properties — the martingale property, the isometry, the simple form of Itô's lemma — break down or require substantial modification. Some diffusion model noise schedules implicitly assume Markovian structure that fails for correlated data.

''Infinite-dimensional state spaces.'' Rigorous Itô calculus extends to Hilbert-space-valued SDEs (stochastic PDEs), but the extension requires careful regularity conditions. The noise operator must be trace-class, cylindrical Wiener processes require renormalization, and many operations that are trivial in finite dimensions (such as pointwise products of functions) require additional technical care. Neural network training viewed as an SDE in weight space involves millions of dimensions and poorly understood geometry, making the formal SDE analysis of SGD an approximation whose validity is rarely verified.

!! Technical Details

- ''Itô (1944)'': Original construction of the stochastic integral and Itô's lemma.
- ''Stratonovich (1966)'': Alternative convention where chain rule holds; preferred for physics.
- ''Girsanov's theorem'': Change of measure for SDEs. If $dX = \mu\, dt + dW$ under $P$, then under a new measure $Q$ defined by the Radon-Nikodym derivative $\frac{dQ}{dP} = \exp\!\left(-\int \mu\, dW - \frac{1}{2}\int \mu^2\, dt\right)$, the process $X$ is a Brownian motion. Fundamental to importance sampling, <<link "MCMC" "MCMC">><</link>>, and risk-neutral pricing.
- ''Feynman-Kac formula'': The solution $u(x, t) = \mathbb{E}[g(X_T) \mid X_t = x]$ of the PDE $\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + \mu \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} = 0$ is the expected value of $g$ along diffusion trajectories. Connects PDE theory to Monte Carlo simulation.
- ''Black-Scholes (1973)'': GBM $dS = \mu S\, dt + \sigma S\, dW$. Itô's lemma on $\log S$ gives $\log S(T) \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left((\mu - \frac{\sigma^2}{2})T,\, \sigma^2 T\right)$.
- ''Anderson (1982)'': Time-reversal formula for diffusions, foundational to score-based generative models.
- ''Song et al. (2021)'': Score-Based Generative Modeling through Stochastic Differential Equations — unified framework for diffusion models as SDEs.
