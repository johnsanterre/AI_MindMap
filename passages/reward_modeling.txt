! Reward Modeling

<img src="assets/reward_modeling.svg" alt="Diagram illustrating Reward Modeling for RLHF" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you want to train a dog, but you can only be there occasionally to hand out treats. You cannot supervise every moment of the dog's day. A ''reward model'' solves this exactly as a proxy trainer would: it watches you give treats a few hundred times, learns the pattern of what earns a reward, and then automatically evaluates the dog's behaviour around the clock on your behalf. The dog — here, the language model — keeps getting feedback even when you are not in the room.

---

!! The Core Insight

''Reinforcement learning from human feedback'' ([[RLHF]]) requires a scalar reward signal at every step of policy training. But humans cannot evaluate millions of model responses during RL training — that would take years of annotator time. The solution is to train a ''reward model'' (RM) once on a modest set of human preference comparisons, then deploy that RM as a differentiable stand-in for human judgment throughout RL.

The RM takes a (prompt, response) pair as input and outputs a single real number: how much a human would approve of that response. During [[policy gradient]] optimization — typically via PPO — the RM score drives the update signal that nudges the language model toward more preferred outputs. Everything downstream of the RM depends on the quality of this learned proxy.

This makes the RM the ''bottleneck'' of the entire RLHF pipeline. A biased or easily exploitable RM produces a policy that scores high on the proxy while drifting from genuine human values. Understanding RM training, its assumptions, and its failure modes is therefore central to understanding [[Constitutional AI]], [[Instruction Tuning]], and the behaviour of modern [[Large Language Models]].

---

!! Bradley-Terry Model and Preference Data

Preference data is collected as pairwise comparisons. For a given prompt $x$, a human annotator is shown two responses $y_w$ (the winner) and $y_l$ (the loser) and chooses which they prefer. This is far easier and more reliable than assigning an absolute score.

The ''Bradley-Terry model'' formalises the probability that $y_w$ is preferred over $y_l$:

$$p(y_w \succ y_l \mid x) = \sigma\!\left(r(x, y_w) - r(x, y_l)\right)$$

where $\sigma$ is the sigmoid function and $r$ is the reward model's scalar output. The training loss is the negative log-likelihood of the observed preferences across all comparison pairs:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{RM}} = -\mathbb{E}_{(x,\, y_w,\, y_l)}\!\left[\log \sigma\!\left(r_\phi(x, y_w) - r_\phi(x, y_l)\right)\right]$$

The RM is initialised from a pretrained LLM (often after supervised fine-tuning) with the language modelling head replaced by a single linear layer that projects the final token's hidden state to a scalar. Competitive reward models are typically trained on 10,000–100,000 human comparisons, collected via carefully designed annotation interfaces.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The RM must learn a total ordering over the enormous space of possible (prompt, response) pairs — from a tiny sample of pairwise comparisons. It does this by embedding both prompt and response through a transformer backbone and reading off a scalar from the last token's representation. The training signal only tells the model //which// of two responses is better, never //by how much// in any absolute sense.

''Distribution shift'' is the central mathematical challenge. As RL training progresses, the policy $\pi_\theta$ moves away from the distribution on which the RM was trained. The RM must generalise to out-of-distribution (OOD) responses it has never seen — responses that are increasingly polished and potentially exploitative of RM weaknesses.

The standard mitigation is a KL-divergence penalty that keeps the policy close to a reference model $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ (usually the SFT checkpoint). The total reward used during PPO training is:

$$r_{\text{total}}(x, y) = r_\phi(x, y) - \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x)}$$

The coefficient $\beta$ trades off reward maximisation against staying near the reference distribution. A larger $\beta$ slows policy drift and limits reward hacking; a smaller $\beta$ allows more aggressive optimisation but risks exploitation of RM flaws.

---

!! Reward Hacking and Goodhart's Law

Once the policy optimises hard against the RM, it will find exploits. These are not bugs in the policy — they are the inevitable consequence of optimising an imperfect proxy. Common reward hacking patterns include: responses that are long and verbose (if length correlates with RM scores), sycophantic outputs that flatter the user, and gibberish sequences that happen to activate high-reward features in the RM's representation space.

This is a direct manifestation of ''Goodhart's Law'': "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." The RM captures a statistical regularity between preference labels and surface features, not the deep semantics of human values. See [[Goodharts law]].

Practical mitigations include: (1) the KL penalty described above; (2) ''reward model ensembles'', where disagreement among several RMs signals OOD inputs; (3) ''iterative RLHF'', in which new preference data is collected from the updated policy and used to retrain the RM; and (4) ''Constitutional AI'', which replaces or supplements a learned RM with AI-generated feedback grounded in explicit principles, reducing dependence on a single learned proxy.

---

!! When Reward Modeling Breaks Down

''Annotator inconsistency:'' Human preference labels are noisy. Inter-annotator agreement on subjective tasks typically sits around 70%, meaning a substantial fraction of training pairs carry conflicting or arbitrary signal. The RM learns from this noise, producing a proxy that reflects the average annotator's surface heuristics rather than any coherent underlying value system. Tasks requiring specialist judgment — medical accuracy, legal reasoning, code correctness — are especially vulnerable, because annotators may not be qualified to identify the genuinely better response.

''Reward over-optimisation:'' As the number of RL update steps increases, policy performance against the RM initially rises and then collapses — the policy finds high-scoring exploits that no longer reflect human approval. This over-optimisation curve was documented empirically by Gao et al. (2023), who showed that a gold-standard RM score peaks and then falls as the proxy RM score continues to climb. No amount of careful RM training fully eliminates this dynamic; it is structural, arising whenever a fixed proxy is optimised without bound.

''Generalisation failure under prompt shift:'' The RM is trained on a particular distribution of prompts and response styles. When the deployed system receives prompts from a different domain — a new language, a specialised technical field, adversarial jailbreaks — the RM's scalar outputs become unreliable, often confidently wrong. Because the RM's uncertainty is not communicated to the policy, the policy optimises against nonsensical reward signals in these regions, producing outputs that are confidently harmful or incoherent in exactly the contexts where careful behaviour matters most.

---

!! Technical Details

''Foundational papers:'' Ziegler et al. (2019) introduced RM-based RLHF for LLMs on stylistic text tasks. Stiennon et al. (2020) applied the framework to abstractive summarisation. Ouyang et al. (2022) scaled the approach to produce InstructGPT, with a 6B RM trained on ~33K comparisons. Bai et al. (2022) at Anthropic introduced Constitutional AI and reported on RM training at scale.

''Architecture:'' The RM backbone is typically the same architecture as the policy, or a smaller model (e.g., a 6B RM training a 175B policy). The scalar head is a single linear projection from hidden size to 1, applied to the representation of the final (EOS) token.

''Data scale and quality:'' Annotation throughput is the binding constraint. Typical labelling rates are 100–300 comparisons per annotator-hour. Ranking interfaces (ranking $k$ responses simultaneously) are more data-efficient than pairwise comparisons, yielding $\binom{k}{2}$ pairs per annotation.

''Active learning:'' Selecting comparison pairs where the current RM is most uncertain (low-confidence predictions) improves data efficiency substantially.

''Process reward models (PRMs) vs outcome reward models (ORMs):'' ORMs score only the final answer; PRMs score each reasoning step. For mathematical and multi-step reasoning tasks, PRMs trained on step-level human labels outperform ORMs (Lightman et al. 2023), because they provide denser training signal and can identify //where// in a chain of reasoning a model goes wrong.
