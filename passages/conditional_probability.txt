! Conditional Probability
[img[assets/conditional_probability.svg]]

Imagine you are at a party with a hundred people. You know that ten of them are engineers. Now someone tells you the person you are about to meet graduated from MIT. Suddenly your estimate that this person is an engineer shoots up, because the new information -- the MIT degree -- changes the landscape of possibilities. That shift in belief when you learn something new is exactly what conditional probability captures. It is the mathematical machinery for updating what you think is likely once you narrow the world down to only the outcomes consistent with what you now know.

!! The Core Insight

Conditional probability answers a deceptively simple question: //given that something has already happened, how does that change the probability of something else?// The key move is ''shrinking the universe''. Instead of considering every possible outcome, you restrict attention to only those outcomes where the given event is true. That restricted set becomes your new sample space, and you re-measure everything relative to it.

This is not just an academic trick. Every time a spam filter sees the word "lottery" in an email, every time a doctor interprets a test result knowing the patient's age, every time a self-driving car updates its model after detecting rain -- conditional probability is doing the heavy lifting underneath.

!! Independence and Conditioning

Two events ''A'' and ''B'' are ''independent'' when learning that B occurred tells you absolutely nothing new about A. Formally, $P(A \mid B) = P(A)$. The conditioning changes nothing.

But most interesting events in the real world are //not// independent. Knowing it rained this morning changes the probability that the road is wet. Knowing a patient smokes changes the probability of lung disease. Dependence is the norm; independence is the special case.

''Conditional independence'' adds a subtlety: two events can be dependent in general but become independent once you condition on a third variable. For example, two students' exam scores might be correlated overall, but //given the difficulty of the exam//, their performances become independent. This concept is the backbone of <<link "Conditional Independence" "Conditional Independence">><</link>> and powers the factorizations used in graphical models throughout machine learning.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The definition of conditional probability is:

$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}, \quad P(B) > 0$$

This says: to find the probability of A given B, take the probability that //both// A and B occur and divide by the probability of B. You are rescaling the <<link "Joint Distribution" "Joint Distribution">><</link>> $P(A \cap B)$ so that the outcomes inside B now sum to one.

From this single definition, several important results follow.

The ''multiplication rule'' rearranges the definition:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A \mid B) \, P(B) = P(B \mid A) \, P(A)$$

This connects conditional probability directly to <<link "Bayes Theorem" "Bayes Theorem">><</link>>, which rearranges the multiplication rule into:

$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A) \, P(A)}{P(B)}$$

The ''law of total probability'' lets you compute $P(B)$ by summing over a partition $\{A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n\}$:

$$P(B) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(B \mid A_i) \, P(A_i)$$

This is how you recover a <<link "Marginal Distribution" "Marginal Distribution">><</link>> from conditional and prior information -- you sum (or integrate) the conditionals weighted by their priors.

The ''chain rule'' generalizes the multiplication rule to multiple events:

$$P(A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n) = P(A_1) \prod_{k=2}^{n} P(A_k \mid A_1, \ldots, A_{k-1})$$

This factorization is the foundation of autoregressive language models, where the probability of a sentence is decomposed into a product of conditional word probabilities.

!! Conditional Probability in ML

Conditional probability is not a sub-topic of machine learning -- it //is// the language machine learning speaks. Nearly every model, loss function, and inference procedure can be expressed as a conditional probability statement.

''Classification'' models estimate $P(Y \mid X)$, the probability of a label $Y$ given features $X$. Logistic regression, neural networks, and random forests all approximate this conditional distribution in different ways.

''Generative models'' work with $P(X \mid Y)$ -- the probability of the data given the class -- and use Bayes' theorem to flip it into $P(Y \mid X)$. Naive Bayes is the simplest example.

''Language models'' compute $P(w_t \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{t-1})$, the conditional probability of the next token given all previous tokens. The chain rule of probability is what licenses this decomposition.

''Bayesian inference'' treats model parameters $\theta$ as random variables and computes the posterior $P(\theta \mid \text{data})$, updating prior beliefs with observed evidence. This is conditional probability applied to the parameter space itself.

Even ''loss functions'' encode conditional reasoning. Cross-entropy loss measures how well a model's predicted conditional distribution $\hat{P}(Y \mid X)$ matches the true conditional distribution.

!! When Conditional Reasoning Breaks Down

''1. Confusing $P(A \mid B)$ with $P(B \mid A)$.'' This is the prosecutor's fallacy. The probability of the evidence given innocence is not the same as the probability of innocence given the evidence. In ML, confusing the likelihood $P(\text{data} \mid \text{model})$ with the posterior $P(\text{model} \mid \text{data})$ leads to choosing models that fit the data well but have absurdly low prior probability.

''2. Conditioning on a collider.'' When you condition on a variable that is //caused by// two independent variables, you can create a spurious association between those variables. For example, if talent and luck both contribute to success, then among successful people, talent and luck appear negatively correlated -- learning someone was lucky makes you think they were less talented. In ML pipelines, selection bias from filtering training data can introduce exactly this kind of distortion.

''3. Assuming independence when it does not hold.'' Naive Bayes assumes features are conditionally independent given the class label. When features are strongly correlated -- say, word bigrams in text -- this assumption breaks down and the model produces poorly calibrated probability estimates, even when its classifications remain surprisingly accurate.

!! Technical Details

Conditional probability can be formalized measure-theoretically as a ''Radon-Nikodym derivative'' when $P(B) = 0$, leading to ''regular conditional distributions''. This is essential for conditioning on continuous random variables where any single point has probability zero.

For continuous variables, the conditional density is:

$$f_{X \mid Y}(x \mid y) = \frac{f_{X,Y}(x, y)}{f_Y(y)}$$

''Conditional expectation'' $E[X \mid Y]$ is a random variable (a function of $Y$) that satisfies the ''tower property'': $E[E[X \mid Y]] = E[X]$.

The ''Kullback-Leibler divergence'' between two conditional distributions is:

$$D_{KL}(P(Y \mid X) \| Q(Y \mid X)) = \sum_y P(y \mid x) \log \frac{P(y \mid x)}{Q(y \mid x)}$$

In graphical models, the ''Markov property'' states that a node is conditionally independent of all non-descendants given its parents, enabling efficient factorization of high-dimensional joint distributions. See <<link "Probability Theory" "Probability Theory">><</link>> for the broader axiomatic framework.
