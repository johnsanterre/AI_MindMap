! Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces

<img src="assets/rkhs.svg" alt="RKHS diagram showing input space mapped into a Hilbert space where inner products equal kernel evaluations" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you have a city map and you want to know the temperature at any location. You could scatter thermometers everywhere, or you could build a smooth surface that passes through your readings and lets you read off the temperature at any point just by "asking" the surface. A ''reproducing kernel Hilbert space'' (RKHS) is exactly that smooth surface — a space of functions where you can always evaluate a function at any input simply by taking an <<link "Inner Product" "Inner Product">> inside the space. The kernel is the thermometer: it encodes how nearby points should influence each other, and it secretly defines the entire geometry of the function space you are working in.

!! The Core Insight

Most function spaces are too wild for point evaluation to be well-behaved. In $L^2$, for instance, two functions that differ on a single point are considered identical, so "evaluate $f$ at $x$" is not even a meaningful operation. An RKHS is a <<link "Hilbert Space" "Hilbert Space">> of functions where point evaluation //is// a bounded linear functional — it cannot blow up, no matter which function you pick from the space or which point you evaluate at.

The moment point evaluation is bounded, the ''Riesz representation theorem'' from <<link "Functional Analysis" "Functional Analysis">> kicks in: there must exist a unique element $k_x$ in the space such that $f(x) = \langle f, k_x \rangle_H$ for every $f \in H$. This element $k_x$ is called the ''representer of evaluation'' at $x$. The function of two variables $k(x, y) = \langle k_x, k_y \rangle_H$ is the ''reproducing kernel'', and it completely determines the space, its norm, and its geometry.

This is why the <<link "Kernel Trick" "Kernel Trick">> works: computing $k(x, y)$ in input space is the same as computing an inner product between the images of $x$ and $y$ in a (possibly infinite-dimensional) Hilbert space. You never need to construct the high-dimensional representation explicitly — the kernel does the computation for you.

!! From Kernels to Function Spaces

The connection between kernels and function spaces runs in both directions. Start with any ''positive-definite kernel'' $k: X \times X \to \mathbb{R}$ — meaning the Gram matrix $[k(x_i, x_j)]$ is positive semi-definite for every finite collection of points. The ''Moore-Aronszajn theorem'' guarantees that there exists a unique RKHS $H_k$ for which $k$ is the reproducing kernel. You do not need to construct the space by hand; the kernel //is// the blueprint.

Conversely, start with a Hilbert space of functions on $X$. If point evaluation is continuous for every $x \in X$, then the space is an RKHS and it has a unique reproducing kernel. The space and the kernel are two sides of the same coin.

Different kernels build very different function spaces. The ''Gaussian (RBF) kernel'' $k(x,y) = \exp(-\|x - y\|^2 / 2\sigma^2)$ produces an RKHS of infinitely smooth functions in an infinite-dimensional feature space. The ''linear kernel'' $k(x,y) = x^\top y$ gives a finite-dimensional RKHS of linear functions — ordinary linear regression. The ''Matern kernels'' produce Sobolev-like spaces whose smoothness you can dial with a parameter. Choosing a kernel is choosing which functions you believe are plausible before seeing any data.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ''reproducing property'' is the defining equation of the entire theory:

$$f(x) = \langle f, k(\cdot, x) \rangle_H \quad \forall f \in H, \; \forall x \in X$$

This says that the kernel section $k(\cdot, x)$ acts as a "delta-like" probe: its inner product with any function $f$ extracts the value $f(x)$. Applying this to the kernel itself gives:

$$k(x, y) = \langle k(\cdot, x), \, k(\cdot, y) \rangle_H$$

The kernel evaluation is literally an inner product in $H$. This is the ''feature map'' interpretation: define $\Phi(x) = k(\cdot, x) \in H$, and then $k(x, y) = \langle \Phi(x), \Phi(y) \rangle_H$.

The ''Riesz representation theorem'' is the engine. In any Hilbert space, a bounded linear functional $\ell: H \to \mathbb{R}$ has the form $\ell(f) = \langle f, g_\ell \rangle$ for a unique representer $g_\ell \in H$. Point evaluation $\delta_x(f) = f(x)$ is bounded in an RKHS by definition, so its representer is $k_x = k(\cdot, x)$.

The ''Moore-Aronszajn theorem'' constructs $H_k$ explicitly. Start with all finite linear combinations $\sum_i \alpha_i k(\cdot, x_i)$, equip them with the inner product:

$$\left\langle \sum_i \alpha_i k(\cdot, x_i), \, \sum_j \beta_j k(\cdot, x_j) \right\rangle_H = \sum_{i,j} \alpha_i \beta_j \, k(x_i, x_j)$$

then complete the space in the resulting norm. The reproducing property follows by construction. Positive-definiteness of $k$ guarantees the inner product is well-defined and the norm is non-degenerate.

''Mercer's theorem'' provides the spectral view. If $k$ is a continuous positive-definite kernel on a compact domain with <<link "Measure Theory" "Measure Theory">> $\mu$, the integral operator $(T_k f)(x) = \int k(x,y) f(y) \, d\mu(y)$ is compact, self-adjoint, and positive. Its spectral decomposition gives:

$$k(x,y) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \lambda_n \, e_n(x) \, e_n(y)$$

where $\lambda_n > 0$ are eigenvalues and $\{e_n\}$ are orthonormal eigenfunctions. The feature map becomes $\Phi(x) = (\sqrt{\lambda_1}\, e_1(x), \sqrt{\lambda_2}\, e_2(x), \ldots)$, revealing that the RKHS norm penalizes components along directions with small eigenvalues — an automatic smoothness prior.

!! How RKHS Powers Machine Learning

''The representer theorem'' is the workhorse result. Consider minimizing a regularized risk:

$$\min_{f \in H} \sum_{i=1}^n L(y_i, f(x_i)) + \lambda \|f\|_H^2$$

Despite $H$ being infinite-dimensional, the minimizer always lies in the span of kernel evaluations at the data: $f^*(x) = \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i \, k(x, x_i)$. This collapses an infinite-dimensional optimization to finding $n$ coefficients $\alpha_i$. <<link "Kernel Methods" "Kernel Methods">> — including <<link "Support Vector Machine" "Support Vector Machine">>s, kernel ridge regression, and kernel PCA — all exploit this fact.

''<<link "Gaussian Processes" "Gaussian Processes">>'' place a probability distribution over functions in an RKHS. The kernel $k(x, y)$ serves as the covariance function, and posterior inference amounts to linear algebra in the Gram matrix. The RKHS norm controls the complexity of sample paths: functions with small RKHS norm are "typical" under the GP prior, while functions with large norm are exponentially unlikely.

''Maximum mean discrepancy (MMD)'' uses the RKHS to define a distance between probability distributions: $\text{MMD}(P, Q) = \|E_{x \sim P}[\Phi(x)] - E_{y \sim Q}[\Phi(y)]\|_H$. When $k$ is a ''characteristic kernel'' (e.g., Gaussian), MMD is zero if and only if $P = Q$, giving a nonparametric two-sample test that avoids density estimation entirely.

''Embedding distributions'' into an RKHS — the ''kernel mean embedding'' $\mu_P = E[\Phi(x)]$ — enables Bayesian inference, causal discovery, and independence testing to be performed as inner product computations in $H$, sidestepping the curse of dimensionality that plagues density-based approaches.

!! When RKHS Breaks Down

- ''The kernel must be chosen wisely.'' The RKHS is entirely determined by the kernel, so a poor kernel choice means the true function may lie outside the space or require enormous RKHS norm to approximate. A Gaussian kernel with a tiny bandwidth produces an RKHS of extremely wiggly functions; too large a bandwidth and the space contains only bland, slowly varying functions. There is no universal kernel that works optimally for all problems, and kernel selection or learning remains an open challenge.

- ''Gram matrix scaling is cubic.'' Standard kernel methods require forming and inverting the $n \times n$ Gram matrix, costing $O(n^3)$ time and $O(n^2)$ memory. For datasets with millions of points, this is prohibitive. Approximations (random Fourier features, Nystrom approximation, inducing points) trade off fidelity to the RKHS geometry for computational tractability, and the quality of these approximations depends on the eigenvalue decay of the kernel — slowly decaying spectra are hard to approximate.

- ''Not all useful function spaces are RKHS.'' An RKHS requires point evaluation to be bounded, which excludes important spaces like $L^2$ and many distribution spaces. Neural network function classes do not generally form an RKHS (except in the infinite-width NTK limit), so the elegant RKHS theory does not directly explain the success of finite-width deep networks. Attempting to force deep learning into the RKHS framework can lead to misleading conclusions about generalization.

!! Technical Details

''Positive-definite kernels'': $k: X \times X \to \mathbb{R}$ is positive-definite if $\sum_{i,j} c_i c_j k(x_i, x_j) \geq 0$ for all finite collections $\{x_i\}$ and coefficients $\{c_i\}$. Equivalently, all Gram matrices are positive semi-definite. Positive-definiteness is necessary and sufficient for the existence of an RKHS (Moore-Aronszajn).

''RKHS norm and smoothness'': The RKHS norm $\|f\|_H$ measures complexity relative to the kernel. For Matern-$\nu$ kernels on $\mathbb{R}^d$, $H_k$ is norm-equivalent to the Sobolev space $H^{\nu + d/2}$. For the Gaussian kernel, $H_k$ consists of entire analytic functions with rapid spectral decay — extremely smooth.

''Universality and characteristic kernels'': A kernel is ''universal'' if its RKHS is dense in $C(X)$ (continuous functions on compact $X$). It is ''characteristic'' if the kernel mean embedding $P \mapsto \mu_P$ is injective. Gaussian and Laplacian kernels are both universal and characteristic. Polynomial kernels are neither.

''Integral operator spectrum'': The eigenvalue decay rate $\lambda_n \sim n^{-\alpha}$ of the kernel integral operator determines the "effective dimensionality" of the RKHS. Fast decay (large $\alpha$, smooth kernels) means the space is "nearly finite-dimensional" and learning rates are fast. Slow decay means the space is genuinely infinite-dimensional and minimax rates deteriorate.

''Connections to regularization'': The RKHS norm acts as a Tikhonov regularizer. The regularization parameter $\lambda$ in kernel ridge regression trades off fitting the data (empirical risk) against smoothness ($\|f\|_H^2$). The optimal $\lambda$ depends on the noise level and the eigenvalue decay of the kernel.
