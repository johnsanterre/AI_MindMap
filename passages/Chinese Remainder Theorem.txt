! Chinese Remainder Theorem

<img src="assets/chinese_remainder_theorem.svg" alt="Diagram showing simultaneous congruences, ring isomorphism, and applications of the Chinese Remainder Theorem" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you have a mysterious number, and all you know is what remainder it leaves when divided by 3, by 5, and by 7. A friend tells you: the remainder is 2 when divided by 3, 3 when divided by 5, and 1 when divided by 7. Remarkably, that is enough information to pin down the number exactly — at least up to multiples of $3 \times 5 \times 7 = 105$. The ''Chinese Remainder Theorem'' (CRT) says that if your divisors share no common factors, these remainders uniquely determine the number modulo their product. It is one of the oldest results in [[Modular Arithmetic|Modular Arithmetic]], dating back to the Chinese mathematician Sunzi in the 3rd century CE, and it remains one of the most practically useful theorems in number theory, powering everything from [[RSA Cryptography|RSA Cryptography]] to fast computer arithmetic.

!! The Core Insight

The CRT is fundamentally a statement about ''decomposition''. A single hard problem involving a large modulus can be broken into several easy problems involving smaller moduli, solved independently, and then reassembled. The theorem guarantees two things: (1) a solution always exists, and (2) it is unique modulo the product of the moduli.

More precisely: if $m_1, m_2, \ldots, m_k$ are pairwise coprime (meaning $\gcd(m_i, m_j) = 1$ for all $i \neq j$), then for //any// choice of remainders $a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_k$, there exists a unique $x$ modulo $N = m_1 m_2 \cdots m_k$ satisfying all the congruences simultaneously. The key intuition is that pairwise coprimality prevents conflicts — the constraints live in "orthogonal" directions, so they never contradict each other.

!! The Ring Isomorphism Perspective

The deeper algebraic statement of the CRT is an ''isomorphism of rings'':

$$\mathbb{Z}/N\mathbb{Z} \;\cong\; \mathbb{Z}/m_1\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/m_2\mathbb{Z} \times \cdots \times \mathbb{Z}/m_k\mathbb{Z}$$

This is not just a bijection of sets — it preserves both addition and multiplication. Working with a single large ring $\mathbb{Z}/N\mathbb{Z}$ is //equivalent// to working with several smaller rings simultaneously. Every arithmetic operation (add, subtract, multiply) in the big ring corresponds to doing the same operation independently in each small ring.

This perspective transforms the CRT from a curiosity about congruences into a structural principle: large algebraic objects decompose into products of smaller ones when the components are "independent" (coprime). The same idea recurs throughout algebra — in the classification of finite abelian groups, in the structure of [[Finite Fields|Finite Fields]], and in the factorization of ideals in number rings.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Given pairwise coprime moduli $m_1, \ldots, m_k$ with $N = \prod_i m_i$, and target remainders $a_1, \ldots, a_k$, the explicit construction proceeds as follows. Define:

$$M_i = \frac{N}{m_i}$$

Each $M_i$ is the product of all moduli //except// $m_i$. Since $\gcd(M_i, m_i) = 1$ (the other moduli are coprime to $m_i$), the ''modular inverse'' $y_i = M_i^{-1} \pmod{m_i}$ exists. Then the solution is:

$$x = \sum_{i=1}^{k} a_i \, M_i \, y_i \pmod{N}$$

Why does this work? Consider the $i$-th term $a_i M_i y_i$. Modulo $m_i$, we have $M_i y_i \equiv 1$, so this term contributes $a_i$. Modulo any other $m_j$ (where $j \neq i$), $M_i \equiv 0$ because $M_i$ contains $m_j$ as a factor. So each term "activates" for exactly one modulus and vanishes for the others — it is an //orthogonal decomposition// in modular arithmetic.

Finding modular inverses uses the ''extended Euclidean algorithm'': given $\gcd(a, m) = 1$, it finds integers $s, t$ such that $as + mt = 1$, which means $s \equiv a^{-1} \pmod{m}$. This runs in $O(\log^2 m)$ time — extremely fast.

For two moduli, the formula simplifies. If $x \equiv a_1 \pmod{m_1}$ and $x \equiv a_2 \pmod{m_2}$:

$$x = a_1 + m_1 \bigl((a_2 - a_1) \cdot m_1^{-1} \bmod m_2\bigr) \pmod{m_1 m_2}$$

This ''Garner's algorithm'' form generalizes to $k$ moduli iteratively and avoids computing large intermediate products, making it the preferred method in practice.

!! Practical Applications

''RSA decryption speedup.'' In [[RSA Cryptography|RSA Cryptography]], the modulus $N = pq$ is a product of two large primes. Decrypting $c^d \bmod N$ directly requires exponentiating modulo a 2048-bit number. Using CRT, you instead compute $c^{d_p} \bmod p$ and $c^{d_q} \bmod q$ separately (where $d_p = d \bmod (p-1)$, $d_q = d \bmod (q-1)$), then combine via Garner's formula. Since modular exponentiation is roughly cubic in the bit-length, two half-sized exponentiations are about 4 times faster than one full-sized one.

''Residue number systems.'' For parallel arithmetic in digital signal processing, large integers are represented as tuples of residues modulo small coprime numbers. Addition and multiplication happen independently on each component with no carry propagation — enabling massive parallelism. The CRT guarantees the representation is faithful (no information loss) as long as the number stays below the product of the moduli.

''Secret sharing.'' The Asmuth-Bloom scheme distributes a secret $S$ as residues modulo $k$ coprime numbers chosen from a larger set of $n$ moduli. Any $k$ shares suffice to reconstruct $S$ via CRT, but fewer than $k$ shares leave $S$ information-theoretically undetermined — a threshold secret-sharing scheme with unconditional security.

''Polynomial interpolation.'' Over [[Finite Fields|Finite Fields]] and polynomial rings, the CRT takes the form: given pairwise coprime polynomials $p_1(x), \ldots, p_k(x)$ and target remainders $r_1(x), \ldots, r_k(x)$, there is a unique polynomial modulo $\prod p_i(x)$ matching all remainders. When each $p_i(x) = x - \alpha_i$, this reduces to Lagrange interpolation — the CRT is the structural reason interpolation works.

!! When the Chinese Remainder Theorem Breaks Down

''Moduli are not pairwise coprime.'' If $\gcd(m_i, m_j) > 1$ for some pair, the theorem simply does not apply. The system $x \equiv 2 \pmod{6}$ and $x \equiv 3 \pmod{4}$ has no solution because $\gcd(6,4) = 2$, and $2 \not\equiv 3 \pmod{2}$ — the two constraints conflict on their shared factor. A generalized version exists: a solution exists if and only if $a_i \equiv a_j \pmod{\gcd(m_i, m_j)}$ for all pairs, but the solution is then unique modulo $\text{lcm}(m_1, \ldots, m_k)$, not the product.

''Comparison and ordering are lost.'' Residue number systems sacrifice the ability to compare numbers. Given $(2, 3, 1)$ and $(1, 4, 5)$ as residue representations, determining which underlying integer is larger requires reconstructing them via CRT — an expensive operation. This makes residue systems unsuitable for algorithms that rely heavily on comparisons, branching, or division.

''Reconstruction cost grows.'' While arithmetic in residue representation is cheap and parallel, converting back to a standard integer representation via CRT requires $O(k^2)$ modular multiplications (using Garner's algorithm) or $O(k \log^2 k)$ with fast algorithms. For applications where you frequently need the actual number — not just its residues — this reconstruction overhead can dominate the savings from parallel arithmetic.

!! Technical Details

The CRT first appears in //Sunzi Suanjing// (~3rd century CE), phrased as: "There are certain things whose number is unknown. If counted by threes, the remainder is 2; by fives, 3; by sevens, 2. What is the number?" The systematic algorithm was refined by Qin Jiushao (1247) in //Shushu Jiuzhang//.

Algebraic generalization: the CRT holds in any commutative ring $R$ for ideals $I_1, \ldots, I_k$ that are pairwise ''comaximal'' ($I_i + I_j = R$ for $i \neq j$). Then $R/(I_1 \cap \cdots \cap I_k) \cong R/I_1 \times \cdots \times R/I_k$. For $R = \mathbb{Z}$ and $I_i = m_i\mathbb{Z}$, comaximality is exactly coprimality.

Euler's totient via CRT: the isomorphism $(\mathbb{Z}/N\mathbb{Z})^\times \cong \prod_i (\mathbb{Z}/m_i\mathbb{Z})^\times$ immediately gives $\phi(N) = \prod_i \phi(m_i)$ when the $m_i$ are pairwise coprime — the multiplicativity of Euler's totient function.

Computational complexity: the explicit CRT construction runs in $O(k \cdot \log^2 N)$ bit operations using the extended Euclidean algorithm for each inverse. Garner's iterative method uses $O(k^2)$ modular operations but avoids large intermediate values.

Connection to Lagrange interpolation: over $\mathbb{F}_q[x]$, the CRT with moduli $(x - \alpha_1), \ldots, (x - \alpha_k)$ yields exactly the Lagrange interpolation formula. The basis polynomials $L_i(x) = \prod_{j \neq i} \frac{x - \alpha_j}{\alpha_i - \alpha_j}$ are the polynomial analogues of $M_i y_i$.
