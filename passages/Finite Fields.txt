! Finite Fields

<img src="assets/finite_fields.svg" alt="Diagram showing GF(5) clock arithmetic, GF(2^n) extension fields, and applications in error correction and cryptography" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine a clock with only 5 hours: after 4 comes 0, and every calculation wraps around. On this clock, 3 + 4 = 2 (since 7 mod 5 = 2), and remarkably, every nonzero number has a multiplicative inverse — 3 times 2 equals 1. This tiny number system where you can add, subtract, multiply, and divide (except by zero) is a ''finite field''. Finite fields are the algebraic backbone of error-correcting codes, modern cryptography, and the secret sharing schemes that protect distributed systems. Every time you scan a QR code, stream music from a scratched disc, or connect over HTTPS, finite field arithmetic is doing the heavy lifting beneath the surface.

!! The Core Insight

A ''field'' is a set equipped with addition and multiplication that satisfy the familiar rules: commutativity, associativity, distributivity, existence of additive and multiplicative identities (0 and 1), additive inverses for all elements, and multiplicative inverses for all nonzero elements. A ''finite field'' (also called a ''Galois field'', written $\text{GF}(q)$ or $\mathbb{F}_q$) is a field with finitely many elements.

The fundamental classification theorem states: a finite field of order $q$ exists if and only if $q = p^n$ for some prime $p$ and positive integer $n$. Moreover, for each such $q$, the finite field is ''unique up to isomorphism''. There is exactly one field with 2 elements, one with 4, one with 8, one with 256 — but none with 6, 10, or 12 elements. The prime $p$ is the ''characteristic'' of the field: adding 1 to itself $p$ times gives 0.

!! From Prime Fields to Extension Fields

The simplest finite fields are the ''prime fields'' $\text{GF}(p) = \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$: integers $\{0, 1, \ldots, p-1\}$ with arithmetic modulo $p$. Since $p$ is prime, every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse (guaranteed by Bezout's identity from the extended Euclidean algorithm). This is precisely [[Modular Arithmetic]] restricted to a prime modulus.

To build $\text{GF}(p^n)$ for $n > 1$, you need ''extension fields''. The construction mirrors how complex numbers extend the reals by adjoining $i = \sqrt{-1}$. Choose an ''irreducible polynomial'' $f(x)$ of degree $n$ over $\text{GF}(p)$ — a polynomial that cannot be factored into lower-degree polynomials. Then:

$$\text{GF}(p^n) \cong \text{GF}(p)[x] / (f(x))$$

Elements are polynomials of degree less than $n$ with coefficients in $\text{GF}(p)$. Addition is coefficient-wise (mod $p$). Multiplication is polynomial multiplication followed by reduction modulo $f(x)$. The critical example: $\text{GF}(2^8)$ — used in AES encryption — consists of 256 elements represented as 8-bit bytes, with addition being XOR and multiplication using the irreducible polynomial $x^8 + x^4 + x^3 + x + 1$.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ''multiplicative group'' $\text{GF}(q)^*$ (all nonzero elements under multiplication) is ''cyclic'' of order $q - 1$. A ''primitive element'' (or generator) $\alpha$ satisfies:

$$\text{GF}(q)^* = \{1, \alpha, \alpha^2, \ldots, \alpha^{q-2}\}$$

This means every nonzero element is a power of $\alpha$, enabling ''discrete logarithm'' computations central to cryptography.

''Frobenius endomorphism'': the map $x \mapsto x^p$ is an automorphism of $\text{GF}(p^n)$. The Galois group $\text{Gal}(\text{GF}(p^n)/\text{GF}(p))$ is cyclic of order $n$, generated by Frobenius. This connects finite fields to the broader Galois theory of field extensions.

Every element of $\text{GF}(q)$ satisfies the equation:

$$x^q = x$$

This means the polynomial $x^q - x$ splits completely over $\text{GF}(q)$, having all $q$ elements as roots. Conversely, $\text{GF}(q)$ is the splitting field of $x^q - x$ over $\text{GF}(p)$.

For ''polynomial interpolation'' over $\text{GF}(q)$: given $n+1$ distinct points $(x_i, y_i)$, the Lagrange interpolation formula works exactly as over the reals:

$$P(x) = \sum_{i=0}^{n} y_i \prod_{j \neq i} \frac{x - x_j}{x_i - x_j}$$

This is why Reed-Solomon codes and Shamir secret sharing work: polynomial evaluation and interpolation over finite fields are exact — no rounding, no floating-point errors.

!! Finite Fields in Practice

''Error-correcting codes'': [[Error Correcting Codes|Reed-Solomon codes]] encode a message as a polynomial over $\text{GF}(2^8)$, evaluate it at many points, and transmit those values. Even if some values are corrupted, the polynomial (and hence the message) can be recovered by interpolation — this is why QR codes work even when partially obscured and why CDs tolerate scratches.

''Cryptography'': [[RSA Cryptography]] operates in $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ (not technically a field, since $n$ is composite), but elliptic curve cryptography works over $\text{GF}(p)$ for large primes $p$. The ''discrete logarithm problem'' — given $\alpha^k$ in $\text{GF}(p)^*$, find $k$ — is computationally hard, forming the security basis for Diffie-Hellman and ECDSA.

''Secret sharing'': Shamir's scheme encodes a secret as the constant term of a random polynomial over $\text{GF}(p)$. Distributing $n$ evaluations means any $k$ shares can reconstruct the polynomial via Lagrange interpolation (using the [[Chinese Remainder Theorem]] in related constructions), but $k-1$ shares reveal nothing.

''AES encryption'': the SubBytes step computes multiplicative inverses in $\text{GF}(2^8)$ followed by an affine transformation. MixColumns multiplies a state vector by a matrix over $\text{GF}(2^8)$. Every modern encrypted connection uses this arithmetic.

!! When Finite Fields Break Down

''Composite orders have no field.'' There is no finite field with 6 elements, or 15, or any non-prime-power order. If you need a structure with $n$ elements for composite $n$, you get a ''ring'' ($\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$) where not every nonzero element has a multiplicative inverse. In $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$, for instance, $2 \times 3 = 0$ — these are ''zero divisors''. Algorithms that depend on division (interpolation, inverse computation) fail silently when the underlying structure is a ring rather than a field.

''Large characteristic fields are computationally expensive.'' While $\text{GF}(2^n)$ arithmetic maps beautifully to hardware bitwise operations (addition = XOR, shift-and-reduce for multiplication), arithmetic in $\text{GF}(p)$ for large primes $p$ requires multiprecision integer arithmetic. Modular multiplication of 256-bit numbers involves full 512-bit products followed by reduction — orders of magnitude slower without specialized hardware or Montgomery multiplication tricks.

''Irreducible polynomial choice matters.'' For $\text{GF}(p^n)$, different irreducible polynomials give isomorphic fields but different ''representations''. Converting between representations is nontrivial, and choosing a poor polynomial can make multiplication slower (sparse polynomials like $x^8 + x^4 + x^3 + x + 1$ enable efficient reduction). Interoperability requires agreement on the specific polynomial — the AES standard mandates one particular choice for $\text{GF}(2^8)$.

!! Technical Details

''Classification'': for each prime power $q = p^n$, there exists a unique (up to isomorphism) finite field $\text{GF}(q)$. Subfield structure: $\text{GF}(p^m)$ is a subfield of $\text{GF}(p^n)$ if and only if $m \mid n$.

''Counting irreducible polynomials'': the number of monic irreducible polynomials of degree $n$ over $\text{GF}(p)$ is given by the necklace polynomial:

$$N_p(n) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{d \mid n} \mu(n/d) \, p^d$$

where $\mu$ is the Mobius function. For $n = 8, p = 2$: there are 30 irreducible polynomials of degree 8 over $\text{GF}(2)$.

''Discrete logarithm algorithms'': baby-step giant-step runs in $O(\sqrt{q})$ time and space. The index calculus method achieves sub-exponential complexity $L_q[1/2, c]$ for $\text{GF}(p)$. For $\text{GF}(2^n)$, quasi-polynomial algorithms exist in certain regimes, which is why binary extension fields are being phased out of some cryptographic standards.

''Wedderburn's little theorem'': every finite division ring is a field (commutativity is automatic). This means there are no "finite non-commutative fields."

Historical note: Evariste Galois developed these ideas around 1830 (published posthumously 1846) while studying polynomial solvability. The theory was extended by E.H. Moore (1893) who proved the uniqueness theorem.
