! Primality Testing

<img src="assets/primality_testing.svg" alt="Timeline showing the evolution of primality testing algorithms from Trial Division through Fermat Test and Miller-Rabin to the AKS algorithm in 2002" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are a bouncer at an exclusive club called "The Primes." Your job is to check every number's ID at the door and decide: are you prime, or are you composite? For small numbers you can just try dividing — but when someone shows up with a 300-digit ID, you need a much cleverer strategy. ''Primality testing'' is the art of answering this single yes-or-no question — "is $n$ prime?" — as efficiently as possible. It sits at the intersection of pure number theory, computational complexity, and applied cryptography, and its history contains one of the most beautiful results in modern mathematics: the proof that this problem lives in the class P.

!! The Core Insight

The fundamental tension in primality testing is the gap between what is //easy to state// and what is //hard to compute//. A number $n$ is prime if its only divisors are 1 and itself. This definition is trivially simple. But //verifying// it naively — trying every possible divisor up to $\sqrt{n}$ — takes $O(\sqrt{n})$ steps. For a 300-digit number, $\sqrt{n}$ has 150 digits, meaning roughly $10^{150}$ divisions. The age of the universe is about $10^{17}$ seconds. Brute force is cosmically hopeless.

The insight that unlocks fast primality testing is ''Fermat's Little Theorem'': if $p$ is prime and $a$ is not divisible by $p$, then $a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$. This gives a //necessary condition// for primality that can be checked in $O(\log^2 n)$ time using modular exponentiation. If $a^{n-1} \not\equiv 1 \pmod{n}$, then $n$ is definitely composite. The deep question is: can we turn necessary conditions into //sufficient// ones without sacrificing speed?

!! Algorithms Through History

''Trial division'' is the oldest method: test whether any integer from 2 to $\sqrt{n}$ divides $n$. It is deterministic and correct but takes $O(\sqrt{n})$ time — exponential in the number of digits of $n$.

''The Fermat test'' picks a random base $a$ and checks whether $a^{n-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{n}$. If not, $n$ is composite. If yes, $n$ is //probably// prime. The fatal flaw: ''Carmichael numbers'' (like 561 = 3 × 11 × 17) pass the Fermat test for //every// base coprime to them, despite being composite. The Fermat test alone cannot reliably distinguish primes from these impostors.

''Miller-Rabin'' (1976/1980) strengthens the Fermat test by also checking for nontrivial square roots of 1 modulo $n$. Write $n - 1 = 2^s \cdot d$ where $d$ is odd. For a random base $a$, compute $a^d \bmod n$ and then repeatedly square it. A prime $n$ must satisfy specific patterns in this sequence. With $k$ independent random bases, the probability of a composite fooling the test drops below $4^{-k}$. For $k = 40$, the error probability is less than $10^{-24}$ — far below the probability of hardware failure during the computation.

''The AKS algorithm'' (Agrawal, Kayal, Saxena, 2002) was the breakthrough. Three computer scientists at IIT Kanpur proved that primality can be decided in ''deterministic polynomial time'': no randomness needed, no unproven assumptions. Their original algorithm ran in $\tilde{O}(\log^{12} n)$, later improved to $\tilde{O}(\log^{6} n)$. It resolved a question open since antiquity and showed that PRIMES is in P.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ''Fermat test'' computes $a^{n-1} \bmod n$ using fast modular exponentiation (repeated squaring), which takes $O(\log n)$ multiplications of numbers with $O(\log n)$ digits:

$$a^{n-1} \bmod n \stackrel{?}{\equiv} 1$$

If $n$ is prime, Fermat's Little Theorem guarantees this holds for all $1 \leq a < n$.

''Miller-Rabin'' writes $n - 1 = 2^s \cdot d$ and checks a stronger condition. Compute $x_0 = a^d \bmod n$, then $x_1 = x_0^2 \bmod n$, and so on up to $x_s = a^{n-1} \bmod n$. For a prime $n$, either:

$$a^d \equiv 1 \pmod{n}$$

or there exists some $0 \leq r < s$ such that:

$$a^{2^r \cdot d} \equiv -1 \pmod{n}$$

Any composite $n$ fails this test for at least 3/4 of all bases $a$ in $\{1, \ldots, n-1\}$, giving the $4^{-k}$ error bound after $k$ rounds.

The ''AKS algorithm'' relies on a polynomial identity generalization of Fermat's theorem. For prime $p$ and $\gcd(a, p) = 1$:

$$(x + a)^p \equiv x^p + a \pmod{p}$$

as polynomials, where all intermediate binomial coefficients $\binom{p}{k}$ for $0 < k < p$ vanish modulo $p$. AKS checks this identity modulo both $n$ and a polynomial $x^r - 1$ for a carefully chosen $r$, reducing the computation from exponential to polynomial.

!! Primality Testing in Practice

''[[RSA Cryptography]]'' depends entirely on primality testing. Generating an RSA key requires finding two large primes $p$ and $q$, typically 1024 bits each. The standard approach: generate a random odd number, apply Miller-Rabin with many rounds, and repeat until a probable prime is found. By the prime number theorem, roughly one in every $\ln(n) \approx 700$ odd 1024-bit numbers is prime, so the search terminates quickly.

Despite AKS proving PRIMES is in P, ''Miller-Rabin remains the practical choice'' for cryptographic applications. Its $O(k \log^2 n \log\log n)$ runtime is orders of magnitude faster than AKS for real-world key sizes. The astronomically small error probability ($< 4^{-40} \approx 10^{-24}$) is negligible compared to other failure modes like cosmic ray bit flips.

''[[Modular Arithmetic]]'' is the computational engine behind all fast primality tests. Every operation — exponentiation, multiplication, comparison — is performed modulo $n$, keeping intermediate values bounded by $O(\log n)$ digits.

The classification of PRIMES in P was a landmark in [[P vs NP|P vs NP]] research — it moved primality from BPP (where Miller-Rabin placed it) to P, confirming the long-held belief that this natural problem is efficiently solvable without randomness.

!! When Primality Testing Breaks Down

''1. Carmichael numbers defeat the Fermat test.'' These composite numbers $n$ satisfy $a^{n-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{n}$ for //every// base $a$ coprime to $n$. The smallest is 561 = 3 × 11 × 17. There are infinitely many Carmichael numbers (proven by Alford, Granville, and Pomerance in 1994). Any system relying solely on the Fermat test — without the square-root refinement of Miller-Rabin — will accept these composites as prime. This is not a theoretical curiosity; it is a real vulnerability if the wrong algorithm is used.

''2. Deterministic polynomial time does not mean fast.'' AKS runs in $\tilde{O}(\log^{6} n)$ — polynomial in the input length, but with large constants and exponents. For a 2048-bit RSA prime, $\log n \approx 2048$, so $\log^6 n \approx 7 \times 10^{19}$. Even with improvements, AKS is far slower than 40 rounds of Miller-Rabin. The complexity class P guarantees //asymptotic// efficiency, not practical speed. Choosing AKS over Miller-Rabin in a real system would make key generation orders of magnitude slower for no meaningful gain in correctness.

''3. Primality testing does not help with factoring.'' Knowing that $n$ is composite does //not// reveal its factors. Factoring is believed to be strictly harder than primality testing — factoring is not known to be in P (it is a candidate for the NP-intermediate class predicted by Ladner's theorem), while primality testing is in P. This asymmetry is precisely what makes RSA work: you can efficiently verify that $p$ and $q$ are prime, but an attacker cannot efficiently factor $n = pq$.

!! Technical Details

''Fermat's Little Theorem:'' If $p$ is prime and $\gcd(a, p) = 1$, then $a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$.

''Euler's criterion:'' For odd prime $p$, $a^{(p-1)/2} \equiv \left(\frac{a}{p}\right) \pmod{p}$, where $\left(\frac{a}{p}\right)$ is the Legendre symbol.

''Miller-Rabin error bound:'' For a single random base, the probability of a composite passing is at most $1/4$. With $k$ independent bases, the error is at most $4^{-k}$. This bound is tight — there exist composites where exactly 1/4 of bases are strong liars.

''Strong probable primes:'' Under the Generalized Riemann Hypothesis (GRH), Miller's deterministic variant proves primality by testing all bases $a \leq 2 \ln^2(n)$. This gives a deterministic $O(\log^4 n)$ test contingent on GRH.

''AKS core theorem:'' $n$ is prime if and only if $n$ is not a perfect power and there exists $r$ such that $\text{ord}_r(n) > \log^2 n$ and for all $a$ with $1 \leq a \leq \sqrt{\phi(r)} \log n$, $(x + a)^n \equiv x^n + a \pmod{x^r - 1, n}$.

''Complexity classification:'' PRIMES $\in$ P (AKS, 2002). COMPOSITES $\in$ NP (a factor serves as certificate). FACTORING is in NP $\cap$ co-NP but not known to be in P.

''Prime number theorem:'' $\pi(x) \sim x / \ln(x)$. The probability that a random $k$-bit number is prime is approximately $1/(k \ln 2)$.
