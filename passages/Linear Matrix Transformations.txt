! Linear Matrix Transformations

<img src="assets/linear_matrix_transformations.svg" alt="Linear Matrix Transformations" width="800">

Imagine you have a rubber sheet with a grid drawn on it, pinned at the center. Every way you can stretch, rotate, flip, or skew that sheet — without tearing it or lifting the pin from the origin — is a ''linear matrix transformation''. The grid lines might tilt or spread apart, but they always stay straight, evenly spaced, and anchored at the origin. This single idea — that matrices are //functions that reshape space// — is the conceptual key that unlocks nearly all of [[Core Linear Algebra|Core Linear Algebra]], from solving equations to training [[Neural Networks|Neural Networks]].

!! The Core Insight

A linear transformation is completely determined by what it does to the ''basis vectors''. In two dimensions, if you know where the two unit vectors $\mathbf{e}_1 = (1,0)$ and $\mathbf{e}_2 = (0,1)$ land, you know where //every// point in the plane lands. The columns of the transformation matrix are simply the new positions of these basis vectors. This means a $2 \times 2$ matrix doesn't just store four numbers — it encodes an entire geometric operation on the plane. Every point $\mathbf{v}$ gets mapped to $A\mathbf{v}$, and the entire space deforms in a smooth, predictable way. Linearity guarantees two properties: $A(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) = A\mathbf{u} + A\mathbf{v}$ (addition is preserved) and $A(c\mathbf{v}) = cA\mathbf{v}$ ([[Scaling|Scaling]] is preserved).

!! How Transformations Reshape Space

Each type of transformation has a distinct geometric signature:

''[[Rotation|Rotation]]'' spins every point around the origin by the same angle. Distances and angles between vectors are perfectly preserved — the grid rotates rigidly. In two dimensions, a rotation by angle $\theta$ sends the basis vectors to $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$ and $(-\sin\theta, \cos\theta)$.

''[[Scaling|Scaling]]'' (and [[Stretching|Stretching]]) pulls the grid along the coordinate axes. Uniform scaling multiplies all distances equally; non-uniform scaling stretches one axis more than another, turning circles into ellipses. This is the geometric heart of [[Singular Value Decomposition|Singular Value Decomposition]].

''[[Shear Transformation|Shear Transformation]]'' tilts one axis while keeping the other fixed. Think of pushing the top of a deck of cards sideways — each card slides proportionally to its height. Shears change angles but preserve area.

''[[Reflection|Reflection]]'' flips space across a line (in 2D) or a plane (in 3D). It reverses orientation — a clockwise-wound shape becomes counterclockwise. You can detect a reflection because the [[Determinants|determinant]] is negative.

''[[Projection|Projection]]'' collapses space onto a lower-dimensional subspace, like casting a shadow. Information is lost: multiple points map to the same image. This is the geometric engine behind [[Principal Component Analysis|Principal Component Analysis]].

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Every linear transformation in 2D can be written as matrix multiplication $\mathbf{y} = A\mathbf{x}$:

''Rotation'' by angle $\theta$:

$$R = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix}$$

''Scaling'' by factors $s_x, s_y$:

$$S = \begin{pmatrix} s_x & 0 \\ 0 & s_y \end{pmatrix}$$

''Shear'' along the $x$-axis by factor $k$:

$$H = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & k \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

''Reflection'' across the $x$-axis:

$$M = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$$

''Orthogonal projection'' onto the $x$-axis:

$$P = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

In each case, the columns of the matrix tell you exactly where the original basis vectors end up. Reading the matrix column-by-column //is// reading the transformation geometrically.

!! Composing Transformations

One of the most powerful properties of linear transformations is that ''composition equals [[Matrix Multiplication|Matrix Multiplication]]''. Applying transformation $B$ first and then $A$ is the same as multiplying by the product $AB$:

$$(A \circ B)(\mathbf{x}) = A(B\mathbf{x}) = (AB)\mathbf{x}$$

//Order matters.// Rotating then scaling generally produces a different result from scaling then rotating. In matrix language, $AB \neq BA$ in general. This non-commutativity is not a quirk — it reflects genuine geometric reality. Try rotating a rectangle 90 degrees then stretching it horizontally versus stretching first then rotating. You get different shapes.

In deep learning, each layer of a [[Neural Networks|Neural Networks]] applies a linear transformation (weight matrix multiplication) followed by a nonlinear activation. The network learns a //composition// of transformations that progressively reshapes data from its raw form into a space where classification or regression becomes easy. [[Computer Vision|Computer Vision]] models, for instance, learn transformations that separate visual features into distinct regions of high-dimensional space.

The ''inverse transformation'' $A^{-1}$ undoes the effect of $A$, sending every transformed point back to its original position. A [[Matrix Inverse|Matrix Inverse]] exists if and only if the transformation doesn't collapse any dimension — that is, when the determinant is nonzero.

!! When Transformations Break Down

* ''Singular transformations collapse dimensions.'' When $\det(A) = 0$, the transformation squashes space into a lower-dimensional subspace. A 3D volume becomes a plane, a line, or even a single point. Information is irreversibly destroyed, and the matrix has no inverse. This shows up numerically as division-by-near-zero instabilities.

* ''Non-linearity sneaks in through translation.'' A "shift" that moves every point by a fixed offset — like $(x, y) \mapsto (x + 3, y + 1)$ — is //not// a linear transformation because it moves the origin. Affine transformations handle this by embedding into one higher dimension (homogeneous coordinates), but confusing linear with affine is a common source of modeling errors.

* ''Floating-point errors compound under composition.'' Every time you multiply matrices, rounding errors accumulate. After many chained transformations, an orthogonal matrix (which should preserve lengths) gradually drifts, causing rotations to distort, scaling factors to creep, and geometric invariants to break. Re-orthogonalization procedures exist specifically to combat this.

!! Technical Details

The ''[[Determinants|determinant]]'' of a transformation matrix measures the factor by which the transformation scales area (2D) or volume (3D). $|\det(A)| > 1$ means expansion, $|\det(A)| < 1$ means contraction, and $\det(A) < 0$ means orientation is reversed.

The ''[[Eigenvalues And Eigenvectors|Eigenvalues And Eigenvectors]]'' of a transformation matrix reveal its //invariant directions// — directions along which the transformation acts as pure scaling. An eigenvalue $\lambda$ tells you the scaling factor along its eigenvector. Rotation matrices in 2D have complex eigenvalues $e^{\pm i\theta}$ because no real direction stays fixed under a nontrivial rotation.

The ''[[Singular Value Decomposition|Singular Value Decomposition]]'' decomposes any matrix $A = U\Sigma V^T$, expressing //every// linear transformation as: rotate ($V^T$), scale along axes ($\Sigma$), rotate again ($U$). The singular values in $\Sigma$ give the maximum and minimum stretching factors. The ''condition number'' $\kappa = \sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min}$ measures how close a transformation is to being singular — large condition numbers signal numerical instability.

An ''orthogonal matrix'' ($Q^TQ = I$) represents a pure rotation or reflection — it preserves lengths and angles. Orthogonal transformations have $|\det(Q)| = 1$ and all singular values equal to 1. They are the isometries of Euclidean space.

''Affine transformations'' extend linear transformations by adding a translation: $\mathbf{y} = A\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}$. They preserve parallelism and ratios of distances along lines, but not the origin. In homogeneous coordinates, they become linear maps in one higher dimension.

The ''rank'' of the transformation matrix equals the dimension of its image — the number of independent output directions. A rank-deficient transformation projects onto a proper subspace, losing information in the null space.
