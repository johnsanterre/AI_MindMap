! Cliques and Independent Sets
<img src="assets/cliques_and_independent_sets.svg" alt="Cliques and Independent Sets diagram">

Imagine a dinner party where some guests are mutual friends. A ''clique'' is a group where //everyone// knows //everyone// else -- no introductions needed. An ''independent set'' is the opposite: a collection of guests where //no two// have ever met. These two ideas are mirror images of each other, and together they capture fundamental tensions in networks -- between density and sparsity, between cohesion and separation. In [[Graph Theory|Graph Theory]], they form the backbone of problems ranging from social network analysis to compiler optimization.

!! The Core Insight

A clique and an independent set are //complementary// views of the same graph. If you take a graph $G$ and flip every edge to a non-edge and every non-edge to an edge (forming the ''complement graph'' $\bar{G}$), then every clique in $G$ becomes an independent set in $\bar{G}$, and vice versa. This duality means that any algorithm or theorem about one concept immediately gives you a result about the other. The hard part -- and it is genuinely hard -- is that finding the //largest// clique or independent set is one of the canonical problems in [[NP-Completeness|NP-Completeness]].

!! Formal Definitions and Key Numbers

A ''clique'' in a graph $G = (V, E)$ is a subset $S \subseteq V$ such that for every pair of vertices $u, v \in S$, the edge $(u, v) \in E$. A complete graph $K_n$ is itself one big clique of size $n$.

An ''independent set'' (also called a ''stable set'') is a subset $S \subseteq V$ such that for every pair $u, v \in S$, the edge $(u, v) \notin E$. No two chosen vertices share an edge.

Two critical graph parameters emerge:

* The ''clique number'' $\omega(G)$ is the size of the largest clique in $G$.
* The ''independence number'' $\alpha(G)$ is the size of the largest independent set in $G$.

The complementarity relationship gives us: $\omega(G) = \alpha(\bar{G})$ and $\alpha(G) = \omega(\bar{G})$.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The decision problem "Does $G$ contain a clique of size $k$?" is NP-complete. So is "Does $G$ contain an independent set of size $k$?" A brute-force search over all $\binom{n}{k}$ subsets is clearly exponential. The ''Bron-Kerbosch algorithm'' is the classic method for enumerating //all// maximal cliques. It uses backtracking with three sets:

$$\text{BronKerbosch}(R, P, X)$$

where $R$ is the current clique being built, $P$ is the set of candidate vertices that can extend it, and $X$ is the set of vertices already processed (used to avoid duplicates). The pivot optimization chooses a vertex from $P \cup X$ that maximizes $|P \cap N(u)|$, pruning branches dramatically. With pivoting, the worst-case complexity is $O(3^{n/3})$, which matches the Moon-Moser bound on the maximum number of maximal cliques in any $n$-vertex graph.

The ''Ramsey number'' $R(s, t)$ from [[Ramsey Theory|Ramsey Theory]] connects directly: it is the smallest $n$ such that every 2-coloring of $K_n$ contains either a red $K_s$ or a blue $K_t$. Equivalently, every graph on $n$ vertices has either a clique of size $s$ or an independent set of size $t$. The famous result $R(3,3) = 6$ means any graph on 6 vertices must contain a triangle or an independent triple. Computing Ramsey numbers is extraordinarily difficult and sits at the intersection of [[Combinatorics|Combinatorics]] and extremal graph theory.

Other key relationships include:

* ''Turan's theorem:'' the maximum number of edges in a $K_{r+1}$-free graph on $n$ vertices is $\left(1 - \frac{1}{r}\right)\frac{n^2}{2}$.
* The ''clique cover number'' $\theta(G)$ (minimum number of cliques that cover all vertices) satisfies $\theta(G) \geq \alpha(G)$.
* For perfect graphs, $\omega(G) = \chi(G)$ (chromatic number equals clique number) and $\alpha(G) = \theta(G)$.

!! Applications in Machine Learning

In ''[[Community Detection|Community Detection]]'', dense subgraphs correspond to tightly knit communities. ''Clique percolation'' (the CFinder/CPM method) finds overlapping communities by identifying adjacent $k$-cliques -- two $k$-cliques that share $k-1$ vertices. Rolling a clique through the network traces out community boundaries in a way that naturally allows nodes to belong to multiple communities.

''Feature selection'' can be cast as an independent set problem: build a graph where vertices are features and edges connect highly correlated features, then find a maximum independent set to get a set of mutually uncorrelated features. While finding the exact maximum is NP-hard, greedy and spectral approximations work well in practice.

Graph neural networks use ''clique complexes'' to extend message passing beyond pairwise interactions. Identifying cliques in molecular graphs helps capture higher-order structural motifs -- ring structures, functional groups -- that pairwise edges alone would miss.

!! When X Breaks Down

* ''Computational intractability:'' For general graphs, no known polynomial-time algorithm finds maximum cliques or independent sets. Approximation is also hard -- it is NP-hard to approximate the maximum clique within a factor of $n^{1-\epsilon}$ for any $\epsilon > 0$. Heuristics work on specific graph families but offer no guarantees.

* ''Sparse graphs mislead:'' In very sparse graphs, the clique number is trivially small (often just 2 or 3), making clique-based analysis uninformative. Community detection methods that rely on finding dense subgraphs will fail when communities are defined by connectivity patterns rather than raw density.

* ''Complement explosion:'' The complement of a sparse graph is dense, and vice versa. Algorithms efficient on sparse graphs (adjacency list traversals) become inefficient on their complements. The theoretical duality between cliques and independent sets does not translate into practical algorithmic equivalence.

!! Technical Details

''Maximal vs. maximum:'' A maximal clique cannot be extended by adding another vertex; a maximum clique is the largest maximal clique. A graph can have exponentially many maximal cliques (up to $3^{n/3}$), but the maximum clique is unique in size (though not necessarily in composition).

''Degeneracy ordering:'' For sparse graphs with degeneracy $d$, Bron-Kerbosch with a degeneracy ordering runs in $O(d \cdot n \cdot 3^{d/3})$ time. Since many real-world graphs have small degeneracy, this is often practical even for millions of nodes.

''Greedy coloring bound:'' $\omega(G) \leq \chi(G)$ always holds (the chromatic number is at least the clique number), and for perfect graphs equality holds. The complement of a bipartite graph is always perfect (Konig's theorem).

''Weighted variants:'' The maximum weight clique problem assigns weights to vertices and seeks the clique with maximum total weight. This arises in MAP inference in Markov random fields and in point cloud registration.

''Relaxations:'' The Lovasz theta function $\vartheta(G)$ is a semidefinite programming relaxation satisfying $\omega(G) \leq \vartheta(\bar{G}) \leq \chi(G)$ and $\alpha(G) \leq \vartheta(G) \leq \bar{\chi}(G)$. It can be computed in polynomial time and provides the best known efficient bound for many graph problems.
