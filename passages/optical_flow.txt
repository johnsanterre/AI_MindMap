! Optical Flow

<img src="assets/optical_flow.svg" alt="Diagram illustrating optical flow: per-pixel motion vectors between video frames and method timeline from Lucas-Kanade to RAFT" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine watching a river from a bridge. You cannot see the water molecules themselves, but you can track the pattern of ripples and leaves on the surface as they slide downstream. If you watch two snapshots a moment apart, each ripple has shifted a little — and by measuring those shifts across the entire surface, you can reconstruct the river's velocity field without ever touching the water. Optical flow does exactly this for video: given two consecutive frames, it assigns every pixel a tiny arrow — a displacement vector — indicating where that pixel's intensity pattern moved. The result is a dense motion field that reveals which parts of the scene are moving, how fast, and in what direction.

---

!! The Core Insight

Optical flow rests on a deceptively simple assumption called the ''brightness constancy constraint'': the intensity of a small patch of the image does not change as it moves between frames — it only changes position. If a pixel has intensity $I(x, y, t)$ in one frame and moves by $(u, v)$ to appear at $(x+u, y+v)$ in the next frame, then $I(x, y, t) = I(x+u, y+v, t+1)$. This constraint converts the problem of //understanding motion// into the problem of //solving a system of equations//: find the displacement $(u, v)$ at every pixel that keeps intensity constant.

The catch is that this single equation has two unknowns — $u$ and $v$ — at every pixel. This is the famous ''aperture problem'': looking through a small window, you can measure motion perpendicular to an edge but not along it. A horizontal stripe moving diagonally looks identical to one moving purely vertically when seen through a tiny aperture. Every optical flow algorithm is, at its core, a strategy for resolving this fundamental ambiguity — by assuming local smoothness (Lucas-Kanade), global regularisation (Horn-Schunck), or learning from data (FlowNet, RAFT).

---

!! From Classical to Learned Methods

The ''Lucas-Kanade'' method (1981) resolves the aperture problem by assuming that all pixels within a small neighbourhood share the same flow. This gives an overdetermined system of equations — more equations than unknowns — which can be solved by least squares. The method is fast and works well for small displacements, but it is //sparse// (only reliable at corners and textured regions) and fails when motion exceeds the neighbourhood size.

''Horn-Schunck'' (1981) takes the opposite approach: it computes //dense// flow everywhere by adding a global smoothness penalty that discourages neighbouring pixels from having wildly different flow vectors. This is a variational method — minimise an energy functional combining data fidelity and regularisation — solved iteratively. It produces beautiful smooth fields but over-smooths motion boundaries where flow should change abruptly.

The deep learning era began with ''FlowNet'' (Dosovitskiy et al., 2015), which framed optical flow as a supervised regression problem: feed two stacked frames into a <<link "CNN" "CNN">><</link>> and train it to directly output the flow field. FlowNet came in two variants — FlowNetS (simple concatenation) and FlowNetC (explicit correlation layer). It was fast but initially less accurate than tuned classical methods.

''PWC-Net'' (Sun et al., 2018) introduced a coarse-to-fine pyramid with learnable cost volumes and warping at each scale, combining classical intuitions with deep features. ''RAFT'' (Teed and Deng, 2020) then reset the state of the art by replacing the coarse-to-fine pyramid with iterative refinement on a single high-resolution correlation volume. RAFT builds a 4D correlation volume between all pairs of feature vectors, then uses a recurrent GRU unit to iteratively update the flow estimate — analogous to <<link "Gradient Descent" "Stochastic Gradient Descent">><</link>> on the flow field. RAFT's accuracy on Sintel and KITTI benchmarks surpassed all prior methods and its architecture became the template for subsequent work.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Start with the brightness constancy assumption:

$$I(x + u, y + v, t + 1) = I(x, y, t)$$

Apply a first-order <<link "Taylor expansion" "Taylor Series">><</link>> around $(x, y, t)$:

$$I(x, y, t) + I_x u + I_y v + I_t \approx I(x, y, t)$$

Cancel $I(x, y, t)$ from both sides to get the ''optical flow constraint equation'':

$$I_x u + I_y v + I_t = 0$$

where $I_x = \partial I / \partial x$, $I_y = \partial I / \partial y$, and $I_t = \partial I / \partial t$ are the spatial and temporal image gradients. This is one equation in two unknowns $(u, v)$ — the aperture problem.

Lucas-Kanade assumes constant flow in a $w \times w$ window and solves the least-squares system:

$$\begin{bmatrix} \sum I_x^2 & \sum I_x I_y \\ \sum I_x I_y & \sum I_y^2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} u \\ v \end{bmatrix} = -\begin{bmatrix} \sum I_x I_t \\ \sum I_y I_t \end{bmatrix}$$

This $2 \times 2$ system is well-conditioned only when both eigenvalues of the structure tensor (the left matrix) are large — which happens at corners, not along edges or in flat regions.

Horn-Schunck instead minimises a global energy:

$$E(u, v) = \iint \left[ (I_x u + I_y v + I_t)^2 + \alpha^2 \left( |\nabla u|^2 + |\nabla v|^2 \right) \right] dx\, dy$$

The first term enforces brightness constancy; the second penalises flow gradients with weight $\alpha$. The Euler-Lagrange equations yield an iterative scheme where each pixel's flow is updated toward the average of its neighbours, balanced against the data term.

RAFT constructs a 4D correlation volume $C_{ijkl} = \langle f^1_{ij}, f^2_{kl} \rangle$ between feature maps $f^1$ and $f^2$ from two frames, then indexes this volume at the current flow estimate to extract a local correlation signal. A convolutional GRU takes the correlation features, the current flow, and context features, and outputs a flow update $\Delta \mathbf{f}$ at each iteration — a learned analogue of gradient-based optimisation.

!! Applications in Practice

''Autonomous driving:'' Optical flow provides dense motion cues that complement <<link "Object Detection" "Object Detection">><</link>> bounding boxes. Flow reveals which parts of the scene are approaching the ego-vehicle, enabling time-to-collision estimates and the detection of independently moving objects that LiDAR alone may miss at long range.

''Action recognition and video understanding:'' Two-stream architectures for <<link "Video Understanding" "Video Understanding">><</link>> use optical flow as a second input channel alongside RGB frames. The flow stream captures motion patterns — a person swinging a bat versus standing still — that are difficult to extract from appearance alone. TV-L1 and RAFT-based flow extraction remain standard preprocessing steps for action recognition benchmarks.

''Video stabilisation and editing:'' Consumer video stabilisers estimate camera motion by computing global optical flow and then applying compensating transforms. Frame interpolation methods (RIFE, FILM) use flow to warp frames to intermediate time steps, enabling slow-motion effects and frame rate upconversion.

''Medical imaging:'' Optical flow tracks tissue deformation in cardiac ultrasound and respiratory-gated CT, measuring heart wall motion or lung expansion with sub-pixel precision.

---

!! When Optical Flow Breaks Down

''Occlusion and disocclusion:'' When an object moves, it reveals background that was hidden and hides background that was visible. At these occlusion boundaries, the brightness constancy assumption is violated — the pixel in frame $t+1$ has no corresponding pixel in frame $t$ at all. Classical methods produce large errors at occlusion boundaries; modern methods like RAFT handle them better through learned priors but still struggle with wide occluded regions.

''Large displacements and fast motion:'' The Taylor-series linearisation that underpins the optical flow equation assumes small displacements — roughly one to two pixels. Fast-moving objects or low frame rates violate this, causing the linearisation to diverge. Coarse-to-fine pyramids and correlation volumes at multiple scales partially address this, but very large motions (50+ pixels) remain challenging, especially for thin structures that vanish at coarse pyramid levels.

''Illumination changes and non-Lambertian surfaces:'' Brightness constancy fails when lighting changes between frames — a cloud passing over the sun, a specular highlight shifting on a shiny surface, or automatic camera exposure adjustment. Robust data terms (using the census transform or ternary patterns instead of raw intensity) and learned features that are invariant to illumination help, but sudden global illumination changes can still corrupt the entire flow field.

!! Technical Details

''Key papers:'' Lucas & Kanade (1981) "An Iterative Image Registration Technique with an Application to Stereo Vision." Horn & Schunck (1981) "Determining Optical Flow." Dosovitskiy et al. (2015) "FlowNet: Learning Optical Flow with Convolutional Networks" (ICCV). Ilg et al. (2017) "FlowNet 2.0" stacked multiple FlowNets for iterative refinement. Sun et al. (2018) "PWC-Net: CNNs for Optical Flow Using Pyramid, Warping, and Cost Volume" (CVPR). Teed & Deng (2020) "RAFT: Recurrent All-Pairs Field Transforms for Optical Flow" (ECCV, best paper).

''Benchmarks:'' MPI Sintel (synthetic movie sequences with ground-truth flow, occlusions, and motion blur), KITTI Flow (real driving scenes with LiDAR-derived sparse ground truth), Middlebury (small-displacement indoor scenes). RAFT achieves end-point error (EPE) below 1.6 px on Sintel Clean and below 5.0 px on KITTI-2015.

''End-point error (EPE):'' The standard metric, defined as the Euclidean distance between predicted and ground-truth flow vectors averaged over all pixels: $\text{EPE} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i \sqrt{(u_i - u_i^{gt})^2 + (v_i - v_i^{gt})^2}$.

''Correlation volume in RAFT:'' Given feature maps $f^1, f^2 \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times D}$, the full correlation volume is $C \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times H \times W}$ with $C_{ijkl} = \sum_d f^1_{ijd} \cdot f^2_{kld}$. RAFT pools this into a 4-level correlation pyramid and performs lookups within a local radius around the current flow estimate, keeping memory manageable.

''Warping:'' Given a current flow estimate $(\hat{u}, \hat{v})$, the second frame is warped toward the first using bilinear sampling: $I_{\text{warp}}(x, y) = I_2(x + \hat{u}, y + \hat{v})$. The residual flow after warping should be small, allowing fine-grained refinement. This is differentiable via the spatial transformer network formulation.

''Multi-scale vs. iterative:'' Classical and early deep methods use coarse-to-fine pyramids — estimate flow at low resolution, upsample, warp, refine. RAFT showed that iterative refinement at a single resolution (using the correlation pyramid for multi-scale context) outperforms explicit image pyramids, because thin objects that vanish at coarse scales are preserved.

''Unsupervised training:'' When ground-truth flow is unavailable, photometric loss (brightness constancy on warped images) plus smoothness regularisation can supervise flow networks. This enables training on arbitrary video but produces lower accuracy than supervised methods on benchmarks.
