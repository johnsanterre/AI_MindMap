! Markov Chains

<img src="assets/markov_chains.svg" alt="Markov chain diagram with transition probabilities and convergence to stationary distribution" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Think about tomorrow's weather. If you want to predict whether it will rain, you probably check today's forecast — not last Tuesday's. A meteorologist doesn't need the entire history of weather going back months; today's conditions contain everything relevant. This intuition is the heart of a ''Markov chain'': a system where the future depends //only// on the present, not on the path that led here.

!! The Core Insight

The defining property of a Markov chain is the ''Markov property'': given the current state, the future is independent of the past. Formally, knowing $X_t$ tells you everything about $X_{t+1}$ that the full history $X_0, X_1, \ldots, X_t$ could tell you.

The system moves between a set of ''states'' according to ''transition probabilities''. These probabilities are collected into a ''transition matrix'' $P$, where entry $P_{ij}$ gives the probability of moving from state $i$ to state $j$. Each row of $P$ sums to 1, making $P$ a ''stochastic matrix''.

Over time, many Markov chains settle into a ''stationary distribution'' $\pi$ — a probability distribution over states that doesn't change once reached. This convergence property is what makes Markov chains extraordinarily useful across statistics, machine learning, and algorithms. See also: <<link "Stochastic Processes" "Stochastic Processes">><</link>> and <<link "Bayesian" "Bayesian">><</link>> inference, where stationary distributions underpin much of modern sampling.

!! Stationary Distributions and Convergence

A ''stationary distribution'' $\pi$ satisfies $\pi = \pi P$: applying one more step of the chain leaves the distribution unchanged. If you start in $\pi$, you stay in $\pi$ forever.

''Ergodic'' chains — those that are irreducible (you can reach any state from any state) and aperiodic (the chain doesn't cycle with a fixed period) — are guaranteed to have a unique stationary distribution, and the chain's distribution converges to it regardless of where you started. This is the ''ergodic theorem'', and it is the theoretical backbone of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (<<link "MCMC" "MCMC">><</link>>).

A stronger condition is ''detailed balance'': $\pi_i P_{ij} = \pi_j P_{ji}$ for all $i, j$. This says the probability flux from $i$ to $j$ equals the flux from $j$ to $i$ — the chain is ''reversible''. Detailed balance implies stationarity, and it is the key equation exploited by algorithms like Metropolis-Hastings.

''PageRank'' — Google's original algorithm for ranking web pages — is a beautiful real-world application. Model the web as a Markov chain: states are pages, and a random surfer clicks links uniformly at random (with occasional random jumps to avoid getting trapped). The stationary distribution of this chain is PageRank: pages visited most often by the long-run random surfer are ranked highest. See <<link "Page Rank" "Page Rank">><</link>> for details.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Start with an initial distribution $\pi_0$ over states (a row vector). After $n$ steps, the distribution is:

$$\pi_n = \pi_0 P^n$$

So matrix exponentiation //is// the computation of running the chain forward $n$ steps. The stationary distribution satisfies:

$$\pi = \pi P$$

meaning $\pi$ is a ''left eigenvector'' of $P$ with eigenvalue 1. For ergodic chains, 1 is the largest eigenvalue of $P$, and it is simple (multiplicity 1). The ''spectral gap'' — the gap between eigenvalue 1 and the second-largest eigenvalue $\lambda_2$ — controls how fast the chain converges:

$$\text{Mixing time} \approx \frac{1}{1 - \lambda_2}$$

A large spectral gap means fast mixing; a gap near zero means painfully slow convergence.

Detailed balance written component-wise:

$$\pi_i P_{ij} = \pi_j P_{ji} \quad \text{for all } i, j$$

This symmetry condition is what allows Metropolis-Hastings to construct a chain whose stationary distribution is //any// target distribution $\pi$, simply by choosing acceptance probabilities that enforce detailed balance.

The ''Chapman-Kolmogorov equation'' links $n$-step and $m$-step transition matrices:

$$P^{n+m} = P^n \cdot P^m$$

which is just the statement that matrix multiplication composes transition probabilities correctly.

!! Markov Chains in ML

Markov chains appear throughout machine learning, often in roles that aren't immediately obvious:

''MCMC Sampling'': When a posterior distribution is too complex to compute analytically, <<link "MCMC" "MCMC">><</link>> constructs a Markov chain whose stationary distribution //is// the target posterior. Run the chain long enough and you collect samples from the posterior. The ''Metropolis-Hastings'' algorithm does this by proposing moves from a simple proposal distribution, then accepting or rejecting them with a probability ratio that enforces detailed balance with respect to the target.

''PageRank'': As described above, ranking web pages as the stationary distribution of a random walk on the web graph. See <<link "Page Rank" "Page Rank">><</link>>.

''Hidden Markov Models'': A <<link "Hidden Markov Machine" "Hidden Markov Machine">><</link>> adds a twist — the true state is hidden, and you only observe noisy emissions. The Viterbi algorithm finds the most likely hidden state sequence; the Baum-Welch algorithm learns the parameters. HMMs dominated speech recognition and computational biology for decades.

''Markov Decision Processes'': In reinforcement learning, a <<link "Markov Decision Process" "Markov Decision Process">><</link>> extends Markov chains with ''actions'' and ''rewards''. The agent influences which transitions occur. The Markov property is the foundational assumption that makes dynamic programming (value iteration, policy iteration) tractable. See <<link "Reinforcement Learning" "Reinforcement Learning">><</link>>.

''Language Models as (approximate) Markov chains'': An $n$-gram language model is literally a Markov chain of order $n-1$: the next word depends only on the previous $n-1$ words. Modern neural language models //implicitly// capture much longer dependencies, but the Markov framing is useful for understanding their limitations and why transformers with attention were such an advance.

!! When Markov Chains Break Down

''Non-Markovian reality — long-range dependencies'': The Markov property is an assumption, not a fact about the world. In natural language, the correct interpretation of a word often depends on context many sentences back. In financial time series, volatility today depends on shocks weeks ago. When reality has long-range dependencies, a first-order Markov model is misspecified, and predictions deteriorate. Higher-order Markov models help but suffer from exponential state-space growth.

''Slow mixing — the chain takes forever to converge'': An ergodic chain is //guaranteed// to converge, but the guarantee says nothing about //how fast//. If the stationary distribution has multiple well-separated modes (think a bimodal posterior), the chain may get stuck in one mode for an astronomically long time before tunneling to the other. The spectral gap collapses, mixing time explodes, and your MCMC samples look converged but are actually sampling only one mode. This is one of the central practical challenges in Bayesian computation.

''Periodic or reducible chains — no unique stationary distribution'': If a chain is ''periodic'' (e.g., it deterministically alternates A→B→A→B), it never settles into a stationary distribution — it oscillates forever. If a chain is ''reducible'' (some states can never be reached from others), there are multiple absorbing communicating classes, and the stationary distribution depends on initial conditions. Neither case has the clean convergence guarantees of the ergodic theorem, and MCMC algorithms that inadvertently construct such chains will give wrong answers.

!! Technical Details

''History'': Andrei Markov introduced Markov chains in 1906, studying the alternation of vowels and consonants in Pushkin's //Eugene Onegin// — one of the first applications of probabilistic modeling to text.

''Chapman-Kolmogorov equation'': $P^{(n+m)}_{ij} = \sum_k P^{(n)}_{ik} P^{(m)}_{kj}$. Composition of transition matrices corresponds to composition of multi-step transitions.

''Mixing time'': Formally, $t_{\text{mix}}(\epsilon) = \min\{t : \max_{x} \|P^t(x, \cdot) - \pi\|_{\text{TV}} \leq \epsilon\}$, where $\|\cdot\|_{\text{TV}}$ is total variation distance. The spectral gap $\gamma = 1 - \lambda_2$ (second eigenvalue of $P$) controls this: $t_{\text{mix}} = O(\gamma^{-1} \log(1/\epsilon))$ for reversible chains.

''Absorbing states'': A state $i$ is absorbing if $P_{ii} = 1$ — once entered, never left. Chains with absorbing states are not ergodic. The ''fundamental matrix'' $(I - Q)^{-1}$, where $Q$ is the transition matrix among transient states, gives expected times to absorption.

''Transient vs. recurrent states'': A state is ''recurrent'' if the chain returns to it with probability 1; ''transient'' if there is positive probability of never returning. For finite irreducible chains, all states are recurrent.

''Continuous-time Markov chains'': Replace the transition matrix with a ''generator matrix'' (or rate matrix) $Q$, where off-diagonal entries are transition rates and diagonal entries ensure rows sum to zero. The distribution evolves as $\dot{\pi} = \pi Q$, and the stationary distribution satisfies $\pi Q = 0$.

''Markov chain order'': A $k$th-order Markov chain has $X_{t+1}$ depending on the last $k$ states. This can always be rewritten as a first-order chain on the state space of $k$-tuples, at the cost of exponentially more states.
