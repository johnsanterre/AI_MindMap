! Anomaly Detection

<img src="assets/anomaly_detection.svg" alt="Diagram showing a normal data cluster with outlier anomaly points highlighted beyond the decision boundary" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are a veteran customs inspector at a busy port. Thousands of shipping containers pass through every day, and after years on the job you have developed an instinct for what a "normal" container looks like — its weight, its declared contents, its origin, the time of year. You do not memorize every legitimate shipment; instead, you build a mental model of normalcy. When a container violates that model — too heavy for its label, arriving on an unusual route — you flag it. ''Anomaly detection'' works the same way: rather than learning what every possible anomaly looks like, the system learns what //normal// looks like, and flags anything that deviates sufficiently from that learned pattern.

---

!! The Core Insight

Most machine learning is built around classification: give me labeled examples of each class, and I will learn the boundaries between them. Anomaly detection flips this assumption. In the real world, anomalies are rare by definition, wildly diverse in form, and often not available as training examples at all. A credit card fraud detector cannot be trained on all future fraud schemes that have not been invented yet. A network intrusion detector cannot anticipate every novel attack vector.

The core insight is therefore to ''model the distribution of normal data'' and define anomalies as points that fall in low-probability regions of that distribution. This is fundamentally different from supervised classification. You are not drawing a boundary between two classes — you are drawing a boundary around one class and declaring everything outside it suspicious. This is why anomaly detection is closely related to <<link "Out-of-Distribution Detection" "Out-of-Distribution Detection">><</link>>, which asks whether a new input comes from the same distribution the model was trained on.

The challenge is subtle: "normal" is not a single thing. Normal data can be multimodal, high-dimensional, and time-varying. The boundary of normalcy must be tight enough to catch real anomalies but loose enough to avoid drowning the operator in false alarms. This tension between sensitivity and specificity is the central engineering problem of every anomaly detection system.

---

!! Statistical, Distance, and Model-Based Methods

Anomaly detection methods fall into three broad families, each with a different philosophy about what makes something anomalous.

''Statistical methods'' assume the normal data follows a known or estimable probability distribution. The simplest approach fits a Gaussian to the data and flags points beyond a certain number of standard deviations. The <<link "Mahalanobis distance" "Mahalanobis Distance">><</link>> generalizes this to correlated multivariate data by accounting for the covariance structure — a point might look normal in each dimension individually but be anomalous in the joint distribution. Kernel density estimation relaxes the parametric assumption entirely, estimating the density nonparametrically and flagging low-density regions. The limitation of statistical approaches is their dependence on distributional assumptions: if the data is not well-described by the chosen family, the detector either misses anomalies or generates false positives.

''Distance-based methods'' define anomalies as points that are far from their neighbors. The $k$-nearest-neighbor anomaly score computes the average distance to a point's $k$ closest neighbors — isolated points score high. ''Local Outlier Factor'' (LOF) refines this by comparing each point's local density to the density of its neighbors, catching anomalies that are far from one local cluster even if close to another. <<link "Isolation Forest" "Isolation Forest">><</link>> takes an elegant dual approach: instead of measuring distance, it measures how //easy// a point is to isolate via random axis-aligned splits. Anomalies, being rare and different, are isolated in fewer splits than normal points. Distance-based methods make no distributional assumptions but struggle in high dimensions where the notion of distance becomes unreliable.

''Model-based methods'' train a model on normal data and use its reconstruction error or likelihood as an anomaly score. <<link "Autoencoders" "Autoencoders">><</link>> learn to compress and reconstruct normal data; anomalies, which the autoencoder has never seen, are reconstructed poorly and produce high error. <<link "One-Class SVM" "One-Class SVM">><</link>> finds the smallest hypersphere in kernel space that contains most of the normal data, placing anomalies outside the sphere. Deep approaches like variational autoencoders and normalizing flows provide explicit density estimates and can handle complex, high-dimensional data. The risk with model-based methods is that an overly powerful model may learn to reconstruct anomalies too, defeating the purpose.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The statistical foundation begins with density estimation. Given normal training data $\{x_1, \ldots, x_n\}$, we estimate the probability density $\hat{p}(x)$ and flag a new point $x^*$ as anomalous if:

$$\hat{p}(x^*) < \tau$$

where $\tau$ is a threshold chosen to control the false positive rate. Under a multivariate Gaussian assumption with mean $\mu$ and covariance $\Sigma$, the anomaly score is the squared Mahalanobis distance:

$$D^2(x) = (x - \mu)^T \Sigma^{-1} (x - \mu)$$

This follows a $\chi^2$ distribution with $d$ degrees of freedom under the null hypothesis that $x$ is normal, giving a principled way to set $\tau$.

For Isolation Forest, the anomaly score for a point $x$ is derived from its average path length $E[h(x)]$ across an ensemble of random trees:

$$s(x, n) = 2^{-E[h(x)] / c(n)}$$

where $c(n)$ is the average path length of an unsuccessful search in a binary search tree with $n$ nodes, serving as a normalization constant. Scores near 1 indicate anomalies (short paths); scores near 0.5 indicate normal points.

For autoencoder-based detection, the anomaly score is the reconstruction error:

$$a(x) = \|x - f_\theta(x)\|^2$$

where $f_\theta$ is the autoencoder trained to minimize this loss on normal data. The key assumption is that the learned low-dimensional manifold captures the structure of normal data, so anomalies — which lie off this manifold — incur high reconstruction cost.

One-Class SVM solves the optimization problem:

$$\min_{w, \rho, \xi} \frac{1}{2}\|w\|^2 - \rho + \frac{1}{\nu n}\sum_{i=1}^{n} \xi_i$$

subject to $w^T \phi(x_i) \geq \rho - \xi_i$ and $\xi_i \geq 0$, where $\nu$ controls the fraction of points allowed to fall outside the decision boundary.

---

!! Anomaly Detection in Practice

''Threshold selection'' is often the hardest practical problem. Theory gives asymptotic guidance (e.g., Mahalanobis distance thresholds from the $\chi^2$ distribution), but in practice the normal distribution is imperfectly known, contaminated with unlabeled anomalies, and non-stationary. Most production systems set thresholds by calibrating against a held-out validation set with known anomalies, or by targeting a fixed false positive rate and tuning the threshold to meet it. The precision-recall tradeoff is severe when anomalies are rare: even a 1% false positive rate can generate more false alarms than real detections when the base rate of anomalies is 0.01%.

''Feature engineering'' dominates performance in tabular and time-series applications. Raw sensor readings, log entries, or transaction records are rarely useful directly. Domain experts encode time-of-day patterns, rolling statistics, ratios, and rate-of-change features that make anomalies separable. In deep learning applications, the representation is learned end-to-end, but the architecture choice (convolutional for images, recurrent for sequences) bakes in equivalent inductive biases.

''Ensemble methods'' are standard practice. Isolation Forest is inherently an ensemble. Combining multiple detectors — for example, a statistical method, a distance-based method, and a model-based method — and aggregating their scores reduces the chance that any single method's blind spot produces a missed detection. Score normalization across methods is a nontrivial engineering concern.

''Streaming and concept drift'' present ongoing challenges. In many applications (network monitoring, manufacturing, financial markets), the notion of "normal" changes over time. A detector trained on last year's data may flag this year's normal behavior. Adaptive methods maintain a sliding window of recent normal data and periodically retrain or update the model. The tradeoff is between responsiveness to genuine distributional shift and robustness to transient fluctuations.

---

!! When Anomaly Detection Breaks Down

''High-dimensional sparse data:'' In very high dimensions, distance-based methods suffer from the curse of dimensionality — all points become roughly equidistant, and the contrast between normal and anomalous distances collapses. Statistical methods require estimating covariance matrices that are singular or poorly conditioned when $d > n$. Even model-based approaches struggle because the normal data occupies a vanishingly small fraction of the feature space, making the boundary of normalcy almost impossible to learn precisely. Dimensionality reduction is often a prerequisite, but aggressive reduction can destroy the very features that make anomalies detectable.

''Contaminated training data:'' Most anomaly detection methods assume the training set consists entirely of normal data. In practice, the training set often contains unlabeled anomalies. If these contaminating anomalies are numerous enough, the model learns to treat them as normal, silently expanding the boundary of acceptability. Robust methods exist (e.g., trimming a fraction of extreme points before fitting), but they require knowing the contamination rate, which is exactly what you do not know. This chicken-and-egg problem is fundamental: you need clean data to build a detector, but you need a detector to clean the data.

''Novel normal patterns vs. true anomalies:'' A detector trained on historical data will flag //any// novel pattern, whether it is genuinely anomalous or simply a previously unseen but legitimate mode of operation. A factory that adds a new product line, a network that onboards a new service, or a patient with an unusual but healthy physiology will all trigger alarms. The detector cannot distinguish "new and dangerous" from "new and fine" without additional context or labeled examples of the new normal. This is the deepest limitation of unsupervised anomaly detection: it detects //novelty//, not //threat//, and the two are not the same thing.

!! Technical Details

''Isolation Forest'' (Liu, Ting, and Zhou, 2008) builds an ensemble of random binary trees by recursively selecting a random feature and a random split value. Anomalies, being few and different, are isolated near the root of the tree (short path length), while normal points require many splits to isolate. The algorithm is linear in time complexity $O(tn \log \psi)$ where $t$ is the number of trees and $\psi$ is the subsample size, making it scalable to large datasets. The subsample size $\psi$ is typically set to 256 regardless of dataset size — a counterintuitive choice that works because anomalies are isolated quickly regardless of total data volume.

''Local Outlier Factor'' (Breunig et al., 2000) computes the ratio of a point's local reachability density to that of its $k$-nearest neighbors. A ratio significantly less than 1 means the point is in a sparser region than its neighbors — a local anomaly. LOF handles datasets with clusters of varying density, where a global threshold would fail.

''Deep anomaly detection'' includes deep autoencoders, variational autoencoders (VAEs), generative adversarial networks (GANs), and normalizing flows. VAEs provide an explicit lower bound on the log-likelihood $\log p(x) \geq \mathbb{E}_{q(z|x)}[\log p(x|z)] - D_{KL}(q(z|x) \| p(z))$, which can be used directly as an anomaly score. However, recent work (Nalisnick et al., 2019) showed that deep generative models can assign //higher// likelihood to out-of-distribution data than to training data, complicating their use for anomaly detection and motivating likelihood-ratio and typicality-based scores.

''One-Class SVM'' (Scholkopf et al., 2001) maps data into a kernel-induced feature space and finds the hyperplane that separates the data from the origin with maximum margin. The parameter $\nu \in (0, 1)$ is both an upper bound on the fraction of outliers and a lower bound on the fraction of support vectors, providing interpretable control over detector sensitivity.

''Evaluation metrics'' for anomaly detection require care. Standard accuracy is meaningless when anomalies are 0.1% of the data. Area Under the ROC Curve (AUROC) and Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve (AUPRC) are preferred. AUPRC is especially informative under extreme class imbalance because it does not credit a detector for correctly classifying the abundant normal class.
