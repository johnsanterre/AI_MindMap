! Large Deviations

<img src="assets/large_deviations.svg" alt="Large deviations rate function and exponential decay diagram" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine flipping a fair coin a thousand times. You expect roughly 500 heads. Getting 501 or 499 is unremarkable — the <<link "Central Limit Theorem" "Central Limit Theorem">><</link>> handles those ordinary fluctuations beautifully. But what about getting 700 heads? That event is not just unlikely — it is //exponentially// unlikely in the number of flips, and the Central Limit Theorem's Gaussian approximation completely fails to describe it. ''Large deviations theory'' is the mathematical framework that picks up exactly where the CLT leaves off, quantifying how fast the probability of rare events decays as sample sizes grow. It turns out the answer is governed by a single convex function — the ''rate function'' — which connects probability theory to thermodynamics, information theory, and the deepest questions about how systems behave in extremes.

!! The Core Insight

For a sequence of IID random variables $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ with mean $\mu$, let $S_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ be the sample mean. The law of large numbers says $S_n \to \mu$ as $n \to \infty$. But //how fast// do deviations from $\mu$ become improbable?

''Cramer's theorem'' (1938) gives the precise answer:

$$P\!\left(\frac{S_n}{1} \in A\right) \approx e^{-n \inf_{x \in A} I(x)}$$

More precisely, for any set $A$ not containing $\mu$:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} \log P(S_n \in A) = -\inf_{x \in A} I(x)$$

The function $I(x)$ is the ''rate function'' (or ''Cramer rate function''). It is non-negative, convex, and equals zero at $x = \mu$ — the most likely value. For any $x \neq \mu$, $I(x) > 0$, meaning the probability of the sample mean landing near $x$ decays exponentially at rate $I(x)$ per sample. The larger the deviation, the faster the decay.

!! Rate Functions and the Legendre Transform

The rate function is not arbitrary — it is computed from the ''moment generating function'' (MGF) via a ''Legendre-Fenchel transform''. Define the ''cumulant generating function'' (also called the log-MGF):

$$\Lambda(\theta) = \log \mathbb{E}[e^{\theta X}]$$

Then the rate function is its convex conjugate:

$$I(x) = \sup_{\theta \in \mathbb{R}} \left[\theta x - \Lambda(\theta)\right]$$

This transform has deep geometric meaning. For each value $x$, you are finding the "tilt" $\theta$ that makes $x$ the //typical// value under an exponentially re-weighted distribution. The rate function $I(x)$ measures how much you had to tilt — equivalently, the <<link "KL Divergence" "KL Divergence">><</link>> between the tilted distribution and the original.

''Example: fair coin flips.'' Each $X_i \in \{0, 1\}$ with $p = 1/2$. The cumulant generating function is $\Lambda(\theta) = \log\!\left(\frac{1 + e^\theta}{2}\right)$. The rate function works out to:

$$I(x) = x \log\!\frac{x}{1/2} + (1-x) \log\!\frac{1-x}{1/2}$$

This is exactly the KL divergence $D_{KL}(\text{Bernoulli}(x) \| \text{Bernoulli}(1/2))$. The probability of seeing fraction $x$ heads in $n$ flips decays as $e^{-n D_{KL}(x \| 1/2)}$.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The ''large deviation principle'' (LDP) formalizes the exponential decay. A sequence of probability measures $\{\mu_n\}$ satisfies an LDP with rate function $I$ if for every measurable set $A$:

$$-\inf_{x \in A^\circ} I(x) \leq \liminf_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} \log \mu_n(A) \leq \limsup_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} \log \mu_n(A) \leq -\inf_{x \in \bar{A}} I(x)$$

where $A^\circ$ is the interior and $\bar{A}$ the closure. The upper and lower bounds use the closure and interior respectively — this sandwiching is what makes the LDP work for general sets.

''Varadhan's lemma'' provides the bridge to statistical mechanics. For any continuous function $\phi(x)$:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} \log \mathbb{E}\!\left[e^{n\phi(S_n)}\right] = \sup_x \left[\phi(x) - I(x)\right]$$

This is the ''Laplace principle'' — exponential integrals are dominated by their most likely point, with the rate function acting as a penalty. In statistical physics, this is exactly the variational principle that determines free energy: the system minimizes energy minus entropy, and $I(x)$ plays the role of the negative entropy.

The ''Gartner-Ellis theorem'' extends Cramer's theorem to non-IID sequences. If the limit $\Lambda(\theta) = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} \log \mathbb{E}[e^{\theta S_n}]$ exists and is differentiable, then the LDP holds with rate function $I(x) = \sup_\theta [\theta x - \Lambda(\theta)]$.

''Sanov's theorem'' extends large deviations from sample means to empirical distributions. The probability that the empirical distribution $\hat{P}_n$ of $n$ IID samples is close to some distribution $Q \neq P$ decays as:

$$P(\hat{P}_n \approx Q) \approx e^{-n D_{KL}(Q \| P)}$$

The rate function for empirical distributions is exactly KL divergence — the foundational link between large deviations and <<link "Information Theory" "Information Theory">><</link>>.

!! Connections to ML and Physics

''Statistical mechanics.'' Large deviations theory is the mathematical backbone of equilibrium thermodynamics. The Boltzmann distribution $p(x) \propto e^{-\beta E(x)}$ arises as the most likely macroscopic state consistent with a given energy constraint — this is precisely a large deviations conditioning argument. The free energy $F = -\frac{1}{\beta}\log Z$ is a cumulant generating function.

''Hypothesis testing.'' The Neyman-Pearson lemma and Stein's lemma describe the optimal error rates in binary hypothesis testing. The error exponent — how fast the probability of error decays with sample size — is given by a KL divergence, which is a rate function. This connects directly to the <<link "Information Theory" "Information Theory">><</link>> notion of channel capacity.

''Concentration inequalities.'' Hoeffding's inequality, Chernoff bounds, and sub-Gaussian tail bounds are all //corollaries// of large deviations theory. The Chernoff bound $P(S_n \geq na) \leq e^{-nI(a)}$ follows directly from optimizing the exponential Markov inequality. See <<link "Concentration Inequalities" "Concentration Inequalities">><</link>>.

''Rare event simulation.'' In Monte Carlo estimation, rare events are hard to sample. ''Importance sampling'' tilts the distribution to make the rare event typical, then corrects with likelihood ratios. The optimal tilt is exactly the exponentially twisted distribution from the Legendre transform — the one where $x$ becomes the mean. Large deviations theory tells you which tilt minimizes variance.

''Stochastic processes.'' For sample paths of <<link "Stochastic Processes" "Stochastic Processes">><</link>>, Schilder's theorem gives a large deviations principle for scaled Brownian motion: the rate function is $I(\phi) = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^T |\dot{\phi}(t)|^2 dt$ — the kinetic energy of the path. The most likely rare fluctuation is the path that minimizes this action, connecting probability to classical mechanics.

!! When Large Deviations Theory Breaks Down

''Heavy-tailed distributions violate Cramer's conditions.'' Cramer's theorem requires that the moment generating function $\mathbb{E}[e^{\theta X}]$ is finite in a neighborhood of zero. For heavy-tailed distributions (Pareto, Cauchy, stable distributions with $\alpha < 2$), the MGF is infinite for all $\theta > 0$. Large deviations still occur, but the decay is polynomial, not exponential — governed by the tail index rather than a rate function. Applying the Cramer machinery to heavy-tailed data gives nonsensical bounds. The correct framework requires entirely different tools (regular variation, subexponential theory).

''Sub-exponential regime between CLT and large deviations.'' The CLT describes fluctuations of order $O(1/\sqrt{n})$ around the mean. Large deviations theory describes fixed deviations $S_n/n \approx a \neq \mu$. But there is a gap: deviations of order $a_n$ where $1/\sqrt{n} \ll a_n \ll 1$ fall into the ''moderate deviations'' regime. Here neither the Gaussian approximation nor the pure exponential decay is accurate. The transition between regimes matters for practical statistics — many real datasets live in this intermediate zone where naive application of either CLT or large deviations gives wrong confidence levels.

''Non-convex rate functions from dependent data.'' For IID data, the rate function is always convex (it is a Legendre transform). But for strongly dependent sequences — long-range correlated time series, interacting particle systems, certain Markov chains — the rate function can be non-convex or even fail to exist. When the Gartner-Ellis theorem's differentiability condition fails, the Legendre transform only recovers the ''convex envelope'' of the true rate function, missing important structure. Phase transitions in statistical mechanics correspond precisely to points where the rate function loses strict convexity, creating flat regions where multiple macroscopic states coexist.

!! Technical Details

''Foundational papers:'' Cramer, H. (1938). "Sur un nouveau theoreme-limite de la theorie des probabilites." //Actualites Scientifiques et Industrielles//, 736. Varadhan, S.R.S. (1966). "Asymptotic probabilities and differential equations." //Communications on Pure and Applied Mathematics//, 19(3), 261-286. Varadhan received the 2007 Abel Prize largely for this work.

''Key books:'' Dembo & Zeitouni, //Large Deviations Techniques and Applications// (2nd ed., 1998) is the standard reference. Den Hollander, //Large Deviations// (Fields Institute Monographs, 2000) is more accessible.

''Cramer's theorem in $\mathbb{R}^d$:'' For vector-valued random variables, the rate function is $I(x) = \sup_{\theta \in \mathbb{R}^d} [\langle \theta, x \rangle - \Lambda(\theta)]$, still a Legendre-Fenchel transform but now over a $d$-dimensional parameter.

''Contraction principle:'' If $\{X_n\}$ satisfies an LDP with rate $I$ and $f$ is continuous, then $\{f(X_n)\}$ satisfies an LDP with rate $J(y) = \inf_{x: f(x)=y} I(x)$. This is the large deviations analog of the change-of-variables formula.
