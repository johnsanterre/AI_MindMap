! Conditional Expectation
<img src="assets/conditional_expectation.svg" alt="Conditional Expectation" width="800">

Imagine you are trying to guess someone's income. Knowing nothing, your best guess is the overall average income across everyone. But now suppose you learn the person has a PhD. Suddenly your guess should shift — you should predict the average income //among PhD holders//, not the average across everyone. That updated, more informed average is a ''conditional expectation''. It is the single best prediction you can make about one quantity once you have partial information about something related. Every regression model you have ever seen, from a simple line of best fit to a deep neural network, is ultimately trying to approximate a conditional expectation.

!! The Core Insight

The conditional expectation $E[Y \mid X]$ is not a single number — it is a //function// of $X$. For every possible value $x$ that $X$ might take, $E[Y \mid X = x]$ tells you the average value of $Y$ among all cases where $X = x$. Plot these averages and you get the ''regression function'', the curve that runs through the centers of the data cloud. This function extracts the systematic, predictable part of $Y$ from $X$ and discards the noise. In fact, $E[Y \mid X]$ is the function of $X$ that minimizes the mean squared error $E[(Y - g(X))^2]$ over all possible functions $g$ — it is the ''optimal predictor'' in the squared-error sense.

!! The Best Prediction Given Partial Information

The power of conditional expectation comes from two remarkable structural properties:

''The Tower Property (Law of Iterated Expectations):'' If you first condition on detailed information and then average over that information, you recover the coarser expectation. Formally, $E[E[Y \mid X]] = E[Y]$. More generally, if $\mathcal{G} \subseteq \mathcal{F}$ are nested information sets, then $E[E[Y \mid \mathcal{F}] \mid \mathcal{G}] = E[Y \mid \mathcal{G}]$. In plain language: averaging a fine-grained prediction over the detailed information you conditioned on always recovers the coarser prediction. This is why you can decompose complex estimation problems into stages — predict given detailed info, then average over the details — and still get the right answer.

''The Regression Function:'' For a pair of [[Random Variables|Random Variables]] $(X, Y)$, the function $m(x) = E[Y \mid X = x]$ is the ''regression function''. It is the best approximation of $Y$ by any function of $X$ alone. Every [[Regression|Regression]] technique, whether linear, polynomial, kernel-based, or neural, is trying to estimate this function. Linear regression restricts the search to $m(x) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x$; nonparametric methods let $m$ be arbitrary. The conditional expectation is the target all of them are chasing.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The conditional expectation of $Y$ given $X = x$ for continuous random variables is defined via the conditional density:

$$E[Y \mid X = x] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} y \, f_{Y \mid X}(y \mid x) \, dy$$

where $f_{Y \mid X}(y \mid x) = f_{X,Y}(x,y) / f_X(x)$ comes from [[Conditional Probability|Conditional Probability]].

''Tower Property:'' The law of iterated expectations states

$$E[E[Y \mid X]] = E[Y]$$

This follows because $E[E[Y \mid X]] = \int E[Y \mid X = x] f_X(x) \, dx = \int \int y \, f_{Y \mid X}(y \mid x) \, dy \, f_X(x) \, dx = \int \int y \, f_{X,Y}(x,y) \, dy \, dx = E[Y]$.

''Law of Total Variance:'' Variance decomposes into two interpretable pieces:

$$\text{Var}(Y) = E[\text{Var}(Y \mid X)] + \text{Var}(E[Y \mid X])$$

The first term is the //average unexplained variance// within each slice $X = x$ (irreducible noise). The second is the //variance of the conditional means// across slices (the signal your model can capture). A good predictor makes the second term large relative to the first — this is exactly what $R^2$ measures.

''Conditional Variance:'' The spread of $Y$ around its conditional mean is:

$$\text{Var}(Y \mid X = x) = E[Y^2 \mid X = x] - (E[Y \mid X = x])^2$$

When this quantity varies with $x$, you have //heteroscedasticity// — the noise level depends on the input.

''MMSE Optimality:'' Among all functions $g(X)$, the conditional expectation minimizes mean squared error:

$$E[Y \mid X] = \arg\min_{g} E[(Y - g(X))^2]$$

The residual $Y - E[Y \mid X]$ is uncorrelated with any function of $X$.

!! Conditional Expectation in ML

''Regression:'' Every [[Regression|Regression]] model targets $E[Y \mid X = x]$. Linear regression constrains this to be a linear function. Neural networks and tree ensembles provide flexible, nonparametric approximations. The universal approximation theorem says neural nets can approximate any conditional expectation to arbitrary accuracy.

''Value Functions in RL:'' In [[Reinforcement Learning|Reinforcement Learning]], the state-value function $V(s) = E[G_t \mid S_t = s]$ is literally a conditional expectation — the expected cumulative reward given the current state. The Bellman equation is a consequence of the tower property applied to this conditional expectation, decomposing the future into one step plus the remaining conditional expectation.

''Bayesian Updating:'' In [[Bayesian Inference|Bayesian Inference]], the posterior mean $E[\theta \mid \text{data}]$ is a conditional expectation. It is the optimal point estimate under squared error loss. As more data arrives, the tower property guarantees consistency: the expectation of tomorrow's posterior mean, given today's information, equals today's posterior mean.

''E-step in EM:'' The [[Expectation Maximization|Expectation Maximization]] algorithm computes $E[\log p(X, Z \mid \theta) \mid X, \theta^{(t)}]$ — a conditional expectation of the complete-data log-likelihood given the observed data. This is literally what the "E" in EM stands for.

!! When Conditional Expectations Mislead

''Simpson's Paradox and Confounding:'' The conditional expectation $E[Y \mid X]$ answers "what is the average $Y$ among units with $X = x$" — a //descriptive// question. It does not answer the //causal// question "what happens to $Y$ if we set $X = x$." When confounders are present, $E[Y \mid X = x]$ conflates the causal effect of $X$ with selection effects. Naive regression on observational data can reverse the sign of an effect entirely.

''Non-Gaussian Heavy Tails:'' Conditional expectation is the optimal predictor only under squared error loss. When $Y$ has heavy tails or extreme outliers, the conditional mean may be dominated by rare events and the conditional //median// (minimizing absolute error) can be far more robust. In financial risk, the conditional expectation of losses in the tail (CVaR) behaves very differently from the overall conditional mean.

''Nonlinear Dependence Beyond the Mean:'' Two variables can have $E[Y \mid X] = \text{const}$ (no relationship in the mean) yet be strongly dependent — for instance, $X$ might determine the //variance// of $Y$ but not its center. Conditional expectation only captures mean dependence. Detecting such relationships requires looking at the full conditional distribution, not just its expectation.

!! Technical Details

''Measure-Theoretic Definition:'' For a [[Random Variables|random variable]] $Y$ on $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ and a sub-$\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{G} \subseteq \mathcal{F}$, the conditional expectation $E[Y \mid \mathcal{G}]$ is the (a.s. unique) $\mathcal{G}$-measurable random variable satisfying $\int_G E[Y \mid \mathcal{G}] \, dP = \int_G Y \, dP$ for all $G \in \mathcal{G}$. Existence is guaranteed by the Radon-Nikodym theorem. This abstract definition is necessary because conditioning on events of probability zero (like $X = x$ for continuous $X$) is not well-defined via elementary ratios.

''$\sigma$-Algebra Conditioning:'' Conditioning on $X$ means conditioning on $\sigma(X)$, the $\sigma$-algebra generated by $X$. Conditioning on $\sigma(X)$ captures exactly the information "knowing the value of $X$." Nesting of $\sigma$-algebras ($\mathcal{G} \subseteq \mathcal{H} \subseteq \mathcal{F}$) gives the tower property: $E[E[Y \mid \mathcal{H}] \mid \mathcal{G}] = E[Y \mid \mathcal{G}]$.

''[[Martingale Theory|Martingale Theory]] Connection:'' If $\{\mathcal{F}_n\}$ is a filtration and $M_n = E[Y \mid \mathcal{F}_n]$, then $\{M_n\}$ is a martingale — by the tower property, $E[M_{n+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_n] = E[E[Y \mid \mathcal{F}_{n+1}] \mid \mathcal{F}_n] = E[Y \mid \mathcal{F}_n] = M_n$. This connects conditional expectation to the theory of fair games and underpins martingale convergence theorems used throughout probability and statistics.

''MMSE Optimality (Formal):'' The conditional expectation is the orthogonal projection of $Y$ onto the space of $\sigma(X)$-measurable $L^2$ random variables. The residual $\varepsilon = Y - E[Y \mid X]$ satisfies $E[\varepsilon \cdot h(X)] = 0$ for every square-integrable function $h$. This is the infinite-dimensional analog of orthogonal projection in Euclidean space, placing conditional expectation at the heart of Hilbert space methods in statistics.

''Conditional Characteristic Function:'' The conditional distribution of $Y \mid X$ is fully characterized by $E[e^{itY} \mid X]$ for all $t$, connecting conditional expectations to Fourier analysis and enabling proofs of conditional independence and distributional results.
