! Exponential Family

[img[assets/exponential_family.svg]]

Imagine you run a factory that produces dozens of different products — bolts, gears, springs, washers — each with its own blueprint. One day an engineer notices that every single blueprint follows the same template: a base shape, multiplied by a scaling factor, raised to a power that depends on the product's key measurement. Instead of maintaining dozens of independent blueprints, you now maintain one master template and a short list of settings for each product. The ''exponential family'' is that master template for probability distributions. The Gaussian, Bernoulli, Poisson, exponential, gamma, beta, and many more all turn out to be instances of a single algebraic pattern. Once you understand the template, you understand them all — their maximum likelihood estimates, their conjugate priors, their moment relationships — for free.

---

!! The Core Insight

Most of the distributions that appear in statistics and machine learning are not as different as they first look. Strip away the surface notation and they all share the same skeleton: a probability density (or mass) that can be written as

$$p(x \mid \boldsymbol{\eta}) = h(x) \exp\!\bigl(\boldsymbol{\eta}^\top T(x) - A(\boldsymbol{\eta})\bigr)$$

where $\boldsymbol{\eta}$ is a vector of ''natural parameters'', $T(x)$ is a vector of ''sufficient statistics'', $h(x)$ is a base measure that depends only on the data, and $A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$ is the ''log-partition function'' that ensures the distribution integrates to one. This single equation is the exponential family form. Any distribution you can cast into this shape automatically inherits a suite of powerful properties: closed-form <<link "Maximum Likelihood" "Maximum Likelihood">><</link>> estimates, convex log-likelihoods, conjugate priors for <<link "Bayesian Inference" "Bayesian Inference">><</link>>, and elegant moment identities.

The reason exponential families dominate statistical practice is not convention — it is mathematics. They are the //only// family of distributions for which a fixed-dimensional <<link "Sufficient Statistics" "Sufficient Statistics">><</link>> exists regardless of sample size. That means no matter how much data you collect, you can compress it to a summary of the same size without losing any information about the parameters. This is a theorem due to Pitman, Koopman, and Darmois, and it essentially says: if you want finite-dimensional sufficiency, you must be in the exponential family.

---

!! Sufficient Statistics and Natural Parameters

The ''sufficient statistic'' $T(x)$ is the data summary that captures everything the data has to say about the parameters. For a Gaussian with unknown mean and variance, the sufficient statistics are $\sum x_i$ and $\sum x_i^2$. For a Bernoulli, it is just $\sum x_i$, the count of successes. For a Poisson, again $\sum x_i$. The specific form of $T(x)$ is what distinguishes one member of the exponential family from another.

The ''natural parameters'' $\boldsymbol{\eta}$ are the parameterization that makes the exponential family form work. They are not always the parameters you are used to. For a Gaussian with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, the natural parameters are $\eta_1 = \mu / \sigma^2$ and $\eta_2 = -1/(2\sigma^2)$, not $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$ themselves. For a Bernoulli with success probability $p$, the natural parameter is the log-odds $\eta = \log(p/(1-p))$. The mapping between natural parameters and the familiar ''mean parameters'' (like $\mu$ or $p$) is given by the gradient of the log-partition function: $\mathbb{E}[T(x)] = \nabla A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$. This identity is the single most useful fact about exponential families.

The ''log-partition function'' $A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$ is always convex. This convexity is what makes maximum likelihood estimation in exponential families a convex optimization problem — there is a unique global optimum, never a landscape of saddle points or local minima. The second derivative of $A$ gives the variance of the sufficient statistics: $\text{Var}[T(x)] = \nabla^2 A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$. Since variance is always non-negative, this confirms the convexity of $A$.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Given $n$ i.i.d. observations $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ from an exponential family, the log-likelihood is:

$$\ell(\boldsymbol{\eta}) = \sum_{i=1}^n \bigl[\boldsymbol{\eta}^\top T(x_i) - A(\boldsymbol{\eta}) + \log h(x_i)\bigr] = \boldsymbol{\eta}^\top \!\left(\sum_{i=1}^n T(x_i)\right) - n A(\boldsymbol{\eta}) + \text{const}$$

Setting the gradient to zero yields the ''moment matching'' condition for the MLE:

$$\nabla A(\hat{\boldsymbol{\eta}}) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n T(x_i)$$

This says: the MLE is the parameter value whose expected sufficient statistic equals the observed sample average of the sufficient statistic. For a Gaussian, this gives $\hat{\mu} = \bar{x}$ and $\hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2$. For a Bernoulli, $\hat{p} = \bar{x}$. The pattern is universal.

Conjugate priors also follow naturally. A prior of the form $p(\boldsymbol{\eta}) \propto \exp\!\bigl(\boldsymbol{\eta}^\top \boldsymbol{\chi} - \nu A(\boldsymbol{\eta})\bigr)$ is conjugate to the exponential family likelihood: the posterior has the same form with updated hyperparameters $\boldsymbol{\chi}' = \boldsymbol{\chi} + \sum T(x_i)$ and $\nu' = \nu + n$. This is why conjugate Bayesian analysis works so cleanly for exponential family models — the update rule is just addition.

The <<link "KL Divergence" "KL Divergence">><</link>> between two members of the same exponential family has a particularly elegant form called the ''Bregman divergence'' of $A$:

$$D_{\text{KL}}(p_{\boldsymbol{\eta}_1} \| p_{\boldsymbol{\eta}_2}) = A(\boldsymbol{\eta}_2) - A(\boldsymbol{\eta}_1) - (\boldsymbol{\eta}_2 - \boldsymbol{\eta}_1)^\top \nabla A(\boldsymbol{\eta}_1)$$

This connection between information geometry and convex analysis is deep: the log-partition function $A$ plays the role of a convex potential, and the natural and mean parameterizations are dual coordinate systems linked by the Legendre transform.

---

!! Exponential Families in ML

<<link "Generalized Linear Models" "Generalized Linear Models">><</link>> (GLMs) are the most direct application. Every GLM pairs a linear predictor $\boldsymbol{w}^\top \boldsymbol{x}$ with an exponential family response distribution. Logistic regression uses the Bernoulli (natural parameter = log-odds = linear predictor). Poisson regression uses the Poisson (natural parameter = log rate = linear predictor). Linear regression uses the Gaussian. The link function in a GLM is precisely the map from the mean parameter to the natural parameter. The entire GLM framework exists //because// the exponential family provides a unified recipe for linking linear predictors to distributions.

In ''variational inference'', the exponential family is the default choice for the variational distribution. The reason is computational: the ELBO involves expectations of log-probabilities, and exponential family distributions have log-probabilities that are linear in $T(x)$. This linearity makes the required expectations tractable, often in closed form. The coordinate ascent updates in mean-field variational inference reduce to moment matching — the same condition as the MLE.

''Sufficient statistics and neural networks'': modern deep learning models are generally //not// exponential families — a neural network likelihood with millions of parameters does not admit a fixed-dimensional sufficient statistic. But exponential family structure still appears at key points: the final layer of a classification network outputs logits, which are natural parameters of a categorical distribution (an exponential family). Softmax is the map from natural to mean parameters. Understanding this connection clarifies why cross-entropy loss is the natural choice for classification — it is the negative log-likelihood of the exponential family at the output.

---

!! When Exponential Family Assumptions Break Down

''Heavy-tailed or multimodal data:'' The exponential family includes distributions with thin tails (Gaussian, Poisson) and specific heavy-tail profiles (certain gamma parameterizations), but it cannot represent arbitrary tail behavior or multimodality. A mixture of Gaussians, for instance, is //not// an exponential family distribution — there is no fixed-dimensional sufficient statistic that works for all sample sizes. If your data has multiple modes or extreme outliers that do not match any single exponential family member, forcing it into the exponential family framework will produce a poor fit and misleading inferences.

''Non-regular or boundary problems:'' Exponential family theory assumes that the natural parameter space is an open set and that certain regularity conditions hold (the support of the distribution does not depend on the parameters). Distributions like the uniform on $[0, \theta]$ violate this — the support depends on $\theta$ — and they are not exponential families. When the MLE lands on the boundary of the parameter space (e.g., estimated variance of zero), the smooth convex optimization story breaks down. Truncated or censored distributions often fall outside the exponential family framework as well.

''Model misspecification in high dimensions:'' In high-dimensional settings, assuming an exponential family model when the true data-generating process is not in that family can cause the MLE to concentrate on a wrong answer very efficiently. The convexity of the log-likelihood, normally an advantage, becomes a liability: the optimization confidently converges to the unique global optimum of the wrong objective. High-dimensional exponential family models (e.g., Ising models for binary data) also face computational challenges — the partition function $A(\boldsymbol{\eta})$ can be intractable to compute, requiring approximate methods like MCMC or pseudolikelihood.

---

!! Technical Details

The ''Pitman-Koopman-Darmois theorem'' states that among distributions with support not depending on the parameter, only exponential families admit sufficient statistics of fixed dimension as sample size grows. This foundational result explains the privileged status of the exponential family in statistical theory.

''Minimal vs. overcomplete representations:'' An exponential family is ''minimal'' if $T(x)$ has no affine redundancies — no component of $T$ can be written as an affine function of the others. The categorical distribution with $k$ categories is naturally overcomplete: the probabilities sum to 1, so one is redundant. The minimal representation has $k-1$ natural parameters. Overcomplete representations are often more convenient computationally (e.g., softmax uses all $k$ logits) but the natural parameter space is then a lower-dimensional manifold within $\mathbb{R}^k$.

''Curved exponential families'' are subfamilies where the natural parameter $\boldsymbol{\eta}$ is constrained to lie on a lower-dimensional manifold within the full natural parameter space. For example, a Gaussian with known mean-variance relationship $\sigma^2 = \mu^2$ is a curved exponential family. These retain some but not all of the nice properties — they still have sufficient statistics, but the MLE may not reduce to moment matching, and conjugate priors may not exist.

''Maximum entropy:'' Among all distributions with specified expected values of certain functions, the maximum entropy distribution is always an exponential family with those functions as its sufficient statistics. This connects exponential families to information theory: they are the least committal distributions consistent with observed moments. The Boltzmann distribution in statistical physics, $p(x) \propto \exp(-E(x)/T)$, is an exponential family with energy as the sufficient statistic and inverse temperature as the natural parameter.

''Key references:'' Barndorff-Nielsen (1978), //Information and Exponential Families in Statistical Theory//; Brown (1986), //Fundamentals of Statistical Exponential Families//; Wainwright and Jordan (2008), //Graphical Models, Exponential Families, and Variational Inference//, Foundations and Trends in Machine Learning — the definitive modern treatment connecting exponential families to graphical models and variational methods.
