! Latent Dirichlet Allocation

<img src="assets/latent_dirichlet_allocation.svg" alt="Latent Dirichlet Allocation" width="800">

Imagine you walk into a library and find thousands of unlabeled books. You start flipping through them and notice patterns: some books keep mentioning "neurons" and "layers," others talk about "priors" and "posteriors," and still others focus on "margins" and "kernels." Without anyone telling you the categories, you have begun to reverse-engineer the hidden //topics// that generated these books. ''Latent Dirichlet Allocation'' (LDA) automates exactly this intuition. It is a [[Bayesian Methods|Bayesian Methods]] model for discovering the hidden thematic structure in a collection of documents, and it remains one of the most influential tools in [[Natural Language Processing|Natural Language Processing]] and [[Unsupervised Learning|Unsupervised Learning]].

!! The Core Insight

Every document talks about a //mixture// of subjects. A machine learning textbook chapter might be 60% about neural networks, 25% about Bayesian statistics, and 15% about optimization. LDA formalizes this by saying each document has a hidden distribution over topics, and each topic is itself a distribution over words. The observed words are the //only// thing we see; everything else — the topics, the per-document mixtures, the per-word topic assignments — is ''latent''. The "Dirichlet" in the name refers to the [[Dirichlet Distribution|Dirichlet Distribution]], a [[Probability Distributions|probability distribution]] over distributions that serves as the prior on these mixtures. By assuming a generative story for how documents were created, LDA lets us work backward from the words to uncover the topics that explain them, using [[Bayesian Inference|Bayesian Inference]].

!! Topics as Building Blocks

LDA treats every document as a bag of words — word order does not matter, only which words appear and how often. The model posits $K$ topics, where each topic $k$ is a [[Multinomial Distribution|multinomial distribution]] $\phi_k$ over the entire vocabulary. Topic 1 might assign high probability to "neuron," "layer," and "activation," while Topic 2 gives high probability to "prior," "posterior," and "likelihood."

Each document $d$ draws its own topic mixture $\theta_d$ from a Dirichlet prior. This mixture tells us the proportion of each topic in that document. Then for every word position in the document, LDA first picks a topic $z$ according to $\theta_d$, and then picks the actual word $w$ from that topic's word distribution $\phi_z$. This two-step draw — topic first, then word — is the heart of LDA's generative process.

Because the Dirichlet prior is ''sparse'' (with concentration parameter $\alpha < 1$), documents tend to focus on just a few topics rather than spreading evenly across all $K$. Similarly, a symmetric Dirichlet prior on the word distributions (with parameter $\beta$) encourages each topic to use a focused subset of the vocabulary. This sparsity is what makes the discovered topics interpretable.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The generative process for a corpus of $D$ documents, each with $N_d$ words, using $K$ topics and vocabulary of size $V$:

$$\phi_k \sim \text{Dir}(\beta), \quad k = 1, \ldots, K$$

$$\theta_d \sim \text{Dir}(\alpha), \quad d = 1, \ldots, D$$

$$z_{d,n} \sim \text{Multinomial}(\theta_d), \quad n = 1, \ldots, N_d$$

$$w_{d,n} \sim \text{Multinomial}(\phi_{z_{d,n}})$$

The joint probability of the entire model is:

$$p(\mathbf{w}, \mathbf{z}, \boldsymbol{\theta}, \boldsymbol{\phi} \mid \alpha, \beta) = \prod_{k=1}^{K} p(\phi_k \mid \beta) \prod_{d=1}^{D} p(\theta_d \mid \alpha) \prod_{n=1}^{N_d} p(z_{d,n} \mid \theta_d)\, p(w_{d,n} \mid \phi_{z_{d,n}})$$

The central computational challenge is that the posterior $p(\mathbf{z}, \boldsymbol{\theta}, \boldsymbol{\phi} \mid \mathbf{w}, \alpha, \beta)$ is intractable — the denominator requires summing over all possible topic assignments for every word in every document. This is why approximate inference is essential.

The Dirichlet prior is ''conjugate'' to the multinomial likelihood, which means the posterior over $\theta$ and $\phi$ remains in the Dirichlet family when conditioned on topic assignments $\mathbf{z}$. This conjugacy is what makes efficient inference algorithms possible.

!! Training LDA in Practice

Two dominant approaches exist for fitting LDA:

''Collapsed [[Gibbs Sampling|Gibbs Sampling]]'': Integrate out $\theta$ and $\phi$ analytically (thanks to conjugacy) and sample only the topic assignments $z_{d,n}$. For each word, the conditional probability of assigning it to topic $k$ is proportional to:

$$p(z_{d,n} = k \mid \mathbf{z}_{-d,n}, \mathbf{w}, \alpha, \beta) \propto (n_{d,k}^{-d,n} + \alpha) \cdot \frac{n_{k,w}^{-d,n} + \beta}{n_{k,\cdot}^{-d,n} + V\beta}$$

where $n_{d,k}$ counts how many words in document $d$ are assigned to topic $k$, and $n_{k,w}$ counts how many times word $w$ is assigned to topic $k$ across the corpus. The superscript $-d,n$ means "excluding the current word."

''[[Variational Inference|Variational Inference]]'': Approximate the true posterior with a simpler factorized distribution and optimize a lower bound on the log-evidence (the ELBO). This was the original inference method proposed by Blei, Ng, and Jordan (2003). It is faster and more scalable than Gibbs sampling but introduces a factorization assumption. Variational methods connect LDA to the broader [[Expectation Maximization|Expectation Maximization]] framework.

''Choosing K'': The number of topics is a hyperparameter. Common strategies include held-out perplexity (lower is better), topic coherence scores (which measure whether top words in a topic co-occur in real text), and domain expertise. If $K$ is too small, topics are too broad; too large, and they become redundant or uninterpretable.

''Hyperparameters'': The Dirichlet concentration parameters $\alpha$ and $\beta$ control sparsity. A common default is $\alpha = 50/K$ and $\beta = 0.01$. Asymmetric priors (different $\alpha_k$ per topic) often improve results. These can be optimized during training using fixed-point updates.

!! When LDA Breaks Down

* ''Short documents'': LDA relies on word co-occurrence patterns within documents to discover topics. With very short texts (tweets, titles, individual sentences), each document contains too few words to estimate a meaningful topic mixture. The signal-to-noise ratio collapses, and topics become incoherent. Specialized variants like Biterm Topic Model address this.

* ''Correlated or hierarchical topics'': The Dirichlet prior assumes topics are essentially independent — knowing a document is about "machine learning" does not make "optimization" more likely. Real topics are correlated. The Correlated Topic Model (CTM) uses a logistic-normal prior instead, and Hierarchical Dirichlet Processes ([[Bayesian Nonparametrics|Bayesian Nonparametrics]]) allow topics to share structure, but standard LDA misses these relationships entirely.

* ''Polysemy and word context'': Because LDA treats documents as bags of words, it cannot distinguish "bank" (financial) from "bank" (river). Modern [[Embeddings|Embeddings]]-based approaches capture word sense through context, which LDA fundamentally cannot do. This limitation is a key reason why neural topic models and transformer-based approaches have gained ground.

!! Technical Details

''Collapsed Gibbs convergence'': Typically requires 500-2000 iterations. Burn-in of 200-500 samples is standard. Point estimates of $\theta$ and $\phi$ are recovered from the final sample's counts:

$$\hat{\theta}_{d,k} = \frac{n_{d,k} + \alpha}{\sum_{k'} n_{d,k'} + K\alpha}, \qquad \hat{\phi}_{k,w} = \frac{n_{k,w} + \beta}{\sum_{w'} n_{k,w'} + V\beta}$$

''Variational EM'': The E-step updates per-document variational parameters (topic proportions $\gamma_d$ and per-word topic responsibilities $\phi_{d,n}$). The M-step updates global topic-word distributions. Online variational Bayes (Hoffman et al., 2010) processes mini-batches and scales to millions of documents.

''Evaluation metrics'': Perplexity measures how well the model predicts held-out words: $\text{perplexity} = \exp\left(-\frac{\sum_d \log p(\mathbf{w}_d)}{\sum_d N_d}\right)$. Topic coherence (e.g., NPMI-based) correlates better with human judgment of topic quality. A model can have good perplexity but poor coherence and vice versa.

''Extensions'': The Hierarchical Dirichlet Process (HDP) learns the number of topics automatically. Dynamic Topic Models capture topic evolution over time. Supervised LDA (sLDA) adds a response variable for prediction. Author-Topic Models associate topics with authors. The work of [[Andrew McCallum|Andrew McCallum]] and collaborators extended LDA into numerous practical applications.

''Relation to matrix factorization'': LDA can be viewed as a probabilistic form of non-negative matrix factorization (NMF). The document-word matrix $\approx \Theta \cdot \Phi$, where $\Theta$ is $D \times K$ (document-topic) and $\Phi$ is $K \times V$ (topic-word). LDA adds Dirichlet priors and a proper generative model on top of this decomposition.
