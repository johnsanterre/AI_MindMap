! Random Graphs

<img src="assets/random_graphs.svg" alt="Random Graphs: subcritical isolated components, critical giant component emergence, supercritical dense connectivity, and phase transition curve" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine scattering a hundred people in a room and flipping a coin for every possible pair — heads means they shake hands. At first, with a very biased coin (almost always tails), you get isolated clusters of two or three. But as the coin becomes slightly less biased, something dramatic happens: suddenly a huge fraction of the room is connected through chains of handshakes. This abrupt shift is the ''phase transition'' at the heart of ''random graph'' theory. A random graph is any graph built by a probabilistic rule, and the study of these objects reveals which structural properties are virtually guaranteed to emerge — and which ones depend on delicate tuning of a single parameter.

!! The Core Insight

The central discovery of Erdos and Renyi (1959) is that graph properties do not appear gradually — they ''snap into existence'' at sharp thresholds. In the $G(n, p)$ model, $n$ nodes each pair independently connected with probability $p$. When $p$ is just below a critical value, a property is almost surely absent; when $p$ is just above it, the property is almost surely present. The most famous threshold is connectivity of a giant component: at $p = 1/n$, a single giant connected component containing a constant fraction of all nodes suddenly emerges from a sea of tiny fragments. At $p = \ln(n)/n$, the entire graph becomes connected with high probability. These are not gradual crossovers — they are as sharp as phase transitions in physics, and they reveal that large random structures exhibit ''deterministic'' behavior despite being built from pure randomness.

!! Models of Random Graphs

The two classical models are:

''$G(n, p)$ (Erdos-Renyi):'' Start with $n$ labeled vertices. Each of the $\binom{n}{2}$ possible edges is included independently with probability $p$. The parameter $p$ can depend on $n$ (e.g., $p = c/n$ for constant $c$). This model is analytically tractable because edges are independent.

''$G(n, M)$ (Erdos-Renyi, uniform):'' Choose uniformly at random among all graphs with exactly $M$ edges on $n$ labeled vertices. For large $n$, $G(n, M)$ with $M \approx pn(n-1)/2$ behaves almost identically to $G(n, p)$.

''Preferential attachment (Barabasi-Albert):'' Nodes arrive one at a time, each connecting to $m$ existing nodes with probability proportional to their current degree. This produces ''scale-free'' networks with power-law degree distributions $P(k) \sim k^{-3}$, unlike the Poisson degree distribution of $G(n,p)$. Many real-world networks (web links, citation graphs, protein interactions) exhibit this heavy-tailed structure.

''Configuration model:'' Fix a degree sequence $(d_1, \ldots, d_n)$ and wire stubs together uniformly at random. This generates random graphs with any prescribed degree distribution, enabling the study of how degree heterogeneity affects connectivity, epidemic spreading, and robustness.

See also: [[Graph Theory]], [[Probabilistic Method]].

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

In $G(n, p)$, define random indicator variables $X_{ij} \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$ for each pair $\{i, j\}$. The expected number of edges is:

$$\mathbb{E}[\text{edges}] = \binom{n}{2} p \approx \frac{n^2 p}{2}$$

The ''degree'' of each vertex follows $\text{Binomial}(n-1, p)$ with mean $(n-1)p$. For $p = c/n$, the mean degree is approximately $c$, and by the Poisson limit theorem, the degree distribution converges to $\text{Poisson}(c)$.

''Giant component threshold:'' Let $c = np$. The fraction of vertices in the largest component satisfies:

$$\rho = 1 - e^{-c\rho}$$

For $c \leq 1$, the only solution is $\rho = 0$ — all components are small, with the largest of order $O(\log n)$. For $c > 1$, a positive solution $\rho > 0$ appears — a giant component of size $\rho \cdot n$ exists.

''Connectivity threshold:'' For $p = (\ln n + c)/n$, the probability that $G(n,p)$ is connected converges to:

$$\Pr[\text{connected}] \to e^{-e^{-c}}$$

Setting $c \to \infty$ (i.e., $p \gg \ln(n)/n$) gives connectivity with probability $\to 1$.

''Threshold functions:'' A monotone graph property $\mathcal{P}$ has a threshold function $p^*(n)$ such that:

$$\Pr[G(n,p) \in \mathcal{P}] \to \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } p/p^* \to 0 \\ 1 & \text{if } p/p^* \to \infty \end{cases}$$

Examples: containing a triangle requires $p^* = 1/n$; containing a Hamiltonian cycle requires $p^* = \ln(n)/n$; having chromatic number $> k$ requires $p^* = n^{-1 + 1/(k-1)}$.

!! Random Graphs in Machine Learning

Random graph theory underpins several areas of modern ML:

''Graph Neural Networks:'' Understanding how GNN message-passing behaves on random graphs reveals fundamental limits. On $G(n,p)$ graphs, oversmoothing occurs at predictable rates tied to the spectral gap. Random graphs serve as null models for benchmarking GNN expressiveness.

''Network analysis:'' Community detection algorithms are tested against the ''stochastic block model'' — a random graph with planted clusters where within-cluster edge probability exceeds between-cluster probability. The information-theoretic threshold for detectability (Decelle et al., 2011) is a random graph phase transition.

''Dropout as random subgraph:'' Dropout in neural networks can be viewed as training on random subgraphs of the computation graph. Random graph connectivity thresholds help explain why moderate dropout rates preserve gradient flow while excessive dropout fragments the network.

''Random features and sketching:'' The Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma and random projection methods exploit concentration of measure on random structures — the same mathematical machinery that drives random graph theory.

See also: [[Ramsey Theory]], [[Graph Theory]].

!! When Random Graphs Break Down

''(1) Real networks are not Erdos-Renyi — degree distributions are heavy-tailed.'' The $G(n,p)$ model produces Poisson degree distributions, but real networks (social, biological, technological) have power-law or log-normal degree distributions with high-degree hubs. Using Erdos-Renyi as a model for the internet or social networks dramatically underestimates the importance of hubs and overestimates robustness to targeted attacks. Preferential attachment or configuration models are necessary but harder to analyze.

''(2) Independence of edges is unrealistic — real edges cluster.'' In $G(n,p)$, edges are independent: knowing that Alice knows Bob and Bob knows Carol tells you nothing about whether Alice knows Carol. Real social networks have high ''clustering coefficients'' (triadic closure). This makes $G(n,p)$ a poor model for local structure, even when global properties (diameter, component sizes) are well-matched. Exponential random graph models (ERGMs) and random geometric graphs address this but sacrifice analytical tractability.

''(3) Sharp thresholds assume infinite size — finite networks blur the transition.'' The beautiful phase transitions of random graph theory hold in the limit $n \to \infty$. For finite networks (hundreds to thousands of nodes), the transition from disconnected to connected is a gradual crossover, not a sharp jump. Practitioners who expect knife-edge behavior in real-sized networks will find wide transition windows where probabilistic predictions are unreliable.

!! Technical Details

''Historical origins:'' Erdos and Renyi (1959, 1960) founded the field. Their original motivation was the ''probabilistic method'' — proving that graphs with certain properties //exist// by showing they occur with positive probability in a random model, even when no explicit construction is known.

''Component structure below threshold:'' For $p = c/n$ with $c < 1$, the largest component has size $O(\log n)$ and is a tree (or tree plus $O(1)$ extra edges). The number of components of size $k$ follows a Poisson distribution with known parameters — a connection to the [[Probabilistic Method]].

''Chromatic number:'' Bollobas (1988) showed that for constant $p$, the chromatic number of $G(n,p)$ concentrates around $\frac{n}{2\log_b n}$ where $b = 1/(1-p)$. This is a striking result: a random graph's chromatic number is essentially deterministic despite being built randomly.

''Sparse random graphs and coding theory:'' LDPC codes (Gallager, 1962) are defined by sparse random bipartite graphs. Their decoding thresholds are random graph phase transitions, and belief propagation on these graphs connects to the cavity method from statistical physics.

''Stochastic block model:'' For $k$ communities of equal size $n/k$, with within-community edge probability $a/n$ and between-community probability $b/n$, exact recovery of communities is possible iff $\sqrt{a} - \sqrt{b} > \sqrt{k}$ (Abbe et al., 2015). Below this threshold, no algorithm can succeed — it is an information-theoretic impossibility.

''Random regular graphs:'' Uniformly random $d$-regular graphs (every node has exactly degree $d$) are excellent expanders with high probability. Their spectral gap satisfies $\lambda_2 \leq 2\sqrt{d-1} + \epsilon$ (Friedman 2003, confirming the Alon conjecture), making them near-optimal for mixing times and error-correcting codes.

See also: [[Graph Theory]], [[Probabilistic Method]], [[Ramsey Theory]].
