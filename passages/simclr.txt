! SimCLR

<img src="assets/simclr.svg" alt="SimCLR pipeline: image augmented into two views, passed through encoder f and projection head g, trained with NT-Xent contrastive loss" style="max-width:100%;margin:1em 0;">

If you show someone a photo of a dog and a cropped, color-shifted version of the same photo, they immediately recognize them as the same dog — the pose, texture, and shape survive the transformation. SimCLR trains a neural network to do exactly that: learn visual representations purely by recognizing that augmented views of the same image should be similar, with no class labels required.

!! The Core Insight

''Chen et al. (2020)'' introduced SimCLR as a surprisingly simple self-supervised recipe: take each image in a batch, apply two independent random augmentations, encode both views with the same network, and pull their embeddings together while pushing them away from every other image in the batch. After pretraining, the encoder is frozen and a single linear layer trained on top reaches ''76.5% top-1 accuracy on ImageNet'' with a ResNet-50 — closing much of the gap to fully supervised learning without a single label during pretraining.

Related topics: [[Contrastive Learning]], [[Self-supervised Learning]], [[Embeddings]], [[CLIP]], [[Autoencoders]].

!! Data Augmentation is Everything

The augmentation pipeline is the most important design decision in SimCLR. Each image is transformed twice, independently, by randomly sampling from a sequence of operations: ''random crop and resize'', random horizontal flip, ''color jitter'' (brightness, contrast, saturation, hue), random grayscale conversion, and ''Gaussian blur''. The pair of transformed images becomes the positive pair for the contrastive objective.

An ablation study in the paper makes the stakes clear: using random crop alone reaches ~60% ImageNet accuracy; adding color jitter brings it to ~70%; the full stack reaches 76.5%. The intuition is that good augmentations define the invariances the model should learn — if two views of the same image look different enough to require a real understanding of content to match, the encoder is forced to extract semantically meaningful features rather than low-level textures.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

For a minibatch of $N$ images, SimCLR generates $2N$ augmented views. Each view $x_i$ is passed through the encoder $f(\cdot)$ (a ResNet) to produce representation $h_i$, then through a small MLP projection head $g(\cdot)$ to produce embedding $z_i = g(f(x_i))$.

The loss for a positive pair $(i, j)$ is the ''NT-Xent'' (normalized temperature-scaled cross-entropy) loss:

$$\ell_{i,j} = -\log \frac{\exp(\text{sim}(z_i, z_j)/\tau)}{\sum_{k=1}^{2N} \mathbf{1}_{[k \neq i]} \exp(\text{sim}(z_i, z_k)/\tau)}$$

where $\text{sim}(u, v) = u^\top v / (\|u\| \|v\|)$ is cosine similarity and $\tau$ is a temperature hyperparameter (typically 0.07–0.5). The denominator sums over all $2(N-1)$ negatives in the batch. The full loss averages $\ell_{i,j}$ over all positive pairs.

Two design choices flow from this. First, ''larger batches are better'': with $N = 4096$–$8192$, each positive pair is contrasted against thousands of negatives, giving a stronger learning signal. Second, the ''projection head $g$ is discarded'' after pretraining — downstream tasks use $h$ directly. Empirically, $h$ contains more transferable features than $z$.

!! SimCLR v2 and Beyond

''SimCLR v2'' (Chen et al., 2020) extended the framework in three directions: a deeper, wider projection head (3-layer MLP vs. 2-layer), semi-supervised fine-tuning on 1% or 10% of ImageNet labels after pretraining, and knowledge distillation using the pretrained model as a teacher.

Subsequent work challenged the assumption that negatives are necessary. ''BYOL'' (Bootstrap Your Own Latent; Grill et al., 2020) uses two networks — an online network and a slowly-updated target network (exponential moving average) — where the online network learns to predict the target network's output. With no negatives and no labels, BYOL matches and often exceeds SimCLR's accuracy. ''MoCo'' (Momentum Contrast; He et al., 2020) takes a different approach: maintain a queue of past embeddings as a large bank of negatives, decoupling batch size from the number of negatives. ''DINO'' (Caron et al., 2021) extends self-distillation to Vision Transformers. Together, these methods show that the contrastive framework is one point in a larger design space of representation learning without labels.

!! When SimCLR Breaks Down

''Augmentation sensitivity:'' If the augmentation strategy is too weak (views are too similar), the model learns trivial shortcuts like matching pixel statistics. If too strong, semantically identical images may map to distant embeddings. The right augmentation set is domain-dependent — the ImageNet-tuned stack performs poorly on medical images or satellite imagery without re-tuning.

''Computational cost:'' SimCLR's performance is tightly coupled to batch size. Batches of 4096–8192 require large-scale distributed training (the original paper used 128 TPU v3 cores). Methods like MoCo and BYOL were developed specifically to break this dependency, but SimCLR itself is expensive to train from scratch.

''Semantic uniformity:'' The loss treats all non-paired images as negatives, including images from the same class. With enough negatives and sufficiently large models this averages out, but with small batches or narrow domains, the model can be penalized for correctly pulling together semantically similar images that happen to appear in the same batch.

!! Technical Details

Key references: Chen et al. 2020 "A Simple Framework for Contrastive Learning of Visual Representations" (SimCLR); Chen et al. 2020 "Big Self-Supervised Models are Strong Semi-Supervised Learners" (SimCLR v2); Grill et al. 2020 "Bootstrap Your Own Latent" (BYOL); He et al. 2020 "Momentum Contrast for Unsupervised Visual Representation Learning" (MoCo v2); Caron et al. 2021 "Emerging Properties in Self-Supervised Vision Transformers" (DINO).

Evaluation protocols: ''Linear evaluation'' — freeze pretrained encoder, train a single linear layer on labeled data, report top-1 accuracy. ''Semi-supervised'' — fine-tune encoder with 1% or 10% of ImageNet labels. ''Transfer'' — evaluate frozen features on CIFAR-10, Oxford Pets, Food-101, etc.

Architecture defaults: ResNet-50 encoder ($h \in \mathbb{R}^{2048}$), 2-layer MLP projection head with hidden size 2048 and output size 128, temperature $\tau = 0.07$, Adam optimizer with LARS for large batches, 100–1000 epochs pretraining.
