! Measure Theory

<img src="assets/measure_theory.svg" alt="Diagram showing sigma-algebra, measurable sets, and Lebesgue vs Riemann integration" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Suppose you want to measure the "size" of a set of real numbers — not just intervals, but arbitrary collections of points. The rationals have zero length despite being dense everywhere. A Cantor set has zero length but uncountably many points. Ordinary intuition breaks down. ''Measure theory'' is the rigorous foundation for assigning sizes to sets, integrating functions over them, and building probability theory on solid ground. Every time you write $P(X \in A)$ in ML, measure theory is the invisible scaffolding making it meaningful.

!! The Core Insight

A ''measure'' $\mu$ assigns non-negative numbers to sets, with one critical property: ''countable additivity''. If you have countably many disjoint sets $A_1, A_2, \ldots$, then:

$$\mu\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \mu(A_i)$$

Probability is just a measure with $\mu(\Omega) = 1$. The ''Lebesgue integral'' extends Riemann integration to handle pathological functions — the kind that arise naturally in probability and analysis. ''$\sigma$-algebras'' (sigma-algebras) define precisely which sets are measurable, resolving paradoxes that arise when you try to assign sizes to every possible subset of the reals.

!! Sigma-Algebras and Measurable Spaces

A ''$\sigma$-algebra'' $\mathcal{F}$ on a sample space $\Omega$ is a collection of subsets satisfying three axioms: (1) $\Omega \in \mathcal{F}$, (2) closed under complement — if $A \in \mathcal{F}$ then $A^c \in \mathcal{F}$, and (3) closed under countable union — if $A_1, A_2, \ldots \in \mathcal{F}$ then $\bigcup_i A_i \in \mathcal{F}$. The pair $(\Omega, \mathcal{F})$ is a ''measurable space''.

The ''Borel $\sigma$-algebra'' $\mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$ is the smallest $\sigma$-algebra containing all open intervals on $\mathbb{R}$. It includes all open sets, closed sets, countable intersections of open sets ($G_\delta$ sets), and much more — essentially every set you would ever naturally construct.

A ''measurable function'' $f: (\Omega, \mathcal{F}) \to (\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$ is one where the preimage of every Borel set is measurable: $f^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{F}$ for all $B \in \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$. A ''random variable'' is simply a measurable function $X: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$. The ''pushforward measure'' (or distribution of $X$) is defined as:

$$P_X(A) = P(X^{-1}(A)) = P(\{\omega \in \Omega : X(\omega) \in A\})$$

This is precisely what $P(X \in A)$ means. The $\sigma$-algebra $\sigma(X)$ generated by $X$ captures exactly the information that $X$ reveals about $\Omega$.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The key innovation of the ''Lebesgue integral'' is to partition the //range// of a function rather than its domain. For a non-negative measurable function $f$:

$$\int f \, d\mu = \sup \left\{ \int \phi \, d\mu : 0 \leq \phi \leq f, \, \phi \text{ simple} \right\}$$

where simple functions are finite linear combinations of indicator functions. Intuitively: slice the $y$-axis into thin strips at heights $y$ to $y + dy$, weight each strip by $\mu(f^{-1}([y, y+dy]))$, and sum. This handles functions that are wildly discontinuous on dense sets — functions where the Riemann integral simply doesn't exist.

The ''Dominated Convergence Theorem'' (DCT) is perhaps the most useful result: if $f_n \to f$ pointwise and $|f_n| \leq g$ for an integrable $g$, then:

$$\lim_{n\to\infty} \int f_n \, d\mu = \int f \, d\mu$$

This justifies swapping limits and integrals — something Riemann integration cannot guarantee.

The ''Radon-Nikodym theorem'' is fundamental for density functions. If $\nu$ is ''absolutely continuous'' with respect to $\mu$ (written $\nu \ll \mu$ — meaning $\mu(A) = 0 \Rightarrow \nu(A) = 0$), then there exists a measurable function $h \geq 0$ such that:

$$\nu(A) = \int_A h \, d\mu \quad \text{for all measurable } A$$

The function $h = \frac{d\nu}{d\mu}$ is the ''Radon-Nikodym derivative'' or density. For continuous distributions, this recovers the familiar PDF. KL divergence requires $\nu \ll \mu$:

$$D_{KL}(\nu \| \mu) = \int \log\!\left(\frac{d\nu}{d\mu}\right) d\nu$$

The ''change of variables formula'': for a diffeomorphism $T$ and pushforward measure, $\int f \circ T \, d\mu = \int f \, |det J_T|^{-1} \, d\nu$ — the foundation of normalizing flows.

!! Measure Theory in ML

Measure theory is the hidden architecture beneath virtually all of ML:

''Probability distributions'' are measures on sample spaces. ''Expectation'' is a Lebesgue integral: $\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_\Omega X \, dP$.

''KL divergence'' requires absolute continuity between distributions. When $Q \not\ll P$, KL divergence is infinite — a fact with real consequences for variational inference and likelihood ratios.

''Convergence modes'' — all defined measure-theoretically — have distinct practical meanings:
- //Almost sure// convergence: $P(\lim_n X_n = X) = 1$ (paths converge everywhere except a null set)
- //In probability//: $P(|X_n - X| > \epsilon) \to 0$ (weaker; allows occasional large deviations)
- //In $L^p$//: $\mathbb{E}[|X_n - X|^p] \to 0$ (controls moments)
- //In distribution//: $\mathbb{E}[f(X_n)] \to \mathbb{E}[f(X)]$ for bounded continuous $f$ (weakest)

''Stochastic processes'' like Brownian motion are functions on path spaces. The ''Wiener measure'' on $C([0,1])$ is the rigorous construction underlying Gaussian processes and diffusion models.

''PAC learning'' uses measure-theoretic arguments to bound the probability that empirical risk minimizers generalize — the uniform convergence results depend on measurability of suprema over function classes.

The ''disintegration theorem'' (regular conditional distributions) underpins conditional probability: given $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ and a sub-$\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{G}$, the conditional expectation $\mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{G}]$ exists as a measurable function. This is why Bayesian conditioning is well-defined.

!! When Measure Theory Becomes Limiting

''Non-measurable sets exist.'' Using the axiom of choice, the ''Vitali set'' partitions $[0,1]$ into equivalence classes of rationals-shifts, producing a set that cannot be assigned any consistent measure. These are pathological and never arise in practice — but they demonstrate the foundations are genuinely subtle. You cannot have a translation-invariant measure defined on all subsets of $\mathbb{R}$.

''Infinite product measures require care.'' Defining probability on infinite sequences (e.g., all possible trajectories of a stochastic process) requires ''Kolmogorov's extension theorem'': consistent finite-dimensional marginals extend uniquely to a measure on the infinite product space. Naive approaches — simply taking limits — break down without this theorem. This is what makes the existence of Brownian motion non-trivial.

''Measure theory is non-constructive.'' Existence proofs guarantee measures and integrals exist without providing algorithms to compute them. In ML, you need actual numbers: expectations, normalizing constants, posterior densities. This requires additional approximation machinery — variational inference, MCMC, numerical quadrature — that measure theory does not supply. The gap between "a unique posterior exists" and "here is how to compute it" is where all the practical work happens.

!! Technical Details

Historical milestones: Borel (1898) introduced $\sigma$-algebras; Lebesgue (1902) constructed his integral; Kolmogorov (1933) published //Grundbegriffe der Wahrscheinlichkeitsrechnung//, placing all of probability theory on measure-theoretic foundations.

Key convergence theorems:
- ''Monotone Convergence Theorem'': if $0 \leq f_n \uparrow f$ pointwise, then $\int f_n \, d\mu \uparrow \int f \, d\mu$
- ''Dominated Convergence Theorem'': pointwise convergence + domination $\Rightarrow$ integral convergence
- ''Fatou's Lemma'': $\int \liminf_n f_n \, d\mu \leq \liminf_n \int f_n \, d\mu$

''$L^p$ spaces'': $L^p(\mu) = \{f : \int |f|^p \, d\mu < \infty\}$ with norm $\|f\|_p = (\int |f|^p \, d\mu)^{1/p}$. These are Banach spaces; $L^2$ is a Hilbert space. The dual of $L^p$ is $L^q$ where $1/p + 1/q = 1$.

''Fubini's theorem'': for $\sigma$-finite measures, $\int\!\int f(x,y) \, d\mu(x) \, d\nu(y) = \int\!\int f(x,y) \, d\nu(y) \, d\mu(x)$ — justifies interchanging integration order. Requires absolute integrability (Tonelli's theorem gives the non-negative case unconditionally).
