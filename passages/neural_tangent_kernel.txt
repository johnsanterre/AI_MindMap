! Neural Tangent Kernel

<img src="assets/neural_tangent_kernel.svg" alt="Diagram showing a finite-width neural network converging to kernel regression with a fixed NTK in the infinite-width limit" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are trying to predict the path of a river. You could simulate every molecule of water and every grain of sediment — that is the finite neural network. Or you could zoom out far enough that the river becomes a smooth, predictable curve governed by simple equations of flow. The ''Neural Tangent Kernel'' (NTK) is that zoom-out for neural networks: in the limit of infinite width, the messy, nonlinear dynamics of training collapse into the clean, well-understood mathematics of <<link "Kernel Methods" "Kernel Methods">><</link>>. The network still interpolates data, but now you can write down exactly how it does so, predict its generalization, and guarantee its convergence — all using a single fixed kernel that depends only on the architecture and initialization.

---

!! The Core Insight

Training a <<link "neural network" "Neural Networks">><</link>> with <<link "gradient descent" "Gradient Descent">><</link>> changes the network's parameters, which changes the function the network computes. In a standard finite network, the relationship between parameter updates and function changes is complicated and nonlinear. But Jacot, Gabriel, and Hongler (2018) proved a remarkable fact: as the width of a network grows to infinity, the function computed by the network evolves during training as if governed by a ''fixed'' kernel — the Neural Tangent Kernel. The kernel does not change during training; it is determined entirely by the architecture, activation function, and parameter initialization.

This means that in the infinite-width limit, training a neural network is ''exactly'' equivalent to performing kernel regression with the NTK. The optimization landscape is convex, gradient descent converges globally, and the final predictor is the minimum-norm interpolant in the reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS) defined by the NTK. All the machinery of <<link "kernel methods" "Kernel Methods">><</link>>, <<link "Gaussian processes" "Gaussian Processes">><</link>>, and <<link "statistical learning theory" "Statistical Learning Theory">><</link>> becomes directly applicable to understanding what neural networks learn and why they generalize.

The NTK is not a statement about a particular trained network. It is a statement about ''the dynamics of training'' — about how the function changes in response to gradient updates. The kernel captures the geometry of function space as seen by the optimizer. Two inputs $x$ and $x'$ have a large NTK value $K(x, x')$ when the gradient of the network output with respect to parameters points in similar directions for both inputs. Training on $x$ then automatically improves prediction at $x'$, and vice versa.

---

!! From Parameters to Kernels

Consider a neural network $f(x; \theta)$ with parameters $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^p$. The NTK is defined as:

$$K(x, x') = \left\langle \frac{\partial f(x; \theta)}{\partial \theta}, \frac{\partial f(x; \theta')}{\partial \theta'} \right\rangle = \sum_{i=1}^{p} \frac{\partial f(x; \theta)}{\partial \theta_i} \frac{\partial f(x'; \theta)}{\partial \theta_i}$$

This is the inner product of the Jacobians — the gradients of the network output with respect to all parameters, evaluated at two different inputs. At initialization, this kernel is random (it depends on the random initial parameters). But as the width goes to infinity, two things happen simultaneously: (1) the kernel at initialization converges to a deterministic limit $K^*$ by the law of large numbers, and (2) the kernel ''stays at'' $K^*$ throughout training because each individual parameter changes by only $O(1/\sqrt{m})$ where $m$ is the width.

This ''lazy training'' regime means the network is effectively linearized around its initialization. The function the network computes at any time $t$ during training is:

$$f_t(x) \approx f_0(x) + \nabla_\theta f(x; \theta_0)^T (\theta_t - \theta_0)$$

The network output changes linearly in the parameters, and gradient descent on this linear model is kernel regression with the NTK. The infinite-width network never ''learns features'' — it uses the fixed random features determined at initialization, combined through the kernel. This is both the power of the theory (tractability) and its limitation (real networks do learn features).

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Under gradient flow (continuous-time gradient descent) with mean squared error loss on training data $\{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^n$, the network predictions on training points evolve as:

$$\frac{d}{dt} f_t(X) = -K(X, X)(f_t(X) - y)$$

where $K(X, X)$ is the $n \times n$ kernel matrix with entries $K(x_i, x_j)$. This is a linear ODE with solution:

$$f_t(X) = y - e^{-K(X, X) t}(y - f_0(X))$$

If the kernel matrix is positive definite (which it is for typical architectures and distinct training points), then $e^{-K(X,X)t} \to 0$ as $t \to \infty$, meaning training converges exponentially to zero loss. The rate of convergence is controlled by $\lambda_{\min}(K(X,X))$, the smallest eigenvalue of the kernel matrix.

At convergence, the prediction on a new test point $x^*$ is:

$$f_\infty(x^*) = f_0(x^*) + K(x^*, X) K(X,X)^{-1}(y - f_0(X))$$

This is exactly the kernel regression formula. The NTK prediction is a linear combination of kernel evaluations against training points, weighted to interpolate the training labels. The connection to <<link "Gaussian processes" "Gaussian Processes">><</link>> is also direct: the infinite-width network at initialization is a Gaussian process with kernel given by the ''NNGP kernel'' (the covariance of the network output), and the trained network's predictions coincide with the GP posterior mean under the NTK.

The ''spectral decomposition'' of the kernel controls what the network learns first. If $K = \sum_k \lambda_k \phi_k \phi_k^T$ is the eigendecomposition, then the component of the residual along eigenvector $\phi_k$ decays at rate $e^{-\lambda_k t}$. Components aligned with large eigenvalues are learned quickly; components along small eigenvalues are learned slowly. This spectral bias means NTK networks learn smooth, low-frequency functions before high-frequency ones — a form of implicit regularization.

---

!! Practical Implications and Finite-Width Effects

The NTK theory, while exact only in the infinite-width limit, has several practical consequences. ''First'', it explains why wide networks are easier to optimize: the loss landscape becomes increasingly convex as width grows, and the minimum eigenvalue of the NTK matrix grows, accelerating convergence. Practitioners have long known that wider networks train faster and more reliably; NTK theory provides the mechanism.

''Second'', the NTK framework enables ''neural network Gaussian process'' (NNGP) inference — computing exact Bayesian posterior predictions for infinite-width networks without ever training a network. Libraries like Neural Tangents (Google) can compute NTK and NNGP kernels analytically for standard architectures and run kernel regression directly. This gives a baseline: if your finite network underperforms the corresponding infinite-width NTK predictor, the gap is due to optimization failure, not architecture.

''Third'', and most importantly, the NTK regime describes what neural networks //cannot// do, not just what they can. Finite-width networks that are trained long enough undergo ''feature learning'' — the representations in intermediate layers adapt to the data. The NTK regime, by construction, has no feature learning: the kernel is fixed at initialization. The empirical success of deep learning on complex tasks like vision and language relies on feature learning, which means real networks must be operating //outside// the NTK regime. Understanding the NTK regime sharpens the question: what makes finite-width feature learning so much more powerful than fixed-kernel regression?

Recent work on ''mean-field'' and ''maximal update'' parameterizations (the $\mu$P framework of Yang and Hu, 2021) characterizes how to scale hyperparameters with width so that feature learning persists even as width grows. In these parameterizations, the kernel //does// evolve during training, and the infinite-width limit is a nontrivial feature-learning regime rather than the lazy NTK regime.

---

!! When the NTK Framework Breaks Down

''Feature learning in finite networks:'' The NTK theory assumes the kernel stays fixed during training — the ''lazy training'' regime. Real networks of practical width violate this assumption significantly. The first few layers learn data-dependent features (edges, textures, syntactic patterns) that are far more informative than the random features at initialization. This feature learning is precisely what makes deep learning powerful, and it is invisible to NTK theory. When the kernel changes substantially during training, the NTK predictions for generalization, convergence rate, and learned function can all be qualitatively wrong.

''Small or structured datasets:'' The NTK is a universal kernel determined by architecture, not data. It cannot adapt its similarity measure to the structure of a particular dataset. On tasks where success depends on discovering the right representation — for example, learning that certain pixel configurations represent edges — the NTK predictor is limited to the representation baked in at initialization. Finite-width networks that learn features can dramatically outperform the NTK baseline on such tasks, and the gap grows with task complexity.

''Depth scaling:'' The NTK theory is cleanest for networks of fixed depth with width going to infinity. For very deep networks, the NTK at initialization can degenerate: the kernel matrix may become approximately rank-1 (all inputs look similar) due to signal propagation issues. Residual connections, careful initialization (e.g., He initialization), and normalization layers are needed to keep the NTK well-conditioned in deep networks. The interaction between depth and width in the NTK regime remains an active research area, and the simple "width to infinity" story does not extend straightforwardly to "depth to infinity."

---

!! Technical Details

''Original paper:'' Jacot, Gabriel, and Hongler (2018), //Neural Tangent Kernel: Convergence and Generalization in Neural Networks//, NeurIPS. Proved that the NTK converges to a deterministic limit and stays constant during training in the infinite-width limit for fully-connected networks with standard parameterization ($1/\sqrt{m}$ scaling). Extended to convolutional architectures (CNTK) by Arora et al. (2019).

''Parameterization matters:'' The NTK limit depends on how weights are scaled with width. The ''standard'' or ''NTK parameterization'' scales the output of each layer by $1/\sqrt{m}$, producing the lazy/kernel regime at infinite width. The ''mean-field parameterization'' scales by $1/m$, producing a feature-learning regime at infinite width. The ''maximal update parameterization'' ($\mu$P, Yang and Hu 2021) interpolates and identifies the unique scaling that preserves feature learning dynamics as width grows.

''Recursive kernel computation:'' For a fully-connected network with $L$ layers and activation $\sigma$, the NTK can be computed recursively. Define the NNGP kernel $K^{(0)}(x, x') = x^T x' / d$ and for layer $l$: $\Sigma^{(l)} = \begin{pmatrix} K^{(l-1)}(x,x) & K^{(l-1)}(x,x') \\ K^{(l-1)}(x',x) & K^{(l-1)}(x',x') \end{pmatrix}$, then $K^{(l)}(x,x') = \mathbb{E}_{(u,v) \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma^{(l)})}[\sigma(u)\sigma(v)]$. The NTK is $\Theta^{(L)} = \sum_{l=1}^{L} K^{(l)} \prod_{l'=l+1}^{L} \dot{K}^{(l')}$ where $\dot{K}^{(l)}$ involves the derivative of the activation.

''Spectral bias and generalization:'' The eigenspectrum of the NTK determines both learning speed and generalization. Components of the target function aligned with top eigenvectors are learned first and regularized least. Empirically, the NTK eigenspectrum for standard architectures on image data exhibits power-law decay, which connects to the ''frequency bias'' of neural networks — they learn low-frequency components before high-frequency ones.

''Connection to double descent:'' In the NTK regime, the trained network is the minimum-norm interpolant in the RKHS. This directly connects to double descent theory: the NTK predictor exhibits kernel double descent as a function of the number of training points relative to the effective dimension of the RKHS. The random matrix theory analysis of kernel double descent applies directly to NTK predictions.

''Finite-width corrections:'' The leading-order correction to the NTK at finite width is $O(1/m)$ and corresponds to feature learning — the kernel matrix changes during training by an amount proportional to $1/m$. Dyer and Gur-Ari (2020) and Huang and Yau (2020) studied these corrections and showed that they can qualitatively change generalization behavior, particularly on tasks where feature learning is essential.
