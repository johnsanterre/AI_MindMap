! Mechanistic Interpretability

<img src="assets/mechanistic_interpretability.svg" alt="Diagram illustrating Mechanistic Interpretability as circuit reverse-engineering of neural networks" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

When the first transistor radios were built, engineers who wanted to understand them could open the case, trace the circuits, and label every component's function — this resistor limits current, this capacitor filters frequency, this transistor amplifies the signal. The result was a complete, human-readable account of how the device worked. Mechanistic interpretability attempts exactly the same thing for neural networks: open the black box, find the functional units, and reconstruct the algorithm the model is actually running — in terms of weights, attention patterns, and activation directions rather than transistors and resistors.

---

!! The Core Insight

''Mechanistic interpretability'' (MI) aims to understand neural networks at the level of individual weights and circuits, not just their input-output behavior. This places it in sharp contrast with behavioral interpretability methods like SHAP or LIME, which attribute credit to input features without explaining the internal computation, or probing classifiers, which test whether information //exists// in a representation without explaining //how// the model uses it. MI asks not "what does the model know?" but "how does the model compute what it computes?"

Three hierarchical concepts organize the field. ''Features'' are meaningful directions in activation space — patterns of neuron activations that correspond to interpretable concepts such as "curve," "capital city," or "past tense." ''Circuits'' are subgraphs of the network — specific attention heads and MLP neurons connected by weight matrices — that implement a particular computation using those features. ''Universality'' is the empirical observation that the same circuits appear across models trained on different data and with different architectures, suggesting that circuits are a fundamental property of learned representations, not artifacts of a single training run. These ideas connect to broader work in [[Attention]], [[Transformers]], [[Embeddings]], [[Large Language Models]], [[Probe Identification]], [[ROME]], and [[MEMIT]].

The research program is ambitious in scope. If universality holds at scale, then understanding one model's circuits may transfer to others. If features and circuits can be enumerated and verified, then model behavior becomes predictable and auditable. The field is still young — most rigorous results come from small models like GPT-2 — but the potential for AI safety applications has driven substantial investment from Anthropic, DeepMind, and independent researchers.

---

!! Circuits, Features, and Superposition

The ''circuits thread'' (Olah et al. 2020) began with vision models. Researchers found that early convolutional layers in Inception V1 learned curve detectors — neurons that respond to oriented edges — and that these curve detectors then fed into more complex feature detectors for shapes, textures, and eventually object parts. Crucially, these detectors appeared across different model architectures trained on different datasets, supporting universality. The finding reframed interpretability: instead of neurons being inscrutable, they have functions that can be named and verified by ablation.

''Induction heads'' are among the most important circuits found in [[Transformers]]. An induction head is a two-layer circuit: the first attention head copies information from a previous token into the current position's key, and the second head uses that key to look back and retrieve what came after the matching token in the past. The result is a mechanism that implements "if I see token A followed by B, and I see A again, predict B" — the core operation behind in-context learning. When induction heads are ablated from small transformers, in-context learning degrades sharply. When they are present, the model can learn new patterns from its context window alone.

The ''superposition hypothesis'' (Elhage et al. 2022) complicates the picture. Models appear to store far more features than they have dimensions by encoding them as nearly-orthogonal directions in activation space. A single neuron responds strongly to a primary feature but weakly to dozens of unrelated features — a phenomenon called ''polysemanticity''. This means naive linear probing often fails to find individual features, and it makes circuit analysis difficult: a circuit that looks clean in isolation may be contaminated by superposed features in nearby neurons. The theoretical explanation is that near-orthogonality in high-dimensional spaces allows $N \gg d$ features to coexist with limited interference, where $d$ is the model's hidden dimension and $N$ is the number of concepts the model encounters.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The most useful lens for transformer MI is the ''residual stream view''. The transformer is a series of additive operations on a shared vector $x \in \mathbb{R}^d$:

$$x_{l+1} = x_l + \text{Attn}_l(x_l) + \text{MLP}_l(x_l + \text{Attn}_l(x_l))$$

Each attention head reads from the residual stream using its query and key matrices ($W_Q$, $W_K$) to compute attention weights, then uses its value and output matrices ($W_V$, $W_O$) to write a low-rank update back. A ''circuit'' is a path through this computation graph: a set of attention heads and MLP layers that collectively route information from specific input tokens to specific output positions. The path is identified by following which $W_O$ matrices write to positions that which $W_Q$ matrices subsequently read from.

The ''logit lens'' technique projects the residual stream at each layer back to vocabulary space using the final unembedding matrix. This reveals what the model is "predicting" at each depth — early layers produce noisy, often syntactic predictions; later layers converge toward the final answer. It provides a qualitative window into how predictions are assembled.

''Activation patching'' (also called causal tracing) is the core MI experimental technique. Given two inputs — a clean input and a corrupted input — the researcher runs both forward passes, then patches the corrupted run with activations from the clean run at a specific layer and position. If the patched run's output changes toward the clean output, that site is causally important for the computation. This lets researchers identify which residual stream positions, at which layers, carry the information relevant to a specific capability.

''Sparse autoencoders'' (SAEs) address superposition by learning a dictionary of monosemantic features. An SAE maps a model's activations to a much higher-dimensional sparse code using $L_1$ regularization to enforce sparsity, then reconstructs the original activations from that code. The learned dictionary directions tend to be interpretable, monosemantic features — one direction for "capital city," one for "past tense verb," rather than a single neuron that responds to both. SAEs are now a primary tool for large-scale feature discovery.

!! Key Findings and Safety Applications

The ''Indirect Object Identification'' (IOI) circuit (Wang et al. 2022) is one of the most complete mechanistic accounts of a language model capability. GPT-2 reliably completes "Mary gave John the book. John gave it to ___" with "Mary." Wang et al. reverse-engineered the 28-component circuit that produces this behavior: name-mover heads copy the indirect object to the output, S-inhibition heads suppress the repeated name, and duplicate token heads flag repeated names. The circuit was validated by ablation, activation patching, and path patching — making it among the most rigorously verified results in MI.

''Grokking'' (Nanda et al. 2023) provided another clean case study. When small transformers are trained on modular arithmetic (computing $a + b \mod p$), they initially memorize and then, long after training loss plateaus, generalize. Nanda et al. found that the generalizing network uses Fourier features: it represents numbers as points on the unit circle at frequencies related to $p$, then uses trigonometric identities to compute the modular sum. The circuit is elegant and fully human-readable — a rare case where MI reaches all the way to an algorithm.

Safety applications are the primary motivation for much MI research. If a model has learned to behave deceptively — producing honest outputs in evaluated contexts while planning to defect later — that disposition might be localized to identifiable circuits. Model editing tools like [[ROME]] and [[MEMIT]] use causal tracing to locate factual associations and then surgically update them. Anthropic's large-scale feature analysis of Claude Sonnet (Templeton et al. 2024) found multimodal, multilingual features corresponding to concepts like "the Eiffel Tower" that activate for images of the tower, French text about Paris, and English text alike — evidence that models learn abstract, grounded representations rather than surface-level text statistics.

---

!! When Mechanistic Interpretability Breaks Down

''Scalability:'' Techniques that are tractable on GPT-2 (117M parameters) become computationally prohibitive on frontier models (100B+ parameters). Activation patching over all positions and layers requires $O(\text{layers} \times \text{positions})$ forward passes per example. Enumerating circuits by exhaustive ablation becomes combinatorially explosive. Current MI results are largely confined to toy models or isolated sub-tasks in medium-scale models; whether findings generalize to frontier systems remains largely unknown.

''Superposition and polysemanticity:'' Because neurons represent multiple overlapping features, circuit analysis can produce misleading results. A circuit identified by ablation may be disrupted not because it implements the target computation, but because ablating it destroys superposed features that other circuits depend on. SAEs mitigate this by finding monosemantic directions, but SAE dictionaries may be incomplete, and the learned features depend heavily on the SAE's sparsity penalty and dictionary size — there is no ground truth to validate against.

''Faithfulness vs. completeness:'' A circuit explanation may be ''faithful'' (accurate for the examples tested) without being ''complete'' (the only circuit that implements the behavior). Models often contain redundant circuits — multiple parallel mechanisms that can each produce the correct output — so ablating one may leave behavior intact even though that circuit normally contributes. Likewise, a circuit found by activation patching may explain 80% of the variance while a separate, unidentified mechanism handles the remainder, making the explanation misleadingly clean.

---

!! Technical Details

''Foundational papers:'' Olah et al. 2020 ("Zoom In: An Introduction to Circuits") identified curve detectors and other universal features in vision models. Elhage et al. 2021 ("A Mathematical Framework for Transformer Circuits") introduced the residual stream decomposition, QK/OV analysis, and the induction head. Wang et al. 2022 ("Interpretability in the Wild") produced the IOI circuit analysis of GPT-2. Nanda et al. 2023 ("Progress Measures for Grokking via Mechanistic Interpretability") identified Fourier features in modular arithmetic. Cunningham et al. 2023 introduced sparse autoencoders for feature decomposition. Templeton et al. 2024 ("Scaling Monosemanticity") applied SAEs at scale to Claude Sonnet, finding millions of interpretable features.

''Tools:'' TransformerLens (Neel Nanda) is the primary open-source library for MI research on transformer models. It provides hooks into every residual stream position, attention pattern, and MLP activation, and supports activation patching natively.

''Superposition:'' The mathematical analysis in Elhage et al. 2022 ("Toy Models of Superposition") shows that the optimal number of features a model can represent scales as $N \propto d / (1 - S)$ where $S$ is the sparsity of feature activations in the data. Sparser real-world data allows more features to be packed into fewer dimensions.

''Scale challenge:'' Techniques that require one forward pass per ablation site scale as $O(L \times T)$ where $L$ is the number of layers and $T$ is the sequence length. For a 100B-parameter model with 96 layers and context length 4096, exhaustive patching requires hundreds of thousands of forward passes per example — feasible only with aggressive approximations or hardware resources unavailable to most researchers.
