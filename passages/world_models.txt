! World Models

<img src="assets/world_models.svg" alt="Diagram illustrating World Models for model-based reinforcement learning" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

A chess grandmaster does not need to physically play out every possible sequence of moves to decide what to do next — they simulate futures in their head, pruning bad lines before a single piece is touched. A ''world model'' gives a reinforcement learning agent the same superpower: a learned, differentiable simulator of the environment that lets the agent rehearse hypothetical futures cheaply, entirely in imagination, before committing a single real action. This dramatically reduces the number of costly environment interactions needed to learn good behavior.

---

!! The Core Insight

''Model-based RL'' separates the agent's learning problem into two parts: first, learn how the world works (the world model); second, use that model to plan or generate synthetic training data. In purely model-free methods such as [[Q Learning]] or [[Soft Actor-Critic]], every policy improvement step requires real environment rollouts. A world model short-circuits this by letting the agent generate thousands of imagined transitions per second at negligible cost, then training the policy on those dreams. The result is orders-of-magnitude better sample efficiency.

The world model's job is to compress high-dimensional observations — raw pixels, sensor readings — into a compact ''latent state'' $z_t$, and then predict how that latent state evolves given an action: $z_{t+1} \approx f(z_t, a_t)$. It also predicts the reward signal $\hat{r}_t$. Because everything happens in a low-dimensional latent space, planning becomes tractable. This connects naturally to the ideas behind the [[VAE]] (variational autoencoders provide the compression) and [[Markov Decision Process]] (the model approximates the MDP's transition and reward functions).

The broader family of model-based methods has a long lineage — [[Dynamic Programming]] requires a perfect model of the environment, and Dyna (Sutton 1991) first proposed mixing real and simulated experience. Modern world models extend these ideas with deep neural networks that can handle raw, high-dimensional sensory input, enabling model-based RL to scale to visually rich environments that were previously the exclusive territory of model-free [[Reinforcement Learning]].

---

!! Ha & Schmidhuber: The Original World Models (2018)

The 2018 paper "World Models" by David Ha and Jürgen Schmidhuber introduced a clean, modular architecture that made the idea concrete. A ''CNN encoder'' (the V model) maps each raw observation frame into a compact feature vector. An ''MDN-RNN'' (the M model — a recurrent network with a mixture density network output head) serves as the world model proper: given the current latent vector and action, it predicts a probability distribution over the next latent state and the reward. A tiny ''linear controller'' (the C model) reads the encoder output and the RNN hidden state and outputs the action.

The key architectural insight is that the controller is so simple — just a linear layer — that it can be trained efficiently using ''evolutionary strategies'' (CMA-ES) entirely inside the dream. The agent never touches the real environment during controller training; it plays out thousands of episodes in the MDN-RNN's imagination. This worked surprisingly well on a car-racing task and a VizDoom level. The RNN's hidden state $h_t$ is critical: it carries temporal context that no single-frame encoder can capture, effectively giving the agent memory of recent history to inform predictions.

A subtle finding: when the world model is expressive enough, the agent can learn to ''exploit'' its own model — finding adversarial latent states that fool the dreamed environment into giving high reward but that do not transfer to reality. This ''model exploitation'' problem foreshadowed important later work on how to regularize imagination.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The world model learns a latent-space transition model. In the Dreamer line of work, this is formalized as the ''Recurrent State Space Model'' (RSSM):

$$h_t = f_\phi(h_{t-1},\, z_{t-1},\, a_{t-1})$$

The deterministic recurrent state $h_t$ is updated by a GRU-like function. From it, two distributions over the stochastic latent $z_t$ are derived:

$$\text{Posterior (with observation):}\quad z_t \sim q_\phi(z_t \mid h_t,\, o_t)$$

$$\text{Prior (imagination, no observation):}\quad \hat{z}_t \sim p_\phi(\hat{z}_t \mid h_t)$$

Training minimizes a combined loss: reconstruction of observations through the decoder, prediction of rewards, and a KL divergence between posterior and prior:

$$\mathcal{L} = \mathbb{E}\!\left[-\ln p(o_t \mid h_t, z_t) - \ln p(r_t \mid h_t, z_t) + \beta\, \mathrm{KL}\!\left[q_\phi(z_t \mid h_t, o_t) \;\|\; p_\phi(\hat{z}_t \mid h_t)\right]\right]$$

The KL term forces the prior to be predictive: during imagination, the agent uses only $p_\phi$ (no real observations), so the prior must be accurate. Once the RSSM is trained, the actor and critic are trained entirely on ''imagined trajectories'' — sequences $(\hat{z}_1, a_1, \hat{r}_1, \ldots, \hat{z}_H)$ rolled out purely inside the model. Gradients flow back through the imagined rollout via the reparameterization trick, making the whole pipeline end-to-end differentiable.

---

!! Dreamer and DreamerV3

''Dreamer'' (Hafner et al. 2020) was the first model to train both actor and critic entirely inside latent imagination using the RSSM, achieving strong results on the DeepMind Control Suite with a fraction of the real environment interactions needed by model-free baselines.

''DreamerV2'' (2021) replaced continuous latent variables with ''discrete categorical latents'' and used straight-through gradients to backpropagate through them. Discrete latents turned out to be more robust and expressive. DreamerV2 was the first model-based algorithm to reach human-level performance on Atari, a benchmark previously dominated by model-free methods.

''DreamerV3'' (2023) is the most significant leap: a single fixed set of hyperparameters that works across wildly different domains — Atari games, continuous control, 3-D navigation, and Minecraft — without any per-domain tuning. Key innovations include the ''symlog'' (symmetric log) transformation applied to all predictions and targets, which handles the extreme scale differences across tasks; ''free bits'' KL balancing, which prevents the latent from collapsing to the prior too early; and robust return normalization for the critic. DreamerV3 became the first algorithm to collect a diamond in Minecraft from scratch without human demonstrations or domain-specific priors, a long-standing challenge in the community.

---

!! When World Models Break Down

''Compounding model error:'' The world model is imperfect, and small errors in each imagined transition accumulate over long rollouts. After $H$ steps, the imagined state distribution diverges from the true distribution, and policies trained on these hallucinated futures degrade. This is the core tension in model-based RL: longer imagination horizons allow more planning but introduce more bias. Techniques such as short rollout lengths, value function bootstrapping at the end of imagination, and model ensembles (as in MBPO) partially mitigate this, but it remains the field's central limitation.

''Model exploitation (Goodhart's Law in imagination):'' A sufficiently flexible policy can discover regions of the model's latent space where the model incorrectly predicts high reward — artifacts of the learned approximation rather than genuine high-value states. When the agent acts on these exploitable hallucinations, real-world performance collapses. This is analogous to adversarial examples for neural networks. Regularization (e.g., acting conservatively in regions of model uncertainty, epistemic uncertainty estimation) is required but adds complexity.

''Distribution shift and non-stationarity:'' The world model is trained on data collected by past policies. When the current policy explores new regions of the state space, the model has no training experience there and its predictions become unreliable. This is a chicken-and-egg problem: the policy needs a good model to explore well, but the model needs data from good exploration to be accurate. Methods that explicitly track model uncertainty (PETS, MBPO) or interleave real and imagined data carefully (Dyna-style) are needed, but they add hyperparameters and computational cost.

!! Technical Details

''Key papers:'' Ha & Schmidhuber 2018 ("World Models," MDN-RNN + CMA-ES controller); Hafner et al. 2020 ("Dream to Control," Dreamer, continuous RSSM); Hafner et al. 2021 ("Mastering Atari with Discrete World Models," DreamerV2); Hafner et al. 2023 ("Mastering Diverse Domains with World Models," DreamerV3); Schrittwieser et al. 2020 ("Mastering Atari, Go, Chess and Shogi by Planning with a Learned Model," MuZero — an //implicit// world model that does not decode observations but predicts value-relevant quantities directly); Sutton 1991 (Dyna, the original model + real-data mixing framework); Janner et al. 2019 ("When to Trust Your Model," MBPO, short model rollouts + SAC).

''Model-free vs model-based sample efficiency:'' Model-free methods such as SAC typically require $10^6$–$10^7$ environment steps on DMControl. DreamerV3 achieves comparable or superior performance in $10^5$ steps. On Atari, DQN needs $200M$ frames; DreamerV2 reaches human level in $~200M$ but with far fewer //unique// environment frames because most training uses imagined data.

''RSSM internals:'' The deterministic path (GRU recurrence on $h_t$) provides temporal memory; the stochastic path ($z_t$) provides representational diversity and enables proper probabilistic prediction. DreamerV2/V3 use ''straight-through categorical'' latents: a 32×32 grid of 32-class categoricals, giving $32^{32}$ representational capacity with discrete structure that the model finds easier to predict than continuous Gaussians.

''MuZero comparison:'' MuZero does not learn to reconstruct observations; instead it learns a latent model that only predicts policy, value, and reward — quantities directly relevant to planning. This makes it more compact but less interpretable. It achieves state-of-the-art on board games and Atari but requires known action spaces and tree search (MCTS), limiting its applicability compared to Dreamer's purely gradient-based planning.
