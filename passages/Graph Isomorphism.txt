! Graph Isomorphism

<img src="assets/graph_isomorphism.svg" alt="Two graphs G and H with different vertex labels but identical edge structure, connected by a bijection mapping showing they are isomorphic" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you have two road maps of the same city, but one uses street numbers and the other uses street names. The intersections and connections are identical — only the labels differ. ''Graph isomorphism'' asks exactly this question: given two graphs, are they secretly the same graph wearing different name tags? Two graphs are ''isomorphic'' if you can relabel the vertices of one to perfectly match the other, preserving every edge. This deceptively simple question turns out to be one of the most fascinating problems in all of computer science — sitting in a mysterious zone where we cannot prove it is hard, yet no one has found a fast algorithm for it in general.

!! The Core Insight

The central idea is that a graph's ''structure'' — the pattern of connections — is independent of how you label its vertices. Two graphs $G$ and $H$ are isomorphic if there exists a bijection $f: V(G) \to V(H)$ such that vertices $u$ and $v$ are adjacent in $G$ if and only if $f(u)$ and $f(v)$ are adjacent in $H$. This bijection is the isomorphism itself.

The difficulty arises from the search space. For graphs with $n$ vertices, there are $n!$ possible bijections to check. For $n = 20$, that is over $2.4 \times 10^{18}$ possibilities. For $n = 100$, it is astronomically larger than the number of atoms in the universe. The question is whether clever algorithms can avoid brute-force enumeration — and for most practical graphs, they can, using structural invariants as shortcuts.

!! Invariants and the Refinement Strategy

The standard approach to testing isomorphism relies on ''graph invariants'' — properties that must be identical in isomorphic graphs. If any invariant differs, the graphs cannot be isomorphic. Common invariants include:

''Degree sequence:'' Sort the list of vertex degrees. Isomorphic graphs must have the same degree sequence. A graph with degree sequence $[2, 2, 3, 3]$ cannot be isomorphic to one with sequence $[1, 2, 3, 4]$.

''Spectrum:'' The eigenvalues of the [[Graph Laplacian]] or adjacency matrix. Isomorphic graphs have the same spectrum — though non-isomorphic graphs can be ''cospectral'' (same eigenvalues, different structure).

''Weisfeiler-Leman (WL) refinement:'' An iterative algorithm that colors vertices based on their neighborhoods. Initially, all vertices get the same color. Each round updates a vertex's color based on the multiset of its neighbors' colors. After convergence, the multiset of final colors is compared. The 1-WL test distinguishes most non-isomorphic graphs but fails on certain regular graphs. Higher-dimensional $k$-WL variants are more powerful but more expensive, running in $O(n^{k+1})$ time per iteration.

''Substructure counts:'' Number of triangles, cycles of length $k$, cliques of size $k$. These are preserved by isomorphism but expensive to compute for large $k$.

No single polynomial-time invariant is known to distinguish all non-isomorphic graphs. This is what makes the problem so subtle.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Formally, graphs $G = (V_G, E_G)$ and $H = (V_H, E_H)$ are isomorphic ($G \cong H$) if there exists a bijection $f: V_G \to V_H$ satisfying:

$$\{u, v\} \in E_G \iff \{f(u), f(v)\} \in E_H$$

This can be expressed via adjacency matrices. If $A_G$ and $A_H$ are the adjacency matrices of $G$ and $H$, then $G \cong H$ if and only if there exists a permutation matrix $P$ such that:

$$A_H = P \, A_G \, P^T$$

This reformulation converts the problem into a search over the ''symmetric group'' $S_n$ (the group of all $n \times n$ permutation matrices). The ''automorphism group'' $\text{Aut}(G)$ — the set of permutations mapping $G$ to itself — plays a central role. Babai's algorithm exploits the group-theoretic structure of $\text{Aut}(G)$, using the classification of finite simple groups to bound the search.

The ''Weisfeiler-Leman'' test computes iterated color refinements. At step $t$, vertex $v$ receives color:

$$c^{(t)}(v) = \text{hash}\Big(c^{(t-1)}(v),\; \{\!\{ c^{(t-1)}(w) : w \in N(v) \}\!\}\Big)$$

where $\{\!\{ \cdot \}\!\}$ denotes a multiset. If at any step the multiset of colors across $G$ differs from that across $H$, the graphs are not isomorphic. The converse is not guaranteed — WL can fail to distinguish certain non-isomorphic graphs, such as strongly regular graphs with the same parameters.

!! Graph Isomorphism in ML and Applications

Graph isomorphism is deeply connected to modern machine learning through ''graph neural networks'' (GNNs). Xu et al. (2019) proved that standard message-passing GNNs are //at most// as powerful as the 1-WL test for distinguishing graphs. This means GNNs inherit WL's blind spots: if two non-isomorphic graphs produce the same WL coloring, no standard GNN can tell them apart. The [[Graph Isomorphism Network]] was designed to be //maximally// expressive within this 1-WL bound, using sum aggregation and injective update functions.

Beyond ML, graph isomorphism arises in:

''Chemistry:'' Molecules are graphs (atoms = vertices, bonds = edges). Determining whether two molecular structures are identical requires isomorphism testing. Chemical databases like PubChem use canonical graph labeling (a harder problem than isomorphism) to index millions of compounds.

''Network analysis:'' Detecting recurring motifs in biological networks, social networks, and communication networks requires counting subgraph isomorphisms — an NP-complete problem even when the full isomorphism problem is not known to be.

''Compiler optimization:'' Detecting equivalent computation graphs enables reuse, memoization, and optimization in dataflow compilers.

''Cryptography:'' Zero-knowledge proofs based on graph isomorphism allow a prover to demonstrate knowledge of an isomorphism without revealing it — one of the earliest examples of zero-knowledge protocols.

!! When Graph Isomorphism Breaks Down

''1. Highly regular graphs defeat simple invariants.'' Strongly regular graphs — where every vertex has the same degree, every pair of adjacent vertices shares the same number of common neighbors, and every pair of non-adjacent vertices also shares a fixed number — can be non-isomorphic yet indistinguishable by the 1-WL test, spectral methods, and standard GNNs. The Shrikhande graph and the $4 \times 4$ rook's graph are a classic non-isomorphic pair with identical degree sequences, spectra, and WL colorings. Only higher-dimensional WL or explicit structural decomposition can distinguish them.

''2. Subgraph isomorphism is NP-complete.'' While graph isomorphism asks "are these two graphs the same?", ''subgraph isomorphism'' asks "does graph $G$ contain a copy of graph $H$?" This is NP-complete in general (it generalizes Hamiltonian cycle, clique detection, and many other hard problems). Confusing these two problems leads to incorrect assumptions about hardness. Graph isomorphism is believed to be easier — likely not NP-complete — but this is unproven.

''3. Scaling to massive graphs remains impractical for worst cases.'' Babai's quasipolynomial algorithm runs in $e^{O((\log n)^c)}$ time, which is theoretically sub-exponential but can still be enormous for very large graphs. Practical tools like //nauty// and //bliss// are fast on most real-world graphs but exhibit exponential worst-case behavior on pathological inputs (e.g., unions of isomorphic strongly regular graphs). For graphs with millions of vertices — common in social networks and web graphs — even practical solvers can struggle without exploiting domain-specific structure.

!! Technical Details

''Complexity status:'' Graph isomorphism is in NP (a permutation serves as a certificate) and in Co-AM (it has a short interactive proof of non-isomorphism). It is not known to be NP-complete, and strong evidence suggests it is not: if GI is NP-complete, the polynomial hierarchy collapses to its second level (Boppana, Hastad, Zachos, 1987). GI is widely believed to be ''NP-intermediate'' — harder than P but easier than NP-complete — as predicted by Ladner's theorem if $P \neq NP$. See [[P vs NP]] and [[Complexity Classes]].

''Babai's breakthrough (2015):'' Laszlo Babai proved that graph isomorphism can be solved in ''quasipolynomial time'' $e^{O((\log n)^c)}$, the most dramatic progress on the problem in decades. The algorithm uses individualization-refinement combined with deep results from group theory (the classification of finite simple groups). This places GI closer to P than to NP-complete but does not settle the question.

''Practical algorithms:'' McKay's //nauty// (1981, updated) and Junttila-Kaski's //bliss// use canonical labeling via individualization-refinement with pruning. On typical graphs (bounded degree, sparse, planar), they run in nearly linear time. //Traces// (Piperno, 2008) extends these ideas with improved heuristics for difficult graph families.

''Polynomial-time special cases:'' GI is solvable in polynomial time for planar graphs (Hopcroft & Wong, 1974), bounded-degree graphs (Luks, 1982), bounded treewidth graphs, and interval graphs. See [[Planar Graphs]] and [[Graph Coloring]] for related structural restrictions.

''WL hierarchy and GNNs:'' The $k$-WL test runs in $O(n^{k+1})$ time per iteration. Standard message-passing GNNs match 1-WL expressiveness. Higher-order GNNs (e.g., $k$-GNN) match $k$-WL but require $O(n^k)$ memory, limiting scalability.

''Graph canonicalization:'' A harder problem than isomorphism — computing a canonical form $\text{canon}(G)$ such that $G \cong H \iff \text{canon}(G) = \text{canon}(H)$. If you can canonicalize, you can test isomorphism by comparing canonical forms, but the converse is not known.

Related topics: [[Graph Theory]], [[Graph Laplacian]], [[Graph Neural Network]], [[Graph Isomorphism Network]], [[Complexity Classes]].
