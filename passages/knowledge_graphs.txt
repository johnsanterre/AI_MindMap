! Knowledge Graphs
[img[assets/knowledge_graphs.svg]]

Imagine a library where every book, author, publisher, and topic is connected by labeled threads — "wrote," "published_by," "covers_topic" — so that instead of searching an index, you simply follow the threads to discover that Author A wrote Book B which covers Topic C which is also covered by Book D written by Author E. A ''knowledge graph'' is exactly this: a structured network of real-world entities (people, places, concepts) connected by typed, directed relationships. Unlike a flat database table, the graph makes implicit connections explicit and traversable — you do not just store facts, you store //how facts relate to each other//. This is the data structure behind Google's Knowledge Panel, drug discovery pipelines, and the structured reasoning engines that power modern question-answering systems.

---

!! The Core Insight

A knowledge graph stores information as ''triples'': (head entity, relation, tail entity). "Einstein developed General Relativity" becomes the triple //(Einstein, developed, GeneralRelativity)//. Each triple is a directed edge in a graph, and the collection of all triples forms a massive, queryable network. The power is ''compositionality'': by chaining triples, the graph can answer multi-hop questions — "What field does the theory developed by the person born in Germany who won the 1921 Nobel Prize belong to?" — by traversing edges step by step. No single triple contains the full answer, but the //path// through the graph does.

Knowledge graphs differ from plain graphs by having ''typed edges'' (the relation label matters) and ''typed nodes'' (entities have categories). This typing enables schema-level reasoning: if a relation "born_in" connects Person to Location, then any new entity linked by "born_in" is automatically constrained to be a Location.

!! Embedding and Reasoning

Raw knowledge graphs are discrete structures — you cannot do calculus on nodes and edges. ''Knowledge graph embeddings'' solve this by mapping every entity and relation into a continuous vector space where the geometric structure encodes the graph's logical structure.

The foundational model is ''TransE'': it represents each relation $r$ as a translation vector, so that for a true triple $(h, r, t)$, the embedding vectors satisfy $\mathbf{h} + \mathbf{r} \approx \mathbf{t}$. If "Einstein + developed $\approx$ GeneralRelativity" holds in vector space, then the embedding has captured the semantic relationship.

More expressive models followed. ''DistMult'' scores triples using a bilinear product: $f(h, r, t) = \mathbf{h}^T \text{diag}(\mathbf{r}) \, \mathbf{t}$. ''ComplEx'' extends this to complex-valued embeddings to handle antisymmetric relations (where "A teaches B" does not imply "B teaches A"). ''RotatE'' models relations as rotations in complex space: $\mathbf{t} = \mathbf{h} \circ \mathbf{r}$, where $|\mathbf{r}_i| = 1$, elegantly capturing symmetry, antisymmetry, inversion, and composition patterns.

These embeddings enable ''link prediction'': given (Einstein, developed, ?) the model scores all candidate tail entities and ranks them. This is how knowledge graphs complete themselves — inferring missing facts from patterns in existing ones.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

A knowledge graph $\mathcal{G} = \{(h, r, t)\} \subseteq \mathcal{E} \times \mathcal{R} \times \mathcal{E}$ is a set of triples over entities $\mathcal{E}$ and relations $\mathcal{R}$.

''TransE'' learns embeddings $\mathbf{h}, \mathbf{r}, \mathbf{t} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ by minimizing:

$$\mathcal{L} = \sum_{(h,r,t) \in \mathcal{G}} \sum_{(h',r,t') \in \mathcal{G}^{-}} \max\big(0, \; \gamma + d(\mathbf{h}+\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{t}) - d(\mathbf{h'}+\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{t'})\big)$$

where $\mathcal{G}^{-}$ is the set of ''negative samples'' (corrupted triples with head or tail replaced), $d(\cdot)$ is L1 or L2 distance, and $\gamma$ is a margin. The model pushes true triples to have low translation error and corrupted triples to have high error.

''ComplEx'' uses a scoring function over complex embeddings $\mathbf{h}, \mathbf{r}, \mathbf{t} \in \mathbb{C}^d$:

$$f(h, r, t) = \text{Re}\big(\sum_{i=1}^{d} r_i \cdot h_i \cdot \bar{t}_i\big)$$

where $\bar{t}_i$ is the complex conjugate of $t_i$. The asymmetry of complex multiplication ($h \cdot \bar{t} \neq t \cdot \bar{h}$ in general) allows ComplEx to model antisymmetric relations that DistMult cannot.

For ''multi-hop reasoning'', queries like $(Einstein, \text{developed}, ?x), (?x, \text{field\_of}, ?y)$ compose into path queries. Neural approaches embed entire query paths as geometric operations — intersections, unions, and projections — in the embedding space, enabling approximate logical reasoning without symbolic search.

!! Applications

''Search engines:'' Google's Knowledge Graph powers the information panels beside search results. Querying "Einstein" triggers a structured traversal: birth date, birthplace, field, key works, awards — all stored as typed triples and rendered directly.

''Drug discovery:'' biomedical knowledge graphs connect genes, proteins, diseases, and drugs. Link prediction identifies candidate drug-target interactions — "this compound might treat that disease" — by finding missing edges in the graph, dramatically narrowing the experimental search space.

''Question answering:'' knowledge-graph-grounded QA systems parse natural language questions into structured queries over the graph. "Who directed the film starring the actor born in Toronto?" becomes a multi-hop traversal. This provides interpretable, verifiable answers with explicit reasoning paths, unlike purely neural approaches.

''Recommendation systems:'' connecting users, items, attributes, and behaviors in a knowledge graph enables explanations — "recommended because you liked movies by the same director" — and combats the cold-start problem by leveraging entity relationships rather than relying solely on interaction history.

''Fraud detection:'' financial knowledge graphs connect accounts, transactions, merchants, and devices. Suspicious patterns — circular money flows, shared devices across unrelated accounts — emerge as subgraph motifs that are invisible in tabular data.

---

!! When Knowledge Graphs Break Down

''Incompleteness:'' real-world knowledge graphs are always missing facts. Freebase, one of the largest, was estimated to be missing over 70% of person birth dates and 90% of nationality facts. Link prediction helps but introduces probabilistic noise — the graph becomes a mix of observed facts and inferred guesses with varying confidence, and downstream applications must handle this uncertainty.

''Schema rigidity:'' triples impose a fixed relational schema — every fact must be reduced to (entity, relation, entity). But knowledge is often fuzzy, contextual, or higher-order: "Einstein developed General Relativity //while working at the Swiss patent office between 1905 and 1915//" does not reduce cleanly to a single triple. Reification (creating nodes for statements) adds complexity and bloats the graph.

''Entity alignment:'' the same real-world entity appears under different names, spellings, and identifiers across data sources. "Albert Einstein," "A. Einstein," and "Einstein, Albert" must all resolve to the same node. Entity resolution is itself a hard ML problem, and errors propagate through the entire graph — a single mismerge can connect unrelated subgraphs, while missed matches fragment knowledge into disconnected islands.

!! Technical Details

''Major knowledge graphs:'' Wikidata (100M+ items, open), Google Knowledge Graph (500B+ facts, proprietary), DBpedia (extracted from Wikipedia infoboxes), YAGO (merges Wikipedia with WordNet), ConceptNet (commonsense knowledge), UMLS (biomedical).

''Query languages:'' SPARQL is the standard for RDF-based knowledge graphs. Cypher is used with property graph databases like Neo4j. Both support pattern matching, path queries, and aggregation over graph structure.

''Embedding dimensions:'' typical models use $d \in [100, 500]$. TransE is fast and works well for one-to-one relations. DistMult handles symmetric relations. ComplEx and RotatE handle all relation patterns. ConvE applies convolution over reshaped entity-relation embeddings for richer interaction modeling.

''Benchmarks:'' FB15k-237 (Freebase subset, 14,541 entities, 237 relations), WN18RR (WordNet subset, 40,943 entities, 11 relations). Metrics are Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) and Hits@k (fraction of correct entities ranked in top $k$).

''Neuro-symbolic integration:'' modern systems combine neural embeddings with symbolic reasoning rules. Neural link prediction handles soft pattern matching; logical rules (e.g., "if X married Y, then Y married X") enforce hard constraints. This hybrid approach achieves both the flexibility of learned representations and the reliability of logical inference.

''Temporal knowledge graphs'' add time annotations to triples — //(Einstein, awarded, NobelPrize, 1921)// — and models like TTransE and TNTComplEx learn time-aware embeddings to predict facts valid at specific time points.
