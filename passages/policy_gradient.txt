! Policy Gradient

[img[assets/policy_gradient.svg]]

Imagine you are coaching an athlete who has never played tennis before. You cannot hand them a rulebook and expect them to win — instead, you watch them play, note which swings led to points, and tell them to do more of those and less of the others. Over thousands of rallies the athlete gradually learns a style of play that wins. ''Policy gradient'' methods work on exactly this principle: an agent tries actions in the world, observes what reward follows, and directly adjusts the probabilities of its decisions to favor what worked. There is no lookup table, no memorized value for every situation — just a parameterized decision-making strategy that gets nudged, step by step, in the direction of higher reward.

---

!! The Core Insight

Most <<link "Reinforcement Learning" "Reinforcement Learning">><</link>> algorithms learn //indirectly//. They first estimate how good each state or action is (a value function), and then derive a policy from those estimates. Policy gradient methods flip this around: they optimize the policy //directly//, treating it as a differentiable function of parameters $\theta$ and maximizing expected reward with gradient ascent.

A ''policy'' $\pi_\theta(a \mid s)$ is a probability distribution over actions given the current state, parameterized by a neural network with weights $\theta$. When the agent is in state $s$, it samples an action $a$ from this distribution, takes it, and receives a reward. The algorithm then asks: "should I have made this action more likely or less likely?" If the reward was good, push $\theta$ so that $\pi_\theta(a \mid s)$ increases. If the reward was bad, push $\theta$ to decrease it.

The elegant surprise is that this intuition is mathematically exact. The gradient of expected reward with respect to $\theta$ turns out to be computable from sampled trajectories alone — no model of the environment, no dynamic programming, no exhaustive value estimates required. Policy gradient methods handle continuous action spaces naturally, because the policy outputs a distribution (such as a Gaussian) over real-valued actions rather than a discrete set. These properties made them the foundation for modern deep RL in robotics, game playing, and language model alignment via <<link "PPO" "Proximal Policy Optimization">><</link>>.

---

!! REINFORCE and Variance Reduction

The ''REINFORCE algorithm'' (Williams, 1992) is the simplest policy gradient method. Collect a complete episode $\{(s_t, a_t, r_t)\}_{t=0}^T$. Compute discounted returns $G_t = \sum_{k=t}^{T} \gamma^{k-t} r_k$ for each time step. Then update:

$$\theta \leftarrow \theta + \alpha \sum_{t=0}^{T} G_t \, \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)$$

This is an unbiased estimate of the true policy gradient, but it suffers from ''extremely high variance''. A single trajectory is a noisy sample of the world — the same policy run twice will produce wildly different returns. This noise feeds directly into the gradient estimate, making training unstable and slow.

The single most important variance reduction technique is subtracting a ''baseline'' $b(s_t)$ from the return:

$$\hat{g} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{t=0}^{T} \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t) \cdot \left(G_t - b(s_t)\right)$$

Any baseline that does not depend on $a_t$ can be shown to leave the expected gradient unchanged — the extra term has zero expectation — while dramatically reducing variance. The optimal baseline is the state value function $V^\pi(s_t)$, which turns the weighting into the ''advantage'' $A(s_t, a_t) = G_t - V(s_t)$. Now the gradient signal becomes: "was this action better or worse //than average// from this state?" — a much more precise signal than "was the total return high or low?"

Additional variance reduction techniques include: using multiple trajectories per update (batching), normalizing advantages to zero mean and unit variance within each batch, and ''Generalized Advantage Estimation'' (GAE), which interpolates between low-bias Monte Carlo returns and low-variance bootstrapped estimates using a parameter $\lambda$.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The core result is the ''policy gradient theorem''. For a policy $\pi_\theta$ and expected return $J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim \pi_\theta}[R(\tau)]$, the gradient is:

$$\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}\!\left[ \sum_{t=0}^{T} \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t) \cdot G_t \right]$$

The key identity that makes this work is the ''score function trick'' (also called the log-derivative trick). You cannot backpropagate through a random sample, but the identity $\nabla_\theta p(\tau \mid \theta) = p(\tau \mid \theta) \cdot \nabla_\theta \log p(\tau \mid \theta)$ converts the gradient of a probability into an expectation that can be estimated from sampled trajectories.

When you expand $\log p(\tau \mid \theta)$, the environment transition probabilities cancel out, leaving only the policy terms:

$$\nabla_\theta \log p(\tau \mid \theta) = \sum_{t=0}^{T} \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)$$

This is what makes policy gradient methods ''model-free'': you do not need to know or learn the environment's dynamics. The gradient estimate uses only the log-probabilities of actions the policy actually chose.

The term $\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)$ is the ''score function'' — it points in the direction in parameter space that would make action $a_t$ more likely. When multiplied by $G_t$, rewarding actions get amplified and poor actions get suppressed. The full update is equivalent to ''weighted maximum likelihood'': treat each $(s_t, a_t)$ pair as a training example with weight $G_t$.

The variance of the REINFORCE estimator scales roughly as $T^2 \cdot \text{Var}[r]$ where $T$ is the horizon. Adding a baseline reduces this to approximately $T \cdot \text{Var}[A]$. GAE further reduces it by trading a small amount of bias for much lower variance.

---

!! Actor-Critic Methods

Pure REINFORCE must wait until the end of an episode to compute $G_t$, since it needs all future rewards. ''Actor-critic'' methods break this constraint by learning a ''critic'' $V_\phi(s)$ that estimates future return, allowing updates at every time step rather than only at episode boundaries.

The ''actor'' is the policy $\pi_\theta$ — the decision maker. The ''critic'' is a value function $V_\phi(s)$ — the evaluator. At each step the critic provides a bootstrapped estimate of future reward, and the actor updates using the ''TD advantage'':

$$\hat{A}_t = r_t + \gamma V_\phi(s_{t+1}) - V_\phi(s_t)$$

This replaces the Monte Carlo return $G_t$ with a one-step estimate, trading some bias for much lower variance and enabling online (non-episodic) learning. The critic is trained to minimize $\|V_\phi(s_t) - G_t\|^2$ or its bootstrapped equivalent.

Modern actor-critic algorithms build on this foundation. ''A2C/A3C'' (Mnih et al., 2016) run multiple parallel environments for data diversity. ''TRPO'' (Schulman et al., 2015) constrains policy updates via a KL divergence trust region. <<link "PPO" "Proximal Policy Optimization">><</link>> (Schulman et al., 2017) replaces the TRPO constraint with a clipped surrogate objective, becoming the default algorithm for most applications. <<link "Soft Actor-Critic" "Soft Actor-Critic">><</link>> adds an entropy bonus to produce stochastic policies for continuous control. All of these are policy gradient methods at their core — they differ only in how they estimate advantages and constrain updates.

---

!! When Policy Gradients Break Down

''Catastrophic variance in sparse-reward environments.'' When rewards are rare — a robot gets +1 only upon task completion, zero otherwise — most sampled trajectories return $G_t = 0$ for every time step. The gradient estimate is almost always zero, and the rare nonzero estimates are extremely noisy because a single lucky trajectory dominates the signal. Training stalls for tens of thousands of episodes. The fix is reward shaping (adding dense intermediate rewards), intrinsic motivation (rewarding novelty or prediction error), or hierarchical decomposition of the task.

''Policy collapse from large updates.'' Unlike value-based methods, policy gradient directly changes the probability distribution over actions. A single large gradient step can make the policy near-deterministic, collapsing all probability mass onto one action. Once collapsed, the policy stops exploring, the gradient estimate becomes even noisier (because all trajectories look identical), and recovery is very slow. The standard remedies are clipping updates (as in <<link "PPO" "Proximal Policy Optimization">><</link>>) or imposing a KL divergence constraint on how much the policy can change per step (as in TRPO).

''Reward hacking through degenerate strategies.'' Policy gradients optimize the //exact// objective you specify. If that objective has an unintended shortcut — a way to get high reward without actually solving the task — the policy will find it. A famous example: a boat-racing agent learned to spin in circles collecting powerup tokens rather than finishing the race, because the powerups gave more reward per second than crossing the finish line. Unlike value-based methods, which have some implicit smoothing through the Bellman equation, policy gradients will exploit any gap between your reward function and your true intent.

---

!! Technical Details

''REINFORCE (Williams, 1992).'' The foundational algorithm. On-policy, unbiased, but high variance. Requires full episode completion before any update (Monte Carlo, not TD). Update: $\theta \leftarrow \theta + \alpha \sum_t G_t \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)$.

''Natural Policy Gradient (Kakade, 2002).'' Preconditions the gradient by the inverse Fisher information matrix $F(\theta)^{-1}$, giving updates that move a fixed KL distance in distribution space regardless of parameterization: $\theta \leftarrow \theta + \alpha F(\theta)^{-1} \nabla_\theta J(\theta)$. More stable and efficient than vanilla gradient ascent.

''TRPO (Schulman et al., 2015).'' Adds a hard KL constraint: maximize $J(\theta)$ subject to $\mathbb{E}_s[\text{KL}[\pi_{\theta_\text{old}} \| \pi_\theta]] \leq \delta$. Solved via conjugate gradient and a line search. Guarantees monotonic improvement under certain conditions. Largely superseded by <<link "PPO" "Proximal Policy Optimization">><</link>> in practice.

''<<link "PPO" "Proximal Policy Optimization">><</link>> (Schulman et al., 2017).'' Replaces the TRPO constraint with a clipped surrogate objective: $L^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_t[\min(r_t \hat{A}_t, \text{clip}(r_t, 1-\varepsilon, 1+\varepsilon)\hat{A}_t)]$ where $r_t = \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t) / \pi_{\theta_\text{old}}(a_t \mid s_t)$. First-order method; the current default policy gradient algorithm across most domains.

''Connection to <<link "Soft Actor-Critic" "Soft Actor-Critic">><</link>>.'' SAC augments the policy gradient objective with an entropy bonus $\alpha \mathcal{H}(\pi)$, producing stochastic policies that maintain exploration. It operates off-policy with a replay buffer, giving 5-10x better sample efficiency than on-policy methods like PPO on continuous control benchmarks.

''On-policy vs. off-policy.'' Vanilla policy gradients and PPO are on-policy: data is discarded after each update. This is sample-inefficient but avoids distribution mismatch. Off-policy corrections (importance sampling) allow reuse of old data but introduce their own variance. SAC sidesteps this with the maximum entropy framework.
