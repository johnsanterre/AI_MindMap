! P vs NP
<img src="assets/p_vs_np.svg" alt="P vs NP Venn diagram showing P subset of NP subset of PSPACE">

Imagine you are given a massive jigsaw puzzle. Solving it from scratch — finding where every piece goes — could take you hours or even days. But if someone hands you a completed puzzle, you can glance at it and //verify// in seconds that every piece fits. That gap between the difficulty of ''solving'' a problem versus ''checking'' a proposed solution is the heart of the P vs NP question, arguably the most important unsolved problem in all of computer science and mathematics.

!! The Core Insight

''P'' is the class of problems a computer can ''solve'' efficiently — in ''polynomial time'' (time that grows as $n^k$ for some fixed $k$ as input size $n$ increases). ''NP'' is the class of problems where a proposed solution can be ''verified'' efficiently, even if finding that solution might be astronomically hard. The P vs NP question asks: //is every problem whose solution can be quickly checked also quickly solvable?// Most computer scientists believe the answer is no — that P $\neq$ NP — but nobody has been able to prove it. This is one of the seven Clay Mathematics Institute ''Millennium Prize Problems'', carrying a $1,000,000 reward.

!! Polynomial Time and Verification

A problem is in ''P'' if there exists an algorithm that solves it in time $O(n^k)$ for some constant $k$. Sorting a list, finding shortest paths, and testing whether a number is prime all live in P. A problem is in ''NP'' (''nondeterministic polynomial time'') if, given a candidate solution (called a ''certificate'' or ''witness''), you can verify its correctness in polynomial time. For example, the ''Boolean satisfiability problem'' ([[Satisfiability|Satisfiability]]) asks whether a logical formula can be made true. Finding a satisfying assignment may be hard, but //checking// one is easy — just plug in the values and evaluate. Every problem in P is automatically in NP, because if you can //solve// it quickly, you can certainly //verify// a solution quickly. So $P \subseteq NP$. The open question is whether $NP \subseteq P$ as well, which would make $P = NP$.

!! NP-Completeness and Cook-Levin

In 1971, Stephen Cook (and independently Leonid Levin) proved a stunning result: the Boolean satisfiability problem (SAT) is ''NP-complete''. A problem is [[NP-Completeness|NP-complete]] if it is in NP //and// every other NP problem can be ''reduced'' to it in polynomial time. This means SAT is at least as hard as every problem in NP. If you could solve SAT efficiently, you could solve //every// NP problem efficiently, and P would equal NP.

Richard Karp then showed that 21 classic problems — including graph coloring, the traveling salesman problem, and integer programming — are also NP-complete. Today, thousands of NP-complete problems are known. They are all computationally equivalent in a deep sense: solve any one of them efficiently and you solve all of them.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

A ''polynomial-time reduction'' from problem $A$ to problem $B$ is a function $f$ computable in polynomial time such that:

$$x \in A \iff f(x) \in B$$

This means an efficient solver for $B$ immediately gives an efficient solver for $A$. The class NP can be defined formally:

$$NP = \{L \mid \exists \text{ polynomial } p, \text{ polynomial-time verifier } V : x \in L \iff \exists w, |w| \leq p(|x|), V(x,w) = 1\}$$

The ''time hierarchy theorem'' tells us that more time strictly gives more power: $\text{DTIME}(n^k) \subsetneq \text{DTIME}(n^{k+1})$. But the gap between P and NP involves a qualitative shift — from deterministic to nondeterministic computation — and closing that gap has resisted every known technique.

The broader landscape of [[Complexity Classes|Complexity Classes]] includes:
- $P \subseteq NP \subseteq PSPACE \subseteq EXPTIME$
- Whether any of these inclusions is strict (other than $P \neq EXPTIME$, which is known) remains open

!! Implications for Cryptography and ML

If $P = NP$, modern ''cryptography'' would collapse. Public-key cryptosystems like RSA rely on the assumption that factoring large integers is not in P. If someone proved $P = NP$ constructively — providing an actual algorithm — every encrypted message on the internet could be broken.

In ''machine learning'', many core optimization problems are ''NP-hard'' (at least as hard as NP-complete problems):
- Training a neural network to global optimality is NP-hard in general
- Feature selection (finding the best subset of $k$ features) is NP-hard
- Finding optimal decision trees is NP-hard
- Exact inference in Bayesian networks is NP-hard

This is why ML relies heavily on ''heuristics'' and ''approximation algorithms'' — gradient descent does not find global optima, but in practice it finds solutions good enough to be useful. The [[Big O Notation|Big O Notation]] framework helps us analyze whether these approximate methods remain tractable. The remarkable effectiveness of gradient descent on high-dimensional non-convex landscapes is one of the great empirical mysteries of deep learning, and understanding it connects to deep questions about the structure of loss surfaces.

!! When P vs NP Breaks Down

<strong>1. Average-case vs worst-case:</strong> NP-hardness is a ''worst-case'' statement. A problem might be hard on pathological inputs but easy on typical ones. Many NP-complete problems (like random SAT instances) have phase transitions where difficulty concentrates at specific parameter ratios, while most instances are easy.

<strong>2. Approximation muddies the picture:</strong> Some NP-hard problems have efficient ''approximation algorithms'' that get provably close to optimal. The traveling salesman problem on metric spaces can be approximated within a factor of 1.5 in polynomial time. But other NP-hard problems are hard even to approximate (unless $P = NP$), a result formalized by the PCP theorem.

<strong>3. Non-uniform and quantum models:</strong> The P vs NP question is about classical [[Turing Machines|Turing Machines]]. Quantum computers occupy a strange middle ground — they can solve some problems (like factoring) faster than any known classical algorithm, but there is no evidence they can solve NP-complete problems efficiently. The class BQP (bounded-error quantum polynomial time) is believed to be incomparable with NP.

!! Technical Details

''Ladner's Theorem'' (1975): If $P \neq NP$, then there exist problems in NP that are neither in P nor NP-complete. These ''NP-intermediate'' problems would live in the gap. Graph isomorphism and factoring are suspected to be NP-intermediate.

''Relativization barrier'' (Baker-Gill-Solovay, 1975): There exist oracles $A$ and $B$ such that $P^A = NP^A$ and $P^B \neq NP^B$. This means no proof technique that ''relativizes'' (works the same with any oracle) can resolve P vs NP.

''Natural proofs barrier'' (Razborov-Rudich, 1997): Under plausible cryptographic assumptions, a large class of proof techniques called "natural proofs" cannot prove $P \neq NP$.

''Algebrization barrier'' (Aaronson-Wigderson, 2009): An extension of the relativization barrier showing that even algebraic tricks do not suffice. Resolving P vs NP will require fundamentally new mathematical ideas.

The strongest known lower bound for any NP problem on a deterministic multitape Turing machine is $\Omega(n \cdot \log^* n)$ — barely above linear — highlighting how far we are from proving $P \neq NP$.
