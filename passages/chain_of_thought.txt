! Chain of Thought

<img src="assets/chain_of_thought.svg" alt="Diagram illustrating Chain of Thought prompting vs direct answering" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Good math teachers have always known something that took AI researchers until 2022 to rediscover: students who show their work catch more of their own mistakes. When a student is forced to write down "Step 1: identify the rate, Step 2: multiply by time," they cannot skip past the part where they get confused — the act of writing each step forces a reckoning with whether it is actually correct. ''Chain of Thought'' prompting applies the same insight to language models. Rather than asking a model to jump from question to answer in a single leap, you ask it to narrate its reasoning along the way. The trigger can be as simple as appending //"Let's think step by step"// to your prompt. That small addition can turn a wrong answer into a correct one — but only if the model is large enough to take the hint.

---

!! The Core Insight

In a landmark 2022 paper, Wei et al. demonstrated that prompting large language models with a few examples that include intermediate reasoning steps — rather than just question-answer pairs — dramatically improved performance on multi-step arithmetic, commonsense reasoning, and symbolic manipulation tasks. The improvement was not marginal: on the GSM8K grade-school math benchmark, chain of thought pushed accuracy from around 18% to over 57% for the largest models. Kojima et al. showed the same year that you do not even need reasoning examples — appending the phrase //"Let's think step by step"// to any question (zero-shot) produces much of the same benefit.

The most striking finding is the ''scale threshold''. For models with fewer than roughly 100 billion parameters, chain of thought prompting has little effect and can even slightly hurt performance. The capability appears to be an emergent property — it simply does not exist below a certain model size, then appears relatively suddenly as scale increases. This has deep implications: techniques evaluated on smaller models may give misleading predictions about large-model behavior. See [[Large Language Models]] for more on emergence, and [[In-Context Learning]] for the broader context of learning from examples at inference time.

The mechanism is straightforward to describe but surprisingly powerful in practice. By generating intermediate tokens before the final answer, the model is given additional "computation time" proportional to the length of its reasoning trace. Each generated token is fed back in as context for the next, allowing the model to build up partial results and refer back to them. A model that cannot compute "60 × 2.5" in a single forward pass can do so reliably if it first writes "60 × 2 = 120, plus 60 × 0.5 = 30, so 150." See also [[Prompt Engineering]] and [[GPT Architecture]] for how this fits into the broader inference picture.

---

!! Variants of Chain of Thought

''Few-shot CoT'' is the original formulation from Wei et al. You include several worked examples in the prompt, each showing a question followed by a step-by-step reasoning chain and then the final answer. The model learns the format from these demonstrations and applies it to new questions. The quality of the examples matters: poorly structured reasoning chains in the prompt produce poorly structured chains in the output.

''Zero-shot CoT'', introduced by Kojima et al., drops the examples entirely and simply appends //"Let's think step by step"// (or similar phrases like //"Let's work through this carefully"//). Remarkably, this often approaches the performance of few-shot CoT at large scale. The phrase acts as a trigger that shifts the model from direct-answer mode into deliberate-reasoning mode. It requires no example curation, making it cheap to apply.

''Self-Consistency'' (Wang et al. 2022) addresses a weakness of greedy decoding: a single reasoning chain can go wrong in a single step and produce a wrong answer. Self-consistency samples $K$ independent chains (e.g., $K = 40$) using temperature sampling, then takes the majority vote over the final answers. Because different chains make different errors, the correct answer tends to win. Formally, if the probability of any individual chain reaching the correct answer is $p$, majority vote over $K$ chains converges to near-certainty as $K$ grows. The tradeoff is obvious: $K$ times the inference cost.

''Tree of Thought'' (Yao et al. 2023) generalizes chains into trees. Rather than generating one linear trace, the model explores multiple branching continuations at each step, evaluates which branches look promising, and backtracks from dead ends using beam search or BFS/DFS. This is substantially more powerful for problems with a structured search space (e.g., game playing, planning) but requires many more model calls.

''Program of Thought'' / ''PAL'' (Program-Aided Language models, Gao et al. 2023) replaces natural-language reasoning steps with code. The model generates a Python (or similar) program, which is then executed by an actual interpreter to get the final answer. This sidesteps arithmetic errors entirely — the model only needs to correctly set up the computation; the computer handles the arithmetic. It works extremely well for numerical tasks and fails on tasks that cannot be expressed as programs.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The core computational argument is about ''serial depth''. A transformer's residual stream performs a fixed, bounded amount of computation per token in the sequence. Roughly speaking, each transformer layer can be thought of as one step of a computation graph. If a problem requires $k$ sequential reasoning steps — where each step depends on the result of the previous — then the model needs at least $k$ layers of sequential processing to solve it.

For problems that exceed this depth, a single forward pass cannot solve the problem correctly regardless of the model's width or the number of parameters. Chain of thought externalizes the intermediate steps as generated tokens, so the "depth" of available computation scales with the length of the reasoning trace:

$$\text{Required reasoning depth} \leq \text{tokens generated before final answer}$$

This connects to circuit complexity theory. Some Boolean functions cannot be computed by constant-depth circuits of polynomial size — they require circuits whose depth scales with the input size. A transformer without chain of thought is analogous to a constant-depth circuit; chain of thought adds the missing depth by routing intermediate results through the token stream.

A complementary view: each generated token is inserted back into the context window and becomes input for the next token's prediction. The model can attend to its own previous reasoning steps. This is a form of ''iterative refinement'' — the model reads its own work and continues from wherever it left off. The price is that errors also propagate: a wrong intermediate step will be attended to by subsequent steps, potentially cascading.

---

!! When Chain of Thought Helps vs Hurts

Chain of thought provides the largest gains on tasks that are ''inherently multi-step'': grade-school and competition math, symbolic manipulation (sorting lists of letters, tracking object positions), formal logic, and science questions that require unit conversion or multi-variable calculation. On these tasks, gains of 20–40 percentage points are common at large scale.

For tasks the model can already solve reliably — simple factual retrieval, sentiment classification, single-hop question answering — chain of thought is neutral at best and introduces unnecessary verbosity. The model may also generate confident-looking but wrong intermediate steps on tasks outside its knowledge, creating a false appearance of careful reasoning.

A significant open question is the ''faithfulness'' of reasoning chains. Do the generated steps actually reflect the internal computation the model used, or are they a plausible post-hoc rationalization constructed to accompany an answer the model had already effectively committed to? Several studies have shown cases of unfaithfulness: perturbing intermediate steps (e.g., inserting a false statement) sometimes does not change the final answer, suggesting the model ignores its own chain; conversely, some models give different answers depending on what reasoning chain they happen to generate first, even when the steps are equivalent. The chain may be better understood as a //learned output format// that //correlates with// correct computation rather than directly //causing// it.

---

!! When Chain of Thought Breaks Down

''Cascading errors:'' Because each step is conditioned on all previous steps, a single error early in the reasoning chain compounds. If the model incorrectly writes "60 × 2 = 100" in step one, every subsequent step builds on a wrong foundation and the final answer will almost certainly be wrong. Greedy decoding makes this worse — the model commits to each token and cannot revise. Self-consistency partially mitigates this by sampling multiple chains, but a systematic error (one that the model makes reliably, not randomly) will dominate even majority voting.

''Unfaithful rationalization:'' On certain tasks, models generate reasoning chains that look logically valid but do not reflect the actual basis for the answer. Research has shown that models can sometimes arrive at the correct answer through a memorized retrieval path while generating a fabricated algebraic derivation as the "reasoning." Conversely, models have been caught changing their stated reasoning when social pressure is simulated (e.g., a human disagrees), while keeping the same answer — suggesting the chain is not a reliable window into the model's computation. This makes chain of thought unreliable as an interpretability or audit tool.

''Cost and latency at inference time:'' Generating a 200-token reasoning chain before a 5-token answer increases inference cost and latency by roughly 40×. Self-consistency with $K=40$ chains multiplies this further. For latency-sensitive applications — real-time dialogue, high-throughput classification — chain of thought may be impractical. Tree of Thought compounds this to an even greater degree, requiring dozens to hundreds of model calls per query. The tradeoff between reasoning quality and computational cost is not yet solved: distillation (training a smaller model to mimic chain of thought outputs) helps but does not fully close the gap.

!! Technical Details

''Foundational papers:'' Wei et al. 2022 ("Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models") introduced few-shot CoT and established the scale threshold. Kojima et al. 2022 ("Large Language Models are Zero-Shot Reasoners") introduced the zero-shot trigger phrase. Wang et al. 2022 ("Self-Consistency Improves Chain of Thought Reasoning in Language Models") introduced the majority-vote decoding strategy. Yao et al. 2023 ("Tree of Thoughts: Deliberate Problem Solving with Large Language Models") introduced the branching search framework. Gao et al. 2023 ("PAL: Program-aided Language Models") introduced code-based reasoning.

''Benchmarks:'' GSM8K (8,500 grade-school math word problems), MATH (competition math, 5 difficulty levels), BIG-Bench Hard (BBH, 23 tasks where standard prompting performs near-random), ARC Challenge (science questions requiring multi-step reasoning), StrategyQA (implicit multi-hop reasoning over Wikipedia facts).

''Scale threshold:'' The emergent benefit appears at roughly 100B parameters in the original studies (PaLM 540B, GPT-3 175B). With instruction tuning and RLHF, smaller models (7B–70B range) can exhibit CoT behavior, suggesting the threshold is partly about //training// to reason step-by-step, not only raw parameter count. Models like GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini are trained with CoT examples and exhibit strong zero-shot reasoning at much smaller effective inference budgets.

''Interaction with instruction tuning:'' Instruction-tuned models (trained on human feedback and instruction-following datasets) tend to produce chain of thought reasoning spontaneously without explicit prompting. RLHF further reinforces producing reasoning traces when humans reward detailed explanations. This blurs the line between "prompting technique" and "model capability" — for frontier models, CoT is often the default behavior rather than a special prompt.

''Faithfulness research:'' Turpin et al. 2023 showed that CoT reasoning can be systematically biased by irrelevant features in the prompt (e.g., the position of the correct answer choice) without that bias appearing in the stated reasoning. Lanham et al. 2023 found that paraphrasing or omitting intermediate steps changed final answers less than expected for many tasks, challenging the assumption that the chain is computationally load-bearing.
