! Model Auditing

<img src="assets/model_auditing.svg" alt="Diagram showing the model auditing pipeline: bias testing, performance audit, safety probes, audit report, and pass/fail decision" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Think of a restaurant health inspection. The inspector does not design the kitchen or write the menu — they arrive with a checklist, test specific things (food temperature, hand-washing stations, pest evidence), and issue a rating. Model auditing works the same way: an independent evaluator systematically probes an AI system against a set of criteria — bias, fairness, safety, calibration, regulatory compliance — and produces a structured report that says where the system passes, where it fails, and what must change before deployment.

---

!! The Core Insight

''Model auditing'' is the practice of systematically evaluating a trained AI system to determine whether it behaves acceptably across dimensions that matter to stakeholders: affected communities, regulators, and the organization deploying the model. Unlike standard model evaluation, which asks "how accurate is this?", auditing asks "is this //fair//? Is it //safe//? Does it //comply// with the law?"

The core tension in auditing is that no single metric captures "acceptable." A model can be highly accurate overall while exhibiting wildly different error rates for different demographic groups. A hiring model might have 90% accuracy overall but reject qualified women at twice the rate of qualified men. Standard aggregate metrics hide this. Auditing forces disaggregated evaluation — slicing performance by protected attributes like race, gender, age, and socioeconomic status — so that hidden harms become visible.

Modern auditing draws from three traditions: algorithmic fairness research (Barocas & Selbst, 2016), software testing and verification, and financial/regulatory audit practice. The EU AI Act (2024) and the NIST AI Risk Management Framework (2023) now formalize auditing requirements for high-risk AI systems, making this a legal obligation in many jurisdictions, not merely a best practice. !! Fairness Criteria and the Impossibility Landscape

The first challenge any auditor faces is choosing which fairness criterion to evaluate. The three most common are:

''Demographic parity'' (also called statistical parity) requires that the model's positive prediction rate be equal across groups: $P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid A=0) = P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid A=1)$, where $A$ is a protected attribute. This is the simplest criterion but the most controversial — it ignores base rates and can require accepting less-qualified candidates in one group over more-qualified candidates in another.

''Equalized odds'' (Hardt et al., 2016) requires that the true positive rate //and// false positive rate be equal across groups: $P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid Y=1, A=a)$ and $P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid Y=0, A=a)$ are the same for all values of $a$. This is stronger than demographic parity because it conditions on the true label.

''Predictive parity'' requires that the positive predictive value be equal across groups: $P(Y=1 \mid \hat{Y}=1, A=a)$ is the same for all $a$. A model that passes this criterion is equally trustworthy when it says "yes" regardless of group membership.

The ''impossibility theorem'' (Chouldechova, 2017; Kleinberg et al., 2016) proves that when base rates differ across groups, a model //cannot// simultaneously satisfy equalized odds and predictive parity (except in trivial cases). This means every audit involves a normative choice about which fairness criterion matters most in a given context — a decision that is ethical and political, not purely technical. Auditors must document which criterion they selected and why.

Beyond individual fairness metrics, audits also examine ''disparate impact'': the ratio of positive outcome rates between groups. The "four-fifths rule" from US employment law states that a selection rate for a protected group below 80% of the highest group's rate constitutes evidence of adverse impact. For a model: $\text{DI} = \frac{P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid A=0)}{P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid A=1)} \geq 0.8$. Related: <<link "Class Imbalance" "Class Imbalance">><</link>>, <<link "Differential Privacy" "Differential Privacy">><</link>>.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

An audit computes fairness metrics by partitioning the test set into demographic slices and measuring performance within each slice. For a binary classifier with predictions $\hat{Y}$, true labels $Y$, and protected attribute $A \in \{0, 1\}$:

''Equalized odds gap:''

$$\Delta_{\text{EO}} = \max\!\Big(\big|TPR_{A=0} - TPR_{A=1}\big|,\; \big|FPR_{A=0} - FPR_{A=1}\big|\Big)$$

where $TPR_a = P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid Y=1, A=a)$ and $FPR_a = P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid Y=0, A=a)$.

''Expected calibration error'' (ECE) measures whether the model's predicted probabilities match observed frequencies, disaggregated by group:

$$\text{ECE}_a = \sum_{b=1}^{B} \frac{|B_b^{(a)}|}{n_a} \left|\text{acc}(B_b^{(a)}) - \text{conf}(B_b^{(a)})\right|$$

where $B_b^{(a)}$ is the set of predictions in confidence bin $b$ for group $a$, $\text{acc}$ is the observed accuracy, and $\text{conf}$ is the mean predicted probability.

''Disparate impact ratio:''

$$\text{DI} = \frac{P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid A=0)}{P(\hat{Y}=1 \mid A=1)}$$

Values below 0.8 or above 1.25 are red flags under the four-fifths rule.

For regression tasks, audits replace classification metrics with group-disaggregated ''mean absolute error'' or ''quantile loss'':

$$\Delta_{\text{MAE}} = \left|\frac{1}{n_0}\sum_{i:A_i=0}|y_i - \hat{y}_i| - \frac{1}{n_1}\sum_{i:A_i=1}|y_i - \hat{y}_i|\right|$$

Statistical significance of disparities is assessed using permutation tests or bootstrap confidence intervals rather than relying on point estimates alone.

!! Conducting an Audit in Practice

A model audit typically follows a structured protocol:

''Step 1 — Scope definition.'' The auditor identifies which model, which version, which deployment context, and which stakeholders are in scope. A credit-scoring model deployed in the US has different regulatory requirements than a content-recommendation model in the EU.

''Step 2 — Data collection.'' The auditor obtains a representative test dataset with demographic annotations. If the deployment data lacks demographic labels (common in practice), the auditor may use proxy variables, synthetic data, or name/zip-code-based imputation — each with known limitations. The test set must be //independent// of the training data.

''Step 3 — Metric computation.'' The auditor computes disaggregated performance metrics (accuracy, precision, recall, AUC, calibration) for each demographic slice, plus the fairness metrics appropriate to the context. Visualization tools like calibration curves, ROC curves per group, and confusion matrix heatmaps are standard deliverables.

''Step 4 — Red teaming and stress testing.'' Beyond statistical metrics, the auditor probes the model with adversarial and edge-case inputs to test safety and robustness. This overlaps with <<link "Red Teaming" "Red Teaming">><</link>> and <<link "Adversarial Examples" "Adversarial Examples">><</link>>.

''Step 5 — Report and remediation.'' The auditor produces a structured report documenting findings, severity ratings, and recommended mitigations — which may include retraining with balanced data, applying post-hoc threshold adjustments, or declining to deploy. The report is a governance artifact that regulators, legal teams, and affected communities can inspect.

Tools commonly used: Aequitas, Fairlearn, Google What-If Tool, IBM AI Fairness 360. For LLM audits: HELM (Stanford), DecodingTrust, and custom red-team frameworks.

---

!! When Model Auditing Breaks Down

''Missing demographic data:'' The most common failure mode. If the test set has no demographic labels, the auditor cannot compute group-disaggregated metrics. Proxy methods (inferring race from zip codes, gender from names) introduce their own biases and legal risks. Without ground-truth group membership, the audit is measuring proxies of proxies, and genuine disparities can be hidden or fabricated. Organizations that do not collect demographic data — sometimes out of well-intentioned privacy concerns — make meaningful auditing impossible.

''Metric selection bias:'' Because multiple fairness criteria are mathematically incompatible (the impossibility theorem), a dishonest or naive auditor can choose the one metric the model happens to pass and ignore the ones it fails. A model that satisfies demographic parity may violate equalized odds; reporting only the former gives a misleading picture. Without clear regulatory guidance on which metric applies, audit shopping — choosing the most favorable evaluation framework — is a real risk.

''Static audit, dynamic deployment:'' An audit evaluates the model at a point in time on a fixed test set. But deployed models encounter distributional shift: the population changes, user behavior adapts, feedback loops alter the data pipeline. A model that passed an audit six months ago may now exhibit significant bias due to <<link "Concept Drift" "Concept Drift">><</link>>. Continuous monitoring — not just one-time auditing — is necessary but rarely mandated.

!! Technical Details

''Key references:'' Raji et al. (2020), "Closing the AI Accountability Gap: Defining an End-to-End Framework for Internal Algorithmic Auditing." Barocas & Selbst (2016), "Big Data's Disparate Impact." Chouldechova (2017), "Fair prediction with disparate impact." Kleinberg, Mullainathan & Raghavan (2016), "Inherent Trade-Offs in the Fair Determination of Risk Scores." Mitchell et al. (2019), "Model Cards for Model Reporting."

''Regulatory landscape (2024-2025):'' The EU AI Act requires conformity assessments for high-risk AI systems, including bias testing and documentation. The NIST AI RMF provides a voluntary framework widely adopted in the US. NYC Local Law 144 requires annual bias audits of automated employment decision tools. The EEOC has issued guidance applying Title VII disparate impact analysis to AI hiring tools.

''Model cards and datasheets:'' Model Cards (Mitchell et al., 2019) and Datasheets for Datasets (Gebru et al., 2021) are standardized documentation formats that audits can reference and populate. A model card reports intended use, performance across demographic groups, and known limitations.

''Post-hoc fairness adjustments:'' When an audit reveals disparities, common mitigations include threshold adjustment (different classification thresholds per group to equalize error rates), reweighting training examples, and adversarial debiasing. Each has trade-offs: threshold adjustment may reduce overall accuracy; reweighting may not address representation gaps in features.

''Intersectionality:'' Auditing by single attributes (race alone, gender alone) can miss harms concentrated at intersections (e.g., Black women). Buolamwini & Gebru (2018) demonstrated that facial recognition error rates were highest for dark-skinned women — a disparity invisible when analyzing race and gender separately. Thorough audits must examine intersectional subgroups despite the combinatorial explosion in slice count.

''Connection to interpretability:'' Auditors often need to explain //why// a model produces disparate outcomes, not just that it does. Techniques from <<link "Mechanistic Interpretability" "Mechanistic Interpretability">><</link>> and feature attribution methods (SHAP, LIME) are used to identify which features drive disparities.
