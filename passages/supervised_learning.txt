! Supervised Learning

[img[assets/supervised_learning.svg]]

Imagine a child learning to name animals by pointing at pictures while a parent says "that's a cat" or "that's a dog." Each example comes paired with the right answer. Over time, the child builds an internal model that can identify animals in photos she has never seen before. ''Supervised learning'' works the same way: you give an algorithm a collection of input-output pairs — the inputs are data, the outputs are the correct answers — and the algorithm learns a function that maps new, unseen inputs to accurate outputs. It is the most widely used paradigm in machine learning and the foundation behind everything from spam filters to medical image diagnosis.

---

!! The Core Insight

The central idea of supervised learning is deceptively simple: given a dataset of labeled examples $\{(x_1, y_1), (x_2, y_2), \ldots, (x_n, y_n)\}$, find a function $f$ such that $f(x_i) \approx y_i$ for training data and, crucially, for //new data the model has never seen//. The word "supervised" refers to those labels $y_i$ — they act as a teacher, telling the algorithm exactly what the correct answer should be for each example.

What makes this hard is that memorizing the training set is easy but useless. A lookup table achieves perfect training accuracy yet fails on anything new. The real challenge is ''generalization'': the model must extract underlying patterns rather than memorize specific examples. This tension between fitting the training data and generalizing to new data is the central drama of supervised learning, formalized through the [[Bias-Variance Tradeoff]].

Every supervised learning method — linear regression, decision trees, neural networks, support vector machines — is a different answer to the same question: what family of functions should we search through, and how should we pick the best one?

---

!! Classification vs Regression

Supervised learning splits into two major branches based on what the labels $y_i$ look like.

''Classification'' is the task when labels are //discrete categories//. Is this email spam or not? Is this image a cat, dog, or bird? Is this tumor malignant or benign? The model outputs a class label (and often a probability for each class). The simplest case is ''binary classification'' (two classes); ''multiclass classification'' handles three or more categories. Algorithms like logistic regression, random forests, and softmax-output neural networks are classification workhorses. Success is measured by metrics like accuracy, precision, recall, and the F1 score.

''Regression'' is the task when labels are //continuous numbers//. What will the temperature be tomorrow? How much will this house sell for? What is this patient's blood pressure? The model outputs a real number. Linear regression is the simplest example; neural networks with a single output neuron and no final activation are a flexible alternative. Success is measured by metrics like mean squared error and mean absolute error.

The boundary between classification and regression is not always sharp. Ordinal regression deals with ordered categories. Quantile regression predicts a distribution rather than a point estimate. But the classification-versus-regression distinction remains the primary organizing principle for supervised methods and their <<link "Loss Function" "Loss Function">><</link>> choices.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Formally, supervised learning seeks a function $f: \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Y}$ from a hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}$ that minimizes the ''expected risk'':

$$R(f) = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim P}[\ell(f(x), y)]$$

where $\ell$ is a <<link "loss function" "Loss Function">><</link>> and $P$ is the true (unknown) data distribution. Since we cannot compute this expectation directly — we do not know $P$ — we minimize the ''empirical risk'' over the training set instead:

$$\hat{R}(f) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \ell(f(x_i), y_i)$$

For regression with squared loss, this becomes the familiar $\frac{1}{n}\sum(f(x_i) - y_i)^2$. For classification with log loss, it becomes $-\frac{1}{n}\sum[y_i \log f(x_i) + (1-y_i)\log(1 - f(x_i))]$.

The gap between empirical risk $\hat{R}(f)$ and true risk $R(f)$ is the ''generalization error''. Statistical learning theory bounds this gap as a function of the hypothesis class complexity (measured by VC dimension, Rademacher complexity, or similar) and the number of training samples $n$. The more complex $\mathcal{H}$ is, the better it can fit training data — but the looser the generalization bound becomes. This is the mathematical version of the [[Bias-Variance Tradeoff]].

Minimizing the empirical risk is typically done by <<link "Gradient Descent" "Gradient Descent">><</link>>: compute the gradient $\nabla_\theta \hat{R}$ with respect to model parameters $\theta$, then update $\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \nabla_\theta \hat{R}$, where $\eta$ is the learning rate. For convex problems like linear regression, gradient descent converges to the global minimum. For non-convex problems like neural networks, it finds local minima that empirically generalize well.

---

!! The Supervised Learning Pipeline

A real supervised learning project is far more than fitting a model. The pipeline involves several interconnected steps:

''1. Data collection and labeling.'' Labels are expensive. Medical labels require expert radiologists. Sentiment labels require human annotators. The quality of labels is the ceiling on model performance — noisy or biased labels produce noisy or biased models, no matter how powerful the architecture.

''2. Feature engineering and preprocessing.'' Raw inputs (images, text, sensor readings) must be converted into numerical representations the model can use. This might mean normalizing pixel values, tokenizing text, or constructing hand-crafted features. Deep learning reduces the need for manual feature engineering but does not eliminate preprocessing entirely.

''3. Train/validation/test split.'' The data is divided into subsets. The model trains on the training set, hyperparameters are tuned on the validation set, and final performance is reported on the held-out test set. <<link "Cross-Validation" "Cross-Validation">><</link>> provides a more robust version of this procedure by rotating the validation fold through the entire dataset.

''4. Model selection and training.'' Choose a hypothesis class (linear model, tree ensemble, neural network), define the loss function, and optimize using gradient descent or a specialized solver. Hyperparameters — learning rate, regularization strength, number of layers — are tuned on validation performance.

''5. Evaluation and deployment.'' The test set gives an unbiased estimate of real-world performance. Once deployed, the model must be monitored for distribution shift: the real world changes, and a model trained on last year's data may fail on this year's inputs.

---

!! When Supervised Learning Breaks Down

''Insufficient or biased labels.'' Supervised learning is only as good as its labels. If the training data systematically underrepresents certain groups — for example, a medical dataset that lacks images from specific demographics — the model will perform poorly on those groups regardless of its architecture. Worse, label noise (mislabeled examples) trains the model to reproduce errors. When labels are scarce or expensive, methods like semi-supervised learning, active learning, or <<link "Unsupervised Learning" "Unsupervised Learning">><</link>> become necessary alternatives.

''Distribution shift.'' The core assumption of supervised learning is that training and test data come from the same distribution $P$. In practice, this assumption breaks constantly. A fraud detection model trained on 2023 transaction patterns may fail on 2025 fraud tactics. A self-driving car trained on California highways may fail on European roundabouts. When the distribution shifts, the generalization guarantee evaporates, and the model can fail silently — still producing confident predictions that are systematically wrong.

''Overfitting to spurious correlations.'' A model may achieve low training loss by learning shortcuts that are correlated with the label in the training set but not causally related. A famous example: a model that learned to classify X-rays as COVID-positive based on the hospital's label overlay rather than any lung pathology. With enough capacity and insufficient regularization, supervised models will exploit any statistical shortcut available, and no amount of <<link "gradient descent" "Gradient Descent">><</link>> can fix a fundamentally confounded dataset.

!! Technical Details

''Empirical risk minimization (ERM)'' is the formal framework underlying most supervised learning. The key theoretical result is Vapnik's theorem: for a hypothesis class with finite VC dimension $d$, with probability at least $1-\delta$, the generalization gap satisfies $R(f) - \hat{R}(f) \leq O\left(\sqrt{\frac{d \log(n/d) + \log(1/\delta)}{n}}\right)$. This bound formalizes the intuition that more data ($n$ large) and simpler models ($d$ small) yield better generalization.

''Structural risk minimization (SRM)'' addresses the bias-variance tradeoff directly: instead of minimizing empirical risk alone, minimize the sum of empirical risk and a complexity penalty. This is the theoretical justification for regularization: $\hat{f} = \arg\min_{f \in \mathcal{H}} \left[\hat{R}(f) + \lambda \cdot \text{complexity}(f)\right]$. L2 regularization (ridge), L1 regularization (lasso), and dropout are all instances of this idea.

''PAC learning (Probably Approximately Correct)'' formalizes learnability: a concept class is PAC-learnable if there exists an algorithm that, given enough training data, produces a hypothesis with low true risk with high probability. The sample complexity of PAC learning quantifies how many examples are needed as a function of accuracy tolerance $\epsilon$ and confidence $\delta$. A concept is efficiently PAC-learnable if the required sample size and computation time are polynomial in $1/\epsilon$, $1/\delta$, and the input dimensionality.

Common supervised algorithms and their inductive biases: ''Linear models'' assume the decision boundary is a hyperplane. ''Decision trees'' assume axis-aligned splits are sufficient. ''K-nearest neighbors'' assumes nearby points share labels (locality). ''Support vector machines'' seek maximum-margin separating hyperplanes via the kernel trick. ''Neural networks'' learn hierarchical feature representations through composed nonlinear transformations. Each inductive bias works well when its assumptions match the data structure and fails when they do not.

The ''no free lunch theorem'' states that no supervised learning algorithm is universally better than any other across all possible data distributions. Superior performance on one distribution must be paid for by inferior performance on some other distribution. This theorem justifies the existence of many algorithms: the right choice depends on the problem.
