! Mixed Precision Training
<img src="assets/mixed_precision.svg" style="max-width:100%;margin:0.5em 0 1em;" alt="Mixed precision training loop diagram"/>

A contractor keeps precise measurements in their notebook (the master record) but uses a slightly rougher tape measure for the actual cutting — faster to use, and as long as they update the notebook with the precise result, nothing is lost. Mixed precision training works the same way: precise FP32 master weights are the notebook, while the heavy matrix multiplications happen in FP16 or BF16 — faster and cheaper — with careful tricks to avoid losing numerical precision along the way.

!! The Core Insight

''Micikevicius et al. 2018 (NVIDIA)'' established the modern mixed precision recipe. Modern GPUs carry //Tensor Cores// — dedicated silicon that runs FP16 and BF16 matrix multiplications 2–8× faster than FP32 general-purpose cores. The recipe: store FP32 master weights; cast to FP16 (or BF16) for the forward and backward pass; accumulate gradients in FP32; update the FP32 master weights with the optimizer. Memory savings are roughly 2× for the model and gradient tensors. The tricky part: FP16 has a limited dynamic range (max ≈ 65504) and can underflow for very small gradient values. The solution is //loss scaling//. Links: [[Backpropagation and Chain Rule]], [[Deep Learning]], [[Hardware Lottery]], [[Gradient Checkpointing]], [[Large Language Models]].

!! FP16 vs BF16 vs FP32

''FP32'' uses 1 sign + 8 exponent + 23 mantissa bits. Range: ±3.4×10^38. Precision: ~7 decimal digits. The gold standard for numerical stability.

''FP16'' uses 1+5+10 bits. Range: ±65504. Precision: ~3 decimal digits. Risk: gradient underflow when values fall below ~6×10^−8. Loss scaling (below) compensates.

''BF16 (Brain Float 16)'' uses 1+8+7 bits. It keeps FP32's full 8-bit exponent (same range: ±3.4×10^38) but sacrifices mantissa precision. BF16 is numerically much safer than FP16 — gradient underflow is rare, and dynamic loss scaling is often unnecessary. A100 and later NVIDIA GPUs, and all recent TPUs, support BF16 Tensor Cores. //Modern practice:// use BF16 by default (Llama, Mistral, Gemma, etc.); use FP16 only on older hardware (V100, T4) that lacks BF16 support.

''FP8'' (H100 Hopper architecture) uses 8 bits in two formats: E4M3 (4 exponent, 3 mantissa — for forward pass activations) and E5M2 (5 exponent, 2 mantissa — for gradients). Provides roughly 2× throughput over BF16 but requires per-tensor scaling and the NVIDIA Transformer Engine library.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

//Loss scaling// prevents gradient underflow. Before the backward pass, multiply the loss by a scale factor $S$; after the backward pass, divide the gradients by $S$ before the optimizer step:

$$g_{\text{scaled}} = S \cdot g_{\text{true}}, \qquad \theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \cdot \frac{g_{\text{scaled}}}{S}$$

The math is equivalent to unscaled training, but gradients are shifted into FP16's representable range during computation. ''Dynamic loss scaling'' starts with a large $S$ (e.g., 65536), halves it when overflow is detected (inf or NaN in gradients), and slowly increases it again during stable steps. PyTorch's `GradScaler` automates this.

//Gradient accumulation// in FP32: even when gradients are computed in FP16, partial sums are accumulated into FP32 buffers to prevent precision loss across many additions.

//Memory layout for full training (e.g., Adam):// model weights 4B/param (FP32), gradients 2B/param (FP16), optimizer first moment $m$ 4B/param (FP32), optimizer second moment $v$ 4B/param (FP32) — total ~14–18B/param. For a 70B-parameter model this implies >900 GB, which is why ZeRO sharding across many GPUs is necessary.

!! Practical Implementation

PyTorch //Automatic Mixed Precision// (AMP, stable from PyTorch 1.6) exposes two primitives:

- `torch.cuda.amp.autocast()` — a context manager that automatically casts eligible operations (matmul, conv) to FP16/BF16 while keeping numerically sensitive ops (softmax, layer norm) in FP32.
- `torch.cuda.amp.GradScaler` — manages dynamic loss scaling and overflow detection.

A minimal training step looks like: model forward under `autocast`, `scaler.scale(loss).backward()`, `scaler.step(optimizer)`, `scaler.update()`. For BF16 on A100+, `autocast(dtype=torch.bfloat16)` and no `GradScaler` needed. The NVIDIA Transformer Engine library (`import transformer_engine.pytorch as te`) enables FP8 training on H100.

Tensor Core alignment: for maximum throughput, matrix dimensions should be multiples of 8 (FP16) or 16 (BF16). Misaligned shapes silently fall back to FP32 CUDA cores at lower speed.

!! When Mixed Precision Breaks Down

''Numerical instability in FP16:'' Softmax and layer normalization accumulate many additions; rounding errors compound and can destabilize training. The fix is to keep these specific operations in FP32 — `autocast` handles this automatically for standard PyTorch ops, but custom CUDA kernels must be annotated explicitly.

''Loss spike recovery failure:'' Dynamic loss scaling can enter a cycle of repeated halving if the model is in a poor loss basin, effectively stalling training. Gradient clipping (e.g., max norm 1.0) mitigates this but does not fully eliminate it.

''Legacy architectures:'' Custom or third-party operation implementations may not expose FP16 paths. Running such ops under `autocast` either silently promotes to FP32 (losing speed) or raises an error. Audit custom kernels before enabling AMP.

!! Technical Details

Foundational paper: Micikevicius et al. "Mixed Precision Training", ICLR 2018. Original implementation: NVIDIA Apex library (now largely superseded by PyTorch native AMP). FP8 training: Peng et al. "FP8-LM: Training FP8 Large Language Models", 2023; NVIDIA Transformer Engine.

BF16 on TPUs: standard since TPUv3; Google's Bfloat16 format was designed specifically to ease the transition from FP32 by preserving exponent width.

Memory math for a 70B model: weights 140 GB in FP16 (70B × 2B), optimizer states 560 GB in FP32 (70B × 4B × 2 moments) — total ~700 GB+ before activations, hence multi-node ZeRO-3 or tensor parallelism is required.

Inference-only mixed precision (not training): models are often quantized to INT8 or INT4 for deployment, which is a separate but related topic covered under model compression and quantization techniques.
