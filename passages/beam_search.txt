! Beam Search

<img src="assets/beam_search.svg" alt="Diagram illustrating Beam Search with k best hypotheses kept at each decoding step" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine you are navigating a maze, but instead of committing to one path at each fork, you send out a small team of scouts who each explore a different promising route simultaneously. At every junction, only the two or three scouts on the most promising paths continue — the rest are called back. That is ''beam search'': an approximate decoding strategy for sequence models that maintains $k$ best partial hypotheses at each time step, where $k$ is the ''beam width''. It sits squarely between greedy search (keeping only the single best token at each step, $k=1$) and exhaustive search (keeping every possible continuation, which is computationally impossible for any realistic vocabulary).

---

!! The Core Insight

Autoregressive sequence models — <<link "Language Models" "Language Models">><</link>>, <<link "Transformers" "Transformers">><</link>>, and earlier recurrent architectures — generate output one token at a time by sampling or selecting from a probability distribution over the vocabulary. Greedy decoding picks the highest-probability token at each step, but this is locally optimal: the best token //right now// may lead to a poor sequence overall. Consider a translation model where the most probable first word forces an awkward continuation, while a slightly less probable first word opens a path to a much better sentence.

Exhaustive search would consider every possible sequence of length $T$ from a vocabulary of size $V$, which means evaluating $V^T$ candidates — a number that grows exponentially and is utterly intractable. For a vocabulary of 50,000 tokens and a sequence of length 20, this is $50{,}000^{20}$, a number with roughly 94 digits.

Beam search provides a practical middle ground. At each time step, it expands every current hypothesis by all $V$ vocabulary items, scores the resulting $k \times V$ candidates, and keeps only the top $k$. The total work per step is $O(k \times V)$ rather than exponential, and in practice $k$ is small — typically between 4 and 10. This makes beam search the dominant decoding strategy for machine translation, speech recognition, and any task where output quality depends on finding a globally coherent sequence rather than a locally greedy one. See <<link "Search Algorithms" "Search Algorithms">><</link>> and <<link "Dynamic Programming" "Dynamic Programming">><</link>> for related algorithmic foundations.

---

!! How Beam Search Operates Step by Step

At time step $t=1$, the model produces a distribution over the full vocabulary given the input context. The top $k$ tokens become the initial $k$ hypotheses. Each hypothesis carries a cumulative ''log-probability'' score: $\text{score}(y_{1:t}) = \sum_{i=1}^{t} \log p(y_i \mid y_{1:i-1}, x)$.

At each subsequent step $t$, every hypothesis is extended by every token in the vocabulary, producing $k \times V$ candidate continuations. Each candidate's score is the parent's cumulative log-probability plus the log-probability of the new token. The candidates are sorted by score, and only the top $k$ survive. This process repeats until all $k$ beams have produced an end-of-sequence token or a maximum length is reached.

The final output is the completed hypothesis with the highest score. In practice, because longer sequences accumulate more negative log-probability terms, a ''length normalization'' factor is applied: the score is divided by $T^\alpha$ where $T$ is the sequence length and $\alpha$ is a tunable parameter (often 0.6 to 1.0). Without length normalization, beam search is biased toward shorter outputs.

The <<link "Tokenizer" "Tokenizer">><</link>> determines the vocabulary $V$ over which these distributions are computed — subword tokenizers like BPE produce vocabularies of 30,000 to 100,000 tokens, and beam search operates directly at this token level.

---

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The objective of decoding is to find the sequence $y^*$ that maximizes the conditional probability:

$$y^* = \arg\max_{y} \; p(y \mid x) = \arg\max_{y} \; \prod_{t=1}^{T} p(y_t \mid y_{1:t-1}, x)$$

Taking logarithms converts this to an additive score:

$$y^* = \arg\max_{y} \; \sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p(y_t \mid y_{1:t-1}, x)$$

Beam search approximates this argmax by pruning the search space at each step. At step $t$, let $\mathcal{B}_t$ denote the set of $k$ active hypotheses. The expansion and pruning operation is:

$$\mathcal{B}_{t+1} = \text{top-}k \Big\{ (h \circ v, \; S(h) + \log p(v \mid h, x)) \;\Big|\; h \in \mathcal{B}_t, \; v \in V \Big\}$$

where $h \circ v$ denotes appending token $v$ to hypothesis $h$, and $S(h)$ is the cumulative log-probability of $h$.

With length normalization, the final scoring becomes:

$$\text{score}(y) = \frac{1}{T^\alpha} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p(y_t \mid y_{1:t-1}, x)$$

The total computation per step is $O(k \times V)$ for scoring plus $O(k \times V \times \log k)$ for selecting the top $k$ — linear in vocabulary size and beam width, repeated for $T$ steps.

---

!! Beam Search in Practice

In ''neural machine translation'', beam search with $k=4$ to $k=8$ is standard. Systems like the original Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) used beam search with $k=4$ and a length penalty to produce final translations. Increasing $k$ beyond about 10 typically yields diminishing returns and can even degrade quality due to the ''beam search curse'': very high-probability sequences under the model are sometimes degenerate (empty, repetitive, or overly generic).

In ''speech recognition'' (ASR), beam search operates over subword or character vocabularies, often combined with an external language model that re-scores hypotheses. The beam width is commonly 10 to 20.

In ''text generation'' with large language models, beam search is less common than sampling-based methods (top-$k$ sampling, nucleus sampling) because beam search tends to produce text that is fluent but bland and repetitive. The highest-probability sequence is often not the most interesting or human-like one. Modern LLM inference typically uses sampling for open-ended generation and reserves beam search for constrained tasks like translation or summarization.

---

!! When Beam Search Breaks Down

''The beam search curse — larger beams, worse output.'' Counterintuitively, increasing beam width can degrade output quality. The highest-scoring sequence under the model may be a degenerate output — an empty string, an endlessly repeated phrase, or a generic hedge. This happens because the model's probability distribution is not perfectly calibrated: it assigns too much mass to certain pathological sequences. With a small beam, these sequences are pruned by accident; with a large beam, they survive. This is well-documented in neural machine translation (Koehn and Knowles, 2017) and is sometimes called the ''curse of beam search''.

''Length bias distorts the ranking.'' Because log-probabilities are negative, longer sequences accumulate lower scores purely by virtue of having more terms in the sum. Without proper length normalization, beam search systematically favors shorter outputs. The normalization factor $T^\alpha$ mitigates this, but choosing $\alpha$ is itself a tuning problem — too large, and the model prefers verbose output; too small, and it truncates prematurely. There is no universally correct value.

''Beam search cannot backtrack.'' Once a partial hypothesis is pruned at step $t$, it is gone forever. If the globally optimal sequence required a token that ranked outside the top $k$ at some early step, beam search will never find it. This makes beam search fundamentally a ''local'' approximation to global search. For tasks with long-range dependencies where critical decisions happen early in the sequence, beam search can miss the best output entirely, and no amount of increasing $k$ short of exhaustive search guarantees finding it.

---

!! Technical Details

Beam search was used in statistical machine translation long before neural sequence models. Its adoption in neural MT was popularized by Sutskever, Vinyals, and Le (2014), "Sequence to Sequence Learning with Neural Networks," and Bahdanau, Cho, and Bengio (2015), "Neural Machine Translation by Jointly Learning to Align and Translate."

The beam search curse was studied by Koehn and Knowles (2017), "Six Challenges for Neural Machine Translation," and further analyzed by Murray and Chiang (2018). Diverse beam search (Vijayakumar et al., 2018) modifies the selection step to encourage diversity among the $k$ beams, reducing the problem of near-duplicate hypotheses. Constrained beam search (Post and Vilar, 2018) forces specific tokens or phrases to appear in the output — useful for terminology enforcement in translation.

The relationship between beam search and exact search is studied by Stahlberg and Byrne (2019), who showed that exact search (finding the true argmax) often produces worse output than beam search, because the model's highest-probability sequence is pathological. This reveals that beam search's pruning acts as an implicit regularizer.

Common implementation details: hypotheses that emit an end-of-sequence token are removed from the active beam and placed in a completed set; the search continues until all beams are complete or a maximum length is reached; the completed set is re-ranked with length normalization to produce the final output. Efficient implementations use batched matrix operations to score all $k \times V$ candidates simultaneously on GPU.
