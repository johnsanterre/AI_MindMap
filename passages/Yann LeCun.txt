! Yann LeCun
<img src="assets/yann_lecun.svg" alt="Yann LeCun" width="800">

If [[Deep Learning]] has a chief engineer, it is Yann LeCun. Where others theorised about how neural networks //might// learn from raw sensory data, LeCun built systems that actually did it — reading cheques for banks, recognising faces, parsing documents — years before the rest of the field caught up. His career is a masterclass in stubborn conviction: he championed [[Neural Networks]] through the long winter when the technique was considered a dead end, and lived to see it become the dominant paradigm in artificial intelligence. Today, as Chief AI Scientist at Meta, he is pushing a vision of machine intelligence grounded not in language prediction but in ''self-supervised learning'' and ''world models'' — the idea that true understanding requires a machine to build an internal simulation of how the world works.

!! Key Contributions

''Convolutional Neural Networks and LeNet.'' LeCun's most enduring contribution is the [[CNN|CNN]] — the convolutional neural network. Working at AT&T Bell Labs in the late 1980s and 1990s, he showed that a neural network with local receptive fields, shared weights, and pooling layers could learn to recognise handwritten digits directly from pixel values, without hand-crafted features. The resulting system, ''LeNet-5'' (1998), was deployed commercially to read millions of cheques per day. The architecture — convolution, activation, pooling, repeat, then fully connected layers — became the template for virtually all modern [[Computer Vision]] systems.

''Backpropagation Advances.'' LeCun was among the first to demonstrate that [[Backpropagation]] could train multi-layer networks on real-world tasks at scale. His 1989 paper applying backprop to handwritten zip code recognition was a landmark proof that gradient-based learning worked outside toy problems. He also contributed key practical insights: efficient weight initialisation, the importance of learning rate schedules, and the use of second-order optimisation methods.

''Energy-Based Models.'' In the 2000s, LeCun developed a unified framework for understanding learning algorithms through the lens of ''energy functions''. Rather than thinking of a model as computing a probability, an energy-based model assigns a scalar energy to every configuration of inputs and outputs — learning pushes down the energy of correct configurations and pushes up the energy of incorrect ones. This framework encompasses supervised, unsupervised, and contrastive learning as special cases.

''Self-Supervised Learning Vision.'' LeCun has been the field's most vocal advocate for ''self-supervised learning'' — training models to predict parts of the input from other parts, without human-provided labels. He argues that [[Self-Supervised Learning]] is how humans and animals learn the vast majority of what they know about the world, and that it is the missing ingredient between current AI and human-level intelligence. His Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture (JEPA) proposes learning representations by predicting abstract features rather than raw pixels.

''FAIR / Meta AI Leadership.'' In 2013, LeCun founded Facebook AI Research (FAIR), building it into one of the world's premier industrial research labs. Under his leadership, FAIR has produced foundational work in [[Computer Vision]], natural language processing, robotics, and open-source tools (PyTorch, Detectron, fairseq). He has been a tireless advocate for ''open science'', insisting that FAIR publish its research and release its code — a stance that shaped the culture of modern AI research.

!! The Core Insight

LeCun's deepest conviction is that ''prediction is the essence of intelligence''. A truly intelligent system does not merely classify inputs or generate text — it builds an internal model of the world and uses that model to anticipate what will happen next. This is what he calls a ''world model''.

Current large language models, he argues, are impressive but fundamentally limited: they predict the next token in a sequence but have no grounded understanding of physical reality. A child who has never read a book nonetheless understands gravity, solidity, and object permanence — knowledge acquired through ''observation and prediction'', not through language. LeCun's research programme aims to build systems that learn the way children do: by watching the world, predicting what will happen, and updating their internal model when surprised.

The mathematical backbone of this vision is the ''energy landscape''. Instead of modelling $P(y|x)$ directly (which requires intractable normalisation over all possible $y$), an energy-based model learns a function $E(x, y)$ that is low for compatible pairs and high for incompatible ones. Inference becomes optimisation: find the $y$ that minimises $E(x, y)$. Training becomes ''contrastive'': push energy down on observed pairs, push it up on corrupted or negative pairs.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

''The Convolution Operation.'' At the heart of the [[CNN]] is the discrete convolution of an input signal $x$ with a learned filter $w$:

$$(x * w)[i, j] = \sum_m \sum_n x[i+m,\; j+n] \cdot w[m, n]$$

Each filter slides across the input, computing a dot product at every position. This enforces ''translation equivariance'': the same pattern is detected regardless of where it appears in the image. Stacking multiple filters at each layer lets the network build a hierarchy of features — edges in early layers, textures in middle layers, object parts in deep layers.

''Weight Sharing.'' A fully connected layer connecting a $32 \times 32$ image to even a modest hidden layer would require millions of parameters. By reusing the same small filter across every spatial position, a convolutional layer reduces the parameter count by orders of magnitude while encoding the prior that visual features are spatially local and position-independent. For a filter of size $k \times k$ applied to an input with $c$ channels, the layer has only $k^2 c$ parameters per filter — independent of the spatial resolution.

''LeNet Architecture.'' LeNet-5 stacked two convolutional layers (each followed by average pooling to reduce spatial dimensions by half) and three fully connected layers. The forward pass can be summarised as:

$$x \xrightarrow{\text{Conv}_1} \xrightarrow{\text{Pool}} \xrightarrow{\text{Conv}_2} \xrightarrow{\text{Pool}} \xrightarrow{\text{Flatten}} \xrightarrow{\text{FC}_1} \xrightarrow{\text{FC}_2} \xrightarrow{\text{FC}_3} \hat{y}$$

Training minimises a loss $\mathcal{L}(\hat{y}, y)$ via [[Backpropagation]], computing $\partial \mathcal{L}/\partial W_\ell$ for each layer $\ell$ and updating weights with gradient descent.

''Energy-Based Learning.'' For an energy function $E_\theta(x, y)$ parameterised by $\theta$, the contrastive loss takes the general form:

$$\mathcal{L} = E_\theta(x, y^+) + \max(0,\; m - E_\theta(x, y^-))$$

where $y^+$ is a correct output, $y^-$ is an incorrect (negative) output, and $m$ is a margin. The first term pushes the energy of correct pairs down; the second term pushes the energy of incorrect pairs up, but only if it is not already above the margin. This avoids the need to compute a partition function over all possible outputs.

!! Impact on the Field

''Turing Award (2018).'' LeCun shared the ACM A.M. Turing Award — often called the Nobel Prize of computing — with [[Geoffrey Hinton]] and [[Yoshua Bengio]]. The citation honoured their "conceptual and engineering breakthroughs that have made deep neural networks a critical component of computing." The three are widely known as the "Godfathers of Deep Learning."

''Founding FAIR.'' By creating a world-class research lab inside a major technology company and insisting on open publication, LeCun helped establish the model of industrial AI research that Google Brain, DeepMind, and others followed. FAIR's open-source contributions — particularly ''PyTorch'' — became the dominant tools of the field.

''Advocacy for Open Science.'' LeCun has consistently argued that AI progress depends on open research, open-source code, and open models. He has been a prominent public voice against proposals to restrict AI publication, arguing that secrecy slows progress and concentrates power.

!! When Deep Learning Faces Challenges

''1. Distribution shift and robustness.'' Deep networks trained on curated datasets can fail catastrophically when deployed in environments that differ from training. A self-driving car trained in California sunshine may not recognise pedestrians in snow. LeCun's proposed solution — world models that understand physical causation — aims to move beyond pattern matching to genuine understanding, but this remains an open research problem.

''2. The labelling bottleneck.'' Supervised learning requires massive labelled datasets, which are expensive, biased, and sometimes impossible to create for novel domains. This is precisely why LeCun champions [[Self-Supervised Learning]]: if a model can learn useful representations from unlabelled data (video, images, sensor streams), it can acquire far more knowledge than any labelled dataset could provide. However, self-supervised methods for vision still lag behind their counterparts in language.

''3. Reasoning and planning.'' Current deep learning systems excel at perception and pattern completion but struggle with multi-step reasoning, planning under constraints, and compositional generalisation. LeCun argues that these capabilities require a fundamentally different architecture — one that includes a world model, a cost module, and an actor that plans by simulating future states — rather than simply scaling up next-token prediction.

!! Technical Details

- ''Born:'' 1960, Paris, France. Studied at ESIEE Paris and Universit&eacute; Pierre et Marie Curie.
- ''PhD (1987):'' Under the supervision of Maurice Milgram; thesis on learning in neural networks.
- ''AT&T Bell Labs (1988–2003):'' Head of the Image Processing Research Department. Developed LeNet, DjVu compression, and applied CNNs to document recognition at industrial scale.
- ''NYU (2003–present):'' Silver Professor of Computer Science, Courant Institute. Founded the NYU Center for Data Science.
- ''FAIR / Meta AI (2013–present):'' Founding director; current title is VP and Chief AI Scientist.
- ''Key papers:'' "Backpropagation Applied to Handwritten Zip Code Recognition" (1989); "Gradient-Based Learning Applied to Document Recognition" (1998, the LeNet-5 paper); "A Tutorial on Energy-Based Learning" (2006); "A Path Towards Autonomous Machine Intelligence" (2022, the JEPA proposal).
- ''Awards:'' ACM Turing Award (2018), IEEE Neural Network Pioneer Award (2014), Legion of Honour (France).
- ''Notable positions:'' LeCun is known for public debates on social media (particularly on the limitations of large language models and the necessity of world models for achieving human-level AI).
