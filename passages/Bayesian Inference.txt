! Bayesian Inference

<img src="assets/bayesian_inference.svg" alt="Bayesian Inference" width="800">

Imagine you hear a strange noise outside your window at night. Your first thought might be "probably a cat" — that's your prior belief. Then you peek outside and see a knocked-over trash can with claw marks too large for a cat. That evidence shifts your belief toward "raccoon" or something bigger. You didn't throw away your original guess entirely — you //updated// it based on new information. That's the essence of ''Bayesian inference'': a formal framework for updating beliefs in the face of evidence, combining what you already suspect with what the data tells you.

!! The Core Insight

Classical ([[frequentist|Maximum Likelihood]]) statistics treats model parameters as fixed, unknown constants and asks "what's the probability of seeing this data, given a particular parameter value?" ''Bayesian inference'' flips the question: "given the data I've observed, what should I believe about the parameter?" This reversal is profound. Parameters become ''random variables'' with their own [[probability distributions|Probability Distributions]], and learning from data means transforming a ''prior distribution'' (what you believed before seeing data) into a ''posterior distribution'' (what you believe after).

This means every Bayesian analysis produces not a single best guess but a //full distribution// over possible parameter values. You get a complete picture of uncertainty — not just "the mean is probably 5.2" but "I'm 90% sure the mean is between 4.8 and 5.6, with 5.2 being the most likely." This is what makes [[Bayesian Methods]] fundamentally different: uncertainty is a first-class citizen, carried through every calculation.

!! Updating Beliefs with Data

The Bayesian update cycle has three ingredients. First, the ''prior'' encodes everything you know (or assume) before seeing data. It might be vague — "the coin's bias could be anything from 0 to 1" — or informed by domain expertise — "most coins are very close to fair." Second, the ''likelihood'' measures how well each possible parameter value explains the observed data. Third, combining these via [[Bayes' Theorem]] yields the ''posterior'' — your updated belief.

The beauty of sequential updating is that today's posterior becomes tomorrow's prior. If you flip a coin 10 times and get 7 heads, you compute a posterior. Tomorrow you flip 10 more times and get 4 heads — you use yesterday's posterior as today's prior and update again. The result is //identical// to having processed all 20 flips at once. This makes Bayesian inference naturally suited to streaming data and online learning.

As more data accumulates, posteriors sharpen. A vague prior — spread across many values — gets overwhelmed by a strong likelihood from hundreds of observations. This is the ''washing out of the prior'': with enough data, two people with very different priors will converge to nearly identical posteriors. The data speaks loudly enough to drown out initial disagreements.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

[[Bayes' Theorem]] gives us the posterior:

$$P(\theta \mid D) = \frac{P(D \mid \theta) \, P(\theta)}{P(D)}$$

where $\theta$ represents the parameters, $D$ is the observed data, $P(\theta)$ is the prior, $P(D \mid \theta)$ is the likelihood, and $P(D) = \int P(D \mid \theta) P(\theta) \, d\theta$ is the ''marginal likelihood'' (or ''evidence''). Since the evidence is constant with respect to $\theta$, we often write:

$$P(\theta \mid D) \propto P(D \mid \theta) \times P(\theta)$$

This "posterior is proportional to likelihood times prior" is the workhorse formula.

''[[Conjugate priors|Conjugate Priors]]'' make the math tractable. When the prior and posterior belong to the same distribution family, updates have closed-form solutions. The ''Beta-Binomial'' conjugacy is the classic example: if your prior on a coin's bias $p$ is $\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ and you observe $k$ heads in $n$ flips, the posterior is $\text{Beta}(\alpha + k, \, \beta + n - k)$. The [[Beta Distribution]] parameters simply absorb the counts. Similarly, a Normal prior on a mean with Normal likelihood yields a Normal posterior (''Normal-Normal'' conjugacy), where the posterior mean is a precision-weighted average of the prior mean and the sample mean.

The ''marginal likelihood'' $P(D)$ is crucial for [[Bayesian model comparison|Bayesian Optimization]]. It measures how well the //entire model// (prior + likelihood family) predicted the data, integrating over all parameter values. Models that spread probability too thinly (overly complex) or concentrate it in the wrong places (too simple) both score poorly — an automatic Occam's razor.

!! Bayesian Inference in Practice

When [[conjugate priors|Conjugate Priors]] apply, posteriors are computed analytically — fast and exact. But most real models lack conjugacy. For these, ''Markov Chain [[Monte Carlo|Monte Carlo Methods]]'' ([[MCMC]]) methods draw samples from the posterior by constructing a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is the target posterior. [[Gibbs Sampling]] updates one parameter at a time from its conditional distribution. Metropolis-Hastings proposes random jumps and accepts or rejects them based on the posterior ratio. These methods are exact in the limit of infinite samples but can be slow for high-dimensional models.

For large-scale problems — millions of data points, thousands of parameters — [[Variational Inference]] trades exactness for speed. It approximates the posterior with a simpler distribution (often factored Gaussians) by minimizing the KL divergence. This turns inference into optimization, enabling Bayesian methods to scale to deep learning contexts like [[Bayesian Neural Networks]].

Choosing priors is both an art and a science. ''Uninformative priors'' (flat or Jeffreys priors) try to "let the data speak," but can be improper or lead to paradoxes. ''Weakly informative priors'' gently regularize — for example, a $\text{Normal}(0, 10)$ prior on regression coefficients says "we don't expect coefficients to be astronomically large." ''Empirical Bayes'' estimates hyperparameters from the data itself, using the marginal likelihood — a pragmatic shortcut that blurs the line between Bayesian and frequentist thinking.

The ''[[Maximum A Posteriori]]'' (MAP) estimate takes the posterior's mode as a point estimate. It's a bridge between Bayesian and frequentist worlds: MAP with a flat prior is just [[Maximum Likelihood]], while MAP with a Gaussian prior is equivalent to L2 regularization (ridge regression). But MAP discards the posterior's shape, losing the uncertainty quantification that makes Bayesian inference valuable. Full [[Bayesian Regression]] keeps the entire posterior and uses it for prediction.

!! When Bayesian Inference Breaks Down

* ''Computational intractability in high dimensions.'' The marginal likelihood integral $P(D) = \int P(D \mid \theta) P(\theta) \, d\theta$ becomes impossible to compute analytically for complex models. MCMC can explore these spaces, but convergence may take prohibitively long when parameters number in the thousands or when the posterior has multiple isolated modes. Even variational methods may find only local optima, giving overconfident and inaccurate approximations.

* ''Prior sensitivity with small data.'' When data is scarce, the posterior is dominated by the prior. If the prior is poorly chosen — too narrow, centered on the wrong value, or structurally wrong — the posterior can be badly misleading. Unlike large-data regimes where priors wash out, small-data Bayesian inference demands careful prior specification, and two analysts with different priors can reach contradictory conclusions from the same data.

* ''Model misspecification.'' Bayes' theorem updates beliefs about parameters //within// a model, but it cannot rescue a fundamentally wrong model. If the true data-generating process isn't in your model family, the posterior will concentrate on whichever parameter value is "least wrong" — and the posterior uncertainty will be misleadingly narrow, suggesting false confidence. [[Bayesian Nonparametrics]] partially address this by allowing model complexity to grow with data, but even they make structural assumptions.

!! Technical Details

''Computational methods'' span a wide range. ''Hamiltonian Monte Carlo'' (HMC) uses gradient information to propose moves along the posterior surface, dramatically improving efficiency over random-walk Metropolis in high dimensions. The ''No-U-Turn Sampler'' (NUTS), used by Stan and PyMC, automatically tunes HMC's trajectory length. The ''Laplace approximation'' fits a Gaussian at the posterior mode — fast but only accurate for unimodal, roughly symmetric posteriors.

''Model comparison'' uses ''Bayes factors'': the ratio of marginal likelihoods $BF_{12} = P(D \mid M_1) / P(D \mid M_2)$. A Bayes factor above 10 is "strong evidence" for model 1. Unlike p-values, Bayes factors can provide evidence //for// a null hypothesis, not just against it. Computation requires the marginal likelihood, which is often the hardest quantity to estimate.

''Credible intervals'' vs ''confidence intervals'': a 95% Bayesian credible interval means "there is a 95% probability the parameter lies in this interval, given the data." A 95% frequentist confidence interval means "if we repeated the experiment many times, 95% of such intervals would contain the true parameter." The Bayesian version directly answers the question practitioners usually want.

The ''Bernstein-von Mises theorem'' provides asymptotic justification: under regularity conditions, the posterior converges to a Gaussian centered at the true parameter value as sample size grows, regardless of the prior. This means Bayesian and frequentist answers agree in the large-sample limit — the prior is eventually overwhelmed by data.

''Posterior predictive checks'' validate Bayesian models by simulating data from the posterior predictive distribution $P(\tilde{D} \mid D) = \int P(\tilde{D} \mid \theta) P(\theta \mid D) \, d\theta$ and comparing against observed data patterns.
