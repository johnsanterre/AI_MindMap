! Image Segmentation
<img src="assets/image_segmentation.svg" alt="Image segmentation types and architecture evolution from FCN to SAM" style="width:100%;max-width:620px;display:block;margin:16px auto 24px auto;">

Imagine coloring a photograph with invisible paint so that every single pixel gets a label: "this is sky," "this is road," "this is the left pedestrian's jacket." That is image segmentation — not just asking //what// is in the picture (classification) or //where// it is (a bounding box), but tracing the exact silhouette of every object at pixel resolution. It is the difference between pointing at a crowd and saying "people" versus carefully outlining each individual person with a different-colored marker.

---
!! The Core Insight

''Image segmentation'' assigns a class label to every pixel in an image. Three increasingly difficult variants exist:

''Semantic segmentation'' labels each pixel with a category (sky, road, car) but does not distinguish between different instances of the same class — two adjacent cars are both just "car." ''Instance segmentation'' separates individual objects, giving each its own mask and ID, but ignores background "stuff" classes. ''Panoptic segmentation'' (Kirillov et al. 2019) unifies both: every pixel gets a class label //and// every countable object gets a unique instance ID.

The foundational architecture is the ''Fully Convolutional Network'' (FCN, Long et al. 2015), which replaced the final fully connected layers of a <<link "CNN" "CNN">><</link>> with convolutional layers, enabling dense per-pixel predictions at any input resolution. Every major segmentation model since — U-Net, DeepLab, Mask R-CNN, SegFormer, SAM — builds on this principle of producing spatially dense outputs from a convolutional (or transformer) backbone.

!! From FCN to U-Net to SAM

''FCN (2015)'' showed that classification networks could be repurposed for dense prediction by upsampling feature maps back to input resolution. The problem: repeated pooling and striding destroyed spatial detail, producing coarse, blobby masks.

''U-Net (2015)'' solved this with an ''encoder-decoder'' architecture and ''skip connections''. The encoder contracts the image through pooling (capturing //what//), the decoder expands it back (recovering //where//), and skip connections copy high-resolution feature maps from encoder to decoder, preserving fine boundaries. Originally designed for biomedical images with very few training samples, U-Net became the default architecture for any task requiring precise pixel boundaries.

''DeepLab (v1–v3+, 2015–2018)'' introduced ''atrous (dilated) convolution'' — filters with holes that expand the receptive field without reducing resolution — and ''Atrous Spatial Pyramid Pooling'' (ASPP), which applies multiple atrous rates in parallel to capture objects at different scales. DeepLabv3+ added a decoder module for sharper boundaries.

''Mask R-CNN (2017)'' extended Faster R-CNN for instance segmentation by adding a small FCN branch that predicts a binary mask for each detected <<link "object" "Object Detection">><</link>>, running in parallel with the existing box and class heads. The key innovation was ''RoIAlign'' — replacing coarse RoIPool quantization with bilinear interpolation to preserve spatial alignment.

''SegFormer (2021)'' brought <<link "Vision Transformers" "Vision Transformer">><</link>> to segmentation with a hierarchical transformer encoder and a lightweight MLP decoder, demonstrating that transformers could match or exceed CNN-based methods on dense prediction.

''SAM (Segment Anything Model, 2023)'' is a ''promptable foundation model'' for segmentation. Trained on over 1 billion masks from 11 million images (SA-1B dataset), SAM accepts points, boxes, or text as prompts and produces high-quality masks for //any// object — zero-shot, without task-specific training.

---
!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

Semantic segmentation is pixel-level classification. For an image with $H \times W$ pixels and $C$ classes, the network produces a tensor of logits $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times C}$. Each pixel $(i,j)$ is classified independently using a softmax:

$$P(y_{ij} = c) = \frac{\exp(z_{ij,c})}{\sum_{k=1}^{C} \exp(z_{ij,k})}$$

The training loss is the pixel-wise <<link "cross-entropy" "Cross-entropy">><</link>>, averaged over all pixels:

$$\mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{HW}\sum_{i=1}^{H}\sum_{j=1}^{W}\sum_{c=1}^{C} y_{ij,c} \log P(y_{ij} = c)$$

where $y_{ij,c}$ is the one-hot ground-truth label for pixel $(i,j)$.

''Class imbalance'' is severe in segmentation — a "road" class might cover 60% of pixels while "traffic sign" covers 0.1%. Common remedies include ''weighted cross-entropy'' (scaling each class's loss inversely with frequency) and the ''Dice loss'', derived from the Dice coefficient:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{Dice}} = 1 - \frac{2\sum_i p_i g_i}{\sum_i p_i + \sum_i g_i}$$

where $p_i$ are predicted probabilities and $g_i$ are ground-truth labels. Dice loss directly optimizes overlap and is less sensitive to class imbalance than cross-entropy.

''Atrous convolution'' inserts $r-1$ zeros between kernel weights, expanding the receptive field from $k$ to $k + (k-1)(r-1)$ without additional parameters or resolution loss. For a 1D signal: $(f *_r g)(i) = \sum_k f(i + r \cdot k) \cdot g(k)$, where $r$ is the dilation rate.

For ''instance segmentation'', Mask R-CNN adds a per-pixel binary cross-entropy loss over the mask head, computed independently for each detected object within its region of interest.

---
!! Evaluation Metrics and Practical Use

''Mean Intersection over Union (mIoU)'' is the standard metric for semantic segmentation. For each class $c$, IoU is:

$$\text{IoU}_c = \frac{|P_c \cap G_c|}{|P_c \cup G_c|}$$

where $P_c$ is the set of pixels predicted as class $c$ and $G_c$ is the ground truth. mIoU averages across all classes, weighting rare and common classes equally.

For instance segmentation, ''Average Precision (AP)'' is computed at multiple IoU thresholds (AP@0.5, AP@0.75, AP@[0.5:0.95]), analogous to <<link "object detection" "Object Detection">><</link>> but using mask overlap instead of box overlap.

''Panoptic Quality (PQ)'' combines recognition quality (classification accuracy of matched segments) with segmentation quality (average IoU of matched segments): $\text{PQ} = \text{SQ} \times \text{RQ}$.

''Practical deployment:'' autonomous driving (Cityscapes, nuScenes), medical imaging (cell/organ segmentation, U-Net variants dominate), satellite imagery (land use mapping), video editing (background removal, rotoscoping), and augmented reality (real-time person segmentation on mobile devices).

---
!! When Image Segmentation Breaks Down

''Boundary ambiguity at object edges:'' Downsampling in encoders destroys the fine spatial information needed for precise boundaries. Skip connections and decoder refinement help, but segmentation masks still struggle with thin structures (bicycle spokes, tree branches, hair) and tightly adjacent objects of the same class. Post-processing with CRFs (conditional random fields) or boundary-aware losses can mitigate this but adds complexity.

''Class imbalance and long-tail categories:'' Real-world scenes are dominated by a few classes (road, sky, building) while safety-critical classes (pedestrian, cyclist, traffic sign) occupy tiny pixel areas. Standard cross-entropy training causes the model to under-segment rare classes. Weighted losses, oversampling, and focal loss help but do not fully resolve the issue — a model may achieve 80% mIoU overall while completely missing rare objects.

''Domain shift between training and deployment:'' A model trained on daytime European street scenes (Cityscapes) will fail on nighttime, rainy, or geographically different environments. Synthetic-to-real transfer (training on rendered data, deploying on real images) suffers from the same gap. Domain adaptation and foundation models like SAM aim to address this, but performance still degrades significantly when the deployment domain diverges from training data.

!! Technical Details

''FCN:'' Long, Shelhamer, Darrell (2015). Replaced FC layers with 1x1 convolutions. Introduced skip architecture combining predictions at multiple scales. Benchmark: PASCAL VOC 2012.

''U-Net:'' Ronneberger, Fischer, Brox (2015). Symmetric encoder-decoder with concatenation skip connections. Originally 23 convolutional layers. Won ISBI cell tracking challenge with very few training images. Spawned V-Net (3D), Attention U-Net, nnU-Net (self-configuring).

''DeepLab v3+:'' Chen et al. (2018). Xception backbone + ASPP + decoder. Atrous rates {6, 12, 18}. mIoU 89.0% on PASCAL VOC 2012 test. Output stride 8 or 16.

''Mask R-CNN:'' He, Gianno, Dollar, Girshick (2017). Extends Faster R-CNN with a 14x14 mask prediction branch per RoI. RoIAlign replaces RoIPool. 37.1 AP on COCO instance segmentation. Foundation for Detectron2.

''SegFormer:'' Xie et al. (2021). Mix Transformer (MiT) encoder with hierarchical feature maps at 1/4, 1/8, 1/16, 1/32 resolution. Lightweight All-MLP decoder. 51.8 mIoU on ADE20K (MiT-B5).

''SAM:'' Kirillov et al. (2023, Meta). ViT-H encoder (632M params) + lightweight mask decoder. Trained on SA-1B: 11M images, 1.1B masks. Promptable: points, boxes, masks, or text. SAM 2 (2024) extends to video with memory attention.

''Datasets:'' PASCAL VOC (21 classes), Cityscapes (19 classes, street scenes), ADE20K (150 classes), COCO (80 thing + 53 stuff classes), SA-1B (1.1B masks).
