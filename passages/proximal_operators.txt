! Proximal Operators
<img src="assets/proximal_operators.svg" alt="Proximal operators diagram showing smooth loss, non-smooth penalty, soft thresholding, and ISTA/FISTA/ADMM algorithms" style="max-width:100%;margin:1em 0;">

Imagine you're walking downhill on a smooth slope, but every few steps you hit a wall of thorny bushes. You can't just barrel through — you need to stop, figure out the cheapest way around (or through) the thorns, and then keep descending. The proximal operator is exactly that "cheapest detour" calculation. It handles the ugly, non-differentiable parts of your objective — like L1 penalties that force weights to zero — so that ordinary <<link "Gradient Descent" "Gradient Descent">><</link>> can keep doing its smooth-slope job on the rest.

!! The Core Insight

Many problems in machine learning ask you to minimize $f(x) + g(x)$, where $f$ is a smooth loss (differentiable, with a usable gradient) and $g$ is a non-smooth ''regularizer'' like the L1 norm $\|x\|_1$ used in the <<link "Lasso" "Lasso">><</link>>. You cannot take the gradient of $g$ at certain points — the L1 norm has a sharp kink at zero, and nuclear norms are similarly non-smooth. Gradient descent alone is helpless here.

The ''proximal operator'' of $g$ at point $v$ with step size $\gamma$ is defined as:

$$\mathrm{prox}_{\gamma g}(v) = \arg\min_{x}\left\{\frac{1}{2\gamma}\|x - v\|^2 + g(x)\right\}$$

This says: find the point $x$ that balances staying close to $v$ (the quadratic term) with keeping $g(x)$ small (the penalty term). For the L1 norm, this has a closed-form solution — the ''soft-thresholding operator'' — which shrinks each coordinate toward zero and sets small values to exactly zero. That is how <<link "Sparsity" "Sparsity">><</link>> is born from optimization.

!! Splitting Smooth and Non-Smooth

The genius of the proximal framework is ''operator splitting'': handle $f$ and $g$ separately. Instead of trying to differentiate the entire objective at once, you alternate between a gradient step on the smooth part and a proximal step on the non-smooth part:

$$x^{(k+1)} = \mathrm{prox}_{\gamma g}\!\left(x^{(k)} - \gamma \nabla f(x^{(k)})\right)$$

This is the ''Iterative Shrinkage-Thresholding Algorithm'' (ISTA). Each iteration takes a gradient step on $f$, then applies the proximal operator of $g$ to clean up. For L1 <<link "Regularization" "Regularization">><</link>>, the proximal step is soft-thresholding; for nuclear norm penalties (used in matrix completion), it is singular value soft-thresholding.

''FISTA'' (Fast ISTA) adds Nesterov momentum, improving the convergence rate from $O(1/k)$ to $O(1/k^2)$ — a dramatic speedup that costs almost nothing extra per iteration.

''ADMM'' (Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers) takes splitting further, decomposing the problem into subproblems connected by dual variables. Each subproblem can be solved with its own proximal operator, making ADMM a workhorse for large-scale distributed optimization.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The proximal operator is a ''generalized gradient step''. For smooth $g$, the proximal operator reduces to $x - \gamma \nabla g(x)$, so it subsumes ordinary gradient descent as a special case. For non-smooth $g$, it replaces the undefined gradient with something well-defined.

''Moreau envelope:'' The proximal operator comes from the ''Moreau envelope'' of $g$:

$$M_\gamma g(v) = \min_x \left\{\frac{1}{2\gamma}\|x - v\|^2 + g(x)\right\}$$

$M_\gamma g$ is always smooth (even when $g$ is not), and its gradient is:

$$\nabla M_\gamma g(v) = \frac{1}{\gamma}\left(v - \mathrm{prox}_{\gamma g}(v)\right)$$

So computing the proximal operator is equivalent to computing the gradient of the smoothed version of $g$. This is why proximal methods converge reliably — they are secretly doing gradient descent on a smooth function.

''Key proximal operators:''

L1 norm: $\mathrm{prox}_{\gamma\|\cdot\|_1}(v)_i = \mathrm{sign}(v_i)\max(|v_i| - \gamma, 0)$ (soft-thresholding)

Indicator function of a convex set $C$: $\mathrm{prox}_{I_C}(v) = \mathrm{proj}_C(v)$ (Euclidean projection)

Nuclear norm: $\mathrm{prox}_{\gamma\|\cdot\|_*}(V) = U\,\mathrm{diag}(\max(\sigma_i - \gamma, 0))\,V^T$ (singular value thresholding)

These closed forms make proximal methods practical. Without them, you'd need an inner optimization loop at every step.

!! Proximal Methods in Practice

''Compressed sensing:'' Recover sparse signals from few measurements by minimizing $\frac{1}{2}\|Ax - b\|^2 + \lambda\|x\|_1$. ISTA and FISTA are the standard solvers — the proximal step enforces sparsity while the gradient step fits the measurements.

''Matrix completion'' (Netflix problem): Minimize $\frac{1}{2}\|P_\Omega(X - M)\|^2 + \lambda\|X\|_*$ where $\|X\|_*$ is the nuclear norm encouraging low rank. The proximal operator applies singular value thresholding.

''Group Lasso:'' Extends L1 to groups of features: $\sum_g \|x_g\|_2$. The proximal operator applies block soft-thresholding — entire groups go to zero or stay, enabling structured <<link "Sparsity" "Sparsity">><</link>>.

''Deep learning connections:'' ReLU activations are the proximal operator of the indicator function of the non-negative orthant. Weight decay with L1 penalties in neural networks uses proximal steps. ADMM has been applied to neural network pruning and distributed training.

!! When Proximal Operators Break Down

''No closed-form proximal operator:'' Many penalties lack an efficient proximal operator. If computing $\mathrm{prox}_{\gamma g}$ requires solving an expensive subproblem, the per-iteration cost destroys the appeal. Non-separable, non-convex regularizers often fall into this category.

''Step size sensitivity:'' ISTA requires the step size $\gamma < 1/L$ where $L$ is the Lipschitz constant of $\nabla f$. Overestimating the step size causes divergence. Backtracking line search helps but adds overhead. In ill-conditioned problems, the required step size is tiny, leading to extremely slow progress.

''Non-convexity of $f$:'' The convergence guarantees of proximal gradient methods assume $f$ is convex (or at least has bounded non-convexity). When $f$ is highly non-convex — as in deep learning — proximal methods converge only to stationary points, and may get stuck in poor local minima. This limits their direct applicability to training neural networks, where stochastic methods dominate.

!! Technical Details

''Convergence rates:'' Proximal gradient (ISTA) converges at $O(1/k)$ for convex $f + g$. FISTA achieves $O(1/k^2)$, matching the Nesterov optimal rate for first-order methods. For strongly convex $f$, both achieve linear convergence $O(\rho^k)$ with appropriate restart strategies.

''Proximal point algorithm:'' Setting $f = 0$ gives $x^{(k+1)} = \mathrm{prox}_{\gamma g}(x^{(k)})$, which converges to a minimizer of $g$ alone. This is theoretically elegant but requires computing the full proximal operator of $g$, which may be as hard as the original problem.

''Douglas-Rachford splitting:'' A generalization that handles $\min\, g_1(x) + g_2(x)$ where //both// functions are non-smooth. ADMM is the dual form of Douglas-Rachford. The iteration alternates proximal steps for $g_1$ and $g_2$ with a reflection step.

''Stochastic proximal methods:'' Replace $\nabla f(x)$ with a stochastic gradient estimate. Convergence is preserved under standard variance assumptions, enabling proximal methods for large-scale machine learning. ''ProxSGD'' and ''ProxSVRG'' are common variants.

''Connection to <<link "Convex Optimization" "Convex Optimization">><</link>>:'' The proximal operator is the resolvent of the subdifferential operator $\partial g$. Fixed points of $\mathrm{prox}_{\gamma g}$ are exactly the minimizers of $g$. This operator-theoretic view unifies proximal methods with monotone operator splitting, a deep area of convex analysis.
