! Sufficient Statistics
<img src="assets/sufficient_statistics.svg" alt="Sufficient Statistics" width="800">

Imagine you have a thousand photographs of a sunset and someone asks you to describe its color. You don't need every pixel from every photo — a single well-chosen color swatch captures everything relevant about the hue. That's what a ''sufficient statistic'' does: it squeezes a mountain of raw data down to a compact summary while preserving //every last drop// of information about the parameter you care about. You could throw the original data away entirely, and you'd be no worse off for estimating the parameter. It's lossless compression, but only with respect to a specific question.

!! The Core Insight

A statistic $T(X)$ is ''sufficient'' for a parameter $\theta$ if the conditional distribution of the data $X$ given $T(X)$ does not depend on $\theta$. In plain language: once you know $T(X)$, the remaining randomness in the data tells you //nothing more// about $\theta$. The statistic has captured the entire "signal" about the parameter, leaving behind only noise that is independent of what you're trying to learn.

This is a remarkably powerful idea. In a world drowning in data, sufficiency tells you exactly what you can safely ignore — and guarantees you'll pay no price for doing so.

!! Compression Without Loss

The magic of sufficient statistics is that they achieve ''data reduction without information loss''. Consider drawing $n$ samples from a [[Exponential Family|Exponential Family]] distribution. You might have millions of data points, but a sufficient statistic compresses them into a fixed-dimensional summary — often just one or two numbers — that retains everything the data can tell you about the parameter.

This connects to a deep principle in [[Statistics]]: not all aspects of data are equally relevant. A sufficient statistic partitions the sample space into equivalence classes. All datasets that share the same value of $T(X)$ are //interchangeable// from the standpoint of inference about $\theta$. The specific arrangement of data within each class is pure noise.

''Minimal sufficiency'' takes this further. A ''minimal sufficient statistic'' achieves the //greatest possible compression// — it creates the coarsest partition of the sample space that is still sufficient. It's the most aggressive lossless compression you can perform. Any further reduction would discard information about $\theta$.

!! What the Math Is Actually Doing

The formal definition rests on the ''Fisher-Neyman Factorization Theorem'': a statistic $T(X)$ is sufficient for $\theta$ if and only if the likelihood can be factored as:

$$p(x \mid \theta) = h(x) \cdot g(T(x), \theta)$$

Here $h(x)$ depends only on the data (not $\theta$), and $g$ depends on $\theta$ only through $T(x)$. The entire dependence of the likelihood on $\theta$ is channeled through the statistic.

''Gaussian mean example:'' For $X_1, \ldots, X_n \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with known $\sigma^2$:

$$p(x \mid \mu) = \left(\frac{1}{2\pi\sigma^2}\right)^{n/2} \exp\left(-\frac{\sum x_i^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) \cdot \exp\left(-\frac{n\mu^2 - 2\mu \sum x_i}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

The term involving $\mu$ depends on the data only through $T(X) = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$. So the sample sum (or equivalently the sample mean $\bar{X}$) is sufficient for $\mu$.

''Bernoulli example:'' For $X_1, \ldots, X_n \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$:

$$p(x \mid p) = p^{\sum x_i}(1-p)^{n - \sum x_i} = 1 \cdot p^{T}(1-p)^{n-T}$$

where $T = \sum x_i$. The total count of successes is sufficient — the //order// in which successes occurred is irrelevant for learning $p$.

''Poisson example:'' For $X_1, \ldots, X_n \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$:

$$p(x \mid \lambda) = \frac{1}{\prod x_i!} \cdot \frac{e^{-n\lambda} \lambda^{\sum x_i}}{1}$$

Again $T = \sum x_i$ is sufficient for $\lambda$. The factorization separates the $\lambda$-dependent piece cleanly.

In each case, the sufficient statistic has the same dimension regardless of $n$ — a dramatic compression that grows more impressive as data accumulates.

!! Sufficiency in ML and Statistics

''Exponential families and natural parameters:'' The [[Exponential Family]] distributions have the form $p(x \mid \theta) = h(x) \exp(\eta(\theta)^T T(x) - A(\theta))$. The vector $T(x)$ is automatically a sufficient statistic — this is //why// exponential families are so central to statistics. The natural parameters $\eta$ and sufficient statistics $T$ are conjugate pairs.

''Bayesian connections:'' In [[Bayesian Inference|Bayesian Inference]], sufficiency means the posterior depends on data only through $T(X)$: $p(\theta \mid x) \propto p(\theta) \cdot g(T(x), \theta)$. This is precisely why [[Bayesian Methods|Bayesian Methods]] work so cleanly with exponential families — the ''conjugate prior'' structure emerges naturally when you update using sufficient statistics.

''[[Maximum Likelihood|Maximum Likelihood]] estimation:'' The MLE depends on data only through the sufficient statistic. This is immediate from the factorization theorem: maximizing $g(T(x), \theta)$ over $\theta$ requires only $T(x)$.

''[[Fisher Information|Fisher Information]]:'' The sufficient statistic carries all the Fisher information about $\theta$. Any other statistic of the same dimension must carry strictly //less// information, formalizing the sense in which sufficiency means "no information loss."

!! When Sufficiency Breaks Down

- ''No finite-dimensional sufficient statistic exists for many non-exponential-family models.'' For instance, the uniform distribution $\text{Uniform}(0, \theta)$ has sufficient statistics (the order statistics), but mixture models and heavy-tailed distributions like the Cauchy often lack low-dimensional sufficient statistics. When the data cannot be usefully compressed, every individual observation matters.

- ''Sufficiency is always relative to a specific model and parameter.'' A statistic sufficient for the mean of a Gaussian is //not// sufficient if you also need to learn the variance. Model misspecification — using the wrong distributional assumption — can make your chosen sufficient statistic discard exactly the information that matters.

- ''In high-dimensional and nonparametric settings, sufficiency becomes vacuous.'' When the parameter space is as rich as the data space, the only sufficient statistic is the data itself. This is why modern deep learning, which operates in effectively nonparametric regimes, cannot rely on classical sufficient statistics and must process raw data directly.

!! Technical Details

''Rao-Blackwell Theorem:'' Given any unbiased estimator $\delta(X)$ of $\theta$ and a sufficient statistic $T$, the conditional expectation $\delta^*(T) = E[\delta(X) \mid T]$ is also unbiased and has variance no greater than $\delta$. This provides a systematic way to improve estimators by "Rao-Blackwellizing" them — conditioning on a sufficient statistic can never hurt and typically helps. Combined with completeness, it yields the unique best unbiased estimator.

''Completeness:'' A sufficient statistic $T$ is ''complete'' if $E[g(T)] = 0$ for all $\theta$ implies $g(T) = 0$ almost surely. Completeness rules out "redundancy" in the statistic. A statistic that is both complete and sufficient is called ''boundedly complete sufficient'', and the Lehmann-Scheffe theorem guarantees that any unbiased function of it is the uniformly minimum variance unbiased estimator (UMVUE).

''Ancillary statistics:'' A statistic $S(X)$ is ''ancillary'' if its distribution does not depend on $\theta$ — it carries no information about the parameter by itself, yet may still be useful for conditional inference. Ancillary statistics are in a sense the "opposite" of sufficient statistics.

''Basu's Theorem:'' If $T$ is a complete sufficient statistic and $S$ is ancillary, then $T$ and $S$ are //independent//. This elegant result connects three seemingly different concepts — completeness, sufficiency, and ancillarity — and provides a powerful shortcut for proving independence in many statistical problems.

''Information inequality connection:'' The Cramer-Rao lower bound states that no unbiased estimator can have variance below $1/I(\theta)$ where $I(\theta)$ is the [[Fisher Information|Fisher Information]]. When a complete sufficient statistic exists, the UMVUE based on it achieves this bound, making it //efficient//. Not all models admit efficient estimators, but when they do, sufficiency is the mechanism.
